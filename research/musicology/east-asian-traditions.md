## I. Korean Traditions

### 1. Samulnori

#### Origins and the 1978 Revolution

Samulnori (literally "four things playing") represents one of the most significant musical innovations in twentieth-century Korean culture. On February 22, 1978, in a small theater called Space Theater in Seoul, four musicians — **Kim Duk-soo**, **Kim Yong-bae**, **Choe Jong-seok**, and **Choe Tae-hyeon** — performed what would become the inaugural samulnori concert. What they created was a stage adaptation of *nongak* (farmers' music), distilling centuries of outdoor communal percussion into an intense, virtuosic indoor concert form.

Kim Duk-soo, widely regarded as the father of samulnori, had grown up immersed in the itinerant performer tradition (*namsadang*). His genius was in recognizing that the raw power of Korean rural percussion could be recontextualized for the concert stage without sacrificing its spiritual depth.

#### The Four Instruments and Cosmological Symbolism

The samulnori ensemble consists of precisely four instruments, each carrying profound cosmological significance rooted in Korean yin-yang philosophy and natural symbolism:

| Instrument | Type | Material | Cosmological Symbol | Yin/Yang |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Kkwaenggwari** (꽹과리) | Small handheld gong | Metal | Thunder (뇌) | Yang |
| **Jing** (징) | Large hanging gong | Metal | Wind (풍) | Yin |
| **Janggu** (장구) | Hourglass drum | Leather/Wood | Rain (우) | Yang |
| **Buk** (북) | Barrel drum | Leather/Wood | Clouds (운) | Yin |

This cosmological mapping is not merely decorative. The metal instruments (kkwaenggwari and jing) represent the heavens, while the leather instruments (janggu and buk) represent the earth. Within each pair, one instrument is yang (active, bright, leading) and the other yin (sustained, deep, supporting). The kkwaenggwari, with its piercing, cutting tone, leads the ensemble and embodies the explosive energy of thunder. The jing, with its long, resonant wash of sound, represents the pervasive quality of wind. The janggu's intricate, rain-like patterns connect the heavens to the earth, and the buk's deep, rolling strikes evoke the mass and presence of clouds.

The performers' musical expression communicates the symbolic ties between humanity, heaven, and earth — reflecting the traditional relationship between farmer-performer, music, and the natural environment. This cosmology is not an abstraction imposed by scholars; it is a living philosophy embedded in the music's structure and the performers' understanding of what they do.

#### Musical Structure

Samulnori performances typically follow a progression of rhythmic cycles (*changdan*) that accelerate from slow and meditative to furiously fast, creating an arc of tension and release. The kkwaenggwari player serves as the musical director, signaling transitions through specific rhythmic cues. The interplay between the four instruments creates dense polyrhythmic textures that shift between unison passages and moments of individual improvisation.

### 2. Pungmul/Nongak

#### The Communal Heart of Korean Music

Pungmul (풍물) is the broader tradition from which samulnori was derived — a Korean folk percussion practice steeped in pageantry, music, dance, and theater, but centrally focused on rhythm. Known also as *nongak* (농악, "farmers' music"), these ensembles have been an integral part of village life for centuries, serving as musical accompaniment in the overlapping contexts of labor, ritual, and entertainment.

#### Agricultural Roots and the Dure System

Pungmul traces its origins to the collective labor systems of pre-modern Korean agrarian society, particularly the *dure* (두레) practice — cooperative farming arrangements where villagers worked together on rice planting, transplanting seedlings, weeding, and harvesting. Percussion ensembles provided rhythmic accompaniment that served multiple practical functions:

- **Synchronization**: The rhythms coordinated physical movements among large groups of laborers
- **Endurance**: Music alleviated the monotony of repetitive agricultural work
- **Solidarity**: Communal music-making reinforced social bonds and collective identity
- **Spiritual protection**: Rhythmic sound was believed to purify spaces and drive away harmful spirits

#### Performance Structure and Roles

A pungmul performance comprises two major sections. In the first, the front players (instrumentalists) perform while forming various dynamic configurations, following the musical direction of the lead kkwaenggwari player, called the *sangsoe*. The sangsoe functions as both musical director and choreographer, signaling changes in rhythm, tempo, formation, and energy through specific patterns on the small gong.

In the second portion, rear players offer diverse performances involving dance, music, and theater, including acrobatic feats such as the *sangmo* (hat spinning with a long ribbon), *sogo* (small hand drum dance), and comedic skits.

Each ensemble includes at minimum:
- **Kkwaenggwari** players (often multiple)
- **Janggu** players
- **Buk** players
- **Jing** player(s)
- Wind instrument players (*taepyeongso* or *hojeok*)
- Dancers, flag bearers, and performers

#### Regional Styles

Korean pungmul traditions vary significantly by region, with five major provincial styles recognized:

- **Gyeonggi/Chungcheong** — characterized by refined, balanced rhythms
- **Honam Jwado** (left Jeolla) — known for intricate kkwaenggwari technique
- **Honam Udo** (right Jeolla) — noted for janggu virtuosity
- **Yeongnam** (Gyeongsang) — powerful, martial character
- **Yeongseo/Yeongdong** (Gangwon) — distinct mountain-region styles

#### UNESCO Recognition

Pungmul was inscribed on UNESCO's Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity under the title "Nongak" in November 2014 by South Korea (and as "Joseonjok Nongak" by China in 2009), recognizing its deep cultural significance and its role as a living tradition.

### 3. Changdan

#### The Korean Rhythmic Cycle System

Changdan (장단, also romanized as *jangdan*) is the foundational concept of rhythm in Korean traditional music. The term literally means "long-short," referring to the alternation of accented and unaccented beats, but it encompasses far more than simple meter. A changdan is a repeating rhythmic cycle — a pattern played on percussion instruments (primarily the janggu) that defines the temporal framework for an entire musical performance. Each changdan carries its own character, emotional quality, tempo range, and structural logic.

Unlike Western time signatures, which primarily indicate groupings of beats, changdan patterns encode specific accent patterns, timbral variation (through different drum strokes), dynamic contour, and emotional affect. Mastering changdan means understanding not just *when* to strike but *how*, *where*, and *why*.

#### Major Changdan Patterns

**Jinyangjo (진양조)** — The slowest changdan, with a cycle of 24 beats in compound meter. Solemn, meditative, and deeply expressive. Used in the opening sections of *sanjo* (solo instrumental music) and the most sorrowful passages of *pansori*. Each beat is so slow that subdivisions within beats become melodic events in themselves.

**Jungmori (중모리)** — A moderate 12-beat cycle (12/4), dignified and stately. Often described as walking pace. Central to pansori narrative passages and sanjo development sections.

**Jungjungmori (중중모리)** — A brisk 12/8 pattern, energetic and forward-moving. Used in exciting, dramatic passages — sometimes expressing struggle, sometimes excitement. The transition from jungmori to jungjungmori marks a significant increase in energy.

**Jajinmori (자진모리)** — A fast 12/8 cycle that generates active, enchanting feelings. Prevalent in pansori, sanjo, nongak, and shamanistic music (*muga*), especially in dramatic and urgent passages. The pattern creates a driving, propulsive energy.

**Hwimori (휘모리)** — The fastest standard changdan, its name literally means "whirling." As fast as a whirlwind, hwimori is used in climactic passages of pansori and sanjo, creating nearly overwhelming rhythmic intensity.

**Gutgeori (굿거리)** — A widely used 12-beat cycle (12/8, moderate tempo) that is the most common changdan in folk music alongside semachi. Its swaying, dance-like quality makes it the rhythmic foundation for countless folk songs. The basic rhythm is often vocalized as *deong-gi-deok kung deo-reo-reo-reo kung-gi-deok kung deo-reo-reo-reo*.

**Semachi (세마치)** — A 9/8 cycle (three groups of three), lighter and more buoyant than gutgeori. Used extensively in shaman music and folk songs with spiritual themes.

**Eotmori (엇모리)** — An irregular 10/8 cycle (groupings of 3+2+3+2 or similar), creating an asymmetric, off-kilter feeling. This changdan is particularly significant for drum machine design as it demonstrates that Korean rhythm has always embraced what Western music theory would call "odd" or "irregular" meters.

#### The Accelerating Arc

A classic progression through changdan in sanjo or pansori follows a trajectory of increasing speed and intensity:

> **Jinyangjo** (very slow) → **Jungmori** (moderate) → **Jungjungmori** (brisk) → **Jajinmori** (fast) → **Hwimori** (very fast)

This is not merely a formal convention but reflects a deep aesthetic principle: the gradual intensification of emotional and physical energy, moving from contemplation to ecstasy. This parallels the Japanese *jo-ha-kyu* concept, suggesting a shared East Asian sensibility about temporal structure.

### 4. Janggu Technique

#### The Two-Voiced Drum

The janggu (장구, also spelled *changgo*) is arguably the most important instrument in Korean traditional music. Its hourglass shape — narrow in the middle, flaring to two drum heads of different diameter and tension — produces two fundamentally different sounds, creating what is essentially a two-voiced instrument.

#### Physical Construction and Philosophy

The two heads of the janggu are believed to represent the harmonious joining of *um* (음, yin) and *yang* (양). The left head (the larger, lower-pitched side) represents the bass voice of the earth, while the right head (smaller, higher-pitched) represents the treble voice of heaven. The hourglass body connecting them represents the human body mediating between these cosmic forces.

#### Playing Technique

**Left Hand — Gungchae (궁채):**
The left hand holds the *gungchae*, a rounded mallet made of wood with a padded tip. It strikes the left (bass) drum head from a perpendicular angle, producing a deep, resonant tone called *gung* (궁). The technique involves a rebounding motion — the mallet strikes and bounces back, allowing the head to vibrate freely. Notably, the gungchae can also reach across to strike the right side of the drum, providing additional tonal variety.

**Right Hand — Yeolchae (열채):**
The right hand holds the *yeolchae*, a thin bamboo stick that produces a sharp, bright, high-pitched tone called *byeon* (변) on the right drum head. The technique is distinct: the stick moves laterally, staying flush against the drum head rather than rebounding, creating a crisp, cutting attack.

#### Stroke Vocabulary

The janggu's stroke vocabulary includes:

- **Gung (궁)**: Deep bass stroke on the left head
- **Ddeok (덕)**: Sharp treble stroke on the right head
- **Gung-ddeok (궁덕)**: Simultaneous strike on both heads
- **Deo-reo-reo-reo (더러러러)**: A rolling pattern on the right head
- Various grace notes, ghost notes, and ornamental strokes

The interplay between the bass and treble voices allows a single janggu player to create remarkably complex rhythmic textures, effectively functioning as a one-person rhythm section.

### 5. Korean Court Music (Jeongak)

#### Jongmyo Jeryeak: Ritual Music for Royal Ancestors

Jongmyo Jeryeak (종묘제례악) is the music performed during the *Jongmyo Jerye*, the Confucian ritual held at the Jongmyo Shrine in Seoul to honor the deceased monarchs of the Joseon Dynasty (1392-1910). This tradition was designated a **Masterpiece of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity by UNESCO in 2001**, and inscribed on the Representative List in 2008.

#### Historical Roots

The ritual was established under King Taejo, the founder of the Joseon Dynasty in the fourteenth century. The order of the ceremony was formalized in the fifteenth century, and most elements have remained virtually unchanged to the present day. The ritual is now practiced once a year, on the first Sunday in May, organized by the descendants of the royal family.

#### Two Orchestras

Jongmyo Jeryeak employs two distinct orchestral ensembles:

- **Deungga (등가)**: An orchestra that performs on the terrace of the shrine
- **Heonga (헌가)**: An orchestra positioned on the ground of the courtyard

Both ensembles use traditional instruments including gongs, bells, lutes, zithers, flutes, and drums. The spatial separation creates a dialogue between the elevated (heavenly) and grounded (earthly) sonic planes.

#### Ritual Dance (Ilmu)

What makes Jongmyo Jeryeak particularly compelling is its holistic integration of instrumental music, vocal music, and ceremonial dance (*ilmu*, 일무). Sixty-four dancers arranged in eight rows perform precisely synchronized movements symbolizing the cosmic order. The dances embody the opposing yet complementary forces of yin and yang as articulated in Confucian texts. The percussion instruments within the ensemble mark the structural divisions of the ritual, providing temporal scaffolding for the dancers and the overall ceremony.

#### Broader Jeongak Tradition

Jongmyo Jeryeak exists within the broader category of *jeongak* (정악, "proper music" or court music), which encompasses various forms of music associated with the Korean aristocratic class. Jeongak is characterized by slow tempos, long melodic lines, and a sense of profound gravity and restraint — qualities that stand in marked contrast to the vibrant energy of folk percussion traditions.

### 6. Shaman Music (Gut)

#### Rhythm as Portal

Korean shamanic music occupies a unique position in world music traditions. Korea is the only country where shamanism appears to have functioned as a state religion practiced by literate classes, during the Three Kingdoms period (57 BCE - 668 CE). This early official status explains why shamanic rituals in Korea developed into highly complex and established forms — and correspondingly, the music used in these rituals is more elaborate than shamanic music found anywhere else in the world.

#### The Gut Ceremony

*Gut* (굿) are the central rites performed by Korean shamans (*mudang*, 무당). These large-scale ceremonies — sometimes lasting several days — are characterized by rhythmic movements, songs, oracles, offerings, and prayers. Their purpose is to create welfare and promote engagement between the spirits and humankind: healing the sick, guiding the souls of the dead, ensuring good harvests, and purifying spaces.

#### Musical Elements

In gut ceremonies, percussion and wind instruments play the most consistent role:

- **Drums and gongs** sustain rhythmic energy throughout the ritual and aid in inducing trance states
- **Loud instruments** are believed to repel malignant spirits
- **Woodwinds** (especially the *piri* oboe) provide melodic depth
- **String instruments** appear in some regional traditions

The musicians, called *aksa* (악사), create ensembles specifically for a kut. However, hereditary *mudang* are themselves musicians, possessing the ability to play many instruments, sing, and conduct the accompanying orchestra. The shaman is simultaneously priest, performer, and musical director.

#### Regional Traditions

Shamanic music exhibits distinct features across five major Korean regions:

- **Eastern (Yeongdong/Gangwon)**: Relies heavily on percussion with complex rhythmic structures; the most rhythmically intense regional style
- **Gyeonggi (central)**: More melodic, with balanced percussion accompaniment
- **Jeolla (southwest)**: Known for emotional depth and musical sophistication
- **Gyeongsang (southeast)**: Martial, powerful rhythmic character
- **Pyeongan/Hwanghae (northwest, now North Korea)**: Distinctive possession-trance styles with specific rhythmic triggers

Each region has its own gods, its own sacred songs, and its own characteristic rhythms. The connection between specific changdan patterns and the invocation of particular deities is a core element — certain rhythms are understood to "call" specific spirits.

#### Rhythm and Trance

The relationship between rhythm and trance states in Korean shamanic practice is one of the most extensively documented examples of this phenomenon worldwide. The gradual acceleration of drumming, combined with specific rhythmic patterns, facilitates the mudang's transition into altered states of consciousness. The drum is not merely accompanying the ritual — it is actively producing the spiritual transformation.

### 7. Pansori

#### Epic Storytelling Through Rhythm

Pansori (판소리) is a Korean genre of epic narrative singing, inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2003. A performance involves only two people: a singer (*sorikkun*, 소리꾼) and a drummer (*gosu*, 고수) who accompanies on the *buk* (barrel drum) while shouting improvised interjections (*chuimsae*, 추임새).

#### The Gosu: More Than a Drummer

The Korean expression *"il gosu, i myeongchang"* (일고수 이명창) — "first the drummer, second the master singer" — reveals the extraordinary status of the gosu in pansori performance. Far from being a mere accompanist, the gosu is the singer's primary scene partner, emotional anchor, and structural architect.

The gosu's responsibilities include:

- **Rhythmic foundation**: Playing the appropriate changdan patterns that underpin each passage
- **Emotional navigation**: Adjusting dynamics, tempo, and rhythmic intensity to match and shape the dramatic arc
- **Chuimsae**: Shouting encouragements, reactions, and emotional punctuation — sounds like *eolssigu!* (얼씨구), *jotha!* (좋다), *geurae!* (그래) — that function as both audience response and performative guidance
- **Structural signaling**: Indicating transitions between changdan patterns, marking the shift from narrative to lyrical passages

The gosu does not simply keep time. Through skillful drumming, the gosu supports and elevates the sorikkun's performance, ensuring that each tale resonates deeply. The intricate dance between the two performers exemplifies the collaborative spirit of this art form.

#### Pansori's Five Surviving Stories

Of originally twelve *madang* (episodes), five survive in active performance:

1. **Chunhyangga** — the story of faithful love
2. **Simcheongga** — the story of filial piety
3. **Heungbuga** — the story of two brothers (kindness vs. greed)
4. **Sugungga** — the underwater palace (the rabbit and the turtle)
5. **Jeokbyeokga** — the story of the Red Cliff battle

Each complete madang can last four to eight hours. The drummer must sustain focus, energy, and musical sensitivity throughout, navigating the full range of changdan from the glacial jinyangjo to the furious hwimori.

### 8. Modern Korean Music

#### K-pop and Rhythm

K-pop's relationship with traditional Korean rhythmic sensibility is complex and contested. On the surface, K-pop overwhelmingly adopts Western pop structures — 4/4 time, backbeat emphasis, electronic production. However, scholars and musicians have identified subtler connections:

- Traditional Korean rhythms often employ compound meters of three-beat or five-beat patterns. In adapting these for pop audiences, producers sometimes reframe them into four-beat structures while preserving the underlying percussive energy.
- Seo Taiji and Boys, who revolutionized Korean popular music in the 1990s, fused aggressive rap and rhythmic beats with what has been described as "Korean musical intricacy."
- Some K-pop choreography echoes the group synchronization principles of pungmul and court dance.
- The *bbang bbang* (빵빵) rhythmic feel — a slightly swung or syncopated groove common in certain K-pop subgenres — has been linked by some musicologists to the "breathing" quality of traditional Korean rhythm.

Yet it would be misleading to overstate this connection. K-pop is primarily a product of global pop production culture, and its rhythmic DNA is more directly traceable to American hip-hop, R&B, and EDM than to changdan patterns.

#### Korean Hip-Hop

The Korean hip-hop movement has deep roots that predate K-pop's global dominance:

- **Tiger JK** and **Drunken Tiger** are widely credited as the founders of commercially successful Korean hip-hop. Tiger JK, along with his wife **Yoon Mi-rae** (a Texas-born rapper who debuted in Korea at age 16), formed the **Movement Crew** — an influential hip-hop community that nurtured groups like Dynamic Duo, Leessang, and Epik High.
- **Keith Ape** became a viral global phenomenon in 2015 with "It G Ma," a collaborative track featuring Korean and Japanese rappers (JayAllDay, Okasian, Kohh, and Loota). Keith Ape led Seoul's **Underwater Squad**, representing a new generation of Korean hip-hop that was natively global.
- The underground scene centered in Seoul's Hongdae district, with clubs like Master Plan hosting performances by Verbal Jint, Dynamic Duo, and early Epik High.
- Korean hip-hop's rhythmic approach often incorporates the double-time flows and syncopated delivery styles that distinguish it from both American and Japanese hip-hop traditions.

#### Korean Electronic Music

Korean electronic producers have made significant inroads into global club culture:

- **Peggy Gou** (Kim Min-ji), born in Seoul and based in Berlin, became one of the most visible DJs in the world through energetic sets flowing through techno, house, and electro. She founded Gudu Records in 2019 and released her debut album *I Hear You* in 2024.
- **Park Hye Jin** blends house music with Korean-language rapping, earning the descriptor "K-House." Her debut album *Before I Die* (2021) demonstrated how Korean vocal delivery could find a natural home within electronic dance music structures.
- **Yaeji** similarly bridges Korean and English vocals over hazy house and experimental electronic production.

#### Fusion: Where Traditional Meets Now

The most musically significant contemporary Korean artists for the BeatForge project may be those explicitly bridging traditional and modern:

- **Jambinai** (잠비나이) — Formed in 2009 by three musicians trained in *gugak* (Korean traditional music), Jambinai combines *geomungo* (six-string zither), *haegeum* (fiddle), and *piri* (oboe) with electric guitar, bass, synth, and drums. The result is a visceral fusion of post-rock, metal, and traditional Korean music that has earned them appearances at major international festivals.
- **SsingSsing** (씽씽) — A now-disbanded six-member group that fused Korean folk singing with glam rock, disco, and psychedelic rock. Their 2017 NPR Tiny Desk Concert went viral, demonstrating the global appeal of Korean folk vocal traditions in contemporary contexts.
- **Black String** — Another ensemble drawing from Korean music traditions, combining gayageum with jazz and world music elements.

These groups represent a growing movement sometimes called **"Joseon Pop"** — artists who draw directly from Korea's pre-modern musical heritage while speaking in contemporary global musical languages.

## II. Japanese Traditions

### 9. Taiko: Deep History

#### Mythological Origins

Taiko drums occupy a central place in Japanese mythology. According to Shinto tradition, taiko was born when the goddess **Ame-no-Uzume** danced wildly atop an empty sake barrel to lure the sun goddess Amaterasu out of a cave where she had hidden, plunging the world into darkness. This origin story links drumming to the restoration of light, life, and cosmic order.

#### Archaeological Evidence

Historical evidence suggests that drums were present in Japan from the earliest periods. Archaeological finds include pottery from the Haniwa period depicting taiko drums, and the earliest evidence of taiko-like instruments dates to Japan's Jomon period (10,000-300 BCE), where drums appear to have been used in religious ceremonies. The instruments as known today were significantly influenced by Chinese and Korean cultural transmission as early as the 6th century CE.

#### Functions Through History

Taiko have served extraordinarily diverse functions across Japanese history:

**Spiritual and Religious:**
- In **Shinto shrines**, taiko mark sacred time, accompany *kagura* (sacred music and dance), and summon the spirits of *kami* (deities)
- In **Buddhist temples**, taiko mark the passage of time during meditation, signal the beginning and end of rituals, and accompany the *Bon Festival* dances honoring ancestors

**Military:**
- **Samurai warriors** used taiko on the battlefield to boost morale, intimidate enemies, and communicate tactical commands. Different rhythmic patterns conveyed specific orders — advance, retreat, rally
- The military use reinforced the association between taiko and masculine power, discipline, and martial virtue

**Community Communication:**
- Villages used various taiko beat combinations as a communication system — signaling approaching storms, the departure and return of hunting parties, and the times for communal activities

**Festival and Celebration:**
- Taiko are essential to *matsuri* (festivals) throughout Japan, with some festival drumming traditions dating back centuries. The Izaku Taiko Odori, for example, originated in 1406 as a victory dance

### 10. Kumi-Daiko

#### Daihachi Oguchi and the Birth of Ensemble Taiko (1951)

The transformation of taiko from a single-drum tradition into the ensemble art form known worldwide today is largely attributable to one man: **Daihachi Oguchi** (1924-2008). A jazz drummer by training, Oguchi was asked in 1951 to interpret an old sheet of taiko music discovered in a warehouse near Suwa Shrine. The notation, written in archaic Japanese style, was initially indecipherable to him. He tracked down an elderly man who had performed the piece and, with his help, decoded the music.

Drawing on his jazz background, Oguchi had a radical insight: if jazz employed multiple drummers playing complementary parts, why not taiko? He broke the ancient rhythms down into separate parts and arranged them for an ensemble of drummers playing different-sized instruments. The result was **Osuwa Daiko**, founded in 1951 — the first *kumi-daiko* (組太鼓, "group taiko") ensemble.

#### The Movement Grows

The kumi-daiko concept spread rapidly:

- **Ondekoza** (鬼太鼓座, "Demon Drummers") — Founded in 1969 by Den Tagayasu on Sado Island, Ondekoza established an austere communal training regimen that included long-distance running (members famously ran the Boston Marathon before performing). Their approach emphasized taiko as a total physical and spiritual discipline.
- **Kodo** (鼓童, "Children of the Drum") — Formed in 1981 when members split from Ondekoza, Kodo became the most internationally recognized taiko group. Based on Sado Island, they tour worldwide and host the annual **Earth Celebration** festival, promoting cultural exchange through drumming.
- **The 1964 Tokyo Olympics** — Taiko performances at the Olympics ignited a national taiko boom that continued for decades.

#### Philosophy and Training

Kumi-daiko is inseparable from its physical and philosophical dimensions. The connection between taiko and martial arts training is explicit: proper form (*kata*), centered posture, disciplined breathing, and the channeling of *ki* (life energy) are as important as rhythmic accuracy. Many taiko groups incorporate running, meditation, and community service into their training regimens. The drum is struck not merely with the arms but with the entire body — the legs, the hips, the core, and the spirit.

### 11. Taiko Notation and Technique

#### Kuchi Shoga: The Oral Notation System

Taiko music is transmitted primarily through an oral tradition called **kuchi shoga** (口唱歌, literally "mouth singing"). Rather than written notation, each type of drumbeat is represented by a specific vocalized sound. This system uses Japanese sound symbolism — the sounds themselves evoke the timbral quality of the stroke they represent.

#### Core Notation Vocabulary

| Sound | Meaning | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Don** (ドン) | Center hit | A deep, sustained, resonant sound from the center of the drum head |
| **Do** (ド) | Lighter center hit | A softer version of don |
| **Ka** (カ) | Rim/edge hit | A sharp, high-pitched tap on the rim of the drum |
| **Tsu** (ツ) | Soft center hit | A quiet, ghost-note touch on the drum center |
| **Su** (ス) | Rest | Silence — a musical pause |
| **Doko** (ドコ) | Right-left alternation | Two strokes in sequence (right hand, left hand) |
| **Tsuku** (ツク) | Two quiet hits | Two soft hits in the center |
| **Kara** (カラ) | Two rim hits | Two strokes on the rim in succession |
| **Doro** (ドロ) | Roll | A sustained rolling pattern |

This system has profound implications for how rhythms are learned and transmitted. When a teacher vocalizes *"DON doko DON DON, DON doko DON DON"*, students simultaneously hear the rhythm, internalize its timbral character, and can reproduce it vocally before ever touching a drum. The oral tradition ensures that rhythm is embodied in the voice and body before it is expressed through the instrument.

### 12. Types of Taiko

#### The Taiko Family

Japanese taiko encompass a wide family of drums, each with distinct construction, sound, and role:

**Odaiko (大太鼓) — The Great Drum:**
The largest taiko, with head diameters typically ranging from 60 to 90 cm (and some reaching over 2 meters). Too large to be carried, the odaiko produces an extraordinarily deep, powerful sound that can be felt physically. Traditionally played by one or two players, the odaiko solo is considered one of the most physically demanding and spiritually intense performances in the taiko repertoire — a single performer facing a massive drum, channeling everything through their body.

**Nagado-daiko (長胴太鼓) — The Long-Bodied Drum:**
The most common taiko in kumi-daiko performance, roughly barrel-shaped and carved from a single piece of wood. The heads are attached with *byou* (tacks/nails), making them non-tunable. The nagado-daiko is the "workhorse" of ensemble taiko — versatile, projecting, and capable of a wide dynamic range.

**Shime-daiko (締太鼓) — The Tightened Drum:**
A smaller, higher-pitched drum roughly the size of a snare drum. The heads are stretched over steel or iron rings and tensioned with ropes or bolts, making the shime-daiko *tunable*. In ensemble playing, the shime-daiko often carries the *jiuchi* (base rhythm), functioning analogously to a hi-hat or ride cymbal in a drum kit.

**Okedo-daiko (桶太鼓) — The Bucket Drum:**
Constructed using a stave technique (narrow wooden strips joined together, like a barrel), the okedo-daiko is lighter than the nagado-daiko and produces a mid-range pitch between the deep nagado and the bright shime. Its rope-tensioned construction allows for some pitch adjustment. The lighter weight makes it suitable for carrying during processions and festival performances.

### 13. Jiuchi: Base Rhythms

#### The Rhythmic Foundation

*Jiuchi* (地打ち, literally "ground striking") is the base rhythm — the underlying pulse that holds the entire ensemble together. Commonly referred to simply as *ji*, it is usually played on the shime-daiko throughout a piece, providing a consistent rhythmic reference for the other instruments.

#### Common Jiuchi Patterns

**Don Doko (ドンドコ):**
The most fundamental ji pattern — a simple, even alternation. Vocalized as "don doko don doko," it establishes a steady pulse in duple meter.

**Don Ko (ドンコ):**
A variation emphasizing the downbeat, with the second stroke lighter or ghost-noted.

**Dongo (ドンゴ) — The Swing Ji:**
A crucial variation where the rhythm is "swung" — the upbeat (*go*) is not evenly spaced between downbeats but pushed toward the back of the beat. This creates a triplet feel where the first note of each pair is roughly twice as long as the second. The similarity to jazz swing is unmistakable — both Oguchi's jazz background and deep Japanese rhythmic traditions contributed to this pattern's prominence.

**Horse-Beat Ji:**
Vocalized as "don dogo don dogo" — a more complex pattern that creates a galloping, propulsive energy. In Western notation, it approximates: 1.&a 2.&a 3.&a 4.&a

**Matsuri (Festival) Ji:**
The quintessential festival rhythm: "DON doko don DON, DON doko don DON" — with heavy accents creating the joyful, driving feel associated with Japanese festivals.

### 14. Gagaku

#### The Oldest Continuous Orchestral Tradition

Gagaku (雅楽, "elegant music") is the imperial court music of Japan and represents the **oldest surviving orchestral music tradition in the world**. The term originated in 701 CE when the *Gagaku-ryo*, a government bureau overseeing music education and performance, was established. Gagaku was inscribed on UNESCO's Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2009.

#### Origins and Transmission

Most gagaku music was imported from China and Korea as early as the 6th century and established as a court tradition by the 8th century. During the Heian period (794-1185), these foreign elements were absorbed, refined, and fused with indigenous Japanese musical styles, creating a distinctly Japanese art form. The music is categorized into:

- **Togaku** — music derived from Chinese (Tang Dynasty) traditions
- **Komagaku** — music derived from Korean traditions
- **Kangen** — purely instrumental ensemble music
- **Bugaku** — music accompanying dance
- **Saibara** and **Roei** — vocal forms influenced by folk and Chinese poetry traditions

#### The Percussion Section

The gagaku ensemble includes three percussion instruments:

- **Kakko** (鞨鼓) — A small, barrel-shaped drum placed horizontally on a stand and struck with two thin sticks. The kakko player serves as the *conductor* of the ensemble, controlling tempo through specific rhythmic signals. In togaku repertoire, the kakko leads the ensemble's temporal flow.
- **Shoko** (鉦鼓) — A small bronze gong struck with two horn-tipped sticks. It provides metallic punctuation at specific structural points.
- **Taiko/Dadaiko** (太鼓/大太鼓) — A large drum suspended in an ornate frame, struck with padded mallets. It marks the largest structural divisions of the music.

#### Jo-Ha-Kyu: The Aesthetic of Temporal Flow

The most profound contribution of gagaku to world music aesthetics is the concept of **jo-ha-kyu** (序破急):

- **Jo (序)** — "Introduction": slow, unmeasured, establishing
- **Ha (破)** — "Breaking/Development": measured, accelerating, building
- **Kyu (急)** — "Rapid/Climax": fast, conclusive, arriving

This tripartite principle, originating in gagaku's temporal structure, was codified and expanded by **Zeami Motokiyo** (c. 1363-1443) — the master of Noh theater — into a universal aesthetic governing all Japanese performing arts. In his treatise *Fushikaden* (c. 1400), Zeami articulated jo-ha-kyu as applicable to the pacing of individual scenes, entire plays, and even the programming of a full day's performances.

Jo-ha-kyu is not merely a structural device but an expression of a natural law — the way energy moves in the universe, from stillness through motion to culmination. It parallels the Korean changdan acceleration principle and finds echoes in Western sonata form (exposition-development-recapitulation), though its emphasis on *rhythmic acceleration* rather than thematic variation is distinctly East Asian.

### 15. Noh Theater

#### The Hayashi Ensemble

Noh (能) theater employs a four-instrument ensemble called the **hayashi** (囃子):

- **Nohkan** (能管) — a bamboo flute
- **Kotsuzumi** (小鼓) — a small shoulder drum
- **Otsuzumi** (大鼓) — a hip drum
- **Taiko** (太鼓) — used in certain plays (not all)

The two hand drums — kotsuzumi and otsuzumi — form the rhythmic heart of Noh performance.

#### Kotsuzumi: The Shoulder Drum

The kotsuzumi is held in the left hand at the right shoulder and struck with the fingers of the right hand. Its most remarkable feature is that the player can alter the pitch by squeezing and releasing the *shirabeo* (cords holding the drum heads to the hourglass body). Before performance, the drum head must be slightly moistened, giving it a softer, more resonant quality. The result is a warm, rounded tone with remarkable pitch flexibility.

#### Otsuzumi: The Hip Drum

The otsuzumi rests on the left hip and is struck by the fingers of the right hand. In contrast to the kotsuzumi, the otsuzumi heads must be extremely dry — musicians heat them over charcoal for approximately one hour before performance. This produces a sharp, piercing crack that cuts through the ensemble with penetrating clarity.

#### Interlocking Rhythms

The contrasting timbres create a continuous fluctuation of color and intensity. While each drum has its own set of patterns, they typically work as an interlocking pair — their individual patterns complementing each other to form a combined eight-beat pattern. The otsuzumi, with its louder sound, tends to be more active in the first four beats, while the softer kotsuzumi picks up in the latter four.

#### Kakegoe: The Vocal Dimension

Perhaps the most distinctive feature of Noh drumming is the **kakegoe** (掛け声) — vocal calls that the drummers produce before and between drum strokes. These calls — *"yo!"*, *"ho!"*, *"yo-ii!"*, *"ii-ya!"* — serve multiple functions:

- **Timing coordination**: Helping musicians synchronize without a visible conductor
- **Emotional coloring**: Setting and shifting the mood of a scene
- **Tempo management**: Communicating acceleration or deceleration between players
- **Spiritual energy**: Adding a human vocal dimension to the percussion that connects breath, body, and instrument

The kakegoe are not incidental — they are integral to the music. A Noh performance without kakegoe would be fundamentally incomplete, like jazz without swing.

### 16. Kabuki

#### Tsuke and Ki: Wood as Percussion

Kabuki (歌舞伎) theater uses percussion in ways that blur the line between music and sound design:

**Ki (木) — Wooden Clappers:**
Two rectangular wooden blocks (*hyoshigi*) struck together produce a sharp, penetrating crack. Ki serves multiple functions:
- Signaling the start and end of performances
- Cueing actors on timing
- Creating dramatic emphasis at key moments
- Building tension through accelerating patterns (the famous *ki* pattern before a curtain rises)

**Tsuke (付け) — Stage-Side Percussion:**
A performer kneels on the stage (*kamite* side) with two wooden sticks and a wooden board (*tsuke-ita*). By striking the board in time with actors' movements, the tsuke player creates percussive punctuation that amplifies physical actions — running footsteps, fighting impacts, dramatic poses (*mie*). The tsuke is essentially live Foley art, performed as percussion.

#### The Kabuki Hayashi

The kabuki musical ensemble, also called hayashi, includes both on-stage (*debayashi*) and off-stage (*geza*) components:

- **On-stage**: Shamisen players and singers visible to the audience
- **Off-stage**: A larger ensemble concealed in a room just off-stage, including drums (kotsuzumi, otsuzumi, shimedaiko), flute, shamisen, and various sound-effect instruments

The off-stage ensemble creates atmospheric and dramatic percussion that underscores the action without being visible, creating a kind of invisible rhythmic architecture for the performance.

### 17. Min'yo and Festival Music

#### Regional Folk Drumming

Min'yo (民謡, "folk song") traditions vary dramatically by region, and drumming plays a central role in festival performances across Japan:

**Nebuta Festival (Aomori):**
One of Japan's most spectacular festivals, the Nebuta features massive illuminated paper floats paraded through streets to the accompaniment of energetic taiko drumming and the singing of traditional min'yo songs. The drumming drives the energy of both performers (*haneto* dancers) and spectators, with the powerful beats creating a physically overwhelming sonic environment.

**Awa Odori (Tokushima):**
The largest dance festival in Japan, attracting over 1.3 million visitors annually. Held August 12-15 during Obon, dancers perform to the rhythm of *Awa Yoshikono*, accompanied by shamisen, taiko drums, shinobue (bamboo flute), and kane (bell). The rhythmic pattern is insistent, repetitive, and hypnotic — designed to sustain hours of continuous dancing.

**Bon Odori:**
The Obon dance tradition varies by region — each community has its own local dance, music, and drumming style. The common element is a taiko drummer perched on a raised wooden scaffold (*yagura*) at the center of the dancing area, providing the rhythmic heartbeat around which the community revolves. Originally a *Nenbutsu* folk dance to welcome ancestral spirits, bon odori represents one of the most direct connections between Japanese drumming and spiritual practice at the community level.

### 18. Okinawan Music

#### The Ryukyuan Tradition

Okinawan music belongs to a distinct cultural tradition — the **Ryukyuan** tradition — that is related to but fundamentally different from mainland Japanese music. The Ryukyu Kingdom maintained independence from Japan until 1879, developing its own court music, folk traditions, scales, and instruments.

#### The Paranku Drum

The **paranku** (パーランクー) is a small hand-held drum about the size of a tambourine, with skin stretched over only one side of the shell. Struck with a stick, the paranku is the characteristic instrument of **eisa** dance. Its portability and simplicity make it ideal for the processional, community-oriented nature of Okinawan festival performance.

#### Eisa Dance

Eisa (エイサー) is a folk dance originating from Okinawa, derived from the Obon dance tradition. Modern eisa is performed by groups of 20-30 dancers, primarily in lines or circles, accompanied by singing, chanting, and drumming. Three types of drums are used:

- **Odaiko** (大太鼓) — large barrel drum
- **Shimedaiko** (締太鼓) — medium drum
- **Paranku** — small hand drum

The rhythmic foundation of eisa is insistent and propulsive, designed to sustain the vigorous movements of the dance. The **sanshin** (Okinawan three-string instrument, cousin of the shamisen) provides melodic accompaniment.

#### Distinct Musical Identity

Okinawan music uses scales and modes distinct from mainland Japanese traditions, with pentatonic scales that include intervals more reminiscent of Southeast Asian music than Japanese. This reflects Okinawa's historical position as a crossroads of maritime trade routes connecting Japan, China, and Southeast Asia. The rhythmic sensibility similarly incorporates elements not found in mainland traditions, with a more openly syncopated, groove-oriented feel.

### 19. Modern Japanese Music

#### Japanese Jazz

Japanese musicians have made extraordinary contributions to jazz, often bringing a distinctive sensibility shaped by their cultural background:

- **Hiromi Uehara** — A pianist known for virtuosic technique and energetic performances that blend stride, post-bop, progressive rock, classical, and fusion. Grammy Award winner (2011, Best Contemporary Jazz Album). Her rhythmic conception is noted for its fearless precision and explosive energy.
- **Akira Jimbo** — A jazz fusion drummer best known for his work with Casiopea. Jimbo pioneered a hybrid drumming approach combining acoustic and electronic percussion, developing custom setups for real-time sound manipulation. His "one-man orchestra" concept reflects a characteristically Japanese approach to technology integration — not replacing acoustic expression but extending it.

#### City Pop

City pop emerged in the mid-1970s as an offshoot of Japan's Western-influenced "new music" and peaked during the 1980s economic boom. The genre encompasses funk, disco, R&B, AOR, soft rock, and boogie, reflecting the optimism and affluence of bubble-era Japan.

**Rhythmic characteristics:**
- Prominent basslines and syncopated drum patterns influenced by American funk and disco
- Elements of Latin and Caribbean rhythmic patterns
- Meticulous production standards — Japanese studio musicians were renowned for their technical precision
- A "groove" sensibility that balanced Western funk rhythmic complexity with Japanese attention to detail and polish

**Tatsuro Yamashita**, often called the "king" of city pop, exemplified this synthesis through his smooth, soulful voice and painstaking production.

**The Global Revival:** Beginning in the early 2010s, city pop found an unexpected international audience through music-sharing blogs, YouTube algorithmic recommendations, and its influence on the sample-based microgenres vaporwave and future funk. This revival demonstrates how rhythm and groove can transcend their original cultural and temporal context.

#### Yellow Magic Orchestra (YMO)

Formed in 1978 by **Haruomi Hosono**, **Yukihiro Takahashi**, and **Ryuichi Sakamoto**, YMO were pioneers of electronic music whose influence is difficult to overstate. They were among the first to extensively use synthesizers, samplers, sequencers, drum machines, and digital recording technology in pop music.

Their rhythmic innovations were specifically groundbreaking:
- Programming drum machines with a precision and complexity that anticipated entire genres (synthpop, electro, techno)
- Incorporating rhythmic elements from traditional Japanese music, exotica, and even arcade game sounds
- Subverting Western stereotypes of Asian culture through ironic, playful adoption and transformation of electronic "exotic" sounds

YMO became a social phenomenon in Japan — so popular that their music permeated everyday life, playing in shops, on television, and in public spaces. Their influence extends directly to **Cornelius** (Keigo Oyamada), who served as a supporting member for YMO and has described them as a formative influence on his own experimental approach to rhythm and sound.

#### Noise and Experimental Percussion

Japan's noise and experimental music scene has produced some of the most radical approaches to percussion in world music:

- **Boredoms** — Formed in Osaka in 1986, Boredoms evolved from chaotic noise-punk into trance-inducing, psychedelia-tinged experimental music centered on percussion. Their **BOA DRUM** concerts — featuring 77 drummers (2007), 88 drummers (2008), and similar massive percussion events — represent percussion as collective ritual, recalling ancient communal drumming traditions through an avant-garde lens. Singer Yamantaka Eye and drummer Yoshimi P-We became iconic figures in global experimental music.
- **Ruins** — A duo/trio led by drummer-vocalist Tatsuya Yoshida, known for extraordinarily complex rhythmic compositions blending elements of progressive rock, noise, and jazz at extreme tempos.

## III. Southeast Asian Traditions

### 20. Gamelan (Java/Bali)

#### The Colotomic Structure

Gamelan — the bronze percussion orchestra of Java and Bali — represents one of the most sophisticated rhythmic organizations in world music. The concept of **colotomy** — coined by Dutch ethnomusicologist **Jaap Kunst** in the 1940s — describes the system by which specific instruments mark off nested time intervals within a cyclical structure.

#### How Gongs Mark Time

In gamelan, percussion instruments are organized hierarchically by the size of the time interval they mark:

| Instrument | Function | Cycle Size |
|---|---|---|
| **Gong ageng** | Marks the largest cycle; signals section endings/repeats | Largest |
| **Gong suwukan** | Marks secondary structural points | Large |
| **Kenong** | Divides the gong cycle into major subdivisions | Medium-large |
| **Kempul** | Further subdivides the kenong sections | Medium |
| **Ketuk** | Regular beat-keeping at the smallest subdivision | Small |
| **Kempyang** | Even faster regular beat (in some ensembles) | Smallest |

The result is a nested hierarchy of time cycles — like concentric circles of different sizes all rotating simultaneously. The gong ageng strikes at the point where the largest cycle completes, the kenong divides that cycle into sections, the kempul subdivides those sections, and the ketuk maintains the fastest regular pulse. A listener can orient themselves within the temporal structure by tracking which instruments have sounded and which are due.

This system has profound implications for drum machine design: gamelan demonstrates that rhythm can be organized not just as a linear sequence of beats but as a *multi-layered hierarchy of interlocking cycles*.

#### Slendro and Pelog

Javanese gamelan uses two distinct tuning systems:

- **Slendro** (5 notes per octave, with roughly equal intervals) — associated with older traditions and a more meditative quality
- **Pelog** (7 notes per octave, with unequal intervals, typically performed in 5-note subsets) — associated with newer compositions and more varied emotional expression

A full gamelan is actually a *double* set — one tuned to slendro, one to pelog. Typically the slendro set faces the front and the pelog set faces the side. The two sets are never played simultaneously. This duality — two complete tonal worlds coexisting in one ensemble — is unique in world music.

#### Balinese vs. Javanese Gamelan

Javanese gamelan tends toward the meditative, refined, and subtle. Balinese gamelan, by contrast, is explosively dynamic — featuring rapid interlocking patterns (*kotekan*) where two musicians play complementary parts at high speed, each filling in the gaps of the other's pattern. Balinese interlocking is directly relevant to drum machine programming as a model for creating complex composite rhythms from simple individual parts.

### 21. Philippine Kulintang

#### Gong Ensemble Tradition

Kulintang music ensembles have been playing for many centuries in regions of the Southern Philippines as part of the larger gong-chime culture of Southeast Asia. The tradition evolved from simple native signaling practices and developed into its present form through the incorporation of knobbed gongs from Sundanese people of Java.

#### The Maguindanao Ensemble

The **Maguindanao kulintang ensemble** consists of five instruments:

- **Kulintang** — A row of eight small, graduated, knobbed gongs laid horizontally on a frame. The lead melodic/rhythmic instrument, played with two wooden beaters.
- **Agong** — A pair of large, heavy hanging gongs providing the bass foundation
- **Dabakan** — A goblet-shaped, single-headed drum providing rhythmic drive
- **Gandingan** — Four suspended gongs of graduated size, used for rhythmic and melodic elaboration
- **Babandir** — A small metallic gong keeping time

#### Rhythmic Modes

Kulintang music is organized around **rhythmic modes** — musical units that bind the entire five-instrument ensemble together. By combining the various rhythms of each instrument, musicians create music; by changing one of the rhythms, they create different music. Among the Maguindanao, terms like *binalig* designate specific rhythmic modes associated with different contexts and styles.

The kulintang tradition emphasizes **improvisation within structure** — the lead kulintang player has considerable freedom to elaborate and vary the melodic-rhythmic pattern while the supporting instruments maintain the cyclic framework. Solo gong contests, where individual players showcase their virtuosity on the agung, gandingan, and kulintang, demonstrate the tradition's emphasis on individual expression within communal music-making.

### 22. Thai Percussion

#### The Pi Phat Ensemble

The **piphat** (ปี่พาทย์) is the primary wind-and-percussion ensemble in Thai classical music, considered the most prestigious form for interpreting sacred and "high-class" compositions. It accompanies Buddhist invocations, classical theater (khon, lakhon), and shadow puppet performances.

#### Taphon: The Leading Drum

The **taphon** (ตะโพน), originally called the Sa Phon, is the most important drum in the piphat ensemble. A barrel-shaped drum with two heads of different sizes, it is played with the hands (no sticks) and functions as the ensemble's rhythmic leader — controlling tempo, cueing transitions, and providing the primary rhythmic framework.

The smallest piphat formation (*piphat khrueang ha*) includes:
- Pi nai (oboe)
- Ranat ek (xylophone)
- Khong wong yai (gong circle)
- Taphon (drum)
- Glong thad (pair of large barrel drums played with sticks)
- Ching (small finger cymbals)

The **ching** (ฉิ่ง) deserves special mention — these small cymbals function as a colotomic instrument analogous to the Javanese ketuk, keeping the basic time cycle with alternating open (*ching*) and closed (*chab*) strokes.

### 23. Vietnamese Percussion

#### Water Puppet Theater (Mua Roi Nuoc)

Vietnamese water puppetry, a tradition with roots in the rice paddies of the Red River Delta, employs a musical ensemble that includes **trong** (drums), wooden bells, cymbals, horns, dan bau (monochord), gongs, and bamboo flutes. The drums play a critical role in the overall theatrical experience:

- Different rhythmic patterns accompany different character types (royalty receives specific drum and cymbal patterns)
- The trong provides not just rhythmic accompaniment but emotional narration
- Each character has distinct musical themes that audiences learn to recognize, with drum patterns serving as sonic signatures

#### Ca Tru

**Ca tru** (歌籌) is a sophisticated vocal music tradition from Northern Vietnam, with roots dating to the sixteenth century. The ensemble is remarkably intimate:

- A female singer who accompanies herself on the **phach** — a small bamboo percussion instrument
- A lutenist playing the three-stringed **dan day**
- A drummer who beats a small "praise drum" (**trong chau**)

The trong chau drummer's role is evaluative as well as musical — the drum was traditionally used to express approval or criticism of the singer's performance in real time, adding strikes of praise for particularly fine passages. Ca tru was inscribed on UNESCO's List of Intangible Cultural Heritage in Need of Urgent Safeguarding in 2009.

## IV. Chinese Traditions

### 24. Chinese Percussion Instruments

#### The Core Instruments

Chinese percussion traditions draw on a vast array of instruments, organized into ensembles called **luogu** (锣鼓, literally "gong and drum"):

- **Tanggu** (堂鼓) — A medium-sized barrel drum dating to the Northern Wei Dynasty, with two heads made of animal skin, played with two sticks. The tanggu serves as the rhythmic backbone of many ensembles.
- **Paigu** (排鼓) — A set of graduated drums arranged in a row, allowing a single player to produce multiple pitches. A distinctly Chinese innovation that creates a melodic percussion voice.
- **Ban/Paiban** (板/拍板) — Wooden clappers that provide sharp rhythmic accents. In opera, the ban player is the conductor of the entire ensemble.
- **Luo** (锣) — Gongs of various sizes:
  - *Daluo* (大锣) — large gong without a boss, beaten with a padded mallet
  - *Xiaoluo* (小锣) — small gong with a brighter, higher pitch
- **Bo** (钹) — Cymbals of various sizes

#### Luogujing: Oral Transmission

Chinese percussion music is taught through a system called **luogujing** (锣鼓经) — a method of oral notation where each percussion pattern is given a specific vocalized name. The syllables indicate which instruments should play and when. This system parallels the Japanese kuchi shoga and Korean oral percussion mnemonics, suggesting either common origin or convergent evolution of oral percussion pedagogy across East Asia.

### 25. Lion and Dragon Dance Drumming

#### Rhythm as Communication

In lion and dragon dance performance, the drum functions as a communication system between the musician and the dancer(s) inside the costume. Since the performers inside the lion or dragon head cannot give audible signals, specific rhythmic patterns correspond to particular movements and routines:

- **The drum represents the lion's heartbeat** — setting tempo, infusing the performance with energy, and establishing the emotional character of each section
- **The gong and cymbals represent the thoughts of the lion** — providing commentary, emphasis, and transitions
- Each martial arts school develops its own distinctive drumming patterns, making every school's lion dance rhythmically unique

#### Symbolic and Spiritual Functions

The combined sounds of drums, gongs, and cymbals serve spiritual purposes rooted in Chinese folk belief:

- The thunderous, penetrating sounds are believed to **ward off evil spirits** and disrupt malevolent entities
- The vibrations are understood to **attract good fortune**, making percussion central to Chinese New Year celebrations
- Specific patterns mark ritual moments — the "awakening" of the lion, the "eating" of lettuce (prosperity symbol), and the climactic poses

#### Training and Coordination

Lion dancers treat drum patterns as tactical signals coordinating maneuvers. The relationship between drummer and dancer is so intimate that experienced pairs develop a form of musical telepathy — the drummer anticipates the dancer's intentions, and the dancer responds to subtle shifts in the drum's energy. This drummer-dancer relationship echoes the gosu-sorikkun relationship in Korean pansori.

### 26. Chinese Opera Percussion

#### The Conductor's Drum

In Peking opera (京剧, *jingju*), the percussion section is not merely accompaniment — it is the **command center** of the entire performance. The primary instruments include:

- **Bangu** (板鼓) — a small, high-pitched frame drum
- **Paiban** (拍板) — wooden clappers
- **Daluo** (大锣) — large gong
- **Xiaoluo** (小锣) — small gong
- **Jingbo** (京钹) — cymbals
- **Tanggu** (堂鼓) — barrel drum

The bangu and paiban are played by a single musician — clappers in one hand, drum stick in the other — who functions as the **conductor of the entire ensemble**. This musician (called the *gushi*, 鼓师) cues:

- Singers' entrances and exits
- Transitions between singing, speaking, and action sequences
- The emotional register of each scene
- Specific stage movements and acrobatic sequences

#### Cueing Stage Action

Percussion patterns in Chinese opera serve a sophisticated cueing function. Each pattern has a name and a specific dramatic meaning:

- Patterns signal whether a character is entering with dignity, urgency, anger, or sorrow
- Fight sequences are choreographed to specific percussion compositions
- When actors cannot appropriately express their characters' emotions through voice alone, the percussion "prompts" the correct emotional state
- The audience, familiar with the percussion vocabulary, understands dramatic meaning directly from the rhythmic patterns

This system represents one of the most highly developed examples of **percussion as dramatic language** in world music — the drums literally tell the story.

### 27. Uyghur Music

#### The Dap and Asymmetric Rhythm

Uyghur music — the traditional and contemporary music of the Uyghur people of Xinjiang (historically East Turkestan) — occupies a fascinating position at the cultural crossroads of East Asia and Central Asia.

#### The Twelve Muqam

The grand suite tradition of the **Twelve Muqam** is the pinnacle of Uyghur musical art, combining instrumental preludes, ornate vocal solos, choral refrains, dance songs, and narrative episodes. Inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2005, the Twelve Muqam represents one of the most extensive and complex musical systems in the world.

#### The Dap Frame Drum

The **dap** (达甫) is a large frame drum that serves as the primary rhythmic instrument in Uyghur music. Its role is particularly important in the dance sections of muqam suites, where it establishes cyclical patterns with accented subdivisions.

What makes Uyghur rhythm especially significant is its embrace of **asymmetric meters**:

- **5/8** — found in the Qumul Muqam and various dance forms
- **7/8** — a characteristic meter in many regional traditions
- **9/8** — used in slower dance sections

These *aqsaq* (limping) rhythms are a defining feature of Uyghur music. Another distinctive characteristic is the tendency to **transform ternary rhythms into binary rhythms**, overlaying a basic 7/8 with varying sets of duolets and quadruplets. This creates a rhythmic surface of extraordinary complexity and fluidity.

#### Bridge Between Worlds

Uyghur music's embrace of asymmetric meters connects it directly to the rhythmic traditions of Central Asia, Turkey, the Balkans, and the Middle East — traditions where 5/8, 7/8, 9/8, and 11/8 are standard rather than exotic. At the same time, the Uyghur tradition's geographic location within China and its historical interactions with Chinese, Mongolian, and other East Asian musical cultures make it a living bridge between rhythmic worlds. For a drum machine honoring every culture, Uyghur rhythms are essential for representing the full spectrum of human rhythmic thought.

## V. Sociopolitical Context

### 28. Music and Spirituality

#### Shinto, Buddhism, and Shamanism

The relationship between drumming and spirituality in East Asia is not metaphorical — it is functional, literal, and deeply embedded in religious practice:

**Shinto:**
Taiko drumming in Shinto contexts serves to summon *kami* (deities), purify sacred spaces, and mark the boundaries between mundane and sacred time. The sound of the drum is understood to travel between worlds, reaching the ears of the divine. *Kagura* performances at shrines combine drumming with dance to create conditions for divine presence.

**Buddhism:**
In Buddhist practice across East Asia, drums mark the temporal structure of monastic life — calling monks to meditation, marking meal times, and punctuating ritual observances. The large *mokugyo* (wooden fish) provides rhythmic accompaniment to sutra chanting, while various drums accompany *Bon Festival* celebrations honoring the dead.

**Korean Shamanism (Muism):**
Korean shamanic practice provides the most explicit example of drumming as a technology for altering consciousness. The gradual acceleration of specific rhythmic patterns is understood to facilitate the *mudang's* transition into trance states where communication with spirits becomes possible. This is not symbolic — practitioners and scholars alike describe the drum as the vehicle through which the shaman travels between worlds.

The common thread across all three spiritual traditions is the understanding that **rhythm is not merely musical but cosmological** — a force that structures time, space, and the relationship between human and divine realms.

### 29. The Taiko Revival and Identity

#### Japanese-American Reclamation

The story of taiko in America is inseparable from the story of Japanese-American identity politics. The three pioneering groups emerged in a specific historical context:

**San Francisco Taiko Dojo (1968):**
Founded by **Seiichi Tanaka**, a Japanese immigrant and martial arts practitioner, the San Francisco Taiko Dojo was the first North American taiko group. Tanaka brought a rigorous, disciplined approach rooted in Japanese training methods and established a direct lineage from Japanese taiko masters to American students.

**Kinnara Taiko (1969):**
Founded at Senshin Buddhist Temple in Los Angeles, Kinnara Taiko emerged from the Japanese-American Buddhist community, linking taiko to spiritual practice and community gathering.

**San Jose Taiko (1973):**
Founded by **Roy Hirabayashi** and **PJ Hirabayashi** during a period of rising Asian-American activism, San Jose Taiko explicitly positioned taiko as a tool of cultural and political empowerment. As Roy Hirabayashi explained: *"Soul and jazz were derived from the Black experience, but nothing on the popular market [was derived from] the Asian American experience... We were Japanese Americans who found taiko as a connection to our ethnic identity."*

#### Breaking Stereotypes

These pioneering groups inspired Japanese-American community activists because taiko offered a **loud and powerful tool of self-expression** for a community stereotypically portrayed as quiet, submissive, and passively assimilated. Taiko claimed space — physically, sonically, and culturally. As San Jose Taiko members articulated: *"This is a Japanese art form and it's not a quiet passive art form... It is something very loud, very powerful, very much in your face, claiming their voice, claiming their heritage."*

#### A Distinctly American Style

Japanese-American taiko developed its own character, distinct from Japanese taiko:

- More **jazzy and syncopated** rhythmic approaches, reflecting the multicultural American musical environment
- Greater emphasis on **visual aesthetics and choreography**
- More **collective, democratic** creative processes (versus the hierarchical Japanese model)
- Incorporation of rhythmic elements from **jazz, funk, Latin music**, and other American traditions

This is not a dilution of tradition but a creative evolution — exactly the kind of cultural exchange that makes living traditions vital.

### 30. Gender in East Asian Drumming

#### Historical Exclusion

For thousands of years, women were largely excluded from taiko drumming in Japan. The physical demands of taiko — and, more significantly, the cultural association between drumming and male martial power — meant that women's roles were typically reserved for singing or dancing. Similar gender exclusions existed in Korean pungmul traditions (particularly in the physically demanding roles) and Chinese lion dance drumming.

#### The Transformation

The gender landscape of East Asian drumming has undergone a dramatic transformation:

- **75% of taiko players today are women**, a striking reversal of historical patterns
- All-female groups like **Genki Daiko**, **Raging Asian Women (RAW)**, and **Taiko Sisters** have emerged as networks of support, leadership, and empowerment
- **Tiffany Tamaribuchi**, initially told she could not play because of her gender, became a leading taiko master and national competition winner in Japan
- **Mizuki Yoneyama** became Kodo's first female odaiko player, breaking one of the most visible gender barriers in traditional Japanese taiko
- **Yoshimi P-We** of Boredoms became an iconic figure in experimental percussion

#### Redefining Body and Power

Women taiko players describe how the art form contradicts stereotypes by encouraging women to own their bodies through physicality and powerful sound. Through performance, women practitioners are able to redefine anachronistic images of Asian women — not by denying the body but by **redefining the body in terms of strength and discipline**. This is not merely a matter of equal access but a fundamental challenge to gendered assumptions about percussion, power, and the body.

### 31. Colonialism's Impact

#### Japanese Colonial Suppression of Korean Music (1910-1945)

The Japanese colonization of Korea brought profound disruption to Korean musical traditions:

**Renaming as Suppression:**
The term *nongak* ("farmers' music") was imposed during the colonial period to restrict the meaning and scope of pungmul — limiting what had been a broad, multifunctional tradition to a narrow "agricultural" category. By re-labeling the tradition, colonial authorities attempted to suppress its wider communal and political significance.

**Institutional Disruption:**
The Japanese colonial government interfered extensively with Korean traditional music in schools, theaters, and the recording industry. The first Japanese governor-general announced in 1911 that "education in Korea is all about making Koreans into enthusiastic Japanese subjects." Western and Japanese music were promoted while Korean traditional forms were marginalized.

**Censorship:**
From 1933, Japanese censorship intensified. Twenty-three Korean records were banned within the first year for "disturbing public order" — indicating that Korean music was recognized as a potent force for cultural identity and resistance.

**The Trot Debate:**
The Japanese colonial period also gave rise to *trot* (트로트), a Korean popular music genre heavily influenced by Japanese *enka*. The so-called "Japanese color" (*waesaek*) debate over trot — whether it represents cultural contamination or creative adaptation — continues to this day, reflecting unresolved tensions about colonialism's musical legacy.

#### Preservation and Recovery

Despite suppression, Korean traditional music survived through the dedication of individual practitioners and rural communities who maintained traditions away from colonial oversight. Post-liberation, the Korean government established systems to protect traditional arts:

- **Important Intangible Cultural Properties** system (from 1962), recognizing master practitioners as "human national treasures"
- Pungmul was first recognized under this system in 1966
- Government-supported traditional music education programs
- The establishment of the National Center for Korean Traditional Performing Arts (now the National Gugak Center)

### 33. East Meets West

#### Historical Currents of Exchange

The interaction between East Asian and Western musical traditions has been ongoing for centuries, though it intensified dramatically in the modern era:

**The Silk Road:**
Long before modern globalization, the Silk Road facilitated musical exchange across Eurasia. Chinese instruments like the pipa traveled to Persia and Central Asia; rhythmic structures from India influenced Middle Eastern percussion; Central Asian instruments and tuning systems reached both China and the Mediterranean.

**Colonial and Modern Exchange:**
Western music entered East Asia through multiple channels — Christian missionaries, colonial education systems, military bands, and commercial recordings. The impact was enormous but not unidirectional:

- **Gamelan** profoundly influenced Western composers including Claude Debussy, who heard Javanese gamelan at the 1889 Paris Exposition and incorporated its timbral and structural ideas into his music
- **Gagaku** influenced 20th-century composers like Olivier Messiaen and Henry Cowell
- **John Cage** drew extensively on Zen Buddhist philosophy and Asian musical concepts in developing his approaches to silence, indeterminacy, and percussion

**What Changed and What Didn't:**
Western rhythmic concepts — particularly the dominance of 4/4 time, the backbeat, and regular bar structures — have deeply penetrated East Asian popular music. K-pop, J-pop, and Mandopop all operate primarily within Western rhythmic frameworks.

However, traditional practices have shown remarkable resilience. Gagaku, pungmul, gamelan, and shamanic traditions continue to operate on their own rhythmic terms. And the most interesting contemporary developments often emerge precisely at the boundary — where traditional and Western rhythmic concepts interact without one absorbing the other.

The fundamental difference persists: East Asian traditional music emphasizes **melodic and rhythmic tension and release** with a line-and-rhythm orientation, in contrast to Western emphasis on harmonic development. This distinction in rhythmic philosophy — rhythm as cycle vs. rhythm as progression — remains a productive creative tension.

### 34. The Global Taiko Movement

#### Worldwide Expansion

From its Japanese and Japanese-American origins, taiko has become a truly global phenomenon:

- Approximately **1,000 taiko groups** exist in the United States and Canada
- Groups have been established in the **United Kingdom, Germany, Russia, Brazil, Australia**, and dozens of other countries
- The **North American Taiko Conference (NATC)**, held since 1997, and the **Taiko Community Alliance (TCA)**, formed in 2013, provide organizational infrastructure
- **Kodo's Earth Celebration** festival on Sado Island has become a major international gathering for taiko and world percussion

#### Cultural Hybridity

The global spread of taiko has produced fascinating hybrid forms:

- **Samba-taiko** in Brazil combines Japanese taiko with Brazilian samba percussion, reflecting the cultural exchange within São Paulo's large Japanese-diaspora community
- **Taiko-jazz** fusions, pioneered by groups like San Jose Taiko, blend Japanese and African-American rhythmic traditions
- **Collegiate taiko** groups at universities worldwide create new compositions that draw on diverse cultural influences

#### Questions of Authenticity and Respect

The global taiko movement raises important questions about cultural exchange, appropriation, and evolution:

- Some Japanese taiko practitioners express concern about the dilution of traditional technique and philosophy
- Others celebrate the global spread as validation of taiko's universal power
- The tension between **preservation and innovation** is itself a creative force — each new group must negotiate its relationship to Japanese tradition while finding its own voice

The model for navigating this tension comes from the Japanese-American taiko pioneers themselves, who demonstrated that deep respect for tradition and bold creative innovation are not contradictory but complementary. As the taiko world expands, this balance remains the central challenge and opportunity.
