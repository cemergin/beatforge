# Turkish, Anatolian, and SWANA Music Traditions: A Musicological Research Report

## Purpose and Scope

This report serves as the cultural and historical foundation for a web drum machine project that seeks to honor every musical tradition it represents. Rather than reducing complex living traditions to mere "world music" samples, this document provides the deep context necessary for respectful, informed representation. Every rhythm pattern in the application carries centuries of spiritual practice, political resistance, community identity, and artistic innovation.

The geographic scope follows the increasingly adopted term SWANA (South West Asia and North Africa), which avoids the Eurocentric framing of "Middle East" and "Near East." The musical traditions covered here span from the Central Asian steppe to the Atlantic coast of Morocco, connected by shared modal systems, rhythmic frameworks, trade routes, imperial histories, and — above all — by musicians who never respected the borders drawn by empires and nation-states.

# PART I: TURKISH MUSIC — HISTORICAL ROOTS

## 1. Central Asian Turkic Origins: Shamanic Drumming and the Rhythm of the Steppe

### The Drum Before Empire

Long before the Seljuks entered Anatolia or the Ottomans built their empire, Turkic peoples on the Central Asian steppe organized their spiritual and social lives around rhythm. The earliest Turkic musical practices are inseparable from Tengriism, the pre-Islamic spiritual system centered on the eternal sky-god Tengri, the sacredness of nature, and the mediating role of the shaman (kam).

The frame drum — known across Turkic cultures as the **tüngür**, **dungur**, or **dap** — was the shaman's primary instrument. It was not understood as a musical instrument in the modern secular sense; it was a vehicle for spiritual travel. The Altaic and Siberian Turkic shamanic traditions held that the drum was the shaman's "horse" upon which they rode between the lower, middle, and upper worlds. The drum's membrane was the saddle; its handle was the backbone of the horse; the beater was the whip (Eliade, 1964; Roux, 1984).

### Tengriist Cosmology and Rhythmic Practice

In Tengriist practice, specific rhythmic patterns corresponded to specific spiritual functions:

- **Invocation rhythms** — accelerating patterns to summon spirits
- **Journey rhythms** — steady, hypnotic ostinatos for trance induction
- **Return rhythms** — decelerating patterns to bring the shaman back to ordinary consciousness

This tripartite rhythmic structure (acceleration, sustain, deceleration) remains embedded in many Anatolian folk music forms, particularly in the semah ceremonies of Alevi communities, who preserve pre-Islamic Turkic spiritual elements within an Islamic framework (Markoff, 1986).

### The Davul in Nomadic Life

The **davul** (also spelled tabl, tupan, or tapan across Turkic languages) — a large, double-headed cylindrical drum played with a heavy mallet (tokmak) on one side and a thin stick (çubuk) on the other — was central to nomadic Turkic life well before its famous role in Ottoman military bands. In nomadic encampments, the davul served as:

- **A communication instrument** — its deep bass carried across vast steppe distances to signal gatherings, warnings, migrations, and celebrations
- **A ceremonial marker** — weddings, circumcisions, seasonal festivals, and funerals were all structured around davul rhythms
- **A symbol of authority** — the right to maintain a davul and a zurna (double-reed oboe) player was a mark of tribal leadership

The davul-zurna pairing, which remains ubiquitous across Turkey, the Balkans, and the Caucasus today, is one of the oldest continuously practiced instrumental combinations in the world. The relationship between the two instruments is not merely accompaniment: the zurna provides melodic content while the davul provides both rhythmic foundation and a second melodic voice through the contrast between its deep (dum) and sharp (tek) strokes (Picken, 1975).

### Horse-Riding and Rhythmic Feel

A thesis that appears across ethnomusicological literature, though it remains debated, is that the characteristic asymmetric meters of Turkic music (5/8, 7/8, 9/8, and more complex groupings) may relate to the gaits of horses. The Turkish musicologist Bülent Aksoy and others have noted that the aksak ("limping") rhythmic feel — a defining feature of Turkish and Balkan music — produces a physical sensation similar to the asymmetric rocking of horseback riding (Aksoy, 1994; Brăiloiu, 1951). Whether or not this is the literal origin, the connection between bodily movement and rhythmic structure is foundational: these rhythms are not abstract mathematical constructions but are rooted in lived physical experience.

## 2. The Seljuk Era: Mehter and the World's Oldest Military Band Tradition

### The Seljuk Arrival in Anatolia

When the Seljuk Turks defeated the Byzantine army at the Battle of Manzikert (Malazgirt) in 1071 and began settling Anatolia, they brought with them a Central Asian military-musical institution that would become one of the most influential musical organizations in world history: the **mehter** (also mehterhane or mehter takımı).

### Origins and Structure of the Mehter

The mehter tradition draws from the Central Asian Turkic institution of the **tuğ takımı** — the ensemble that accompanied the royal standard (tuğ). In the Turkic-Mongol political tradition, the right to maintain a military band was a marker of sovereignty granted by divine mandate. The number of instrument groups (kat) in a ruler's mehter directly corresponded to their rank: a bey might have three kat, a sultan nine (Farmer, 1936).

The core mehter instrumentation:

- **Davul** — the large bass drum, the rhythmic and dynamic foundation
- **Zurna** — the double-reed oboe, carrying the melody
- **Nakkare** (naqqara) — small paired kettledrums, providing rhythmic punctuation and counter-rhythms
- **Boru** — a natural trumpet, for fanfares and sustained tones
- **Zil** — large cymbals (not to be confused with the finger cymbals of belly dance)
- **Kös** — enormous kettledrums mounted on camels or carried by multiple men, producing earth-shaking bass tones used for psychological warfare
- **Çevgan** (later called zil-li maşa or çağana) — a percussion staff with jingling crescents, often called the "jingling Johnny" in European adaptations

### The Mehter as Psychological Weapon

The mehter was not merely ceremonial. It was a calculated instrument of psychological warfare. Ottoman armies marched to the mehter, and the ensemble played throughout battles. The effect on enemies was well-documented by European chroniclers who described the "terrible noise" that preceded Ottoman attacks. The kös drums were so large (some over a meter in diameter) that their infrasonic frequencies could be felt physically before they were heard — a deliberate exploitation of what we would now call psychoacoustics (Behar, 1987).

The mehter played in a distinctive crescent formation, and its performance practice included a characteristic dynamic swell: beginning softly, building to an overwhelming fortissimo, then suddenly cutting to silence — a technique designed to disorient and terrify.

### Influence on European Military Music

The mehter's influence on European music is one of the most significant and underacknowledged cases of cultural transmission in Western music history. After the Ottoman sieges of Vienna (1529 and 1683) and subsequent military encounters, European armies began incorporating elements of the mehter:

- **The bass drum** entered European orchestras directly from the davul. Before Ottoman influence, European ensembles had no comparable large bass drum.
- **Cymbals** (from the mehter's zil) were adopted into European military and orchestral music.
- **The triangle** entered European music via the çevgan/jingling crescent.
- **The piccolo** was influenced by the high-register zurna playing.

The 18th-century European fashion for **"Alla Turca"** or **"Turkish music"** (Janissary music) was a direct response to the mehter. Mozart's "Rondo alla Turca" (1783), Beethoven's "Turkish March" from *The Ruins of Athens* (1811), and the "Turkish" percussion in Haydn's "Military" Symphony (No. 100, 1794) all reference mehter sonorities. The entire bass drum + cymbal + triangle combination that became standard in the European orchestra was imported from the mehter tradition (Pirker, 1984).

European military bands adopted the mehter's function of marching music, the concept of matched percussion sections, and even specific instruments. The Janissary-influenced military band became the prototype for all modern Western military bands — and by extension, for marching bands, concert bands, and the rhythmic foundation of popular music (Farmer, 1950).

### The Mehter's Dissolution and Revival

Mahmud II dissolved the Janissary corps and the mehter in 1826 as part of military modernization. The tradition was revived in 1914 at the Istanbul Military Museum, where a mehter ensemble still performs today. Contemporary mehter performances, while valuable as cultural preservation, inevitably carry the tension of reviving an imperial military tradition in a republican nationalist context.

## 3. Ottoman Classical Music: Usul, the Fasıl, and the Codification of Rhythm

### The Divan Musical Tradition

Ottoman classical music (Osmanlı sanat müziği, also called divan müziği) represents one of the most sophisticated musical systems ever developed, comparable in complexity to Hindustani raga, European counterpoint, or the Persian dastgah. It evolved over roughly six centuries (14th–early 20th century) in the cosmopolitan environment of the Ottoman court and urban centers, absorbing and synthesizing elements from Arabic, Persian, Byzantine Greek, Armenian, Kurdish, Romani, and Jewish musical traditions.

Ottoman classical music operated on two interlocking systems:

- **Makam** — a system of melodic modes (over 600 named makams exist in historical treatises, though approximately 80-100 were in common use)
- **Usul** — a system of rhythmic cycles

### Usul: The Rhythmic Cycle System

The **usul** (plural: usuller) system is the Ottoman/Turkish codification of rhythmic modes — cyclical patterns of strong and weak beats that provide the temporal framework for composition and improvisation. Usul is not equivalent to Western "time signature" or "meter." A time signature tells you how beats are grouped; an usul tells you:

1. **The total cycle length** — ranging from 2 beats (nim sofyan) to 120 beats (zencir) and beyond
2. **The internal pattern of strokes** — specified as **düm** (deep, resonant, strong) and **tek** (sharp, high, light), with their variants (düm = strong bass, tek = sharp treble, te-ke = double light strokes, tâ-hek = accented variants)
3. **The aesthetic character** — each usul carries emotional and contextual associations
4. **The relationship to melody** — specific usuls are traditionally paired with specific makams

Key usuller and their significance:

| Usul | Beats | Character |
|------|-------|-----------|
| **Nim Sofyan** | 2 | The simplest cycle; basis for folk-influenced pieces |
| **Sofyan** | 4 | Common, versatile; used across genres |
| **Türk Aksağı** | 5 (2+3 or 3+2) | The "Turkish limp"; characteristic aksak feel |
| **Yürük Semai** | 6 (3+3) | Light, flowing; used in the semai form |
| **Devr-i Hindi** | 7 (3+2+2) | "Indian cycle"; connections to Hindustani tal |
| **Düyek** | 8 (4+4 or 3+2+3) | One of the most common; basis for many vocal forms |
| **Aksak** | 9 (2+2+2+3) | Quintessential Turkish asymmetric meter |
| **Çifte Düyek** | 16 | Extended cycle for elaborate compositions |
| **Devr-i Kebir** | 28 | Large cycle requiring extraordinary memory and concentration |
| **Darb-ı Fetih** | 88 | "Stroke of Conquest"; among the longest, used in monumental compositions |
| **Zencir** | 120 | "Chain"; the longest standard usul, demanding extreme musical memory |

The existence of cycles spanning 88 or 120 beats challenges fundamental Western assumptions about musical cognition. Performers must internalize these vast patterns so deeply that they can navigate them in real time while simultaneously improvising melodic material. This is not rote repetition — within the usul cycle, there is constant micro-variation, ornamentation, and responsive interplay between the rhythmic and melodic elements (Signell, 1977).

### The Fasıl Ensemble

The **fasıl** is both a concert format and an ensemble type. As a format, it is an ordered suite of compositions and improvisations in a single makam, moving through different usuls from slow and heavy to fast and light. As an ensemble, the classical fasıl included:

- **Tanbur** — long-necked lute, the "king of instruments" in Ottoman music
- **Ney** — end-blown reed flute, associated with Sufi mysticism
- **Kemençe** (classical kemençe, also called armudi kemençe) — a small pear-shaped bowed string instrument, distinct from the Black Sea kemençe
- **Kanun** — a plucked zither with levers for microtonal adjustment
- **Ud** — short-necked, fretless lute (the ancestor of the European lute)
- **Kudüm** — small paired kettledrums, the primary percussion in the classical ensemble
- **Def** (bendir) — frame drum, sometimes with snares

### The Kudümcü: Keeper of the Cycle

The **kudüm** player (kudümcü) held a position of extraordinary musical authority in the fasıl ensemble. The kudüm — a pair of small copper or wooden kettledrums covered with skin, played with two light wooden sticks — provided the usul pattern that held the entire ensemble together.

The kudümcü was traditionally the most musically learned member of the ensemble. They needed to know the complete usul repertoire (dozens of cycles, some enormously long), all the traditional compositions in the fasıl repertoire, and the conventions governing transitions between sections. The kudümcü functioned as a conductor without a baton — their subtle variations in dynamics, accent, and ornamentation guided the ensemble through the intricate architecture of the fasıl.

The playing technique of the kudüm is notably understated compared to the bombast of the davul or the flash of the darbuka. The strokes are relatively quiet, focused on precision and subtlety. This reflects the aesthetic of Ottoman classical music: refinement over spectacle, suggestion over statement (Feldman, 1996).

## 4. Mevlevi (Whirling Dervish) Music: Rhythm as Spiritual Practice

### The Sema Ceremony

The **Mevlevi Order** (Mevlevîye), founded in the 13th century in Konya by followers of Jalal ad-Din Muhammad Rumi (known in Turkish as Mevlânâ), developed one of the most sophisticated integrations of music, movement, and spiritual practice in any world tradition. The **sema** (or semâ) ceremony — the ritual "whirling" that gives the order its popular name — is a carefully structured liturgical practice in which music is not accompaniment to worship but is itself the mode of worship.

Rumi's philosophy placed music at the center of spiritual experience. His famous opening lines of the *Masnavi* invoke the ney flute:

> "Listen to the reed, how it tells a tale, complaining of separations — saying, ever since I was parted from the reed-bed, my lament has caused man and woman to moan."

For Rumi and the Mevlevi tradition, music is the echo of the primordial divine sound, and the soul's longing to return to its source is expressed through melody and rhythm. This is not metaphor — in Mevlevi theology, music literally facilitates the soul's journey toward union with the divine (Lewis, 2000).

### Structure of the Sema and Its Rhythmic Framework

The sema ceremony follows a precise structure, each section with its own musical and rhythmic character:

1. **Na't-ı Şerif** — A vocal praise of the Prophet Muhammad, sung in the makam rast, unmetered (free rhythm). This opening establishes the sacred space.

2. **Kudüm velvele** (kettledrum roll) — A single stroke of the kudüm, symbolizing the divine command "Kun!" ("Be!") — the creative word of God. This is followed by a taksim (improvisation) on the ney.

3. **Peşrev** — An instrumental composition in a measured usul, during which the semazenler (whirlers) process and bow. The rhythmic cycle here is typically a long, stately usul (devr-i kebir or similar).

4. **Four Selams** (salutations) — The four main sections of whirling, each in a different usul and tempo:
   - **First Selam** — Slow, in a long usul; represents the human recognition of God
   - **Second Selam** — Faster; represents ecstasy before God's power
   - **Third Selam** — The climactic section; represents the dissolution of self in divine love
   - **Fourth Selam** — Return to calm; represents the acceptance of destiny and return to service

5. **Son Peşrev and Son Yürük Semai** — Final instrumental compositions
6. **Quran recitation and prayer** — Closing the ceremony

### Rhythm as Spiritual Technology

In the Mevlevi tradition, rhythm is not merely a framework for music — it is a technology of consciousness. The whirling itself is a physical engagement with rhythmic cycles: the semazen's right hand faces upward (receiving divine grace), the left hand faces downward (transmitting it to the earth), and the body revolves on its own axis while simultaneously orbiting the central post (the sheikh's position) — mirroring the rotation of planets around the sun and electrons around the nucleus.

The rhythmic cycles of the sema are calibrated to induce specific states of consciousness. The progression from slow, long cycles to faster, shorter ones mirrors the acceleration pattern of shamanic drumming (connecting back to pre-Islamic Turkic practices), but here it is codified into an elaborate liturgical form. The repetition of the rhythmic cycle — the return to the "one" after traversing 7, 10, 14, or 28 beats — becomes a metaphor for and enactment of the soul's cyclical journey from and back to the divine source (During, 1988).

### The Mevlevi Musical Legacy

The Mevlevi lodges (mevlevihane) were among the most important conservatories of Ottoman classical music. Because the order valued musical sophistication as spiritual practice, the mevlevihane attracted the finest composers and performers, regardless of religious or ethnic background. Many of the greatest Ottoman composers were either Mevlevi or closely associated with the order, including:

- **Mustafa Itrî** (1640–1712) — Often considered the greatest Ottoman composer
- **Dede Efendi** (1778–1846) — Whose work represents the pinnacle of the fasıl tradition
- **Buhurizade Mustafa Efendi (Itrî)** — Master of both sacred and secular forms

The Mevlevi order was officially banned by Atatürk in 1925 as part of the secularization reforms, along with all Sufi orders. The sema ceremony continues today as a "cultural performance" under government regulation — a transformation that Mevlevis and scholars debate: does official recognition preserve or museumify a living spiritual practice? (Bates, 2011).

## 5. The Shared Musical Ecosystem: Armenian, Greek, Kurdish, Assyrian, and Jewish Traditions Within Ottoman Borders

### The Ottoman Musical Commonwealth

One of the most important — and most politically sensitive — facts about Ottoman music is that it was never the product of a single ethnic or religious community. The musical system that we now call "Turkish classical music" or "Ottoman music" was built by Turks, Arabs, Persians, Armenians, Greeks, Kurds, Jews, Assyrians, and Romani musicians working together within shared theoretical and practical frameworks. The Ottoman Empire's millet system (which organized non-Muslim communities as semi-autonomous religious groups) paradoxically both separated communities socially and allowed them to develop deep musical interconnections (O'Connell, 2005).

### Armenian Musical Contributions

Armenian musicians were central to Ottoman musical life, particularly from the 17th century onward. The **Hampartsoum notation system**, developed by the Armenian musician Hampartsoum Limondjian (1768–1839), was the first widely adopted system for notating Ottoman music and remained in use until the adoption of Western staff notation in the early 20th century. Before Hampartsoum, Ottoman music was transmitted almost entirely through oral tradition — the adoption of his system represented a fundamental shift in how the tradition was preserved and transmitted.

Key Armenian contributions:

- **Tatyos Efendi** (Tateos Ekserciyan, 1858–1913) — One of the most beloved composers in the Ottoman canon, whose works remain standards of the Turkish classical and urban folk repertoire. His compositions in saba and hüzzam makams are considered masterpieces.
- **Kemani Tatyos** — His violin playing helped establish the instrument in Ottoman fasıl
- **Udi Hrant** (Hrant Kenkulian, 1901–1978) — Master ud player who bridged the Ottoman classical tradition into the Republican era
- **Armenak Şahmuradyan** and the tradition of Armenian church music (sharakan) that shared modal elements with Ottoman secular music

The Armenian Genocide of 1915 devastated this musical community. The loss was not only human but cultural: entire repertoires, regional styles, and oral traditions were destroyed. The musicologist Harold Hagopian's work documenting surviving Armenian musical traditions represents crucial salvage ethnomusicology (Hagopian, 1996).

### Greek Musical Contributions

Greek Orthodox (Rum) musicians were equally embedded in Ottoman musical culture:

- **Byzantine chant** — The modal system of Byzantine ecclesiastical music shared deep structural connections with Ottoman makam, reflecting either common origins or centuries of mutual influence (the direction of influence remains debated among scholars). The Byzantine octoechos (eight-mode system) and the Ottoman makam system overlap significantly in their pitch relationships and melodic behavior.
- **Secular contributions** — Greek musicians were prominent in the Ottoman fasıl tradition and in the lighter urban entertainment music (especially in tavern music and the café-aman tradition). The **rebetiko** tradition of urban Greece, often called "Greek blues," was born in the shared musical environment of Smyrna (Izmir), Constantinople (Istanbul), and the Aegean — before and after the catastrophic 1923 population exchange that forcibly separated Greek and Turkish communities.
- **Dimitrie Cantemir** (1673–1723) — The Moldavian prince who lived in Istanbul and produced one of the earliest and most important written collections of Ottoman instrumental music, using a notation system of his own devising.

### Kurdish Musical Traditions

Kurdish music within the Ottoman sphere maintained distinct characteristics while sharing the broader modal and rhythmic framework:

- The **dengbêj** tradition — epic oral storytelling through song — predates and transcends Ottoman influence (discussed in detail in Section 11)
- Kurdish dance music, particularly the **govend** (group circle dance) traditions, contributed specific rhythmic patterns to the broader Anatolian repertoire
- The **tembûr** (Kurdish long-necked lute) tradition maintained tuning systems and playing techniques distinct from the Turkish tanbur

### Assyrian (Syriac) Musical Traditions

The Assyrian/Syriac Christian communities of southeastern Anatolia and northern Mesopotamia maintained ancient musical traditions:

- Syriac liturgical chant, one of the oldest continuous Christian musical traditions, with modal structures that predate and may have influenced both Byzantine and Islamic modal systems
- Folk music traditions incorporating instruments shared with Kurdish and Turkish neighbors — the zurna, dahol (davul), and various frame drums
- Like the Armenian tradition, Assyrian musical heritage suffered enormously from the Sayfo (the Assyrian genocide of 1914–1920) and subsequent displacement

### Jewish Musical Contributions

Jewish communities in the Ottoman Empire — both Romaniote (Greek-speaking Jews present since antiquity) and Sephardic (expelled from Spain in 1492 and welcomed by Sultan Bayezid II) — made distinctive musical contributions:

- **Sephardic romansas and kantigas** — songs in Ladino (Judeo-Spanish) set to melodies that blended Iberian, Ottoman, and Greek elements, creating a unique repertoire that preserves medieval Spanish musical features otherwise lost
- **Synagogue cantorial tradition** — the makam system was used in Ottoman synagogues for liturgical chanting, with specific makams assigned to specific prayers and holidays, paralleling the Mevlevi practice of assigning spiritual significance to musical modes
- **Professional musicians** — Jewish musicians were active in Ottoman court music and especially in urban entertainment. The tradition of Jewish instrumental musicians continued into the Republican era, with figures like the clarinetist **Şükrü Tunar** emerging from this milieu

### The "Shared Meal" of Ottoman Music

The metaphor used by the musicologist Martin Stokes is apt: Ottoman music was a "shared meal" to which different communities brought different dishes. The makam-usul framework was the common table. What made Ottoman music so rich was precisely this diversity of contributors within a shared system. When the empire dissolved and nation-states drew new borders, this shared tradition was partitioned — and each successor state claimed parts of it as exclusively "their own" music, erasing the contributions of others. This is not merely an academic point; it is central to the ethical responsibility of any project that engages with these traditions (Stokes, 1992).

# PART II: TURKISH MUSIC THROUGH HISTORY

## 6. Republican Era (1923+): Atatürk's Music Reforms

### The Cultural Revolution

When Mustafa Kemal Atatürk founded the Republic of Turkey in 1923, he initiated one of the most radical cultural transformation programs in modern history. Music was not peripheral to this program — it was central. Atatürk explicitly stated that the measure of a civilization was its music, and he viewed Ottoman classical music as a relic of a decadent past that had to be replaced.

### The Ban of 1934

In November 1934, Ottoman/Turkish classical music was banned from state radio broadcasts. Atatürk declared:

> "The music called 'alaturka' is not adequate to represent the modern Turkish nation... We must collect and adapt our folk music with Western methods."

This ban, which lasted approximately 20 months, reflected the Kemalist cultural program:

1. **Ottoman classical music** (now labeled "alaturka") was deemed "Eastern," passive, backward-looking, and associated with the decadent court culture
2. **Western classical music** ("alafranga") was promoted as the model for a new Turkish national music
3. **Anatolian folk music** was championed as the "authentic" Turkish heritage — raw material to be "elevated" through Western harmonic and orchestral techniques

### The Dual Strategy

The Republican music policy pursued two tracks simultaneously:

**Western classical music adoption:** A state conservatory was established in Ankara. Composers known as the **"Turkish Five"** (Türk Beşleri) — Cemal Reşit Rey, Hasan Ferit Alnar, Ulvi Cemal Erkin, Ahmet Adnan Saygun, and Necil Kazım Akses — were sent to Europe (primarily Paris) to study Western composition. They were charged with creating a new national music that used Anatolian folk melodies within Western symphonic forms. Béla Bartók visited Turkey in 1936 to assist with folk music collection, drawing parallels with his own work in Hungary (Signell, 2002).

**Folk music collection and "purification":** The state dispatched researchers across Anatolia to collect folk songs (türkü). These were transcribed into Western notation, harmonized, and broadcast on state radio. The process of transcription inevitably distorted the music: microtonal inflections were "corrected" to fit the Western twelve-tone system; asymmetric rhythms were regularized; and the regional, ethnic, and religious contexts were stripped away in favor of a homogeneous "Turkish folk" identity (Stokes, 1992).

### The Deeper Ideology

The music reforms were not merely aesthetic preferences. They reflected a deeper ideological project: the construction of a new Turkish identity that was simultaneously "Western" (modern, scientific, secular) and "authentically Turkish" (rooted in Central Asian and Anatolian folk traditions) — but emphatically not "Ottoman" (multiethnic, Islamic, "Eastern"). Music was a battlefield in this identity war. The makam system, with its microtones and its associations with Sufi mysticism, was too "Eastern." Western harmony, with its rationality and its associations with European power, was the future (Tekelioğlu, 1996).

### Consequences and Resistance

The music reforms had lasting consequences:

- A generation of Ottoman classical musicians was marginalized and their art nearly lost
- The transmission chain of master-to-student (meşk) was severely disrupted
- Musical knowledge that had been preserved orally for centuries was not adequately documented before its carriers died
- Regional musical diversity was flattened into a national "folk" category
- Non-Turkish musical traditions within Turkey's borders (Kurdish, Armenian, Assyrian, Laz, Circassian) were suppressed or assimilated

Yet the tradition survived, largely through informal channels — private gatherings, neighborhood meyhanes (taverns), and the quiet persistence of musicians who continued to teach and perform despite official disfavor.

## 7. TRT and State Music Classification

### The State as Musical Gatekeeper

**TRT** (Türkiye Radyo ve Televizyon Kurumu), the state broadcasting corporation established in 1964, became the primary institution through which the state controlled musical life in Turkey. TRT's classification system organized Turkish music into rigid categories:

1. **Türk Sanat Müziği** (Turkish Art Music) — the renamed and domesticated version of Ottoman classical music, now stripped of its Ottoman/Islamic associations and presented as "Turkish"
2. **Türk Halk Müziği** (Turkish Folk Music) — Anatolian folk music, collected, notated, and standardized
3. **Türk Hafif Müziği** (Turkish Light Music) — Western-influenced popular music
4. **Batı Müziği** (Western Music) — Western classical and pop

### The TRT Repertoire System

TRT maintained an official repertoire — a catalog of approved songs that could be broadcast. To be included, a piece had to be reviewed by a committee and transcribed in Western notation. Songs in Kurdish, Armenian, or other minority languages were excluded. Pieces deemed too "arabesque" (arabesk) or too sexually suggestive were rejected. The TRT repertoire became, in effect, the canon of Turkish music — and what was excluded from it was rendered culturally invisible on the national stage.

The TRT folk music archive, despite its ideological framing, is also an invaluable resource. Researchers traveled to remote villages and recorded elderly musicians who carried repertoires reaching back generations. Many of these recordings preserve musical traditions that have since vanished as rural populations migrated to cities (Markoff, 2002).

### Classification as Control

The very act of classification was a form of control. By dividing music into "art," "folk," and "popular" categories, TRT imposed a hierarchy: art music was elevated but contained; folk music was celebrated but sanitized; popular music was tolerated but regulated. Music that crossed categories — that mixed art music makams with folk rhythms and popular song forms — was difficult to classify and therefore difficult to control. It is no coincidence that the most disruptive musical movements in Turkish history (arabesk, Anatolian rock, Kurdish music) were precisely those that defied TRT's categories.

## 8. Arabesk: The Music of the Margins

### Origins: Rural Migrants and Urban Pain

**Arabesk** is one of the most culturally significant and politically charged genres in Turkish music history. Emerging in the 1960s and reaching its peak in the 1970s–1980s, arabesk was the music of the **gecekondu** — the squatter settlements built literally overnight ("gecekondular," from gece "night" + kondu "landed/placed") by millions of rural migrants flooding into Istanbul, Ankara, and other cities during Turkey's rapid urbanization.

These migrants found themselves caught between worlds: no longer villagers, not yet accepted as urbanites. They were too "Eastern" for the Westernized urban elite, too modernized for the traditions they had left behind. Arabesk gave voice to this liminal experience — its themes of loneliness, unrequited love, fate (kader), and suffering (acı) expressed the emotional reality of displacement (Stokes, 1992).

### Musical Characteristics

Arabesk blended:

- **Turkish makam** — especially the more melancholic modes (hicaz, nihavend, kürdî)
- **Arabic musical elements** — particularly Egyptian film music orchestration (lush string sections, the "Eastern" aesthetic of tarab)
- **Western instruments** — electric guitar, bass, drum kit, synthesizer
- **Turkish folk elements** — bağlama patterns, türkü-derived melodies

The rhythmic basis of arabesk drew from both Turkish usul and Arabic iqa', with the darbuka (or dümbelek) providing characteristic patterns. The çiftetelli rhythm (a variant of the Arabic maqsum/masmoudi) became strongly associated with arabesk.

### Orhan Gencebay: The Pioneer

**Orhan Gencebay** (b. 1944) is universally acknowledged as the founder and most important figure of arabesk. A virtuoso bağlama and electric guitar player trained in Turkish classical music, Gencebay created a synthesis that was genuinely new. His 1968 hit "Bir Teselli Ver" ("Give Me Some Consolation") is often cited as the first arabesk song. His 1977 film and album *Bir Araya Gelemeyiz* ("We Cannot Come Together") sold millions.

Gencebay's musical sophistication was key: he was not simply copying Arabic music (as critics charged) but creating a new modal and rhythmic language that drew on Turkish, Arabic, Persian, and Western elements. His orchestral arrangements were complex, his bağlama playing innovative, and his vocal style combined Turkish folk singing with the ornamented melisma of Arabic tarab (Solak, 2017).

### Other Key Arabesk Artists

- **Müslüm Gürses** (1953–2013) — "The Voice of Pain" (Acıların Sesi), whose intense emotional delivery and devoted fanbase (the "Müslüm Baba" phenomenon) represented arabesk at its most visceral
- **Ferdi Tayfur** (b. 1945) — Known for his high-pitched, keening vocal style
- **İbrahim Tatlıses** (b. 1952) — Kurdish-Turkish singer whose voice and persona transcended genre categories
- **Bergen** (Belgin Sarılmışer, 1958–1989) — Whose murder by her ex-husband after years of abuse, including an acid attack, made her a tragic symbol of violence against women and has led to her recent cultural reappraisal

### State Resistance and Class Politics

The Kemalist establishment's hostility to arabesk was intense. Arabesk was banned from TRT until the 1990s. It was condemned as:

- Culturally regressive — undoing the Western modernization project
- Arabizing — threatening Turkish national identity with "foreign" (Arab) influence
- Fatalistic — encouraging passive acceptance of suffering rather than modern agency
- Vulgar — lacking the refinement of either Turkish art music or Western classical music

This hostility was fundamentally a class issue. Arabesk was the music of the poor, the rural, the Kurdish, the dark-skinned, the uneducated — everything the Kemalist elite defined itself against. The ban on arabesk, like the earlier ban on Ottoman classical music, revealed that musical policy in Turkey was always about social control and identity engineering (Özbek, 1997).

## 9. Anatolian Rock: Psychedelia Meets the Saz

### The Movement

**Anatolian rock** (Anadolu rock) is arguably the most globally celebrated Turkish music movement. Emerging in the late 1960s and flourishing through the 1970s, it represented a remarkable synthesis: Anatolian folk music — with its bağlama, asymmetric rhythms, and makam-based melodies — fused with Western psychedelic and progressive rock.

### The Pioneers

**Barış Manço** (1943–1999) — Perhaps the most beloved Turkish musician of the 20th century. Manço began as a rock-and-roll singer in the early 1960s, lived in Europe, and returned to Turkey to create a synthesis of Turkish folk and rock. His band **Kurtalan Ekspres** developed a sound that was simultaneously Turkish and psychedelic. Songs like "Dağlar Dağlar" ("Mountains Mountains") used bağlama riffs as the basis for rock arrangements, while maintaining the aksak meters and modal inflections of the folk originals. His children's TV show *7'den 77'ye* made him a national institution.

**Erkin Koray** (1941–2024) — The "Jimi Hendrix of Turkey," Koray was a true experimentalist who ran his electric guitar through effects pedals while playing in makam scales. His album *Elektronik Türküler* ("Electronic Folk Songs," 1974) is a landmark: traditional türküs reimagined with wah-wah guitar, fuzz bass, and psychedelic production. Koray's rhythmic approach often foregrounded the clash between rock's 4/4 grid and Turkish folk's asymmetric meters, creating a productive tension.

**Cem Karaca** (1945–2004) — The most politically engaged of the Anatolian rock pioneers. Karaca's powerful baritone and politically charged lyrics (drawing on leftist poets like Nazım Hikmet and Pir Sultan Abdal) made him a figure of enormous cultural and political significance. After the 1980 military coup, his citizenship was revoked and he lived in exile in Germany until 1987. His bands — **Apaşlar**, **Kardaşlar**, **Moğollar**, and **Dervişan** — each explored different aspects of the Turkish-rock synthesis.

**Moğollar** ("The Mongols") — A band that served as a laboratory for Turkish-rock fusion. Various members collaborated with Barış Manço, Cem Karaca, and others. Their instrumental track "Dangling on a String" and the album *Anatolian Sun* showcase their approach to Turkish asymmetric rhythms within a rock context.

**Selda Bağcan** (b. 1948) — The most prominent female voice of Anatolian rock. Bağcan's powerful voice and politically charged folk-rock arrangements, particularly on songs like "Yaz Gazeteci Yaz" ("Write, Journalist, Write"), led to her arrest and imprisonment after the 1980 coup. Her music has been sampled internationally (notably by Mos Def/Yasiin Bey) and she remains a towering figure.

### Rhythmic Innovation in Anatolian Rock

The rhythmic dimension of Anatolian rock is particularly relevant to this project. These musicians faced a fundamental challenge: how do you reconcile 9/8, 7/8, and 5/8 meters with a rock drum kit designed for 4/4?

Solutions included:

- **Maintaining the asymmetric meter on the drum kit** — playing 9/8 (2+2+2+3) or 7/8 (2+2+3) on hi-hat, snare, and kick, essentially translating davul patterns to the kit
- **Superimposing meters** — bağlama in 7/8 against drums in 4/4, creating polymetric tension
- **Using the bağlama as a rhythmic instrument** — the bağlama's percussive "şelpe" technique (right-hand fingernail strokes on the strings) provided a rhythmic layer independent of the drum kit
- **Incorporating traditional percussion** — darbuka, bendir, and kaşık (spoons) alongside the kit

### The 1980 Coup and Its Aftermath

The September 12, 1980 military coup devastated Turkish cultural life. Musicians were arrested, exiled, or silenced. The Anatolian rock movement, with its associations with leftist politics, was particularly targeted. The junta promoted apolitical pop music (Turkish pop, or "Türk pop") and arabesk — ironically, the very genre the Kemalist establishment had previously suppressed — as politically harmless alternatives. The vibrant musical experimentation of the 1970s gave way to a more cautious, commercially oriented musical landscape in the 1980s (Bulut, 2020).

## 10. Turkish Pop and Global Moments

### Sezen Aksu: The "Little Sparrow"

**Sezen Aksu** (b. 1954) is the most important figure in Turkish popular music, a songwriter, singer, and producer whose influence spans five decades. Known as "Minik Serçe" (Little Sparrow), Aksu's genius lies in her ability to synthesize Turkish melodic and emotional sensibilities with contemporary pop production while maintaining artistic integrity.

Her songwriting draws deeply on makam-inflected melody — her songs often sit in ambiguous modal spaces that feel simultaneously Turkish and universal. Rhythmically, her work ranges from straightforward pop meters to songs incorporating aksak patterns and çiftetelli grooves. She launched the careers of numerous Turkish pop stars, including Tarkan and Sertab Erener.

### Tarkan: Pop Diplomacy

**Tarkan** (Tarkan Tevetoğlu, b. 1972) achieved a rare feat: a Turkish pop star with genuine global reach. His 1997 hit "Şımarık" ("Kiss Kiss") reached number one in multiple European countries and became an international phenomenon. Musically, "Şımarık" is significant because it does not disguise its Turkish elements — the darbuka pattern, the çiftetelli rhythm, and the melodic ornamentation are foregrounded rather than smoothed away.

**Sertab Erener** won the Eurovision Song Contest in 2003 with "Everyway That I Can," bringing a Turkish-inflected pop sound to the continent's biggest music competition.

## 11. Kurdish Music: The Dengbêj and Resistance

### The Dengbêj Tradition

The **dengbêj** (from deng, "voice") tradition is one of the great oral literary and musical traditions of the world. Dengbêjs are epic singers who preserve and perform vast narrative songs — some lasting hours — recounting historical events, tribal genealogies, love stories, and tragedies. The tradition is predominantly found among Kurmanji-speaking Kurds of southeastern Turkey and northern Iraq/Syria.

Dengbêj performance is almost entirely vocal, with minimal or no instrumental accompaniment. The voice carries everything: narrative, emotion, rhythm, and melodic structure. The vocal techniques include:

- **Sustained melisma** — elaborate ornamentation on single syllables
- **Vocal breaks and cries** — stylized emotional outbursts (the "ax" or "lo" interjections)
- **Dynamic extremes** — from whisper to full-throated projection
- **Rhythmic freedom** — dengbêj songs follow the rhythm of the narrative rather than a fixed meter, making them closer to recitative than to metered song

The dengbêj repertoire includes specific song types: **stran** (songs), **kilam** (laments), **lawik** (epic narratives), and **payizok** (autumn songs of separation). The tradition was passed through apprenticeship, with young singers memorizing repertoires from masters (Blum & Hassanpour, 1996).

### The Political Suppression of Kurdish Music

The history of Kurdish music in Turkey is inseparable from the history of Kurdish political suppression:

- The Turkish state denied the existence of a distinct Kurdish identity for decades, officially referring to Kurds as "Mountain Turks"
- The Kurdish language was banned in public life, education, and broadcasting from 1924 until gradual liberalization beginning in 1991
- Singing in Kurdish was effectively criminalized — musicians were arrested, records were confiscated, and performances were shut down
- The 1980–1990s saw the most intense suppression during the conflict between the Turkish state and the PKK

Kurdish musicians who persisted did so at enormous personal risk. **Şivan Perwer** (b. 1955), the most famous Kurdish musician, has lived in exile since 1976. His music combines dengbêj vocal traditions with modern instrumentation and politically charged lyrics that made him the voice of Kurdish identity for millions — and a target for the Turkish state.

### Kurdish Music and Resistance

The suppression of Kurdish music transformed it into an act of resistance. Simply singing in Kurdish became a political statement. This produced a rich tradition of resistance music:

- **Dengbêjs of exile** — singers who preserved the tradition in diaspora communities in Europe
- **Kurdish popular music** — artists like **Ahmet Kaya** (1957–2000), who sang in both Turkish and Kurdish, whose announcement at a 1999 awards ceremony that he planned to release a Kurdish-language song caused a scandal that forced him into exile (he died in Paris the following year)
- **Contemporary Kurdish artists** — **Aynur Doğan** (b. 1975), whose extraordinary voice brings Kurdish folk music to international audiences while insisting on singing in her mother tongue

### The Dengbêj House (Mala Dengbêjan)

The establishment of the **Dengbêj House** in Diyarbakır in 2007 — a cultural center where elderly dengbêjs gather to perform — represents both preservation and loss. It preserves a tradition that might otherwise die with its last practitioners. But it also transforms an organic, community-embedded practice into an institutional, performative one. The dengbêjs who gather there are mostly elderly men whose apprentices were scattered by decades of war, migration, and cultural suppression (Scalbert-Yücel, 2009).

## 12. Alevi Music: The Semah and the Saz

### Alevism and Music

**Alevism** is a syncretic spiritual tradition practiced by an estimated 15–25 million people in Turkey (exact numbers are politically contested). Alevi theology and practice blend elements of Shi'a Islam, Sufism, pre-Islamic Turkic Tengrism, and Anatolian folk traditions. Music and dance are not supplementary to Alevi worship — they are its primary liturgical form.

This stands in sharp contrast to Sunni Islamic practice, where music's permissibility is debated. In Alevism, the sacred gathering (cem) cannot occur without music. The **semah** — a ritual dance performed during the cem — is a form of prayer expressed through the body's movement in time with sacred songs (nefes and deyiş).

### The Bağlama/Saz: The Sacred Instrument

The **bağlama** (commonly called saz) is the central instrument of Alevi music and holds a sacred status: it is called "telli Kuran" ("the stringed Quran") — the word of God expressed through strings rather than pages.

The bağlama is a long-necked lute with moveable frets, typically with seven strings in three courses. Its importance in Turkish music overall cannot be overstated — it is effectively the national instrument — but in Alevi practice it carries spiritual authority. The **aşık** (literally "one who is in love" — with the divine) who plays the bağlama during the cem is not an entertainer but a liturgical leader. The most revered aşıks are considered saints.

### The Semah Ceremony and Rhythm

The semah dance-rituals are performed during the **Cem** (gathering/assembly), which is the central Alevi worship service. The cem includes twelve services (on iki hizmet), of which music and semah are integral parts. The semah involves:

- **Paired movement** — men and women dance together (a distinctive feature that differentiates Alevi practice from Sunni norms)
- **Circular patterns** — symbolizing the unity of creation
- **Accelerating rhythm** — semah typically begins slowly and builds to ecstatic speed, mirroring the shamanic acceleration pattern noted in Section 1
- **Specific named semah** — the Turnalar Semahı ("Crane Semah"), Kırklar Semahı ("Semah of the Forty"), and others, each with prescribed movements and musical accompaniment

The rhythmic patterns of semah music often use asymmetric meters — 7/8, 9/8, 5/8 — reflecting the broader Anatolian folk tradition but deployed in a sacred context.

### Pir Sultan Abdal and the Aşık Tradition

**Pir Sultan Abdal** (16th century) is the most revered figure in Alevi literary and musical tradition. A poet, revolutionary, and saint, Pir Sultan Abdal's deyiş (sacred poems/songs) form the core of the Alevi musical repertoire. His work is characterized by:

- Mystical love poetry addressed to God through the metaphor of human love
- Social justice themes — critiques of oppression, calls for equality
- Revolutionary defiance — tradition holds that he was executed by the Ottoman governor of Sivas for his rebellious poetry

The aşık tradition continues today, though increasingly in concert settings rather than strictly liturgical ones. Notable modern aşıks and Alevi musicians include **Arif Sağ**, **Musa Eroğlu**, **Yavuz Top**, and **Erdal Erzincan** — masters of the bağlama who bridge the traditional and the contemporary (Markoff, 1986).

## 13. Romani (Roman) Music: The 9/8 Masters

### Romani Musicians in Turkey

The **Romani** (known in Turkish as "Roman") communities of Turkey, concentrated in Thrace (particularly Edirne, Kırklareli, and the Sulukule neighborhood of Istanbul), have been central to Turkish musical life for centuries. Romani musicians occupied a unique social position: marginalized as an ethnic group but indispensable as professional musicians. In Ottoman and Republican Turkey alike, Romani musicians performed at weddings, circumcision celebrations, and festivals across communities — Turkish, Greek, Armenian, Jewish — functioning as a musical bridge between communities that might otherwise not interact (Seeman, 2006).

### The 9/8 Roman Havası

The characteristic rhythm of Turkish Romani music is the **Roman havası** (Romani tune/air), built on the **9/8 aksak** meter, typically grouped as **2+2+2+3**. This rhythm — pulsing, asymmetric, irresistibly dance-inducing — is the heartbeat of Romani celebration music.

The 9/8 Roman havası is not merely a rhythm; it is a rhythmic ecosystem. Within the basic 2+2+2+3 framework, there is enormous variation:

- The darbuka (or dümbelek) provides the foundational pattern, with the düm-tek vocabulary creating the groove
- The davul adds a bass layer with its own accent patterns
- The clarinet and zurna provide melodic content that rhythmically interlocks with the percussion
- Dancers' feet provide a fifth rhythmic voice, their steps and stamps adding polyrhythmic layers

### The Clarinet Revolution

The adoption of the Western clarinet in the late 19th century transformed Romani music in Turkey and the Balkans. The clarinet's range, agility, and ability to produce microtonal inflections made it the perfect vehicle for the virtuosic, ornament-heavy Romani melodic style. Turkish Romani clarinetists have created a performance practice of extraordinary technical brilliance:

- **Mustafa Kandıralı** (1930–2015) — Often considered the greatest Turkish clarinetist, Kandıralı's ability to play in complex makam-based melodic lines at breakneck speed over shifting 9/8 patterns set the standard for Turkish Romani clarinet.
- **Selim Sesler** (1957–2014) — Known as the "Czardas King," Sesler was a clarinetist of extraordinary sensitivity who brought Thracian Romani music to international audiences, working with musicians across genres and borders.
- **Hüsnü Şenlendirici** (b. 1976) — The contemporary master who has brought Turkish Romani clarinet to global stages, collaborating with jazz, world, and electronic musicians while maintaining the tradition's core virtuosity.

### Sulukule: The Lost Neighborhood

The **Sulukule** neighborhood of Istanbul, located inside the old Theodosian walls, was one of the oldest continuously inhabited Romani settlements in the world — over a thousand years of continuous residence. It was the epicenter of Istanbul's Romani musical culture: a neighborhood where music flowed from every window, where children learned darbuka before they learned to write, where the fasıl tradition was kept alive in Romani hands.

In 2006–2009, the Sulukule neighborhood was demolished as part of an urban renewal project. Residents were relocated to distant suburban housing blocks. The destruction of Sulukule was widely condemned as cultural erasure — the deliberate elimination of a living musical community in the name of real estate development. The scattering of the Sulukule community dealt a severe blow to the living transmission of Roman musical traditions in Istanbul (Marsh, 2010).

### The Fasıl in Romani Hands

The Ottoman fasıl tradition (see Section 3) found its most vibrant surviving home in Romani performance practice, particularly in the meyhane (tavern) culture of Istanbul. While the court fasıl was formalized and hierarchical, the Romani meyhane fasıl was interactive, improvisatory, and deeply responsive to the audience. The Romani fasıl tradition preserved repertoire and performance practices that might otherwise have been lost when the Ottoman court tradition was disrupted by Republican reforms.

## 14. Modern Turkish Music: New Syntheses

### Turkish Hip-Hop

Turkish hip-hop emerged in the late 1980s and early 1990s, primarily among Turkish diaspora communities in Germany (especially Berlin). Early artists like **Cartel** (a German-Turkish group whose 1995 self-titled album was a massive hit in Turkey) and **Ceza** (b. 1976, often considered the greatest Turkish rapper) developed a style that incorporated Turkish melodic sensibilities — samples from arabesk, folk songs, and classical Turkish music — within hip-hop production.

The rhythmic dimension of Turkish hip-hop is noteworthy: while the basic beat structure follows global hip-hop conventions (4/4 with emphasis on 2 and 4), many Turkish producers incorporate aksak patterns, darbuka fills, and bağlama samples that create a distinctly Turkish rhythmic feel within the hip-hop framework.

### Anatolian Bass and Electronic Music

The contemporary **Anatolian bass** movement represents the latest chapter in the long history of Turkish musical synthesis. Artists and producers are combining electronic music production techniques with Anatolian musical elements — not as exotic samples but as structural foundations.

**Baba Zula** — Formed in 1996, Baba Zula creates what they call "Istanbul psychedelia" — a live electronic music that uses electrified saz, darbuka, spoons, and electronic processing. Their sound is rooted in the Anatolian rock tradition but pushes into dub, electronica, and experimental territory.

**Gaye Su Akyol** (b. 1985) — One of the most compelling contemporary Turkish musicians, Akyol combines surf rock, psychedelia, Turkish classical music, and Anatolian folk elements with sharp lyrical commentary on contemporary Turkish society. Her rhythmic approach is notable for its fluid movement between Western rock meters and Turkish asymmetric patterns.

**Jakuzi** — Synthpop/post-punk band that incorporates Turkish melodic elements into a contemporary electronic framework.

**İpek Görkem**, **Kozmonot**, and **Yin Yin** (Dutch-Vietnamese, but deeply engaged with Turkish musical elements) represent the growing international interest in Turkish rhythmic and melodic resources within electronic music.

# PART III: ARABIC MUSIC — HISTORICAL ROOTS

## 15. Pre-Islamic Arabian Music: Poetry, Rhythm, and the Caravan

### The Qayna and the Oral Tradition

Music in pre-Islamic Arabia (the Jahiliyya period) was deeply embedded in the oral poetic culture that defined Arabian civilization. The **qayna** (plural: qiyan) — highly trained female musicians who were simultaneously singers, instrumentalists, poets, and entertainers — held a complex social position: often enslaved, yet wielding enormous cultural influence. The qiyan were the professional musicians of pre-Islamic society, trained in vocal technique, instrumental performance (particularly the **mizhar**, a skin-bellied lute), poetry, and the art of conversation (Sawa, 1989).

Pre-Islamic Arabian music was overwhelmingly vocal. The voice, carrying poetry, was the primary musical instrument. Rhythmic patterns were derived from the meters (buhur) of Arabic poetry, codified by **Al-Khalil ibn Ahmad** (718–786 CE) into the science of prosody (arud). The fifteen poetic meters Al-Khalil identified were themselves based on patterns of long and short syllables — essentially rhythmic modes encoded in language.

### Camel Caravan Rhythms: The Hida'

The **hida'** (also hudā') — a form of caravan chanting — is often cited as one of the oldest documented rhythmic practices in Arabic music. Camel drivers chanted rhythmic songs to regulate the pace of their caravans, and the rhythmic pattern of the hida' is said to have derived from the natural gait of the camel. The musicologist Henry George Farmer recorded the traditional account:

> "The Arabs say that a man named Mudar was the first to use hida'. When he fell from his camel and broke his hand, he cried out 'Yā yadāh! Yā yadāh!' ('Oh, my hand!'). The camels responded to his rhythmic cries by adjusting their pace." (Farmer, 1929)

Whether or not this origin story is literal, the hida' tradition demonstrates a fundamental principle that runs through all SWANA music: rhythm is functional before it is aesthetic. It organizes labor, regulates movement, structures communication, and mediates between human beings and their environment.

### The Iqa' al-Hazaj

The term **hazaj** appears both as a poetic meter and as a rhythmic mode, and this overlap is significant. The hazaj meter in poetry (a pattern of long-short-long repeated) maps directly onto a musical rhythmic pattern that became one of the foundational iqa'at (rhythmic modes) of Arabic classical music. This is not coincidence — it reflects the deep unity of poetry and music in Arabic culture, a unity that predates Islam and continues to shape Arabic music today (Wright, 1978).

## 16. The Islamic Golden Age: Al-Farabi, Al-Kindi, and the Science of Rhythm

### Music as Mathematical Science

The Islamic Golden Age (roughly 8th–13th centuries) produced some of the most sophisticated music theory in world history. For the great Islamic philosophers-scientists, music was not a "mere" art — it was a branch of mathematics, connected to cosmology, medicine, and the nature of the soul.

**Al-Kindi** (c. 801–873 CE), the "Philosopher of the Arabs," wrote multiple treatises on music. His work established the framework for Arabic music theory by:

- Connecting the four strings of the ud to the four elements, four humors, four seasons, and celestial spheres
- Analyzing rhythmic patterns mathematically, as ratios of time values
- Arguing that specific musical modes and rhythms had therapeutic effects — particular modes to cure particular ailments

**Al-Farabi** (c. 872–950 CE), arguably the most important music theorist in Islamic history, wrote the *Kitāb al-Mūsīqā al-Kabīr* (Grand Book of Music), which:

- Systematically described the iqa'at (rhythmic modes) used in his time, with precise notation of their internal structures
- Analyzed rhythm in terms of attacks (naqra) and silences (sukun), establishing a binary analytical framework
- Distinguished between light (khafif) and heavy (thaqil) rhythmic cycles
- Connected musical rhythm to prosodic (poetic) rhythm, showing their shared mathematical foundations
- Described instruments and their tuning systems with a precision that allows modern scholars to reconstruct medieval practice

**Safi al-Din al-Urmawi** (1216–1294), working in Baghdad shortly before the Mongol destruction, produced the *Kitāb al-Adwār* (Book of Cycles) and the *Risālah al-Sharafiyyah*, which codified the modal and rhythmic system with unprecedented rigor. His work became the foundation for all subsequent Arabic, Persian, and Ottoman music theory (Wright, 1978; Sawa, 2004).

### Rhythm and Mathematics

The Islamic theorists' approach to rhythm was fundamentally mathematical. Al-Farabi analyzed rhythmic patterns as sequences of time-units (azminah, singular zaman), where each attack or silence occupied a specific number of units. This allowed him to:

1. Precisely describe complex rhythmic patterns
2. Classify them into families based on their mathematical properties
3. Analyze the relationships between different patterns
4. Distinguish between the abstract pattern (the iqa') and its concrete realization in performance (where ornaments, dynamics, and micro-variations bring the pattern to life)

This mathematical approach did not reduce music to abstraction. Al-Farabi and his successors recognized that the "same" mathematical pattern could sound entirely different depending on tempo, dynamics, timbre, and performance context. The mathematical description was a framework for understanding, not a replacement for musical experience.

## 17. The Iqa'at System: How Arabic Rhythmic Modes Work

### Beyond Time Signatures

The **iqa'** (plural: iqa'at) system is the Arabic framework for organizing rhythm. Understanding how it differs from the Western time signature system is crucial for respectful representation.

A Western time signature (e.g., 4/4) tells you:
- How many beats per measure
- What note value gets one beat

An iqa' tells you:
- The total cycle length
- The specific pattern of **dum** (deep bass stroke), **tak** (sharp treble stroke), and **sa** (rest/silence) within the cycle
- The aesthetic character and contextual associations
- The relationship to specific melodic forms and genres

The dum-tak vocabulary is not merely notation — it is an embodied pedagogy. Students learn iqa'at by vocalizing them: "DUM tak tak DUM tak" etc. This oral learning method ensures that the pattern is internalized physically, not merely intellectually. The body "knows" the pattern before the mind analyzes it (Marcus, 2007).

### Major Iqa'at

| Iqa' | Beats | Pattern (simplified) | Character/Use |
|------|-------|---------------------|---------------|
| **Maqsum** | 4/4 | D-t--t-D--t-- | The most common Egyptian/Levantine iqa'; the "default" rhythm of Arabic music |
| **Baladi** | 4/4 | D-D--t-D--t-- | "Countryside" — a heavier variant of maqsum with double dum opening |
| **Saidi** | 4/4 | D-t--D-D--t-- | Associated with Upper Egypt (Sa'id); the rhythm of tahtib (stick fighting) |
| **Malfuf** | 2/4 | D--tt-- | A rolling, propulsive rhythm; common in processions and fast songs |
| **Masmoudi Saghir** | 4/4 | D-D--tD--t-- | "Small masmoudi"; a versatile medium-tempo rhythm |
| **Masmoudi Kabir** | 8/4 | D--D----t--D----t-- | "Large masmoudi"; a stately, expansive cycle used in classical and tarab settings |
| **Wahda** | 4/4 | D-------t--t-- | A slow, spacious rhythm with a single strong dum; associated with tarab and the muwashshah |
| **Samai Thaqil** | 10/8 | D--t--t-D-t | A classical rhythm used in the samai compositional form; notable for its 10-beat cycle |
| **Jurjina** | 10/8 | D--tD--t-t | Another 10-beat cycle, distinct from samai thaqil; found in Iraqi and Gulf music |
| **Karachi** | 2/4 | D-t-t | A light, fast rhythm from Gulf traditions |
| **Ayyub** | 2/4 | D--D-t | A driving, hypnotic rhythm associated with Sufi dhikr and zar ceremonies |
| **Ciftetelli** | 8/4 | D--t--D-D-t | A slow, sinuous rhythm associated with solo dance; shared with Turkish and Greek traditions |
| **Bambi** | varies | Regional variants | A Levantine rhythm used in dabke |

### The Dum-Tak System as Embodied Knowledge

The specificity of dum and tak (and their variants: dum = center of the drum head for bass, tak = edge for treble, ka = ghost note, sa = silence) means that an iqa' is inseparable from the instrument that produces it. The darbuka (also called tablah, doumbek) is the primary vehicle for iqa'at, and the instrument's acoustics — its sharp attack, sustained bass, and crisp treble — are integral to the rhythmic system's sound.

Different regional darbuka traditions produce different timbres: the Egyptian tablah (typically aluminum or ceramic with a synthetic head) has a drier, more defined sound than the Turkish darbuka (often copper with a synthetic head, producing a more resonant bass). These timbral differences are not trivial — they affect how the iqa'at feel and function in their respective musical contexts.

## 18. Andalusian Music (Al-Andalus): Ziryab and the Bridge Between Worlds

### The Cultural Crucible

**Al-Andalus** — Islamic Iberia (711–1492) — was one of the most extraordinary cultural environments in human history. For nearly eight centuries, Muslim, Jewish, and Christian communities in the Iberian Peninsula created a civilization of remarkable intellectual and artistic achievement. Music was central to this culture, and the musical exchanges that occurred in Al-Andalus had lasting effects on both the Arabic and European musical traditions.

### Ziryab: The Revolutionary

**Ziryab** (Abu al-Hasan Ali ibn Nafi', c. 789–857) is the most famous musician of Al-Andalus and one of the most influential figures in world music history. A student of the great Baghdad musician Ishaq al-Mawsili, Ziryab left the Abbasid court (supposedly because his teacher was jealous of his talent) and eventually settled in Córdoba under the patronage of the Umayyad emir Abd al-Rahman II.

Ziryab's contributions, as recorded by later historians (and possibly embellished by legend), include:

- **Adding a fifth string to the ud** — the four-string ud was standard; Ziryab added a fifth, expanding its range and establishing the five-course instrument that influenced the European lute
- **Establishing a music conservatory in Córdoba** — with a systematic pedagogy that trained generations of Andalusian musicians
- **Developing the nuba form** — a suite-like musical form organized by mode, moving through distinct rhythmic sections from slow to fast (the nuba concept profoundly influenced the fasıl traditions of the Ottoman and North African worlds)
- **Innovating performance practice** — using an eagle-quill plectrum instead of a wooden one, changing the stringing materials

### The Muwashshah

The **muwashshah** is a strophic poetic-musical form that originated in Al-Andalus (10th–11th century) and spread throughout the Arabic world, where it remains a living tradition. Its significance for rhythm is that the muwashshah uses complex meters (often 10/8 samai thaqil or 7/8 patterns) that do not occur in standard Arabic qaṣīda (ode) poetry, suggesting that the musical rhythm drove the poetic form rather than vice versa — a reversal of the usual Arabic relationship between poetry and music.

The muwashshah tradition survives most vibrantly in **Aleppo** (Syria), where it was cultivated for centuries, and in North African **malouf** and **gharnata** traditions that trace their ancestry to Andalusian refugees (Davila, 2013).

### Legacy: The Musical Bridge

The musical exchanges of Al-Andalus influenced European music in ways that are still being uncovered:

- The European **lute** is directly descended from the Arabic **ud** (the word "lute" comes from al-ud)
- The **rebec** derives from the Arabic **rabab**
- The **guitar** has complex etymological and organological connections to Arabic instruments (though the precise lineage is debated)
- Medieval European troubadour poetry shows formal and thematic parallels with Arabic Andalusian poetry that suggest influence (though this too is debated)
- The concept of organized musical suites and the practice of systematic music education both have Andalusian precedents

## 19. The Maqam-Iqa' Relationship

### Two Interlocking Systems

Arabic music operates through the interaction of two modal systems: **maqam** (melodic mode) and **iqa'** (rhythmic mode). These are not independent parameters that can be freely combined — traditional practice involves specific affinities between certain maqams and certain iqa'at.

For example:

- The maqam **bayati** (one of the most common Arabic modes, with a characteristic neutral/half-flat second degree) has natural affinities with the **masmoudi** family of iqa'at in classical contexts and with **maqsum/baladi** in folk and popular contexts
- The maqam **saba** (a deeply melancholic mode with a distinctive lowered fourth degree) tends toward slower, more expansive iqa'at that allow its emotional weight to unfold
- The maqam **hijaz** (with its characteristic augmented second interval) is associated with both the stately **masmoudi kabir** in classical settings and the more driving **saidi** in folk contexts

This maqam-iqa' interaction means that choosing a rhythm is not merely a technical decision but an aesthetic and cultural one. The "right" iqa' for a given maqam, genre, and emotional intention is a matter of deep musical knowledge — the kind of knowledge that takes years of immersion to develop (Racy, 2003).

# PART IV: ARABIC REGIONAL TRADITIONS

## 20. Egyptian Music: Tarab, the Firqa, and the Street

### The Golden Age and Tarab

Egypt has been the cultural capital of the Arabic-speaking world for much of the 20th century, and Egyptian music has exerted an outsized influence on Arabic music globally. The concept of **tarab** — often translated as "musical ecstasy" or "enchantment" — is central to understanding Egyptian (and broader Arabic) musical aesthetics.

Tarab is not merely a pleasant listening experience. It describes a specific state of emotional transport in which the boundaries between performer and audience dissolve, where a perfectly executed melodic phrase or rhythmic shift produces an involuntary physical and emotional response — tears, sighs, cries of appreciation ("Allah!", "Ya salam!"). The cultivation of tarab is the ultimate goal of Arabic musical performance (Racy, 2003).

### Umm Kulthum: The Voice of a Century

**Umm Kulthum** (c. 1898–1975) is not merely the most important Egyptian singer; she is arguably the most influential vocalist of the 20th century, with a career spanning five decades and an audience of hundreds of millions across the Arabic-speaking world.

Her significance for rhythm includes:

- **Extended temporal forms** — her concerts featured single songs lasting 45 minutes to over an hour, during which she would repeat phrases, extend improvisations, and respond to audience reactions. The rhythmic framework had to be flexible enough to accommodate these expansions.
- **The firqa** — her orchestra (firqa) was a large ensemble that combined Arabic instruments (ud, qanun, nay, riqq) with a Western-style string section. The **riqq** player (riqq being a small tambourine with heavy jingles, the prestige percussion instrument of the Arabic classical tradition) had the role of holding the rhythmic framework together through these extended improvisations.
- **Rubato and rhythmic freedom** — Umm Kulthum's vocal style involved constant rhythmic play: stretching phrases, compressing them, arriving early or late on the beat. The riqq player and the ensemble had to follow her, not the reverse.

### Mohamed Abdel Wahab and Abdel Halim Hafez

**Mohamed Abdel Wahab** (1902–1991) was the great modernizer of Egyptian music. As composer and performer, he introduced Western instruments (electric guitar, accordion, orchestral percussion) and compositional techniques into Arabic music. His rhythmic innovations included incorporating Latin rhythms (particularly the bolero and tango), jazz-inflected syncopation, and cinematic orchestration into the Arabic framework.

**Abdel Halim Hafez** (1929–1977) — "The Dark Nightingale" — was the romantic idol of the Arab world. His music, often composed by Abdel Wahab or Baligh Hamdi, bridged classical tarab and popular song.

### Shaabi: Street Music

**Shaabi** (literally "of the people") in the Egyptian context refers to the working-class popular music of Cairo's dense neighborhoods. Its founder is generally considered to be **Ahmed Adaweyya** (b. 1945), whose raw, powerful voice and colloquial lyrics about everyday life scandalized the establishment in the 1970s.

Shaabi's rhythmic character draws on the **baladi** and **maqsum** iqa'at but with a heavier, more driving feel — louder, faster, more physical. The tabla baladi (a large goblet drum) is the signature instrument.

### Mahraganat: The Digital Revolution

**Mahraganat** (literally "festivals") is the street electronic music that has exploded from Cairo's working-class neighborhoods since approximately 2007. Created on laptops with pirated software, mahraganat features:

- Autotuned vocals with rapid-fire delivery
- Synthesized rhythms based on baladi/maqsum patterns but accelerated and layered with electronic effects
- Bass-heavy production influenced by global electronic dance music
- Lyrics in heavy Cairo street slang about everyday life, partying, and social commentary

Key figures include **Islam Chipsy** (a keyboard virtuoso whose electro-shaabi is like nothing else on earth), **DJ Figo**, **Oka Wi Ortega**, and **Wegz** (who has brought mahraganat aesthetics into mainstream Arabic pop).

Mahraganat's relationship to traditional iqa'at is transformative rather than preservationist: the foundational patterns are recognizably Egyptian, but they are pitched up, speeded up, layered, and distorted in ways that would be unrecognizable to classical percussion masters. Yet the bodily response — the physical compulsion to move — connects mahraganat to the deepest functions of rhythm in Egyptian culture (Sprengel, 2019).

## 21. Levantine Music: Fairuz, the Rahbanis, and Dabke

### Fairuz and the Rahbani Brothers

**Fairuz** (Nouhad Haddad, b. 1934) is, alongside Umm Kulthum, one of the two greatest voices in Arabic music. While Umm Kulthum represented the grandeur of classical tarab, Fairuz — and her collaborators, the **Rahbani brothers** (Assi and Mansour) — created a Lebanese musical identity that blended Arabic melody with Western harmony, theatrical forms, and a distinctly Levantine sensibility.

The Rahbani brothers' compositions for Fairuz drew on Lebanese folk music, particularly the **dabke** rhythmic tradition, but arranged with Western orchestral techniques, operetta-style dramatic structures, and jazz harmonies. Their musical plays at the **Baalbeck International Festival** in the 1960s and 1970s created a new art form.

### Dabke: The Earth Dance

**Dabke** (also dabka, debke) is the folk line dance of the Levant — shared across Palestine, Lebanon, Syria, and Jordan — and one of the most important rhythmic traditions in Arabic music. The word comes from the Arabic root d-b-k, meaning "to stomp" or "to stamp," and the dance is exactly that: a line of linked dancers stamping the earth in unison.

The dabke rhythm is built on several characteristic iqa'at:

- A driving 4/4 or 2/4 pattern with strong emphasis on the downbeat
- The **tableh** (a large frame drum or, in modern practice, a darbuka) provides the foundation
- The **mijwiz** (double-pipe reed instrument) or **arghul** (another double-pipe) provides the melody
- The **yarghoul** and **shababa** (end-blown flute) add melodic color

The lead dancer (**lawweeh** or **raas**) improvises footwork while the line maintains the basic step, creating a tension between individual virtuosity and collective unity that is central to dabke's social meaning.

Dabke is not merely entertainment — it is a expression of community solidarity, land attachment, and collective identity. This is especially significant for **Palestinian** communities, for whom dabke has become a symbol of cultural persistence and resistance to displacement. Palestinian dabke troupes perform worldwide as acts of cultural assertion: "We are still here. Our culture is still alive" (McDonald, 2013).

## 22. Iraqi Music: The Maqam Tradition and Chobi

### The Iraqi Maqam (UNESCO Heritage)

The **Iraqi maqam** (al-maqam al-iraqi) is a distinct regional tradition within the broader Arabic maqam system, recognized by UNESCO in 2003 as an Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity. Unlike the Egyptian or Levantine traditions where maqam refers primarily to a scale/mode, the Iraqi maqam is a complete performance form — a suite-like structure with prescribed melodic progressions, rhythmic transitions, and emotional arcs.

The Iraqi maqam is performed by a **chalghi** ensemble, traditionally consisting of:

- **Santur** — hammered dulcimer (the lead melodic instrument, distinguishing the Iraqi tradition from other Arabic traditions where the ud leads)
- **Joza** — a spike fiddle (a four-stringed bowed instrument held upright), unique to Iraqi music
- **Tabla/Dunbak** — goblet drum
- **Riqq** — tambourine
- A solo vocalist (qari' al-maqam)

The rhythmic character of Iraqi maqam performance is distinctive: the patterns are often more elastic and ornamented than in Egyptian or Levantine traditions, with the dunbak and riqq players engaging in sophisticated interplay with the vocalist's rhythmic phrasing.

### Chobi

**Chobi** (also chopi) is the folk dance tradition of Iraq, related to the Levantine dabke but with its own character — typically faster, with more energetic footwork. The chobi rhythm is a driving pattern that propels group line dancing at celebrations. The **zurna and dahol** (double-reed and drum) combination is the traditional ensemble for outdoor chobi.

## 23. Gulf/Khaliji Music

### Traditional Gulf Rhythms

The Arabian Gulf states (Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Bahrain, Qatar, UAE, Oman) have distinct musical traditions that reflect their maritime and desert heritage:

**Samri** — A traditional Najdi (central Arabian) form featuring call-and-response vocals accompanied by hand clapping and frame drum (tar). The rhythmic pattern is a distinctive 6/8 that drives the swaying movement of the performers. Samri is associated with Bedouin poetry gatherings.

**Ardha** — The sword dance tradition, now the national dance of Saudi Arabia. Ardha involves two lines of men facing each other, brandishing swords, chanting poetry in unison, while drummers play large frame drums (tar) in a distinctive pattern. Originally a pre-battle ritual, ardha now features at national celebrations and diplomatic events.

**Fijiri** — Pearl-diving songs from Bahrain, Kuwait, and the UAE. These work songs, sung aboard diving vessels during the pearl season, feature complex polyrhythmic hand clapping, drumming on clay water jars (jahlah), and call-and-response vocals. Fijiri is one of the few surviving work-song traditions that preserves genuinely functional polyrhythm.

**Liwa and Fann al-Tanbura** — Afro-Gulf traditions reflecting the historical connections between the Gulf and East Africa. The tanbura (a lyre) and specific polyrhythmic patterns trace directly to East African musical traditions, brought to the Gulf through trade, migration, and the slave trade. These traditions are a reminder that the Gulf has always been connected to the Indian Ocean world, not only to the Mediterranean and Central Asian spheres.

### Mohammed Abdu

**Mohammed Abdu** (b. 1949), the "Artist of the Arabs," is the most celebrated Saudi musician. His music has evolved from traditional Hijazi (western Arabian) folk styles through pan-Arab pop to sophisticated fusions that incorporate Khaliji rhythmic patterns into contemporary production.

## 24. North African Traditions

### Gnawa (Morocco): The Trance Tradition

**Gnawa** music is a Moroccan spiritual-musical tradition with roots in sub-Saharan West Africa. The Gnawa are descendants of formerly enslaved peoples brought to Morocco from the Sahel and Sudan, and their music preserves African spiritual practices within an Islamic framework (a syncretic blend comparable to Afro-Caribbean traditions like Santeria or Candomblé).

The Gnawa **lila** (all-night ceremony) is a healing ritual featuring:

- The **guembri** (also sintir or hajhuj) — a three-stringed bass lute with a deep, resonant tone, played by the **maalem** (master musician)
- **Qraqeb** — large iron castanets played by the ensemble, producing a metallic, cyclical rhythmic pattern
- Call-and-response vocals invoking spirits (mluk) associated with specific colors, saints, and emotional states
- Trance possession — participants are "mounted" by spirits, and specific musical modes and rhythmic patterns correspond to specific spirits

The rhythmic character of Gnawa music is distinctive in the SWANA context: the qraqeb provide a constant, driving ostinato in a compound meter feel (often approximated as 6/8), while the guembri plays bass lines that are both melodic and rhythmic. The result is a polyrhythmic texture that connects more to West African traditions than to the Arabic iqa' system — though centuries of coexistence have created hybrid forms (Kapchan, 2007).

The **Essaouira Gnawa and World Music Festival** (established 1998) has brought Gnawa to international attention, leading to collaborations with jazz, blues, and rock musicians — collaborations that are sometimes celebrated as cross-cultural dialogue and sometimes criticized as cultural appropriation.

### Raï (Algeria): The Music of Rebellion

**Raï** (from Arabic ra'y, "opinion" or "point of view") emerged in the 1920s–1930s in western Algeria (particularly Oran) as a form of musical expression that challenged social conventions. The original raï was sung by **cheikhate** (female performers) and **cheikhs** (male poets) at festivals and in cabarets, addressing taboo subjects — alcohol, sex, heartbreak — with blunt directness.

Modern raï crystallized in the 1970s–1980s when young musicians (taking the title "cheb" meaning "young") electrified the tradition with synthesizers, drum machines, and Western pop structures. **Cheb Khaled** (b. 1960), the "King of Raï," became the genre's global ambassador with his 1992 hit "Didi."

Raï's rhythmic foundation draws on Algerian folk patterns — particularly a driving 2/4 or 4/4 feel with heavy emphasis on the downbeat — but the modern raï sound incorporated drum machines and electronic beats that connected it to global dance music. The gasba (end-blown flute) and the gallal (clay drum) of traditional raï were replaced or augmented by synthesizers and drum machines, but the underlying rhythmic feel — propulsive, celebratory, physically compelling — remained (Schade-Poulsen, 1999).

Raï's political dimension is crucial: during Algeria's "Black Decade" (civil war of the 1990s), raï musicians were targeted by Islamist militants who deemed the music haram. **Cheb Hasni** was assassinated in 1994. Many raï artists fled to France, where raï became the soundtrack of the North African diaspora.

### Chaabi (Algeria)

**Chaabi** (Algerian) — distinct from Egyptian shaabi — is the popular music of the Algerian Casbah (old city of Algiers). Founded by **El Hadj M'Hamed El Anka** (1907–1978), Algerian chaabi blends Andalusian modal structures with the rhythms and language of urban working-class Algiers. The genre uses a distinctive rhythmic approach rooted in Andalusian-origin iqa'at.

### Malouf (Tunisia/Libya)

**Malouf** is the classical music tradition of Tunisia and Libya, directly descended from the Andalusian nuba tradition brought by refugees from Al-Andalus. The malouf nuba follows a suite structure with prescribed modal and rhythmic progressions. The Rashidiyya Institute in Tunis (founded 1934) has been central to preserving and codifying the Tunisian malouf tradition (Davis, 2004).

## 25. Palestinian Music: Resistance and Preservation

### Music Under Occupation

Palestinian music exists in a unique political context: a national musical tradition maintained under military occupation, in exile, and across diaspora communities scattered worldwide. Music serves multiple functions simultaneously — aesthetic expression, cultural preservation, political resistance, and community solidarity.

### Traditional Forms

- **Dabke** — (see Section 21) takes on heightened significance as a symbol of Palestinian identity and connection to the land
- **Ataaba and Mijanah** — forms of vocal improvisation based on poetic wordplay, performed at weddings and gatherings
- **Zajal** — oral poetry performed competitively, with audience participation

### Hip-Hop as Resistance

Palestinian hip-hop has become one of the most powerful musical expressions of resistance in the contemporary world:

- **DAM** (Da Arabian MC's) — formed in 1998 in Lod by Tamer Nafar, Suhell Nafar, and Mahmoud Jreri, DAM is widely considered the first Palestinian hip-hop group. Their 2001 track "Meen Erhabi?" ("Who's the Terrorist?") became an anthem of Palestinian resistance. Their music blends Arabic and Hebrew lyrics, sampled Arabic melodies, and hip-hop production.
- **Shadia Mansour** — "The First Lady of Arabic Hip-Hop," whose music foregrounds Palestinian identity and resistance
- **The Ramallah hip-hop scene** — including groups like **Katibeh 5** and **Stormtrap**, who use music to document daily life under occupation

The rhythmic dimension of Palestinian hip-hop is significant: many tracks incorporate dabke patterns, Arabic percussion samples, and traditional melodic fragments into hip-hop production, creating a sound that is simultaneously global and specifically Palestinian (McDonald, 2013).

## 26. Yemeni Music

### Unique Traditions

Yemeni music stands apart within the Arabic tradition due to Yemen's geographic isolation (mountainous terrain, distance from other major Arab cultural centers) and its ancient, distinct cultural heritage. Yemen's musical traditions include:

- The **mizmar** — an oboe-type instrument central to Yemeni folk music, played at weddings and celebrations with accompanying drumming
- **Bara'** — a traditional group dance involving daggers, performed in a circle with complex footwork and a distinctive rhythmic framework (UNESCO Intangible Heritage)
- **Dan** — a Yemeni song form with distinctive melodic contours and rhythmic patterns influenced by both Arabic and East African traditions
- The **tasa** and **marfa'** drums provide rhythmic foundations distinct from the darbuka-centered traditions further north
- **Hadrami** music — from the Hadhramaut region, which has influenced musical traditions across the Indian Ocean world (Indonesia, Malaysia, East Africa) through centuries of Hadrami trade diaspora

# PART V: PERSIAN/IRANIAN MUSIC

## 27. The Dastgah System

### Persian Classical Music

Persian classical music (musiqi-e assil or musiqi-e sonnati) is one of the great art music traditions of the world, with a theoretical sophistication that rivals any system on earth. Its organizational framework is the **dastgah** — a system of twelve modal frameworks (seven dastgahs and five avazes) that govern melodic behavior through prescribed phrases (gushehs) collected in a compendium called the **radif**.

Unlike the Arabic maqam or Turkish makam, where the mode is primarily a scale with behavioral rules, a Persian dastgah is closer to a repertoire: a collection of hundreds of melodic phrases (gushehs) organized in a traditional order, which the performer navigates through a combination of memorization and improvisation. The radif is not a fixed composition but a vast, loosely structured reservoir of musical material.

### The Role of Rhythm

Rhythm in Persian classical music occupies a paradoxical position: it is simultaneously highly developed and deliberately understated. The most prestigious form of Persian classical performance — the **avaz** (vocal improvisation) — is **unmeasured** (without fixed rhythmic cycle). The performer moves freely through the gushehs of the dastgah, with rhythm determined by breath, phrasing, and emotional contour rather than by a cyclical pattern.

Measured rhythm appears in:

- **Tasnif** — composed songs with fixed rhythm and melody
- **Reng** — instrumental dance pieces
- **Zarbi** sections — metered passages within an otherwise free-rhythm performance
- **Chahar-mezrab** — fast, virtuosic instrumental pieces with driving rhythmic patterns

This creates a characteristic alternation in Persian classical performance: free-rhythm sections (avaz, taksim) expressing the modal and emotional essence of the dastgah, punctuated by measured sections (tasnif, reng, chahar-mezrab) that provide rhythmic contrast and relief (Nettl, 2015).

---

## 28. Persian Rhythmic Modes

### How Persian Rhythm Differs

While Arabic iqa'at and Turkish usul are systematized, named, and theoretically codified, Persian rhythmic patterns are less formally organized. There is no Persian equivalent of the comprehensive usul or iqa' classification systems. Instead, rhythmic patterns in Persian music tend to be:

- **Associated with specific genres** — the zarbi patterns of classical music, the dance rhythms of regional folk traditions, the rhythmic patterns of specific composed forms (tasnif, reng)
- **More flexible and improvisatory** — the tombak player has greater freedom to vary and ornament the pattern than the darbuka player in a typical Arabic ensemble
- **Less hierarchically structured** — there is less emphasis on a "correct" foundational pattern and more on moment-to-moment rhythmic invention

Common Persian rhythmic patterns:

- **Kereshmeh** — 6/8 (or 6/4), a graceful, flowing pattern associated with dance
- **Sheshta** — 6/16, a rapid pattern
- **Chaharmezrab** — typically 6/8, the driving rhythm of the virtuosic chaharmezrab form
- **Rengi** — various patterns associated with dance forms

The most distinctive feature of Persian rhythm is the **tombak** tradition's approach to ornamentation: the basic rhythmic pattern is a skeleton upon which the player builds an elaborate edifice of rolls, fills, snaps, and tone colors that can render the underlying pattern almost unrecognizable. The pattern is felt more than heard — a structural implication rather than a stated fact (During, Mirabdolbaghi, & Safvat, 1991).

## 29. The Tombak Tradition

### The Instrument

The **tombak** (also tonbak, donbak, zarb) is a large wooden goblet drum, carved from a single block of wood (traditionally walnut), with a goatskin head. It is the primary percussion instrument of Persian classical music and arguably the most technically demanding hand drum in the world.

### Hossein Tehrani: The Revolutionary

**Hossein Tehrani** (1912–1974) single-handedly elevated the tombak from an accompanying instrument to a solo art form. Before Tehrani, the tombak was considered a secondary instrument in Persian classical music — useful for keeping time but not worthy of serious artistic attention. Tehrani developed a vocabulary of techniques that revealed the instrument's full expressive potential:

- **Tom** (bass tone), **bak** (treble tone), **pel** (snap/grace note) — the fundamental strokes
- **Riz** — a roll technique involving rapid alternation of fingers
- **Eshareh** — finger snaps against the rim
- A system of combining these strokes into rapid, complex phrases that function as both rhythmic pattern and melodic contour

### Modern Masters

After Tehrani, subsequent generations have continued to expand the tombak's vocabulary:

- **Nasser Farhangfar** (1947–2011) — extended Tehrani's technical innovations
- **Bahman Rajabi** (b. 1940) — a scholar-performer who systematized tombak technique
- **Mohammad Esmaili** — contemporary virtuoso known for extreme speed and complexity
- **Pejman Hadadi** — who has brought Persian percussion into dialogue with jazz and world music

The tombak tradition demonstrates a principle relevant to drum machine design: rhythm in Persian music is not merely a pattern to be looped but a language to be spoken, with grammar (technique), vocabulary (stroke types), syntax (pattern construction), and rhetoric (expressive variation).

## 30. Iranian Pop: Golden Age and Underground

### The Pre-Revolution Golden Age

Before the 1979 Islamic Revolution, Iran had a vibrant popular music scene. The 1960s and 1970s saw:

- **Googoosh** (Faegheh Atashin, b. 1950) — The most famous Iranian pop star, whose voice, style, and persona defined an era. After the revolution, she was forbidden from performing publicly for 21 years.
- **Dariush** (Dariush Eghbali) — romantic pop vocalist who went into exile after the revolution
- **Hayedeh** (1942–1990) — powerful vocalist who combined classical Persian vocal technique with pop production

Rhythmically, pre-revolutionary Iranian pop blended Persian melodic sensibilities with Western rock and pop rhythms, funk grooves, and psychedelic production. The 6/8 kereshmeh rhythm appears frequently, as do adaptations of Western dance rhythms inflected with Persian ornamentation.

### Post-Revolution: Restriction and Resistance

After 1979, popular music was severely restricted. Women were banned from singing solo in public. Western-influenced pop was condemned. Music itself was viewed with suspicion by revolutionary authorities.

This created an extraordinary underground music scene:

- **Bedroom studios** producing pop, rock, metal, electronic, and hip-hop music distributed through bootleg cassettes and later through the internet
- The **Tehran underground** electronic scene, which emerged in the 2000s, with producers creating techno, house, and experimental music despite the lack of legal venues
- **Iranian diaspora musicians** — particularly in Los Angeles ("Tehrangeles"), where exiled musicians maintained and evolved Persian pop

## 31. Kurdish Music Across Borders

### A Shared Tradition Divided by States

Kurdish music exists across four states (Turkey, Iraq, Iran, Syria) without being fully claimed or fully suppressed by any of them. Despite political borders, Kurdish musical traditions maintain deep commonalities:

- The **dengbêj/dengbêj** tradition (see Section 11) exists in both Turkey and Iraq
- The **tembûr/tanbur** tradition spans Turkey, Iraq, and Iran
- **Govend/heyranok** circle dances with their associated rhythmic patterns exist across all Kurdish regions
- The **daf** (large frame drum) is prominent in both Iranian and Iraqi Kurdish music, and its use in Sufi Kurdish ceremonies connects to both Sunni and Alevi/Yezidi spiritual practices

### Regional Distinctions

While sharing a core identity, Kurdish music varies by region:

- **Kurmanji-speaking areas** (Turkey, northern Iraq/Syria) — emphasis on vocal traditions (dengbêj), the bağlama/tembûr, and zurna-davul
- **Sorani-speaking areas** (Iraqi Kurdistan, western Iran) — greater integration with Arabic/Persian musical frameworks, use of the santour, influence of Iraqi maqam traditions
- **Gorani/Hawrami areas** (Iran-Iraq border) — unique vocal traditions and melodic styles

# PART VI: SOCIOPOLITICAL CONTEXT

## 1. Music and Colonialism

### Borders Through Music

The European colonial powers that drew the modern borders of the SWANA region in the 19th and 20th centuries — primarily Britain and France — divided musical communities with no regard for cultural continuity:

- The **Sykes-Picot Agreement** (1916) and subsequent mandate borders split the Levantine musical world: the dabke tradition was divided among Syria, Lebanon, Palestine/Israel, Jordan, and Iraq
- French colonialism in the **Maghreb** imposed artificial separations between Algerian, Tunisian, and Moroccan musical traditions that had been unified under the Andalusian-origin nuba system
- British control of **Egypt** and the **Gulf** created administrative boundaries that affected patronage, media distribution, and musical exchange
- The creation of **Israel** (1948) and the subsequent Nakba (catastrophe) scattered Palestinian musical communities and disrupted the shared Palestinian-Levantine musical ecosystem

### Colonial Cultural Policy

Colonial powers also actively shaped musical culture:

- The French promoted Western-style conservatories in North Africa that systematized (and partially distorted) traditional music
- The British influenced Egyptian musical life through their control of media infrastructure
- Colonial-era "Orientalist" scholarship both preserved and misrepresented SWANA musical traditions, often filtering them through European theoretical frameworks that could not capture their actual structures (Stokes, 2004)

---

## 2. Music and Nationalism

### State Music-Building

Across the SWANA region, newly independent states used music as a tool for building national identity:

- **Turkey** — the most dramatic case (see Section 6), where the state actively attempted to create a new national music through top-down reform
- **Egypt** — under Nasser, Egyptian music (particularly Umm Kulthum's broadcasts) became a tool of pan-Arab nationalism, with Cairo Radio reaching the entire Arabic-speaking world
- **Israel/Palestine** — a particularly charged case where Israeli nation-building involved constructing a "new Hebrew music" that drew on both European and Middle Eastern sources while the Palestinian musical tradition was simultaneously being disrupted by displacement. The Mizrahi (Middle Eastern Jewish) musical traditions that immigrants brought from Iraq, Yemen, and Morocco were initially marginalized in favor of a European-influenced national style, only gaining recognition later.
- **Iraq** — the Ba'athist regime under Saddam Hussein promoted music that glorified the state while suppressing Kurdish and Shi'a musical expressions

---

## 3. Music and Censorship

### The Control of Sound

Music censorship across the SWANA region has taken many forms:

- **Turkey** — the progressive banning and unbanning of Ottoman classical music, Kurdish music, and arabesk (detailed in Sections 6, 8, 11)
- **Iran** — post-1979 restrictions on women singing solo, banning of pop music, tight control of all public musical performance. Musicians require permits; lyrics must be approved; even the emotional tenor of permitted music is regulated
- **Egypt** — periodic censorship of shaabi and mahraganat lyrics deemed vulgar or politically threatening; the 2020 ban on mahraganat from official media platforms
- **Saudi Arabia** — until the recent "Vision 2030" liberalization, public musical performance was severely restricted. Concerts were banned for decades. The recent opening of concert venues and music festivals represents a dramatic reversal.
- **Iraq under Saddam** — promotion of state-approved nationalist music; persecution of musicians associated with opposition communities
- **Algeria during the civil war** — Islamist militants' campaign against raï and other musicians in the 1990s

---

## 4. Music and Resistance

### Sound Against Power

Music has been a primary medium of political resistance across the SWANA region:

- **Kurdish resistance music** (see Section 11) — singing in Kurdish as itself an act of defiance
- **Palestinian resistance music** — from folk songs about the land to contemporary hip-hop documenting occupation
- **Arab Spring music** — the 2011 uprisings generated an explosion of protest music, particularly in Tunisia, Egypt, and Syria. The Tunisian rapper **El Général** (Hamada Ben Amor) released "Rais Lebled" ("President of the Country") in 2010, widely credited as one of the catalysts of the Tunisian revolution
- **Iranian underground music** — the very existence of an underground scene is a form of resistance
- **Algerian raï** — defiance of both state conservatism and Islamist violence

### Hip-Hop as the New Protest Form

Hip-hop has emerged as the dominant form of political music across the SWANA region. The reasons are structural: hip-hop requires minimal equipment (a microphone and a beat), can be produced and distributed outside institutional channels, foregrounds lyrical content (allowing direct political messaging), and connects local struggles to a global language of resistance. From DAM in Palestine to El Général in Tunisia to Hichkas in Iran, hip-hop has become the soundtrack of SWANA political dissent (Kahf, 2007).

## 5. Music and Religion

### The Halal/Haram Debate

The question of whether music is permitted (halal) or forbidden (haram) in Islam has been debated for over a millennium. There is no clear, unambiguous prohibition of music in the Quran. The hadith literature (sayings attributed to the Prophet Muhammad) contains statements that have been interpreted both for and against music. The major positions include:

1. **Strict prohibition** — held by some Hanbali and Salafi scholars, who interpret certain hadiths as banning all musical instruments (with possible exceptions for the duff/frame drum)
2. **Conditional permission** — held by many scholars across Sunni and Shi'a traditions, who permit music that does not lead to sinful behavior
3. **Full permission** — held by some scholars, particularly in the Sufi traditions, who argue that music is not only permitted but spiritually beneficial

### Sufi Music as Spiritual Practice

The Sufi traditions across the SWANA region have consistently championed music as a path to God:

- **Mevlevi sema** (Turkey) — see Section 4
- **Qawwali** (South Asia, but with roots in Persian Sufi practice) — devotional singing featuring intense rhythmic cycles
- **Dhikr/zikr ceremonies** — rhythmic chanting of divine names, often accompanied by percussion, found across Sufi orders
- **Gnawa lila** (Morocco) — see Section 24
- **Alevi cem** (Turkey) — see Section 12

The Sufi position is that music, properly deployed, is not a distraction from God but a vehicle toward God. This theological position has been the most important institutional support for musical practice in Islamic civilization, and many of the greatest musicians in SWANA history have been associated with Sufi orders (During, 1988).

## 6. Music and Gender

### Women's Complex Position

Women's roles in SWANA music reflect the complex, variable, and frequently contradictory gender dynamics of the region:

- **Umm Kulthum** — achieved unparalleled stature in a patriarchal society through extraordinary talent, shrewd self-management, and careful public image cultivation. She never married until late in life, presented a conservative personal image, and wielded her cultural power with strategic brilliance.
- **The qayna/almeh tradition** — professional female musicians who were simultaneously celebrated and stigmatized, valued for their artistic skill and suspected for their sexual autonomy
- **Fairuz** — cultivated a quasi-maternal, almost saintly public image that placed her beyond the stigma often attached to female performers
- **Bergen** — whose tragic life and murder exposed the violence faced by women in the Turkish music industry
- **Googoosh** — silenced for 21 years after the Iranian revolution's ban on female solo singing
- **Contemporary artists** — women like Aynur Doğan, Gaye Su Akyol, Yasmine Hamdan, and Deena Abdelwahed navigate between artistic freedom and societal expectations

The persistent tension between the extraordinary cultural influence of female musicians and the social constraints they face is not merely a "background" fact — it shapes the music itself, influencing what can be sung, how it can be performed, and who can hear it.

## 7. The "Oriental" Gaze

### Orientalism and Music

Edward Said's concept of Orientalism (1978) — the Western construction of the "East" as exotic, sensual, irrational, and timeless — has had profound effects on how SWANA music is perceived, consumed, and represented in the West:

- **Exoticization** — SWANA music reduced to "exotic" sounds, divorced from their cultural context
- **Homogenization** — the vast diversity of SWANA musical traditions collapsed into a single "Middle Eastern" or "Oriental" category
- **Rhythmic stereotyping** — complex rhythmic systems reduced to "belly dance beats"
- **Timelessness** — SWANA music presented as "ancient" and "unchanged," denying its history of innovation and evolution
- **The "snake charmer" scale** — the Western association of the hijaz maqam (with its augmented second interval) with generic "Eastern" exoticism, stripping one of the most sophisticated modal systems in the world of its specificity

For a drum machine project, this means that the presentation of SWANA rhythms must actively resist these patterns: providing specific cultural context, acknowledging diversity, crediting sources, and presenting these traditions as living, evolving practices rather than museum artifacts (Stokes, 2004).

# PART VII: MODERN REFLECTIONS

## 1. SWANA Rhythms in Western Electronic Music

### Sampling, Borrowing, and Orientalism

SWANA rhythmic patterns have entered Western electronic music through multiple channels:

- **Direct sampling** — loops from Arabic, Turkish, and Persian recordings used in Western dance music
- **"World music" patches** — synthesizers and drum machines offering generic "Middle Eastern" preset sounds
- **Rhythmic borrowing** — Western producers adopting specific iqa'at (particularly maqsum/baladi variants) and recontextualizing them

The ethical terrain is complex. When **Dead Can Dance** uses Middle Eastern rhythmic and melodic elements, it is informed by deep study and genuine respect. When a generic EDM producer drops a "snake charmer" melody over a maqsum loop, it is textbook Orientalism. The difference lies in depth of engagement, specificity of attribution, and willingness to present the source tradition on its own terms.

## 2. Arab Electronic Music Scene

### The New Synthesis

A vibrant scene of SWANA musicians is creating electronic music that draws on traditional elements without exoticizing them:

- **Mashrou' Leila** (Lebanon, 2008–present) — indie rock/electronic band whose music integrates Arabic melodic and rhythmic elements into contemporary alternative music. Their lyrics addressing sexuality, politics, and identity have made them one of the most important and controversial bands in the Arab world.
- **Acid Arab** (France/SWANA) — a DJ/production project that blends North African and Middle Eastern music with acid house, techno, and electro. Founded by Guido Minisky and Hervé Carvalho, their work treats Arabic and North African musical elements as structural foundations rather than exotic decoration.
- **Deena Abdelwahed** (Tunisia) — electronic producer whose work deconstructs Tunisian and broader North African rhythmic patterns within experimental techno frameworks. Her album *Khonnar* is a landmark in Arab electronic music.
- **Muqata'a** (Ramallah) — Palestinian electronic producer who creates dense, challenging soundscapes incorporating Arabic percussion, field recordings, and spoken word.
- **Nadah El Shazly** (Egypt) — experimental musician whose work draws on Egyptian musical traditions within an avant-garde electronic context.
- **Aya Metwalli** and **ZULI** — Cairo-based producers whose work engages with mahraganat rhythms within experimental electronic frameworks.
- **Hamed Sinno** — (Mashrou' Leila vocalist) whose solo work explores the intersection of queer Arab identity and electronic music.

## 3. Turkish Electronic Scene

### Anatolian Bass and Beyond

- **Baba Zula** — (see Section 14) pioneers of "Istanbul psychedelia," blending electrified saz and darbuka with dub and electronic production
- **Anatolian bass** — a loosely defined genre that applies bass music production techniques to Anatolian folk rhythms and melodies
- **Islandman** (Tolga Boyuk) — producer who creates organic electronic music deeply rooted in Turkish folk traditions
- **Karsu** — Dutch-Turkish musician who blends Turkish folk, jazz, and contemporary production
- **Oceanvs Orientalis** — Istanbul-based producer creating ambient/electronic music infused with Turkish and broader SWANA elements

## 4. The Mahraganat Phenomenon

### From Cairo Streets to Global Stages

Mahraganat (see Section 20) deserves special attention as a modern phenomenon. What began as hyperlocal street music from Cairo's informal neighborhoods has become a global phenomenon:

- **Islam Chipsy and EEK** — keyboardist Islam Chipsy and his percussionists (the EEK collective) create a live electronic music of staggering intensity, with Chipsy's organ/keyboard playing racing over electro-shaabi rhythms at superhuman speed
- **Wegz** — who has brought mahraganat-influenced production into mainstream Arabic pop
- **Global connections** — mahraganat has been recognized by international electronic music communities, with festivals, compilations, and collaborations bringing it to new audiences

The mahraganat case raises important questions about cultural ownership, class politics, and musical innovation. The genre's creators — working-class young men from Cairo's poorest neighborhoods — have seen their music celebrated internationally while remaining marginalized domestically. The Egyptian Musicians' Syndicate has repeatedly attempted to restrict mahraganat performers, revealing that class-based gatekeeping of music is as relevant today as it was in the arabesk era (Sprengel, 2019).

## 5. Diaspora Musicians Bridging Worlds

### Living Between Traditions

The SWANA diaspora — millions of people living in Europe, the Americas, and Australia — has produced musicians who navigate between their heritage traditions and their adopted environments:

- **Dhafer Youssef** (Tunisian, based in Europe) — vocalist and ud player who creates a jazz-inflected music rooted in Sufi vocal traditions
- **Anouar Brahem** (Tunisia) — ud player whose ECM recordings have brought Arabic modal music into dialogue with European jazz and classical minimalism
- **Kayhan Kalhor** (Iran) — kamancheh player who has collaborated with Yo-Yo Ma's Silk Road Ensemble and with Turkish, Indian, and Western musicians
- **Tamer Abu Ghazaleh** (Palestine/Egypt) — singer-songwriter-producer who blends Arabic tradition with experimental production
- **Yasmine Hamdan** (Lebanon/France) — who creates electronic-inflected Arabic pop that defies categorization
- **Sevdaliza** (Iran/Netherlands) — whose dark electronic pop draws on Persian vocal sensibilities
- **Noga Erez** (Israel) — whose electronic pop incorporates Middle Eastern rhythmic elements

These musicians are not simply "mixing" traditions — they are creating new musical languages that reflect the reality of lives lived across borders, languages, and cultural systems. Their work demonstrates that SWANA musical traditions are not museum pieces to be preserved under glass but living systems that continue to evolve and interact with the world.

# CONCLUSION: IMPLICATIONS FOR A DRUM MACHINE

## Principles for Respectful Representation

This research suggests several principles for a drum machine that seeks to honor the traditions it represents:

1. **Specificity over generalization** — Never present a generic "Middle Eastern" rhythm. Every pattern has a name, a tradition, a cultural context. Use specific names (maqsum, not "Arabic beat"; aksak, not "Turkish rhythm").

2. **Context alongside pattern** — Each rhythm in the application should carry accessible contextual information: where it comes from, how it is traditionally used, what it means to the people who play it.

3. **Acknowledge living traditions** — These rhythms are not artifacts of the past. They are played today, at weddings, in clubs, in places of worship, in studios. Present them as living practices.

4. **Credit and attribution** — Where specific communities have developed specific rhythmic traditions, credit those communities. Romani 9/8 patterns are Romani. Gnawa rhythms are Gnawa. Kurdish dance rhythms are Kurdish.

5. **Resist the "Oriental" gaze** — Do not present SWANA rhythms as exotic, mysterious, or primitive. Present them with the same seriousness and specificity given to any other musical tradition.

6. **Acknowledge complexity** — Some of these rhythmic systems (Ottoman usul, Arabic iqa'at, the tombak tradition) represent centuries of accumulated knowledge. Do not oversimplify.

7. **Recognize political dimensions** — Some of these musical traditions have been suppressed, banned, or weaponized by states. Kurdish music, Palestinian music, and many others carry political weight. Acknowledging this is not "being political" — ignoring it is.

8. **Engage with living practitioners** — Where possible, consult with musicians from these traditions. The best source of knowledge about a musical tradition is the people who practice it.
