# Part I: The Andes — Pre-Columbian Roots

## 1. Inca Music: What the Evidence Tells Us

We have no Inca notation system and no surviving recordings — yet the archaeological, iconographic, and chronicler evidence is surprisingly rich. The Inca Empire (Tawantinsuyu, c. 1438–1533) maintained a vast musical culture that served religious ceremony, agricultural ritual, warfare, and governance.

### Archaeological Evidence

Through excavations across Peru, Bolivia, and Ecuador, archaeologists have uncovered:

- **Ceramic instruments** from pre-Inca cultures (Nazca, Moche, Chimú) depicting musicians playing sikus during religious ceremonies and funerals
- **Bone quenas** — the quena (end-notched flute) is the oldest known wind instrument on the continent, with specimens predating Christ by centuries
- **Shell trumpets (pututu/huaylaquipa)** — conch shells used as signal instruments in ceremony and warfare
- **Ceramic drums and rattles** from coastal cultures showing sophisticated percussion practice

### The Instrumental Categories

The Inca organized their instruments into functional groups:

**Wind instruments:**
- **Quena** — end-notched flute made of cane, bone, or ceramic
- **Siku/Zampoña** — panpipes played in interlocking pairs
- **Pinkullu** — a long vertical flute
- **Pututu (huaylaquipa)** — conch shell trumpet for signaling
- **Cuyhui** — a five-voice whistle

**Percussion:**
- **Wankara (huankar)** — the large bass drum, struck with a stick
- **Tinya** — the small hand drum, believed to have been played exclusively by women
- **Silver rattles and chilchiles (bells)** — used in noble and ceremonial contexts

### Music in Ceremony and Warfare

The Incas used music to communicate with ancestors, heal the sick, and bury the dead. Music accompanied them in war and on pilgrimages, providing what the chroniclers described as "supernatural power." Dances were categorized by social rank:

- **Nobiliary dances** (uaricsa arawi, guayara) for the Sapa Inca and the panacas (royal lineages)
- **Guari** — dances for young nobles
- **Masked war dances (wacon)** — performed by men before and after battle

The wankara's deep boom carried across valleys, serving as both a war drum and a communication instrument. When the Spanish arrived, they recorded their terror at the sound of thousands of wankaras beating simultaneously as Inca armies approached.

## 2. The Quipu: Knotted Strings and the Question of Musical Encoding

The quipu (khipu) — a recording device of knotted strings — is one of the most enigmatic artifacts of the pre-Columbian Americas. Primarily a system of numerical record-keeping using base-10 positional notation encoded in knots, the quipu served the administrative needs of a vast empire without written language.

### The Musical Encoding Hypothesis

Historians Edward Hyams and George Ordish proposed that certain quipus functioned like musical notation — "basic information" that a performer would "bring to life." Under this theory:

- **Color variations** distinguished episodes and locations
- **Knot types** encoded events and characters
- **Cord order** represented narrative chronology
- **Rhythmic patterning** — highly patterned, redundant, and symmetrical arrangements that some scholars interpret as rhythmic templates

This remains speculative. No narrative khipu has been definitively deciphered. However, the theory aligns with what we know about Andean oral tradition: that knowledge was transmitted through performance, and that mnemonic devices (including textile patterns, ceramic imagery, and possibly quipus) served as scores for ritual performance — not literal transcriptions but *cues* for performers who already knew the tradition.

The quipu thus occupies a provocative liminal space: not quite notation, not quite not-notation. It is a reminder that Western conceptions of "written music" are not the only way to encode rhythmic and musical information.

## 3. Tinkuy: The Andean Concept of Encounter and Musical Dualism

**Tinkuy** (also tinkuñacama, "to fall or enter into combat") is a foundational concept in Andean cosmology that permeates music, ritual, agriculture, and social organization. At its core, tinkuy means "the coming together of two different groups" — a meeting of opposites that creates new life energy.

### The Ritual Combat (Tinku)

Tinku is a pre-Hispanic ritual practice, particularly strong in Norte Potosí, Bolivia, in which communities meet in fierce, often bloody encounters:

- Fighters represent their **ayllu** (community)
- Winning brings prestige, land rights, and favor from the losing party
- The ritual takes place on sacred or religious grounds
- Blood spilled is an offering to Pachamama (Mother Earth)

### Cosmological Significance

The philosophical depth of tinkuy cannot be overstated. It embodies **complementary dualism** — the Andean principle that:

- Opposites must be honored in their difference
- Those differences must be brought together to form a union
- From that union, something *greater than the two parts* emerges — a new being endowed with vitalizing life force

This principle maps directly onto musical practice. The **siku (panpipe) playing technique** — where ira and arca pipes alternate to create a complete melody — is a sonic manifestation of tinkuy. Neither player alone can play the tune. Only through their encounter does music emerge.

### Tinku as Musical Genre

Tinku is also a musical rhythm traditional to Bolivia, representing a marching movement. The music has:
- A loud, constant drum beat giving it a "native warlike feel"
- Charangos, guitars, and zampoñas playing melodies over the percussion
- An energetic, driving quality that accompanies both ritual combat and contemporary dance

```
TINKU BASIC RHYTHM (in 2/4):

Bombo:  |X . . x . x X .|X . . x . x X .|
         1 e & a 2 e & a  1 e & a 2 e & a

X = strong bass hit, x = lighter hit, . = rest

The steady pulse drives the march-like quality,
with accented downbeats and a militaristic energy.
```

## 4. Andean Cosmovision: The Three Worlds and Music's Mediating Role

The Andean cosmovision divides the universe into three interconnected realms (pachas):

### Hanan Pacha (The Upper World / Celestial Realm)
- The world of the stars, the sun (Inti), the moon (Mama Killa)
- Associated with the condor
- Crucially, **music is associated with Hanan Pacha** — understood as connected to the celestial realm
- The quena's high, clear tone is said to reach this world

### Kay Pacha (This World / The Present)
- The world of humans, animals, plants
- Associated with the puma
- Where music is performed, where ritual takes place
- The meeting ground between upper and lower worlds

### Ukhu Pacha (The Inner/Lower World)
- The world of the dead, of seeds, of potential
- Associated with the serpent (amaru)
- The wankara's deep resonance is said to speak to this realm
- The source of agricultural fertility

### Music as Cosmic Mediator

In Andean thought, music does not merely *represent* the connection between worlds — it *creates* it. The shaman (paqo) or ritual specialist uses music to:

- Open channels between the three pachas
- Communicate with apus (mountain spirits) and Pachamama
- Restore balance when illness or disaster indicates cosmic disharmony
- Guide the dead from Kay Pacha to Ukhu Pacha

The **Altomisayoq** (high priest) serves as a mediator between Hanan Pacha and Kay Pacha, and musical performance is central to this mediating role. Each action in one world has direct impact on the others; balance between them guarantees harmony of the cosmos and daily life.

## 5. The Agricultural Calendar and Music: Seasonal Instruments

One of the most remarkable features of Andean musical practice is the strict association of instruments with agricultural seasons. This is not merely convention — it is cosmological law.

### The Wet/Dry Season Division

**Dry season (May–October — harvest, frost, death):**
- **Quenas** are played — the clear, bright, individual voice
- **Siku/zampoña panpipes** — julajula and siku traditions
- **Charango and kitarra** — stringed instruments
- Associated with Hanan Pacha, the upper world, sunlight, aridity

**Wet season (November–April — planting, growth, life):**
- **Pinkillu** (vertical duct flutes) — the breathy, communal sound
- **Tarkas** — deep wooden block flutes
- **Drums predominate** — tinya and wankara
- Associated with Ukhu Pacha, the lower world, rain, fertility

### The Logic of the Division

This is not arbitrary. The Andean understanding is that:

- **Wind instruments that require individual breath** belong to the dry season because the air is clear and the individual voice carries
- **Duct flutes and large ensembles** belong to the wet season because they "call the rain" — the breathy, airy sound of pinkillu and tarka mimics the sound of water and wind
- **Drums belong to the wet season** because their deep resonance speaks to the earth, encouraging seeds to germinate

Ethnomusicologist Henry Stobart documented these "musical calendars" for Quechua-speaking communities, revealing the intricate articulation of music-making and dance with the annual cycle of agricultural production, animal husbandry, courting, and cosmology. Playing the wrong instrument in the wrong season is not merely a faux pas — it risks drought, crop failure, or cosmic imbalance.

# Part II: Andean Instruments — The Percussion and Rhythm Arsenal

## 6. Wankara/Huancara: The Andean Bass Drum

The wankara (also wankar, huancara, huancar) is a large, round, double-headed drum covered with animal skin (typically llama or goat). It produces a deep, resonant bass sound that carries across Andean valleys.

### Construction and Sound
- Cylindrical shell, traditionally carved from a single tree trunk
- Two heads of stretched animal skin, laced together
- Played with a single wooden beater (larger than a tinya stick)
- The low, booming tone was used for signaling across mountain terrain

### Historical Role
- **In warfare:** Thousands of wankaras beating simultaneously terrorized the Spanish conquistadors. The drum served as both a psychological weapon and a coordination tool for troop movements
- **In festivals:** The wankara provides the foundational pulse for large communal dances
- **In agriculture:** Its deep resonance was believed to speak to Ukhu Pacha, encouraging the earth to yield

```
WANKARA BASIC FESTIVAL PATTERN (in 2/4):

R = right hand (beater), L = left hand (palm mute)

|B . . B . B . .|B . . B . B . .|
 1 e & a 2 e & a  1 e & a 2 e & a

B = deep bass stroke
The pattern emphasizes downbeats with a secondary accent
creating a driving march-like pulse.
```

## 7. Tinya: The Women's Drum

The tinya (also tanya) is a small, double-headed hand drum — essentially a smaller wankara — with a distinctive, higher-pitched sound. In the Inca Empire and continuing today, the tinya is **women's instrument par excellence**.

### The Gendered Instrument

The tinya's association with women is not incidental but structural:
- Women play tinya while singing during agricultural work (planting, harvesting)
- The tinya accompanies the *harawi* — women's songs of sowing and mourning
- While men blow the large pututu and beat the wankara, women's tinya provides a complementary high percussion — another manifestation of **tinkuy** (dualistic complementarity)

### Playing Technique
- Held in one hand, struck with a beater in the other
- Often played while simultaneously singing and walking
- The tinya player typically leads the singing, with the drum marking rhythmic emphasis

```
TINYA AGRICULTURAL WORK PATTERN (in 2/4):

T = strike (high, open), t = lighter tap

|T . t . T . t .|T . t . T t T .|
 1 e & a 2 e & a  1 e & a 2 e & a

Simple, repetitive, designed to accompany
continuous physical labor and singing.
```

## 8. Bombo: The Modern Andean Bass Drum

The bombo is the large drum used in modern Andean music, evolved from both indigenous wankara traditions and European military drums introduced during colonization. There are several regional variants:

### Bombo Legüero (Argentina)
- Made from a hollowed tree trunk covered with animal skin
- Named because its sound can be heard "a league away" (una legua)
- The signature instrument of Argentine folk music (chacarera, zamba)
- Has a deep, resonant boom with long sustain
- Played with two beaters: one on the head, one on the rim (aro)

### Bombo Andino (Bolivia/Peru)
- Used in brass band music, carnival, and contemporary Andean groups
- Larger than the tinya, smaller than concert bass drums
- Provides the foundational pulse for ensemble music

```
BOMBO LEGÜERO — CHACARERA PATTERN (in 6/8):

H = head strike (bass), R = rim (aro) strike

|H . R H . R|H . R H R R|
 1 . 2 . 3 .  1 . 2 . 3 .

The alternation between head (low) and rim (high)
creates the characteristic chacarera swing.
Parche (head) on beats 1 and 4, aro on 3 and 6.
```

## 9. Charango: Rhythmic Frameworks from a String Instrument

The charango — a small, 10-string Andean lute traditionally made from armadillo shell (now mostly wood) — is primarily melodic, but its strumming patterns create fundamental rhythmic frameworks for Andean music.

### The Rhythmic Dimension
- Rapid **rasgueo** (strumming) patterns define the pulse of huayno, bailecito, and other genres
- The charango's high-pitched, bright timbre gives its rhythmic patterns a percussive quality
- In ensemble playing, the charango often functions as much as a rhythm instrument as a melodic one

```
CHARANGO HUAYNO RASGUEO (in 2/4):

↓ = downstroke, ↑ = upstroke, x = muted strum

|↓ ↑ x ↑ ↓ ↑ x ↑|↓ ↑ x ↑ ↓ ↑ ↓ ↑|
 1 e & a 2 e & a   1 e & a 2 e & a

The muted strums (x) create a percussive ghost note,
giving the pattern its characteristic bounce.
```

## 10. Siku/Zampoña: Interlocking Panpipes and the Hocket Principle

The siku (Aymara) or zampoña (Spanish) is the Andean panpipe — and its traditional playing technique represents one of the world's most sophisticated examples of musical cooperation.

### The Ira and Arca System

The siku is split into two halves:
- **Ira** ("the one who leads") — corresponding to the male principle
- **Arca** ("the one who follows") — corresponding to the female principle

Each half contains alternating notes of a complete scale. **A single player cannot play a melody alone.** Two players must interlock, alternating notes in a technique called **hocket** — identical in principle to **Balinese kotekan** and to the interlocking bell patterns of West African music.

### The Sonic Result

When many musicians divide into ira and arca groups, the alternation creates a distinctive **stereophonic effect** — the melody bounces between the two groups, giving Andean panpipe music its shimmering, ethereal quality. Expert duos can play in perfect alternation, which demands extraordinary coordination, lung capacity, and mutual listening.

### The Cosmological Dimension

This is tinkuy in sound: neither ira nor arca is complete alone. Only through their encounter — their rhythmic interlocking — does the melody emerge. The siku thus embodies Andean complementary dualism at the most fundamental musical level.

```
SIKU INTERLOCKING PATTERN (schematic):

Scale: C D E F G A B C

IRA:   C . E . G . B .
ARCA:  . D . F . A . C

Combined melody: C D E F G A B C

Each player breathes while the other plays,
creating a continuous sound from two discontinuous sources.

RHYTHMIC PATTERN IN 2/4:
IRA:   |x . x . x . x .|
ARCA:  |. x . x . x . x|
COMBINED:|x x x x x x x x|

The alternation creates a rhythmic pulse faster
than either player alone could sustain.
```

## 11. Cajón: Peru's Gift to World Percussion

The cajón is arguably Peru's most significant contribution to global percussion — a wooden box drum born from oppression that conquered the world.

### Origins: From Shipping Crate to Soul of a Nation

In the 16th century, enslaved people from West and Central Africa were brought to coastal Peru. Spanish colonial authorities prohibited African drums, viewing them as tools for organizing protests and revolts. Enslaved people working in Lima's shipyards improvised, turning wooden shipping crates (cajón = "box" or "drawer") into percussion instruments.

The cajón emerged in the 18th century, particularly in port cities like Lima and Ica. It was a **percussive substitute** born of necessity — but it quickly developed its own sophisticated vocabulary of sounds and techniques.

### Anatomy and Playing Zones

The modern cajón is a rectangular wooden box, typically:
- ~48 cm tall x 30 cm wide x 30 cm deep
- The **tapa** (front face/striking surface) is thinner plywood
- The back has a sound hole
- Modern flamenco cajóns add internal snare wires or guitar strings

**Playing zones:**
```
┌─────────────────────┐
│     TREBLE EDGE      │  ← Sharp, cracking "slap" sound
│  (fingers/palms)     │
├─────────────────────┤
│                     │
│   MID ZONE          │  ← Moderate tone, ghost notes
│                     │
├─────────────────────┤
│                     │
│   BASS CENTER       │  ← Deep, resonant low tone
│                     │
└─────────────────────┘
    (Player sits on top)
```

### Playing Techniques
- **Bass stroke:** Full palm strike near center of tapa — deep, resonant boom
- **Slap/Tone:** Fingers strike near the top edge — sharp, cracking high sound
- **Ghost notes:** Light finger taps for texture and subdivision
- **Heel-toe:** Rocking motion between palm heel (bass) and fingers (tone)
- **Fingertip rolls:** Rapid alternating finger taps for buzz/roll effects
- **Brushes:** Metal or plastic brushes for jazz and funk applications (Pen Technique developed by Patrizio Migliarini)

### The Cajón Goes to Flamenco: The Paco de Lucía Story

In the late 1970s, the Paco de Lucía Sextet was on tour in Peru. At a party at the Spanish Embassy in Lima, iconic Peruvian singer **Chabuca Grande** was accompanied by her percussionist **Caitro Soto** on cajón.

**Rubem Dantas**, the Brazilian-born percussionist in Paco's sextet, heard "something very deep" in the sound of the cajón. Before the party was over, Dantas had borrowed money from Paco de Lucía and bought the cajón to take home.

Dantas developed an original style of playing adapted to flamenco — and debuted it on Paco de Lucía's 1981 album **Solo Quiero Caminar**. The cajón's sound — wooden, warm, cracking — proved a perfect match for flamenco's palmas (handclaps) and zapateado (footwork). Within a decade, the cajón became *the* standard percussion instrument in flamenco — a Peruvian instrument of African origin, played by a Brazilian, adopted by Spanish Romani musicians.

From flamenco, the cajón spread to:
- **Pop and rock** (unplugged sessions, singer-songwriter accompaniment)
- **World music fusion** (global touring Andean and Latin groups)
- **Jazz** (as an alternative to drum kit in intimate settings)
- **Street music** (portable, affordable, versatile)

```
CAJÓN — BASIC FESTEJO PATTERN (in 12/8):

B = Bass (center), S = Slap (edge), g = ghost note

|B . g S . g B . S . g S|
 1 . . 2 . . 3 . . 4 . .

CAJÓN — BASIC LANDÓ PATTERN (in 12/8):

|B . S . B B . S B . S B|
 1 . . 2 . . 3 . . 4 . .

CAJÓN — VALS CRIOLLO PATTERN (in 3/4):

|B . g S . g|B . g S g S|
 1 . . 2 . .  1 . . 2 . .

CAJÓN — FLAMENCO BASIC (Rumba):

|B . g S . g B g S . g .|
 1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a
```

# Part III: Peru — Coast, Highland, and Jungle

## 12. Música Criolla: The Sound of Coastal Peru

Música criolla is the coastal Peruvian tradition — a mestizo music born in the working-class neighborhoods (callejones and solares) of Lima, blending European (especially Viennese waltz and Spanish) forms with Afro-Peruvian rhythmic sensibilities and indigenous melodies.

### The Vals Criollo (Peruvian Waltz)

The vals criollo emerged around 1900–1920 during the era known as **La Guardia Vieja** ("the old guard"). It is an adaptation of the European waltz in 3/4 time, but with distinctive:

- **Syncopation** — Afro-Peruvian rhythmic displacement that shifts the waltz's "oom-pah-pah" into something swinging and off-kilter
- **Guitar-centric accompaniment** — the lead guitarist plays solos and strongly plucked phrases on the upper strings; a second guitarist performs riffs (bordones) on the two lowest strings and strums rhythmically
- **Cajón patterns** — adding Afro-Peruvian percussive depth to the waltz framework
- **Intimate atmosphere** — performed in the peña (music club/gathering)

Key figures: Chabuca Granda, Felipe Pinglo Alva, Lucha Reyes, Eva Ayllón.

```
VALS CRIOLLO GUITAR RHYTHM (in 3/4):

Guitar 1 (bordón/bass):
|B . . ↓ . ↑|B . . ↓ . ↑|
 1     2     3  1     2     3

Guitar 2 (rasgueo):
|↓ ↑ ↓ ↑ ↓ ↑|↓ ↑ x ↑ ↓ ↑|
 1 & 2 & 3 &  1 & 2 & 3 &

Cajón (supporting):
|B . g S . g|B . g S g S|
 1 . . 2 . .  1 . . 2 . .
```

## 13. Festejo: The Afro-Peruvian Celebration

Festejo is the high-energy Afro-Peruvian celebration rhythm — the sound of Lima's Black communities in full musical expression.

### Musical Characteristics
- Written in **12/8 time** (four groups of three eighth-notes)
- Unlike Cuban music where the clave drives everything, in festejo **the cajón is the motor** — everything revolves around its patterns
- There are different *golpes* (patterns) named after the songs that gave birth to them
- The cajón produces a variety of sonorities by combining center (low) and top edge (high) strikes

### The Percussion Ensemble
- **Cajón** — the heart and soul
- **Cajita** — a small wooden box played with a stick, producing a high, clicking pattern
- **Quijada** (quijada de burro) — a donkey jawbone whose loose teeth rattle when struck, creating a buzzing, shaking sound — one of the most distinctive timbres in world percussion

```
FESTEJO — FULL PERCUSSION ENSEMBLE (in 12/8):

Cajón:   |B . g S . g B . S . g S|
Cajita:  |x . x . x . x . x . x .|
Quijada: |. . . Q . . . . . Q . .|
          1 . . 2 . . 3 . . 4 . .

B = cajón bass, S = cajón slap, g = ghost note
x = cajita click, Q = quijada strike + rattle

FESTEJO VARIATION — "SON DE LOS DIABLOS":

Cajón:   |B . S . g B . S g . B S|
Cajita:  |. x . x . . x . x . . x|
Quijada: |. . Q . . . . . Q . . .|
          1 . . 2 . . 3 . . 4 . .
```

## 14. Landó: The Slow, Sensual Afro-Peruvian Blues

If festejo is the party, landó is the seduction. It is the slower, bluesier Afro-Peruvian rhythm — deeply syncopated, hypnotic, and emotionally intense.

### Musical Characteristics
- Often in **minor key**
- Slower tempo than festejo
- Notated in 12/8 or 6/4 (scholars debate which better represents its slow pulse)
- **Incredibly syncopated** — except for the downbeat, the bass almost never plays on the pulses, giving the music "an almost rootless, floating feel"
- A two-bar figure defines the basic cajón pattern

### Susana Baca and the Revival

**Susana Baca** is the most important figure in the modern revival of landó and Afro-Peruvian music generally. Her 1995 album, compiled by David Byrne for the Luaka Bop label, introduced Afro-Peruvian music to the world. Her signature piece, "María Landó," became an international sensation.

Baca brought landó from the margins of Peruvian culture to global recognition, earning multiple Latin Grammy awards and even serving briefly as Peru's Minister of Culture (2011).

```
LANDÓ — BASIC CAJÓN PATTERN (in 12/8):

Two-bar phrase:

Bar 1: |B . S . B B . S B . S B|
Bar 2: |B . S . B B . S . S . B|
        1 . . 2 . . 3 . . 4 . .

B = bass, S = slap

The syncopation creates a "floating" quality —
the low tones avoid the strong beats,
pulling the listener off-center.

LANDÓ — WITH BASS GUITAR LOCK (in 12/8):

Cajón: |B . S . B B . S B . S B|
Bass:  |x . . . x x . . x . . x|
        1 . . 2 . . 3 . . 4 . .

The bass locks with the cajón's low tones,
creating a deep, hypnotic groove.
```

## 15. Zamacueca to Marinera: The Courtship Dance

The **zamacueca** emerged in 16th-17th century Lima, in the neighborhoods of Rímac and Barrios Altos — a mestizo creation fusing African, indigenous, and Spanish elements among enslaved and free people of color.

### The Dance
- A courtship dance: the man pursues, the woman evades
- Both dancers hold **white handkerchiefs** in their right hands, waving them energetically above their heads
- Pelvic movements reflect African influence
- The zapateo (footwork) functions as percussion

### Musical Structure
- The cajón plays a combination of **3/4 and 6/8** patterns — indicating deep African influence
- The hemiola (sesquiáltera) is central: the simultaneous presence of duple and triple subdivision

### From Zamacueca to Marinera
The zamacueca spread throughout South America, becoming:
- **Cueca** in Chile and Bolivia
- **Zamba** in Argentina (NOT related to Brazilian samba)
- **Marinera** in Peru

The name change to "marinera" came after the War of the Pacific (1879–1884): journalist Abelardo Gamarra lobbied for the name change to honor the Peruvian navy (marina). The northern marinera (marinera norteña from Trujillo) is in 6/8; the Arequipeña variant is in 3/4.

```
MARINERA NORTEÑA CAJÓN PATTERN (in 6/8):

|B . S . B S|B . S B . S|
 1 . 2 . 3 .  1 . 2 . 3 .

The 6/8 feel creates a lilting, swinging quality
that mirrors the courtship dance's approach-retreat dynamic.

ZAMACUECA HEMIOLA (3/4 against 6/8):

Guitar (6/8): |x . x . x .|x . x . x .|
Cajón (3/4):  |B . . S . .|B . . S . .|
Combined:      1 . 2 . 3 .  1 . 2 . 3 .

The simultaneous presence of 3/4 and 6/8 creates
the sesquiáltera — the rhythmic engine of much
Latin American music.
```

## 16. Huayno: The Andean Heartbeat

Huayno (also wayno, wayñu) is the most widespread and representative folk dance of the Andes — the social glue of highland Peru, Bolivia, and Ecuador.

### Origins and Character
- Predates the Spanish conquest — possibly an Inca funeral dance, now purely festive
- A couple dance performed in the highlands at every social occasion
- **The bounce:** a distinctive rhythm where the first beat is stressed, followed by two short beats, creating a characteristic hopping, bouncing feel

### Musical Structure
- Typically in **2/4 time**
- High-pitched vocals accompanied by quena, harp, siku, charango, violin, guitar, and in modern versions, saxophone and accordion
- The basic rhythmic cell: LONG-short-short (quarter-eighth-eighth) creates the "bounce"

### Regional Variations
- **Carnavalito** (Argentina) — faster paced
- **Bolivian huayno** — more relaxed
- **Ecuadorian and Chilean** interpretations with local inflections

```
HUAYNO BASIC RHYTHM (in 2/4):

The "bounce":
|♩  ♪♪|♩  ♪♪|
 1  & a  2  & a

Bombo pattern:
|B . x x B . x x|
 1 e & a 2 e & a

B = bass, x = rim tap

The long-short-short pattern creates
the characteristic hopping feel that
defines highland Andean dance.

HUAYNO WITH CHARANGO (in 2/4):

Charango: |↓ . ↑ ↑ ↓ . ↑ ↑|
Bombo:    |B . x x B . x x|
Quena:    |♩  ♪♪  ♩  ♪♪  |
           1 e & a 2 e & a
```

## 17. Chicha: Psychedelic Andean Cumbia

Chicha is one of the great cultural fusions of the Americas — a genre that blends highland huayno with Colombian cumbia, psychedelic rock, and surf guitar into something unmistakably Peruvian.

### Origins

Chicha emerged in the 1960s–70s in Lima, created by **Andean migrants** who had moved to the capital seeking economic opportunity. They brought their huayno tradition and encountered Colombian cumbia on the radio and psychedelic rock in the air.

**Los Destellos** (formed 1966) are considered the founding group, integrating electric guitars with Andean folklore, criollo waltz, Cuban music, and rock. The first chicha hit was "La Chichera" by **Los Demonios del Mantaro** from the central highlands of Junín.

### The Sound

- **Electric guitars** make extensive use of the **fuzzbox and wah-wah pedal** (psychedelic rock/surf rock influence)
- Up to **three electric guitars** playing simultaneous melodies — derived from the harp and guitar lines of Andean huayno
- **Keyboards/synthesizers** layering over the guitar textures
- **Cumbia's steady bass drum pulse** provides the rhythmic foundation
- **Huayno's pentatonic melodies** give the music its Andean soul

### Los Shapis and the 1980s Explosion

**Los Shapis** made history in 1983 when they filled a stadium in Lima, then toured the world. Their 1981 hit "El Aguajal" signaled that chicha had become a mass phenomenon — the music of Lima's migrant working class.

### The Cultural Significance

Chicha represents the **urbanization of indigenous identity**. It refused to choose between tradition and modernity, between highland and coast, between Andean and global. It was — and remains — the sound of Peru's internal migration: millions of people creating new identities in the spaces between their origins and their destinations.

```

Snare/timbales:|. . X . . . X .|. . X . . . X .|

               1 & 2 & 3 & 4 &  1 & 2 & 3 & 4 &

Electric guitar (huayno-derived melody):
Wah-wah pedal creates rhythmic pulsing over
the steady cumbia groove.

CHICHA — HUAYNO-CUMBIA HYBRID FEEL:

          1 & 2 & 3 & 4 &

creating the distinctive chicha tension.
```

## 18. Música Afroperuana Revival: Reclaiming Black Heritage

For centuries, Peru's African heritage was systematically marginalized and erased. The revival movement of the mid-20th century is one of the most important cultural reclamation projects in South American history.

### Nicomedes Santa Cruz (1925–1992): The Founding Father

**Nicomedes Santa Cruz Gamarra** was a poet, singer, songwriter, and musicologist — primarily a **decimista** (composer in the décima form: ten verses of eight syllables in ABBACCDDC rhyme scheme, sung or recited with audience participation).

In the 1950s, he began:
- Compiling and rejuvenating Afro-Peruvian cultural forms
- Using radio and television to bring unknown Afro-Peruvian musicians to public attention
- Organizing a theater company with his sister **Victoria Santa Cruz** (1959–1961)
- Introducing instruments from other African diasporic traditions (congas, cowbells)
- Recording the landmark album **Cumanana**

His sister Victoria Santa Cruz became equally important, choreographing Afro-Peruvian dance and creating the legendary piece "Me Gritaron Negra" (They Shouted 'Black' at Me).

June 4 (Nicomedes's birthday) has been celebrated since 2006 as the **Day of Afro-Peruvian Culture**.

### Susana Baca: Taking It Global

Susana Baca built on the Santa Cruz foundation, bringing Afro-Peruvian music to international audiences through her partnership with David Byrne's Luaka Bop label. Her voice — delicate, haunting, precise — over the cajón and guitar proved irresistible to world music audiences.

### Perú Negro

The ensemble **Perú Negro**, founded in 1969, became the premier performance group for Afro-Peruvian music, preserving and staging festejo, landó, zamacueca, and other traditions.

## 19. Peruvian Electronic: The Digital Frontier

### Novalima: Afro-Peruvian + Electronic

**Novalima** (founded in Lima) are pioneers of the "Afrotronic" scene — adding futuristic electronic textures to classic Afro-Peruvian repertoire, turning traditional rhythms into hypnotic electronic loops. Grammy-nominated, they bridge past and future, tradition and innovation, bringing together "seemingly irreconcilable genres, communities and generations."

### Dengue Dengue Dengue: Tropical Bass

**Felipe Salmón and Rafael Pereira** perform behind tribal masks as Dengue Dengue Dengue. Their music is an infectious mixture of dancehall, techno, dub, dubstep, and bass mixed with traditional Afro-Peruvian instrumentation. They are driving a movement called **tropical bass** — mixing old cumbia records with bass-heavy electronic production.

### The Broader Scene

The second decade of the 2000s saw a wave of Peruvian artists articulating Afro-Peruvian culture with contemporary electronics: **Tribilin Sound, QOQEQA, Shushupe** — freely synthesizing grime, dubstep, trap, and global bass with local rhythmic traditions.

# Part IV: Bolivia — Carnival, Brass, and Ritual

## 20. Morenada: The Heavy Tuba-Driven March

Morenada is one of Bolivia's most recognizable dance-music traditions — a slow, heavy, brass-band-driven rhythm that represents the suffering of enslaved Africans.

### The Dance
- Easily recognizable: many rattles and drums, groups of women in **polleras** (multilayered skirts) with bowler hats
- Men wear costumes supposedly representing barrels, with silver or black masks
- The choreography's tired, labored rhythm is said to represent the pain and suffering of slaves upon their arrival in the Americas
- Large, ornate costumes with elaborate headpieces weighing up to 30 kg

### The Music
- Dominated by **brass bands** — tubas, trumpets, trombones, euphoniums
- Heavy, plodding **tuba bass line** drives the rhythm
- Drums provide a steady march pulse
- The tempo is deliberately slow, matching the labored dance movements

```
MORENADA BASIC RHYTHM (in 2/4):

Tuba:        |B . . B . . B .|
Snare drum:  |. x . . x . . x|
Cymbals:     |X . . X . . X .|
Bass drum:   |B . . . B . . .|
              1 e & a 2 e & a

B = bass/hit, x = snare roll or tap, X = crash
The slow, heavy pulse reflects the dance's
depiction of enslaved people laboring under burden.
```

## 21. Caporales: Acrobatic Energy and Boot-Bell Percussion

Caporales is one of Bolivia's most spectacular dance-music forms — athletic, flashy, and rhythmically driven by an unusual percussion source: **bells attached to dancers' boots**.

### The Dance
- Male dancers perform acrobatic leaps, kicks, and spins
- Each dancer wears **cascabeles** (jingle bells) on leather straps around the boots
- The collective sound of dozens or hundreds of boot bells creates a shimmering percussion layer
- Female dancers in short skirts perform synchronized choreography

### The Music
- Faster tempo than morenada
- Brass band driven with driving snare patterns
- The boot bells add a layer of high-frequency percussion that is unique in world dance music

```
CAPORALES RHYTHM (in 2/4):

Brass:       |♩ ♩ |♩ ♩ |  ← punchy, marcato
Snare:       |x.x.x.xx|x.x.x.xx|
Bass drum:   |B . . . B . . .|
Boot bells:  |ssssssss|ssssssss|  ← continuous shimmer
              1 e & a 2 e & a

s = continuous jingle shimmer from dancers' boot bells
The interplay between the driving brass, sharp snare,
and shimmering bells creates an exhilarating texture.
```

## 23. Saya: The Afro-Bolivian Heartbeat

Saya is the communal Afro-diasporic music and dance tradition from the **Yungas valleys** of Bolivia — the subtropical region east of La Paz where enslaved Africans were brought to work during the colonial period.

### The Three Drums

Saya uses a sophisticated interlocking drum system:
- **Asentadores** — the largest drums, "setting" the basic duple-meter beat
- **Cambiadores** — medium drums that interlock a **triplet pattern** with the asentador's duple beat
- **Gangengo** — the smallest drum, interlocking an upbeat pattern with the asentador

### Musical Structure
- **March-like yet sinuous** — duple meter with syncopated snare figures
- **Call-and-response singing** — a leader calls, the chorus responds
- **Dense shaker textures** from chajchas (seed/hoof rattles) and metal jingles
- **Bells on the dance captain's legs** lead the dancers

### The Polyrhythmic Core

The interaction between the three drum sizes creates a polyrhythmic texture remarkably similar to West African drumming traditions — asentadores providing a steady "timeline," cambiadores adding cross-rhythmic tension, gangengos providing high-frequency rhythmic commentary.

```
SAYA — THREE-DRUM INTERLOCKING PATTERN (in 4/4):

Asentador: |B . . . B . . .|B . . . B . . .|
Cambiador: |. . x . . x . .|. . x . . x . .|
Gangengo:  |. x . x . x . x|. x . x . x . x|
Chajchas:  |x.x.x.x.x.x.x.x|x.x.x.x.x.x.x.x|
            1 e & a 2 e & a  1 e & a 2 e & a

B = deep bass, x = stroke/tap

The asentador sets the duple pulse.
The cambiador adds triplet-like cross-rhythm.
The gangengo fills in upbeats.
Together: a polyrhythmic texture of African origin
preserved in the Andean valleys.
```

## 24. Diablada: The Devil Dance of Oruro

The Diablada (Devil Dance) is the most iconic dance of the **Carnaval de Oruro**, recognized by UNESCO as a Masterpiece of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity.

### The Dance
- Elaborate devil costumes and masks representing the battle between good and evil
- The Archangel Michael leads a troupe of devils in choreographed combat
- Costumes feature mirrors, embroidery, and dragon and serpent motifs
- The dance represents the triumph of good over evil, syncretizing Catholic and Andean cosmology

### The Music
- **Brass band driven** — trumpets, tubas, trombones in full fortissimo
- Driving percussion from bombo and snare
- The tempo varies from processional march to frenetic dance

## 25. Tarqueada: The Deep Flute Ensemble

The tarqueada is a musical tradition centered on the **tarka** — a thick, angular block flute made of wood, producing a deep, penetrating sound quite unlike the bright quena.

### The Three Tarkas
- **Large tarka** — the bass foundation
- **Medium tarka** — tuned a fifth above
- **Small tarka** — tuned an octave above the large

All three play the **same melody at fixed intervals**, accompanied by tinya and wankara percussion. This parallel harmony in three octave registers creates a thick, rich, almost organ-like sound.

### Seasonal and Ritual Context
- Played in **January and February** (wet season) — consistent with the seasonal instrument calendar
- Associated with agricultural festivals (Anata/carnival)
- Played in large groups (tropas) — never as a solo instrument

```
TARQUEADA MARCH RHYTHM (in 2/4):

Tinya:   |T . t T . t T t|
Wankara: |B . . B . . B .|
Tarkas:  |♩ ♪♪ ♩ ♪♪ |  ← all three sizes in parallel
          1 e & a 2 e & a

T = tinya strike, t = light tap, B = wankara bass
The march-like quality drives processional movement.
```

## 26. Oruro Carnival: The Great Musical Convergence

The **Carnaval de Oruro** is one of the world's great musical festivals — a convergence of all Bolivian dance-music traditions in a single, spectacular event.

### The Entrada
The main event is the **Entrada** — a massive parade lasting over 20 hours, featuring:
- Over 28,000 dancers and 10,000 musicians
- Multiple **fraternities** (comparsas), each performing a different traditional dance
- Diablada, Morenada, Caporales, Tinku, Tobas, Kullawada, Waca Waca, and more

### The Music
- Each fraternity is accompanied by its own **brass band**
- A separate **Festival de Bandas** showcases dozens of ensembles before the Entrada weekend
- The sound environment is total immersion: brass bands, drums, and choirs in near-constant rotation
- The volume and density of sound is overwhelming — dozens of bands playing simultaneously in the streets

### UNESCO Recognition
In 2001, UNESCO proclaimed the Carnival of Oruro a Masterpiece of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity, recognizing it as a living expression of Andean cultural diversity and syncretic religious practice.

# Part V: Argentina — Tango, Folk, and the New Wave

## 27. Tango: The Complete Origin Story

Tango is arguably the most globally recognized musical form to emerge from South America — and its origins are far more complex, multicultural, and rhythmically sophisticated than the popular image suggests.

### The African Roots

The word "tango" itself almost certainly derives from **Bantu/Niger-Congo languages** — various theories link it to:
- The Kikongo word *ntangu* (sun, time)
- A general term for African gathering places or dances
- The Bantu word for "closed place" or "reserved ground"

People of African descent in the Río de la Plata contributed forms of music and dance "of decisive importance for tango's birth." Their influence is:

**Direct:** Originally African populations of Río de la Plata brought their rhythmic sensibilities — syncopation, polyrhythm, and the centrality of the body in music-making.

**Indirect (Afro-Cuban):** The **habanera rhythm** — a classic Latin accompaniment pattern of a dotted-eighth-sixteenth followed by two eighth notes — arrived in Buenos Aires via Havana, carrying African rhythmic DNA.

### The Conventillo: Where It All Fused

In the late 19th century, Buenos Aires's **conventillos** (tenement houses) and arrabal (outskirts) neighborhoods brought together:
- **African descendants** with their rhythmic traditions
- **Italian immigrants** with their melodic sensibilities
- **Spanish immigrants** with guitar traditions
- **Criollo** (native-born) Argentines with milonga and payada traditions
- **Jewish immigrants** from Eastern Europe

In these cramped, multicultural spaces, tango was born — not as any single tradition but as a **creole synthesis**.

### The Bandoneón: A German Church Instrument Becomes Tango's Soul

The **bandoneón** is a free-reed instrument similar to a concertina, invented by Heinrich Band in Germany around 1846 for use in small churches that couldn't afford organs. German and Italian immigrants brought it to Buenos Aires in the late 19th century.

The instrument's qualities proved perfect for tango:
- A wide dynamic range from whisper to wail
- A capacity for sustained, breathing-like tones
- An inherently melancholic timbre
- The ability to play both melody and harmony

**Eduardo Arolas** popularized the bandoneón in tango; **Vicente Greco** standardized the **orquesta típica**: piano, double bass, two violins, and two bandoneóns.

### The Rhythmic Architecture of Tango

Tango's rhythm is far more complex than its 4/4 time signature suggests. There are several distinct rhythmic modes:

**1. The Habanera Pattern (early tango):**
The foundation — a dotted-eighth-sixteenth followed by two eighths:
```
HABANERA / EARLY TANGO PATTERN (in 4/4):

|♩.  ♬  ♩  ♩|
 1  .  2   3   4

Or in subdivisions:
|x . . x x . x .|
 1 e & a 2 e & a

This is the rhythmic DNA from which tango evolved —
arriving via the habanera from Cuba, itself
descended from West African rhythm.
```

**2. Marcato (the strong beat emphasis):**
```
TANGO MARCATO (in 4/4):

|X . . . X . . . X . . . X . . .|
 1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a

Strong, even quarter notes — the driving pulse.
Accented, punchy, with each beat weighted equally
or with slight emphasis on 1 and 3.
```

**3. Síncopa (syncopation):**
```
TANGO SÍNCOPA (in 4/4):

|. x x . . . x .|
 1 e & a 2 e & a

The first half: two quick eighth notes (pickup)
The second half: accent on beat 3
This is the direct descendant of the habanera —
shifted forward, creating tension against the marcato.

SÍNCOPA ANTICIPADA:
|x . x . . x . .|
 1 e & a 2 e & a

The habanera mutated: first and last notes
shifted forward, creating maximum syncopation.
```

**4. The 3-3-2 Pattern:**
```
TANGO 3-3-2 / BORDONEO (in 4/4):

|x . . x . . x .|
 1 e & a 2 e & a

Three + three + two eighth notes.
Endemic in Argentina as the bordoneo guitar
accompaniment of milonga campera — even more
popular among payadores of the Pampa
than the common habanera rhythm.

This is the same tresillo found in Cuban son,
New Orleans jazz, and West African rhythm —
the rhythmic thread connecting the entire
African diaspora.
```

**5. Marcato-Síncopa Alternation:**
```
TANGO — MARCATO/SÍNCOPA ALTERNATION (in 4/4):

Bar 1 (Marcato): |X . . . X . . . X . . . X . . .|
Bar 2 (Síncopa): |. x x . . . x . . x x . . . x .|
                   1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a

After the mid-1920s, the alternation of marcato
and síncopa became the primary rhythmic fuel of tango.
```

### Astor Piazzolla: The Revolution

**Astor Piazzolla** (1921–1992) reinvented tango as **Nuevo Tango** by incorporating:
- **Jazz harmonics** — extended chords, II-V-I progressions in minor
- **Classical counterpoint** — Bach-influenced fugal writing
- **Rock energy** — driving rhythmic intensity
- **Dissonance** — modern harmonic language

His 1974 composition **"Libertango"** (a portmanteau of "libertad" + "tango") became the manifesto of the new style. The title declared tango's liberation from its traditional constraints.

Piazzolla's innovations preserved the distinctive *fraseo* (phrasing) of traditional tango while elevating it with classical compositional techniques and opening it with the improvisatory freedom of jazz. His **Octeto Buenos Aires** and later the **Quinteto Nuevo Tango** became the vehicles for this revolution.

## 28. Milonga: Tango's Older, Faster Relative

Milonga predates tango and is generally faster, more rhythmic, and more closely connected to the African-influenced tradition of the Río de la Plata.

### Two Types
- **Milonga campera** (rural milonga) — slower, guitar-based, from the gaucho/payador tradition, featuring the 3-3-2 bordoneo pattern
- **Milonga ciudadana** (urban milonga) — faster, used in tango dance settings

### The Habanera/Tresillo Connection
Milonga's rhythmic backbone is the habanera pattern, which transferred directly into tango. The milonga thus represents the transitional form between the habanera (arriving via Cuba) and the fully developed tango rhythm.

```
MILONGA CAMPERA (in 2/4):

Guitar bordoneo:
|x . . x . . x .|x . . x . . x .|
 1 e & a 2 e & a  1 e & a 2 e & a

The 3-3-2 pattern in its purest form.

MILONGA CIUDADANA (in 2/4):

|x x . x . x x .|x x . x . x x .|
 1 e & a 2 e & a  1 e & a 2 e & a

Faster, more syncopated, approaching
the full tango rhythmic vocabulary.
```

## 29. Chacarera: Northwestern Argentine 6/8

Chacarera is a widely popular folk dance dating to the 19th century, originating in the **Santiago del Estero** region of northwestern Argentina.

### Musical Characteristics
- **6/8 meter** with characteristic accent on beat 1 and cross-accents
- **Bombo legüero** provides the driving rhythm — alternating head (parche) and rim (aro) strikes
- Guitar, violin, and accordion fill the harmonic and melodic roles
- Energetic, joyful, communal

### The Dance
- Performed in pairs facing each other
- Features zapateo (footwork), vuelta (turns), and castañetas (finger snaps)
- Multiple sections (introductory, first position, zapateo, second position)

```
CHACARERA — BOMBO LEGÜERO PATTERN (in 6/8):

H = head (parche) strike, R = rim (aro) strike

|H . R . H R|H . R . R H|
 1 . 2 . 3 .  4 . 5 . 6 .

Guitar rasgueo:
|↓ . ↑ . ↓ ↑|↓ . ↑ . ↓ ↑|
 1 . 2 . 3 .  4 . 5 . 6 .

The bombo's alternation between head and rim
creates the chacarera's signature "galloping" feel.
```

## 30. Zamba: The Slow Argentine Folk Dance

**Zamba** (NOT to be confused with Brazilian samba — entirely different despite the similar name) is a slow, romantic Argentine folk dance from the northwest.

### Musical Characteristics
- **6/8 time** (or sometimes felt in 3/4)
- Slow, measured tempo
- Primarily guitar and bombo legüero
- Melancholic, romantic character
- The handkerchief (pañuelo) is central to the dance choreography

```
|H . . R . .|H . . R . R|
 1 . . 2 . .  1 . . 2 . .

Slower and more spacious than chacarera —
the longer spaces between strikes create
the zamba's contemplative character.

         1 . . 2 . .  1 . . 2 . .
```

## 31. Cumbia Villera: Argentine Slum Cumbia

Cumbia villera ("villa" = slum/shantytown) was born in the late 1990s amid Argentina's devastating economic crisis. It is cumbia stripped to its electronic essentials and loaded with the raw experiences of urban poverty.

### Musical Characteristics
- **Heavy use of synthesizers, sound effects, keyboard voices, keytars**
- **Electronic drums** replace live percussion
- Simplified bass drum pattern in 4/4
- Fast tempo, high energy
- Lyrics about drugs, crime, poverty, sexuality — unfiltered and unapologetic

### Cultural Significance
Cumbia villera was the sound of the 2001 economic collapse — Argentina's most severe crisis. Its rawness and refusal to romanticize poverty made it controversial but culturally unavoidable.

## 32. Argentine Folk Music Revival: Mercedes Sosa and Atahualpa Yupanqui

### Atahualpa Yupanqui (1908–1992)
Considered the most important Argentine folk musician of the 20th century. A singer, songwriter, guitarist, and writer, Yupanqui drew on classical, flamenco, and Argentine folk rhythms (zamba, chacarera, bailecito, vidala). He traveled the country collecting folk music, living with indigenous and gaucho communities, and crafting songs of extraordinary beauty and political depth.

### Mercedes Sosa (1935–2009)
"La Negra" Mercedes Sosa — the **"voice of the voiceless"** and **"conscience of Latin America"** — rooted in Argentine folk music, became the greatest exponent of nueva canción in the Southern Cone. She launched the **Movimiento del Nuevo Cancionero** in Mendoza with Armando Tejada Gómez and Oscar Matus, vindicating marginalized folk figures like Yupanqui.

Exiled during Argentina's military dictatorship (1976–1983), she returned in triumph, her voice even more powerful as a symbol of democratic resistance.

## 33. Argentine Electronic: ZZK Records and Digital Cumbia

In the late 2000s, a movement emerged in Buenos Aires that fused cumbia, Latin American folk, and electronic production into what became known as **digital cumbia** or **cumbia digital**.

### ZZK Records

Founded in 2008 by American expat **Grant C. Dull** and Argentines **Guillermo Canale and Diego Bulacio**, ZZK Records grew out of the **Zizek Club** nights in Buenos Aires. The club was a meeting ground for artists experimenting with cumbia beats mixed with electronic sounds — hip-hop, reggaeton, dancehall, dubstep, grime.

Key artists: **Chancha Vía Circuito, Tremor, Fauna, El Remolón, Frikstailers, King Coya**

The movement took cumbia from "the music of the poor" to a globally recognized electronic music genre, toured international festivals, and influenced producers worldwide.

# Part VI: Chile — Cueca, Protest, and Indigenous Resistance

## 34. Cueca: Chile's National Dance

The cueca was officially declared Chile's national dance in 1979. Derived from the zamacueca (see Section 15), it is a courtship dance performed throughout Chile with regional variations.

### The Dance
- The man pursues the woman, who adopts an elusive stance
- Both dancers hold **handkerchiefs** above their heads
- Said to mimic the courtship of a **rooster displaying its feathers** to a hen
- No physical touching — contact is maintained through facial expressions, movements, and the waving handkerchief

### Musical Structure
- Written in a combination of **6/8 and 3/4** simultaneously (the sesquiáltera)
- Very rigid three-section structure:
  1. **Cuarteta** — four verses, eight syllables each
  2. **Seguidilla** — a transitional section
  3. **Remate** — the closing section
- Accompanied by guitar, harp, and percussion (including handclaps)

```
CUECA RHYTHM (in 6/8 + 3/4 simultaneously):

Guitar (6/8 feel):  |x . x . x .|x . x . x .|
Handclaps (3/4):    |X . . X . .|X . . X . .|
```

## 35. Nueva Canción: Music as Political Weapon

Chile's nueva canción (New Song) movement represents perhaps the most powerful example in modern history of music as an instrument of political resistance — and of music's power to provoke state violence.

### Violeta Parra (1917–1967): The Foundation

Violeta Parra traveled Chile collecting over **3,000 songs, recipes, traditions, and proverbs** from rural communities. She revived forgotten folk forms, composed original works of startling beauty and political depth, and laid the foundation for nueva canción. Her song "Gracias a la Vida" (Thanks to Life) became one of the most beloved songs in the Spanish language.

### Víctor Jara (1932–1973): The Martyr

Víctor Jara was a singer, songwriter, theater director, and political activist who aligned with Salvador Allende's socialist government. His songs spoke directly to issues of poverty, imperialism, and human rights.

**The Brutal Ending:**

On September 11, 1973, Augusto Pinochet's military overthrew Allende in a bloody coup. Jara was arrested and taken to the **Estadio Chile** (now renamed Estadio Víctor Jara). There, soldiers smashed his hands and mocked him: "Now try to play guitar." He was tortured for four days, then riddled with 44 bullets and his body thrown in the street.

Pinochet then **outlawed the playing of many Andean instruments** — quena, charango, zampoña — in an attempt to suppress the New Song Movement. The instruments themselves had become symbols of resistance.

### The Movement's Legacy

Other key groups — **Inti-Illimani, Quilapayún** — went into exile and became international ambassadors for Chilean resistance. Nueva canción spread across South America, fueling resistance movements against military dictatorships in Argentina, Uruguay, and beyond.

## 36. Mapuche Music: Indigenous Resistance Through Sound

The **Mapuche people** ("people of the earth") of southern Chile and Argentina have maintained their musical traditions for over 1,000 years — music that serves as both spiritual practice and political resistance.

### The Kultrun (Cultrun)

The **kultrun** is the most important Mapuche musical instrument — a ceremonial drum used by the **machi** (shaman/healer) for religious purposes.

- A hollowed wooden bowl covered with leather
- The surface is painted with symbols representing the four cardinal directions, the sun, and the moon
- Contains sacred objects inside (seeds, stones, herbs) that rattle
- Played exclusively by the machi during healing ceremonies and nguillatún (communal prayers)

### The Trutruka (Trutruca)

A long trumpet made of colihue (bamboo) wood with a cattle horn bell:
- Can be straight or spiral-shaped
- Produces a deep, powerful sound
- Used in ceremonies and increasingly in protest music

### Music as Resistance

Mapuche music has become a vehicle for political resistance against land dispossession, cultural erasure, and state violence. Contemporary Mapuche musicians blend traditional instruments with hip-hop, rock, and electronic music to reach new audiences while maintaining cultural identity.

# Part VII: Uruguay — Candombe and Murga

## 37. Candombe: The Afro-Uruguayan Drum Tradition

Candombe is the rhythmic heart of Uruguay — an Afro-Uruguayan drum tradition that pulses through Montevideo's streets every weekend and explodes during Carnival.

### The Three Drums

Candombe uses three sizes of drum (tambores), each with a distinct function:

**1. Chico (small, high-pitched):**
- Marks the tempo
- Relatively simple patterns
- The "timekeeper" of the ensemble

**2. Repique (medium, mid-pitched):**
- Provides syncopation and improvisation
- Technically complex
- Plays the madera (clave) pattern between phrases during the llamada
- The "soloist" and "communicator"

**3. Piano (large, low-pitched):**
- Provides the "melody" — pitched patterns that create tonal movement
- The most technically complex drum
- Greater variation and improvisation than the chico
- The "foundation" of the rhythm

### The Madera/Clave Pattern

All three drums share a **madera** pattern (clave) that functions like the timeline in Afro-Cuban and sub-Saharan African music:
- Serves as temporal organization and synchronization
- Played by all drums as an introduction
- During the llamada, played only by the repique between phrases

### The Llamada: The Street Procession

The **llamada** (literally "the call") is candombe's most spectacular manifestation — a street procession where **hundreds of drummers** march through Montevideo's historic Barrio Sur and Palermo neighborhoods.

- Each neighborhood has its own **cuerda** (drum corps)
- The cuerdas compete in organized llamadas during Carnival
- The sound of hundreds of drums in a narrow street is physically overwhelming — you feel it in your chest
- The llamada is a living continuation of African communal drumming traditions

### Rhythmic Structure
- The pattern period is four tatums (subdivisions)
- Tempo range: approximately **110–150 BPM**
- The resulting metrical structure: a four-beat measure with regular subdivision into 16 tatums

```
CANDOMBE — FULL THREE-DRUM PATTERN (in 4/4):

Repique: |..X...X.X...X.X...|..X...X.X...X.X...|
Piano:   |X.....X...X.X.....|X.....X...X.X.....|
          1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a

X/x = stroke (uppercase = accent)

CANDOMBE — MADERA/CLAVE PATTERN:

|X . . X . X . . X . X . . . . .|
 1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a

This functions like the Afro-Cuban clave —
organizing the entire rhythmic ensemble.

Open (O), Slap (S), Muted (m):

|O . . m . S . . O . S . m . . .|
 1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a

The piano drum's pitched tones create

|. . X . . X X . . . X . X X . .|
 1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a

The repique's syncopated pattern signals

```

### Candombe's Influence on Tango and Milonga

The African-descended communities of Montevideo and Buenos Aires shared a common cultural space. Candombe's rhythmic sensibilities — its syncopation, its emphasis on the and-beats, its polyrhythmic layering — directly influenced the development of milonga and tango. The habanera pattern that drives early tango is, in this context, not just a Cuban import but a manifestation of a broader African-diasporic rhythmic language that was already present in the Río de la Plata.

## 38. Murga: Satirical Carnival Theater-Music

Murga is a Uruguayan Carnival tradition that combines theater, comedy, political satire, and music into performances that are equal parts entertainment and social critique.

### Origins

Murga was introduced to Latin America through **Spanish performers from Cádiz** who arrived in Montevideo around 1908 as part of a zarzuela troupe. Stranded after failing to secure return passage, they formed the inaugural group **"Gaditana que se va"** — adapting the Cádiz *chirigotas* (satirical choral sketches) into carnival performances.

### Structure
- **17 members**: 13 singers, 1 director, 3 percussionists
- **Percussion:** bombo (shallow bass drum worn at the waist, played horizontally), redoblante (snare drum), and platillos (clash cymbals) — derived from European military band instruments
- **Largely a cappella singing** with percussion accompaniment
- Lyrics parody well-known songs, advertising jingles, and political speeches

### The Content

Murguistas are not professional performers — they are **bakers, vendors, mechanics, factory workers**. Their performances critique everything from presidents to soccer teams to social issues, constructing "clever, humorous critiques" through wordplay and musical parody.

"Murga is one of the only things that a lot of Uruguayans feel is only theirs," notes musicologist Natalie Kirschstein. "So it's viewed with a lot of pride."

```

             1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a

The percussion supports the chorus,
punctuating comedic and dramatic moments
rather than providing a steady dance rhythm.
```

# Part VIII: Paraguay — Guarania and the Harp

## 39. Guarania: Slow Melancholy

**Guarania** was created by musician **José Asunción Flores** in 1925, with the explicit purpose of expressing the character of the Paraguayan people. It is a deeply emotive, melodic genre capturing the essence of rural Paraguayan life.

### Musical Characteristics
- Slow tempo, melancholic character
- Based on **6/8 rhythm**
- Themes of love, nature, and human experience
- The **Paraguayan harp** is the quintessential accompanying instrument
- Lyrics often in **Guaraní** or a Guaraní-Spanish mix (jopará)

---

## 40. Polca Paraguaya: Not European Polka

The polca paraguaya (Paraguayan polka) is Paraguay's best-known folk genre — and despite its name, it is vastly different from German/Czech polkas.

### Musical Characteristics
- Also based on **6/8 rhythm** (like guarania, but faster and more energetic)
- Originally derived from European polkas but extensively transformed
- Lively, dance-oriented
- Guitar and harp accompaniment

## 41. The Guaraní Harp Tradition

The Paraguayan harp was introduced by **Jesuit missionaries** in the 17th century, originally used in church music in place of the organ or harpsichord. The Guaraní people adopted it and transformed it into something uniquely their own.

### The Instrument
- Diatonic (no sharps or flats without retuning)
- Lighter and more portable than classical harps
- 36–38 strings
- Shape and size consolidated by **Félix Pérez Cardozo** in the early 20th century
- Declared the **national instrument of Paraguay**

### Playing Style
- Fingers of both hands pluck the strings
- The right hand plays melody on the upper strings
- The left hand plays bass notes and arpeggiated accompaniment
- Rapid tremolo and arpeggiation create shimmering textures
- The harp provides both melody, harmony, and rhythm simultaneously

The Paraguayan harp tradition represents one of the most remarkable cases of musical syncretism in the Americas — a European instrument, brought by missionaries, adopted by indigenous people, and transformed into the voice of a nation.

# Part IX: Venezuela — Joropo, Gaita, and Tambor

## 42. Joropo: The National Music of Venezuela

Joropo is Venezuela's national music — a genre of extraordinary rhythmic complexity built on the interplay between harp, cuatro, maracas, and the human body.

### The Instrumental Trio
- **Arpa llanera** (plains harp) — the melodic and harmonic foundation
- **Cuatro** — a small four-string guitar whose loosely tensed strings create a multitude of rhythmic-percussive effects, essential to the joropo sound
- **Maracas** — a deceptively simple-looking pair of rattles that produce a great variety of subtle sonorities and complex rhythmic patterns

### The Complex Cross-Rhythms

Joropo's defining characteristic is the **sesquiáltera** (hemiola) — the simultaneous use of **3/4 and 6/8**. This is not merely alternation between the two meters but their *superimposition*, creating a constant rhythmic ambiguity that gives joropo its electrifying energy.

The cuatro typically plays in 3/4 while the maracas play in 6/8, the harp weaves between both, and the singer adds yet another rhythmic layer.

### Types of Joropo
- **Joropo llanero** — from the llanos (plains), the most common form
- **Joropo central** — from the central region, with distinct harmonic language
- **Joropo oriental** — from the eastern region, incorporating different instruments

### The Zapateo: Feet as Percussion

The **zapateo** (footwork) is an integral percussion element in joropo. Dancers' heels strike the floor in complex patterns that interlock with the instrumental rhythm. In performance, the zapateo is not accompaniment — it is a full percussion voice in the ensemble.

### UNESCO Recognition

In 2014, joropo was inscribed on UNESCO's Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity.

## 43. Gaita: The Zulia Christmas Tradition

Gaita (specifically **gaita zuliana**) is a musical genre from Zulia state (western Venezuela, around Lake Maracaibo) that has become the quintessential Venezuelan holiday music.

### Origins
- Born in Zulia from an Afro-Zulian tradition
- Heavily reliant on drum beats from the **tambora** (larger drum) and **tamborito** (smaller drum)
- Originally devotional/religious, now associated with Christmas season
- Lyrics revolve around Zulian themes, religious devotion, and social commentary

### The Sound
- Call-and-response singing over percussion and cuatro
- The gaita spread from Zulia to become synonymous with December festivities across Venezuela
- Families and neighborhoods form gaita groups during the holiday season

## 44. Tambor: Afro-Venezuelan Drumming Traditions

Venezuela possesses one of the richest Afro-diasporic drumming traditions in South America, characterized by a great diversity of drums of African origin.

### The San Juan Festival

The **Fiestas de San Juan** (June 24) offers a unique window into Afro-Venezuelan culture. Communities along the central coast celebrate with powerful drumming as participants dance in processions carrying the saint's statue. This is Catholic saint worship fused with African spiritual practice — the drums speak to both traditions simultaneously.

### Key Drums and Traditions

**Cumaco:** A large, elongated drum widespread along the central coastal region. Used in San Juan celebrations and secular *bailes de tambor* (drum dances).

**Culo e'puya:** Important drums in the **Barlovento** region (Miranda state) — a center of Afro-Venezuelan culture with some of the densest drumming traditions in South America.

**Quitiplás:** Unique bamboo tube drums played by striking them on the ground — creating a deep, resonant bass from hollow bamboo. Multiple players with different-length tubes create interlocking rhythmic patterns.

### San Benito de Palermo Festivals

Communities across the Andes and Zulia celebrate San Benito de Palermo (a Black saint) around December/January with festivals renowned for their powerful Afro-Venezuelan drumming.

# Part X: Ecuador — San Juanito, Marimba, and Bomba

## 45. San Juanito: The Andean Dance Rhythm

San Juanito (sanjuanito) is the signature Andean dance of Ecuador's Sierra (highland) region — a festive, cheerful genre heard at all events, urban and rural.

### Origins
- Roots in **pre-Columbian Kichwa rituals**, originally performed during the **Inti Raymi** festival to honor the sun god Inti
- The dance is performed in a **circular pattern** — forming the round shape of the sun, stomping hard, "raising dust"
- One of the oldest continuously performed dances in the Americas

### Musical Characteristics
- Written in **2/4 time**
- **Pentatonic melodies** (five-note scale) in **minor mode**
- High-pitched vocal and instrumental timbres
- Traditional instruments: pinguillo (flute), rondador (panpipe), bandolín (chordophone), dulzaína (oboe-like)

```

Bombo:     |B . x B . x|B . x B x x|
Rondador:  |♩ ♪♪ ♩ ♪♪|♩ ♪♪ ♩ ♪♪|
            1 e & a 2 e & a

Pentatonic, minor mode, with the characteristic
Andean bounce — related to huayno but with
distinctly Ecuadorian phrasing.
```

## 46. Pasillo: The Ecuadorian Waltz

The **pasillo** is Ecuador's waltz tradition — a fusion of European waltz with indigenous rhythmic sensibilities. In Ecuador, the pasillo absorbed the influence of san juanito, making it slower and more melancholic than Colombian or Venezuelan pasillos.

### Character
- Slow, romantic, melancholic
- 3/4 time with subtle syncopation
- Guitar-based accompaniment
- Often considered the "soul of Ecuador"
- Themes of love, loss, and longing

## 47. Marimba Esmeraldeña: Afro-Ecuadorian Heritage

The **marimba esmeraldeña** is the Afro-Ecuadorian marimba music tradition from Esmeraldas Province — inscribed on UNESCO's Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2015.

### The Instrument and Music
- A palm wood **xylophone with bamboo tube resonators**
- Accompanied by drums (bombos, cununos) and maracas
- Played at ritual, religious, and festive events
- Call-and-response singing accompanies the music
- Functions as celebration of life, worship of saints, or farewell to the deceased

### Origins
During the Atlantic slavery period, enslaved Africans and their descendants populated the northern coast of Esmeraldas province. With African musical foundations — instruments, musical forms, and choreographic elements — the Esmeraldeño Black community created their own musical traditions.

## 48. Bomba del Chota: Valley Music

The **Bomba del Chota** is the signature Afro-Ecuadorian music of the Chota-Mira valley (Imbabura and Carchi provinces).

### A Multi-Dimensional Tradition
"Bomba" in this context refers simultaneously to:
- A **musical genre**
- A **dance** style
- A **musical instrument** (a drum made from animal skin stretched over a hollow log)
- A type of **musical ensemble**

### The Music
- Energetic, percussion-driven
- Guitar and bomba drum are the core instruments
- Call-and-response vocal style
- Lyrics about daily life, love, community, and identity
- The dance famously involves balancing a **bottle on the head** while dancing

# Part XI: Sociopolitical Dimensions

## 1. Colonialism and Erasure

The Spanish colonization of the Americas involved systematic suppression of indigenous and African musical traditions. From the 16th century onwards:

- **Catholic missionaries** suppressed indigenous musics and re-educated indigenous peoples in European chant, polyphony, and instruments
- **Spanish authorities** strategically allowed some indigenous music at civic celebrations — not out of tolerance, but as a tool for gradual replacement of "pagan" sounds with European ones
- **African drums were banned** in many regions, as colonial authorities recognized their role in communication and resistance — directly leading to innovations like the cajón
- **Andean instruments were associated with paganism** and their use was restricted in many contexts

The colonizers understood what musicologists now confirm: music is not entertainment — it is social organization, spiritual practice, and political power. To control a population's music is to control its identity.

---

## 2. The African Erasure in Andean Countries

Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador, and other Andean nations have historically **minimized their African heritage**. Despite significant Afro-descended populations:

- National narratives emphasized indigenous-Spanish mestizaje while ignoring the African contribution
- Afro-Peruvian, Afro-Bolivian, and Afro-Ecuadorian communities were rendered culturally invisible
- Musical traditions of African origin (festejo, landó, saya, bomba del Chota) were marginalized or folklorized
- The African roots of "national" instruments like the cajón were downplayed

The revival movements of the mid-20th century (Nicomedes and Victoria Santa Cruz in Peru, saya revivalists in Bolivia, marimba and bomba advocates in Ecuador) represented not just musical preservation but **political acts of visibility** — forcing nations to acknowledge the African presence that had always been there.

---

## 3. Indigenous Rights and Music

Andean music has increasingly become a **symbol of indigenous pride** and a vehicle for political mobilization:

- The quena, charango, and zampoña became emblems of pan-Andean indigenous identity
- Indigenous musicians use traditional instruments and languages (Quechua, Aymara, Guaraní) as assertions of cultural sovereignty
- The Mapuche in Chile use the kultrun and trutruka in protest music
- Contemporary indigenous artists blend traditional forms with hip-hop, rock, and electronic music — reaching young audiences while maintaining cultural roots

---

## 4. Nueva Canción: Music Against Dictatorships

The nueva canción movement (see Sections 32 and 35) was the most explicitly political musical movement in South American history:

- **Chile:** Violeta Parra, Víctor Jara, Inti-Illimani, Quilapayún — culminating in Jara's murder and Pinochet's banning of Andean instruments
- **Argentina:** Mercedes Sosa, Atahualpa Yupanqui, the Movimiento del Nuevo Cancionero — Sosa exiled during the military dictatorship
- **Uruguay:** Daniel Viglietti, Alfredo Zitarrosa — forced into exile
- **Across South America:** nueva canción became the soundtrack of resistance, with folk instruments explicitly opposed to the cultural homogenization favored by authoritarian regimes

The movement demonstrated that music could be so politically powerful that dictatorships would murder musicians and ban instruments.

---

## 5. The Coca Leaf and Music

The **coca leaf** (Erythroxylum coca) holds a sacred place in Andean culture, and its ritual use is intimately connected to music:

- **Despacho ceremonies** (offerings to Pachamama) include music, song, and coca leaf offerings
- The **kintu** — selected coca leaves offered to Andean deities — is accompanied by prayers and songs
- **Hallpay** — communal coca-chewing gatherings — are occasions for singing, storytelling, and social bonding
- Traditional music accompanies all stages of coca cultivation and harvest
- The **Varayoc inauguration ceremony** includes music, dances, and purification rituals involving coca

The coca leaf, like music, mediates between the three worlds of Andean cosmovision — it is an offering to Pachamama (Kay Pacha/Ukhu Pacha) that opens communication with the divine (Hanan Pacha).

---

## 6. Global Circulation: El Cóndor Pasa and Beyond

### El Cóndor Pasa

"El Cóndor Pasa" is the most globally recognized Andean melody, with an estimated **4,000 versions and 300 sets of lyrics** produced worldwide.

**The story:**
- Originally composed by **Daniel Alomía Robles** in 1913 as an orchestral piece based on a huayno melody
- Virtually unknown outside Peru until **Simon and Garfunkel** included their English-language version on *Bridge Over Troubled Water* (1970)
- The Simon and Garfunkel version credited **Jorge Milchberg** as composer (Robles's family later sued)
- The song's international success launched a wave of "Andean music" tourism and recording

**The paradox:**
Although the piece has become a symbol of Peruvian identity, its global circulation largely ignores indigenous agency. The internationally famous tune "sounds indigenous and/or Peruvian" to audiences worldwide, but its pathway to global recognition passed through white-mestizo composers, classical orchestration, and American folk-rock — a process of "metacultural production" where the music's essence is reconstituted to fit national and international agendas.

### The Broader Pattern

Andean music's global circulation follows a familiar pattern:
- **Appropriation without attribution** — melodies borrowed, origins obscured
- **Exoticization** — Andean music reduced to "pan-flute background music" in Western imagination
- **Economic extraction** — global music industry profits from indigenous traditions with little return to source communities
- **Paradoxical preservation** — global interest sometimes drives local preservation efforts even as it distorts the music's meaning

## Cross-Cultural Rhythmic Connections: A Summary

The rhythmic traditions of South America reveal deep interconnections — both within the continent and with global musical traditions:

### The African Thread
- The **habanera/tresillo** pattern (3-3-2) appears in tango, milonga, and connects to Cuban son, New Orleans jazz, and West African bell patterns
- **Interlocking drum patterns** in saya (Bolivia), candombe (Uruguay), and tambor (Venezuela) connect directly to West and Central African polyrhythmic traditions
- The **cajón** represents African rhythmic intelligence surviving colonial suppression through material innovation

### The Andean Thread
- The **sesquiáltera** (6/8 vs 3/4 hemiola) appears in cueca, zamba, chacarera, marinera, joropo, and connects the entire continent
- The **wet/dry seasonal instrument division** shows how rhythm is cosmic, not merely musical
- **Hocketing** (interlocking) in siku playing connects to Balinese kotekan and Central African vocal techniques

### The Colonial Legacy
- European waltz (3/4) was absorbed and transformed: vals criollo, pasillo, guarania
- European polka was absorbed and transformed: polca paraguaya
- European military band instruments were absorbed: murga, Bolivian brass bands
- In every case, the European form was creolized — syncopated, polyrhythmized, and made to serve local expressive needs

### The Synthesis
South American rhythm is not a single tradition but a web of connections — African, indigenous, and European threads woven together over 500 years of encounter, conflict, resistance, and creation. The tinkuy principle is not just Andean philosophy — it describes the entire musical history of the continent: the coming together of differences to create something greater than the sum of its parts.
