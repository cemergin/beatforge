## 1. Historical Roots

### 1.1 The Griot Tradition -- Oral Historians, Musicians, and Keepers of Cultural Memory

In the savannah kingdoms of West Africa -- from the ancient Mali Empire to the Jolof of Senegambia and the Hausa city-states -- a hereditary caste of oral historians, musicians, praise singers, and storytellers has served as the living archive of entire civilizations. These are the *griots* (in French colonial terminology) or, more precisely, the *jeli* (Mande), *gewel* (Wolof), *gawlo* (Fula), and *maabo* (Haalpulaar). The word "griot" itself is a European approximation; within these cultures, the role carries names that speak to its deep specificity.

The jeli is born into the tradition. Knowledge is transmitted within families across generations -- a child raised in a jeli household learns genealogies, epic narratives, moral parables, musical technique, and the art of diplomatic speech from infancy. This is not casual storytelling; it is a rigorous intellectual discipline. A master jeli can recite the genealogy of a noble family back twenty or more generations, narrating the deeds, alliances, migrations, and moral lessons of each ancestor. The most celebrated of these oral epics is the *Sunjata* (also Sundiata), the founding narrative of the Mali Empire, which recounts how the crippled prince Sundiata Keita rose to defeat the sorcerer-king Sumanguru Kante at the Battle of Kirina around 1235 CE. This epic has been transmitted orally for nearly eight centuries without interruption.

Griots are not merely historians -- they are social mediators, diplomats, praise singers, and counselors. At naming ceremonies, weddings, and funerals, the griot's voice sanctifies the occasion. In political contexts, griots served as advisors to kings, ambassadors between kingdoms, and witnesses to treaties and oaths. Their instruments -- the *kora* (a 21-string bridge harp), the *balafon* (a wooden xylophone), the *ngoni* (a lute), and various drums -- are not mere accompaniment but mnemonic devices, with specific tunings, melodic patterns, and rhythmic frameworks tied to particular historical narratives and lineages.

The griot tradition challenges a widespread Western assumption that literacy is the prerequisite for historical accuracy. As the Malian scholar Amadou Hampate Ba famously declared: "In Africa, when an old person dies, it is a library burning." This statement, while often quoted as a lament, is equally a testament to the extraordinary capacity of oral tradition to preserve knowledge with precision and depth.

### 1.2 Pre-Colonial Musical Systems -- How Rhythm Organized Community Life

Before European colonization disrupted -- and in many cases deliberately dismantled -- indigenous social structures, West African musical systems were deeply interwoven with the fabric of daily life. Music was not a separate category of experience labeled "entertainment" or "art" in the Western sense. It was a technology for organizing collective labor, marking the passage of time, mediating between the human and spiritual worlds, governing social interactions, and encoding community values.

**Work songs** (*agriculture, fishing, construction*): Across the region, communal labor was organized through rhythmic song. In farming communities, work songs synchronized the swinging of hoes, the planting of seeds, and the harvesting of grain. Among the Mande peoples, *Kassa* rhythms (from the word for "granary") accompanied agricultural work, with the tempo and intensity of the drumming calibrated to the demands of the task. The rhythm did not merely accompany the work -- it *organized* it, coordinating the movements of dozens or hundreds of workers into a unified, efficient collective action. This is rhythm as social technology in its most literal sense.

**Ceremonies and rites of passage**: Every significant transition in human life -- birth, naming, initiation into adulthood, marriage, installation of a chief, and death -- had its own specific musical repertoire. These were not interchangeable: playing the wrong rhythm at the wrong ceremony would be a serious breach of social protocol. Among the Malinke, *Soli* family rhythms are exclusively associated with male initiation, while *Mendiani* rhythms are played for young female dancers. The specificity of these associations means that hearing a particular rhythm immediately signals to everyone within the cultural context what event is taking place and what social protocols are in effect.

**Communication**: In the era before telecommunications, drums served as long-distance communication networks. The talking drum traditions of the Yoruba, Dagomba, and other peoples allowed messages to travel across distances of four to five miles, with relay systems extending the range indefinitely. This was not a simple code -- it was a sophisticated exploitation of the tonal properties of African languages, allowing drums to reproduce actual speech with remarkable fidelity.

### 1.3 The Talking Drum (Dundun) -- Tonal Language and Long-Distance Communication

The talking drum -- known as *dundun* (Yoruba), *lunga* (Dagomba), *tama* (Wolof), and by many other names across the region -- is one of the most remarkable musical instruments ever devised. It is an hourglass-shaped pressure drum with two goatskin heads connected by leather tension cords. By squeezing these cords against the body under the arm, the player can continuously vary the pitch of the drum across a range of approximately an octave, allowing it to mimic the tonal contours, rhythm, and stress patterns of human speech.

This is not metaphor. A 2021 study published in *Frontiers in Communication* compared thirty verbal recordings in Yoruba -- both spoken and sung -- with corresponding performances on the *dundun* drum by professional drummers in Nigeria. The research confirmed that the drum could reproduce the lexical and grammatical tones of Yoruba with remarkable accuracy, allowing fluent speakers of the language to understand drum "speech" with a high degree of comprehension.

The Yoruba *dundun* ensemble typically includes:
- **Iya ilu** (the "mother drum") -- the lead talking drum, largest in the ensemble, played by the master drummer
- **Omele** -- supporting drums of various sizes
- **Gudugudu** -- a smaller, fixed-pitch drum that provides a rhythmic foundation

Among the Dagomba of northern Ghana, the *lunga* (a cognate instrument) plays a parallel role. Dagomba *lunsi* (drummers) can narrate entire historical episodes, recite proverbs, praise chiefs, and relay news through drum language. The talking drum traditions of West Africa represent one of humanity's earliest and most sophisticated telecommunication systems, predating the electric telegraph by centuries and operating on entirely different -- and in some ways more expressive -- principles.

### 1.4 Rhythm as Social Technology -- How Polyrhythm Encodes Community Values

The polyrhythmic structures of West African music are not merely aesthetic choices -- they are sonic expressions of deeply held social values. Where Western classical music traditionally emphasizes a single dominant melody supported by harmonic accompaniment (reflecting, some scholars argue, hierarchical social structures), West African ensemble music creates a texture in which multiple independent rhythmic lines interlock to form a whole that is greater than the sum of its parts.

Consider a typical Ewe drum ensemble: the *gankogui* (bell) sounds the timeline pattern; the *axatse* (rattle) adds its own complementary rhythm; the *kagan* (small drum) plays a steady supporting pattern; the *kidi* (medium drum) responds to and comments on the other parts; the *sogo* (large drum) adds another layer of rhythmic conversation; and the *atsimevu* (master drum) improvises over and around all of these. Each part is relatively simple on its own, but when combined, they produce a rhythmic texture of extraordinary complexity and richness.

This musical structure encodes several core community values:

- **Interdependence**: No single part makes complete sense in isolation. Each drummer depends on hearing and responding to the others. The music literally cannot exist without collective participation.
- **Conversation**: The relationship between parts is dialogic -- drums "speak" to each other, call and respond, agree and contradict. This mirrors the West African value placed on communal dialogue and consensus-building.
- **Individual expression within collective structure**: The master drummer improvises freely, but always in relationship to the fixed patterns of the ensemble. This reflects a social philosophy in which individual creativity and leadership are valued, but always in service to the community.
- **Shared responsibility**: If any one drummer drops out or loses their pattern, the entire texture collapses. Every participant bears equal responsibility for the whole.

The ethnomusicologist John Miller Chernoff, in his landmark work *African Rhythm and African Sensibility* (1979), argued that the aesthetic principles of African drumming are inseparable from the ethical principles of African communal life. The music does not merely reflect these values -- it teaches and reinforces them through direct bodily experience.

### 1.5 The Role of the Master Drummer -- Improvisation, Spiritual Authority, Training Lineage

The master drummer (*atsimevu* player among the Ewe, *djembefola* among the Mande, *iya ilu* player among the Yoruba) holds a position of extraordinary cultural authority. This is not simply a musical role -- it is spiritual, intellectual, historical, and social.

**Training and lineage**: Becoming a master drummer typically requires a minimum of twelve years of intensive apprenticeship under an established master. In many traditions, drumming knowledge is hereditary -- passed within specific families or castes -- though the Mande djembe tradition is notably open, with no hereditary restrictions on who may become a djembefola. The apprentice learns not only rhythmic patterns and technique but also the cultural contexts in which each rhythm is appropriate, the histories and stories associated with each piece, the spiritual protocols for drum construction and performance, and the art of reading and responding to dancers, other musicians, and the social dynamics of the gathering.

**Improvisation**: The master drummer's role is fundamentally improvisatory, but this improvisation is not "free" in the Western jazz sense. It is deeply structured by tradition, responding to specific patterns played by the ensemble, to the movements of dancers, to the social dynamics of the occasion, and to the spiritual energies perceived to be present. The master drummer must know hundreds of rhythmic patterns and their variations, understand their cultural meanings, and deploy them with precision and creativity in real time. Among the Ewe, the *atsimevu* player uses specific drum strokes and patterns to signal dancers, cue changes in the ensemble, and narrate aspects of the history or mythology associated with the piece being performed.

**Spiritual authority**: In many West African traditions, the drum itself is regarded as a living spiritual entity. The construction of a drum involves rituals, prayers, and offerings. The wood, the animal skin, the cords -- each material carries spiritual significance. The master drummer serves as an intermediary between the human community and the spiritual world, and the act of drumming can invoke the presence of ancestors, spirits, or deities. Among the Yoruba, specific *bata* rhythms are believed to contain the spiritual force necessary to summon the *orisha* (deities). The drummer who plays these sacred rhythms carries enormous responsibility -- incorrect playing is not merely a musical error but a spiritual transgression.

## 2. The Rhythm Traditions

### 2.1 Ewe (Ghana/Togo) -- Agbekor, Gahu, Kpanlogo

The Ewe people, inhabiting the coastal and inland regions of southeastern Ghana, southern Togo, and southwestern Benin, have developed one of the most extensively documented and analytically rich drumming traditions in all of West Africa. The scholarship of C.K. Ladzekpo (himself an Ewe master drummer and ethnomusicologist), David Locke, and others has made Ewe drumming a touchstone for the study of African polyrhythm worldwide.

#### The Bell Pattern as "Timeline"

At the foundation of Ewe ensemble music is the *gankogui* (iron double-bell) pattern, which functions as what ethnomusicologists call a "timeline" -- an asymmetric rhythmic pattern that orients all other parts in the ensemble. This timeline is not a "beat" in the Western sense of a regularly recurring pulse. It is an asymmetric, cyclic pattern that creates a field of rhythmic tension and resolution against which all other parts are positioned.

The most common Ewe bell pattern (and one of the most widespread rhythmic patterns in all of sub-Saharan Africa) is a 12-pulse pattern often notated as: **x.x.xx.x.x.x.** (where x is a stroke and . is a rest). This pattern -- sometimes called the "standard pattern" -- has been the subject of extensive musicological analysis by scholars including A.M. Jones, J.H. Kwabena Nketia, Gerhard Kubik, and David Locke. Its asymmetry is the key to its function: because it is not evenly divisible, it creates multiple possible points of reference, allowing different musicians and dancers to orient themselves to different "downbeats" within the same cycle. This is a fundamentally different approach to musical time than the symmetrical meters of Western classical music.

#### Agbekor

*Agbekor* (also Atsiagbekor) is a warrior dance-drumming tradition of the Anlo Ewe, originally performed in connection with warfare and later adapted for funerals and other significant occasions. The word derives from *agbe* (life) and *kor* (clear/great), and the tradition preserves choreographic and musical memories of historical military campaigns. The percussion ensemble features a complex lead drumming part (*atsimevu*) riding on a rich polyrhythmic texture established by bells, rattles, and drums of graduated sizes. The choreography mimics the heroism and tactics of the battlefield, and the entire performance serves as a collective act of historical remembrance and cultural affirmation.

David Locke's detailed transcriptions and analyses of Agbekor (published in his *Drum Damba* and other works, and through his Tufts University research) have been foundational in demonstrating the systematic complexity of Ewe polyrhythmic structures, showing how each instrumental part relates to the timeline and to every other part through precise cross-rhythmic relationships.

#### Gahu

*Gahu* is a relatively recent addition to the Ewe repertoire, having been adopted from the Yoruba-speaking Gun people of Benin in the mid-twentieth century. It became one of the most popular recreational dance-drumming forms among the Ewe. The Gahu bell pattern is a 24-pulse cycle that notably "crosses the barline," creating a fascinating sense of rhythmic ambiguity. The dance features elegant, flowing movements that contrast with the more angular, martial choreography of Agbekor, and the ensemble includes the standard Ewe percussion instruments along with additional drums adapted from the original Beninese tradition.

#### Kpanlogo

*Kpanlogo* emerged in the early 1960s among urban Ga youth in the Bukom neighborhood of Accra, created by Otoo Lincoln and others who fused older Ga fishing community styles (*kolomashie*, *gome*, *oge*) with contemporary influences. It became the soundtrack of newly independent Ghana's youthful energy and optimism. The Arts Council of Ghana initially banned Kpanlogo in 1964 due to concerns about the sensuality of the dance, but the tradition was vindicated through a demonstration and quickly became one of Ghana's most popular recreational forms. The Kpanlogo ensemble typically features three drums (designated "male voice," "female voice," and "master drum"), a *nono* (metal bell) playing the timeline, and *fao* (gourd rattle). The tradition exemplifies how West African rhythmic principles continuously generate new forms while maintaining deep structural continuity with older traditions.

### 2.2 Mandinka/Malinke (Guinea/Mali/Senegal) -- Djembe Traditions

The djembe -- a goblet-shaped, skin-covered hand drum -- is perhaps the most globally recognized West African instrument, though its cultural specificity and depth are often flattened by its worldwide popularity. Its origin is associated with the *Numu* (blacksmith caste) of the Mandinka and Malinke peoples, and its wide dispersion across West Africa may trace back to Numu migrations during the first millennium CE. The Numu occupy a special position in Mande society: as workers of fire and metal, they are believed to possess *nyama* (a dangerous spiritual power), and their association with the djembe imbues the instrument with spiritual significance from its very origin.

#### The Djembefola

The term *djembefola* literally means "one who makes the djembe speak." Despite the djembe's association with the Numu, there are no hereditary restrictions on who may become a djembefola -- unlike the kora or balafon, which are traditionally reserved for the jeli caste. This relative openness has contributed to the djembe's dynamism and its ability to attract extraordinarily talented players from diverse backgrounds.

The great master djembefolas of the twentieth century -- *Fadouba Oularé*, *Famoudou Konaté*, *Mamady Keita*, and others -- developed the instrument's technical vocabulary to astonishing levels of speed, precision, and expressiveness. Mamady Keita (1950-2021), born in the village of Balandugu in northeastern Guinea, was perhaps the most internationally influential djembefola in history. He studied at Guinea's national ballet (Les Ballets Africains, founded by Fodeba Keita in 1952), later founded his own ensemble *Sewa Kan*, and eventually established the *Tam Tam Mandingue* school with branches worldwide. In a 2015 interview with *African Arts* (MIT Press), Keita spoke of his mission "to preserve the tradition well" while sharing it with the world.

#### The Dunun Family

The djembe never performs alone in traditional contexts. It is always accompanied by the *dunun* (also dundun, not to be confused with the Yoruba talking drum of the same name) family -- a set of cylindrical, double-headed bass drums played with sticks, each typically paired with a bell:

- **Dundunba** (also dununba) -- the largest and lowest-pitched, providing the deep foundational pulse
- **Sangban** -- the medium drum, often playing the most characteristic identifying pattern of a particular rhythm
- **Kenkeni** (also kensedeni) -- the smallest and highest-pitched, typically playing a steady ostinato

In a full traditional ensemble, one or two accompanying djembefolas play fixed supporting patterns while the lead djembefola improvises, and the three dunun players provide the rhythmic architecture over which the djembe voices soar and converse.

#### Key Rhythm Families

Mandinka/Malinke djembe rhythms are organized into families associated with specific social occasions:

- **Kuku**: Originally a rhythm of the forest-dwelling peoples adopted by the Malinke, played for celebrations and at full-moon festivities. It features a driving, joyous energy and has become one of the most widely taught rhythms in the global djembe community.
- **Soli**: A family of rhythms associated with male circumcision/initiation ceremonies. These are among the most sacred rhythms, traditionally played only during the weeks of the initiation process, and they carry enormous spiritual weight.
- **Dununba** (the rhythm, distinct from the drum): A powerful, virtuosic piece associated with strong men's dances and displays of physical prowess. The word means "the dance of the strong men."
- **Kassa**: Agricultural rhythms associated with the communal work of the granary. Different Kassa rhythms accompany different phases of the agricultural cycle.
- **Mendiani**: Rhythms played for young female dancers, associated with beauty and grace.

### 2.3 Yoruba (Nigeria) -- Bata Drums and the Sacred Connection

The Yoruba people of southwestern Nigeria and neighboring regions of Benin and Togo have developed one of the most elaborate and spiritually charged musical systems in the world. Yoruba music is inseparable from Yoruba religion -- the complex system of *orisha* (deities) worship that has survived colonization, the transatlantic slave trade, and modernity, and has given rise to major diasporic religions across the Americas.

#### Bata Drums and Orisha Worship

The *bata* is a set of three double-headed, hourglass-shaped drums of graduated size:

- **Iya ilu** ("mother drum") -- the largest, played by the master drummer, capable of the widest pitch range and the most complex patterns
- **Itotele** -- the middle drum, which responds to and converses with the iya ilu
- **Okonkolo** (or omele ako) -- the smallest, playing a steady foundational pattern

Yoruba oral tradition credits the creation of the bata drums to the *orisha Eshu* (the divine trickster, guardian of crossroads and communication), in whose worship the ensemble was originally employed. Eshu subsequently gave permission for his drums to be used by the worshippers of *Shango* (Sango), the orisha of thunder, lightning, fire, drumming, and justice -- historically a deified king of the fifteenth-century Oyo Empire. Shango remains the orisha most intimately associated with the bata, and the most frequently stated primary purpose of the sacred bata is the glorification of Shango.

Each orisha has its own repertoire of rhythms, songs, and dances, and the bata ensemble plays a crucial role in the *wemilere* (drum ceremony) in which these rhythms are performed to invoke the presence and blessings of the orisha. The rhythms are not merely musical -- they are liturgical. Specific patterns are believed to contain the spiritual force (*ashe*) necessary to summon the orisha, and the transition from one rhythm to another during a ceremony follows a prescribed liturgical order. The master bata drummer must know the full repertoire of rhythms for all orisha, understand the spiritual protocols governing their performance, and be sensitive to the spiritual dynamics of the ceremony in real time.

#### The Dundun (Talking Drum) Tradition

Alongside the bata, the Yoruba *dundun* ensemble represents the other great pillar of Yoruba drumming. The dundun is the quintessential "talking drum" -- an hourglass-shaped pressure drum whose pitch can be continuously varied to reproduce the tonal patterns of the Yoruba language (a tonal language with three basic tones: high, mid, and low, plus gliding tones).

The *gangan* (the lead drum of the dundun ensemble, also called *iya ilu dundun*) is played by a master drummer who can reproduce Yoruba speech, proverbs, praise poetry, and historical narratives through the drum's tonal inflections. This is not a code or a simplified signaling system -- it is a genuine form of speech surrogacy, exploiting the fact that in tonal languages, the melodic contour of speech carries lexical and grammatical meaning.

### 2.4 Ashanti (Ghana) -- Fontomfrom and Royal Court Music

The Ashanti (Asante) people of central Ghana built one of the most powerful pre-colonial states in West Africa -- the Ashanti Empire, centered on the Golden Stool at Kumasi. Music, and drumming in particular, was integral to the exercise and display of royal power.

#### Fontomfrom

The *Fontomfrom* is the largest and most prestigious of the Ashanti drums -- a massive barrel drum with an open bottom that produces a deep, resonating tone capable of projecting across great distances. The Fontomfrom ensemble provides music for the most important state ceremonies: the enstoolment of chiefs, royal processions, funerals of important persons, and the great *Adae* festivals that punctuate the Ashanti ceremonial calendar.

Fontomfrom drumming is considered the most complex of all Akan musical types. It encompasses a series of warrior dances performed in religious, ceremonial, and social contexts at the courts of chiefs. The drums "speak" in a manner analogous to the Yoruba dundun, reproducing the tonal patterns of the Twi language to recite honorific titles, historical narratives, and proverbial wisdom.

The *Kete* ensemble is another important Ashanti royal court tradition -- a dance-drumming form that is the exclusive prerogative of Ashanti royalty. Kete performances are reserved for the court and for occasions honoring the paramount chief (Asantehene). The ensemble includes drums, a flute (*atenteben*), and vocals, and its repertoire is closely guarded as royal cultural property.

#### Adinkra and Musical Symbolism

The Ashanti *Adinkra* symbol system -- a collection of visual symbols, each encoding a proverb, concept, or historical reference -- represents a parallel symbolic tradition to the musical one. While Adinkra symbols are visual rather than sonic, they share with Ashanti drumming a fundamental characteristic: the encoding of complex philosophical, moral, and historical knowledge in compact, stylized forms. Both traditions demonstrate the Ashanti genius for creating systems of symbolic communication that preserve and transmit cultural values across generations. Symbols such as *Sankofa* ("go back and get it" -- the importance of learning from the past) and *Gye Nyame* ("except for God" -- the supremacy of the divine) find resonance in the thematic content of the praise poetry recited through Fontomfrom drums.

### 2.5 Wolof (Senegal) -- Sabar Drumming, Mbalax, and the Tassou

The Wolof people of Senegal and The Gambia have created a percussion tradition centered on the *sabar* -- a set of carved wooden drums (typically made from dimb or mango wood) covered with goatskin and played with one bare hand and one thin stick.

#### Sabar Ensemble

The sabar ensemble consists of up to seven differently tuned drums, each with a specific name and role:

- **Nder** -- the lead drum, played by the master drummer
- **Mbung mbung** -- bass drums providing the rhythmic foundation
- **Talmbat**, **cul**, and others -- supporting drums that fill out the ensemble texture

Wolof musicians were traditionally drawn from the *gewel* (griot) caste, who served as praise singers, historians, and counselors to noble families. The gewel were also masters of the *tama* (a small talking drum held under the arm, cognate with the Yoruba dundun and Dagomba lunga), which plays an important role in Wolof musical culture alongside the sabar.

#### Tassou (Praise Singing/Chanting)

*Tassou* (also *tassu*) is a vocal tradition of rhythmic praise-chanting, historically performed by women (often griotte, or female griots) at social gatherings, wrestling matches, and ceremonies. The tassou performer delivers rapid-fire rhythmic speech -- praising individuals, recounting genealogies, or offering social commentary -- in tight synchronization with the sabar drums. The tradition is remarkable for its rhythmic precision, wit, and improvisatory boldness, and it represents one of the most direct antecedents of rap and spoken-word poetry in the global context.

#### Mbalax

In the 1970s, Senegalese musicians began fusing indigenous sabar drumming, tassou vocal techniques, and Wolof musical aesthetics with Cuban son, Afro-Cuban jazz, funk, and Western pop instrumentation. The result was *mbalax* -- a genre that became Senegal's dominant popular music form and one of the most distinctive sounds in global popular music.

The great popularizer of mbalax was Youssou N'Dour, whose band Le Super Etoile de Dakar integrated sabar drumming and tassou into a modern band format with electric guitars, keyboards, horns, and studio production. N'Dour's work demonstrated that West African polyrhythmic traditions could not only survive in a modern pop context but could fundamentally reshape that context, creating a form of popular music that was unmistakably rooted in Wolof culture while engaging with global audiences.

### 2.6 Dagomba (Ghana) -- Lunsi Drummers and Chieftaincy

The Dagomba (Dagbamba) people of northern Ghana's Northern Region have a drumming tradition that is intimately linked to their system of chieftaincy and governance. The *lunsi* (drummers) are hereditary custodians of Dagomba history, royal genealogy, and praise traditions.

#### Origins of the Lunsi

According to Dagomba oral tradition, the first drummer was *Bizung*, a son of the legendary ruler *Naa Nyagsi*. When offered the paramount chieftaincy, Bizung declined, asking only that he be allowed to play his music in peace. Because of this founding narrative, the lunsi consider themselves royalty and maintain a hierarchical system of chieftaincy that parallels the political one. Every chief in Dagbon has his own set of drummers, and the relationship between chief and drummer is one of mutual dependence: the chief provides patronage and protection, while the drummer provides historical legitimation, praise, counsel, and the musical accompaniment essential to the exercise of chiefly authority.

#### Drums and Performance

The lunsi play two types of drums:

- **Lunga** -- an hourglass-shaped pressure drum (cognate with the talking drums found across the region), held under the arm and played with a curved stick. The lunga can reproduce the tonal patterns of the Dagbani language, serving as a form of speech surrogacy.
- **Gung-gong** -- a large, single-headed drum played with sticks, providing a bass foundation.

The lunsi are, as a research article in *Cogent Arts and Humanities* (2023) describes them, "vessels of speech" who "not only transmit messages and entertain audiences but more importantly encapsulate the Dagomba culture and strive to preserve it." David Locke's extensive research on Dagomba dance drumming (documented through his Tufts University project) has provided detailed analysis of the musical structures and cultural contexts of traditions such as *Takai* and *Bamaya*.

*Bamaya* is a particularly significant Dagomba dance-drumming tradition -- a graceful, undulating dance originally associated with a historical episode of drought and the community's supplication for rain. It has become one of the most widely performed Dagomba traditions and exemplifies the way in which historical memory, spiritual practice, and aesthetic expression converge in West African drum-dance forms.

## 3. The Diaspora -- How West African Rhythm Shaped the World

### 3.1 The Middle Passage -- Rhythmic Knowledge Carried in Bodies and Memories

Between the sixteenth and nineteenth centuries, an estimated twelve million Africans were forcibly transported across the Atlantic in the largest forced migration in human history. The majority of those enslaved came from West and West-Central Africa -- precisely the regions whose rhythmic traditions we have been examining.

The enslaved were stripped of nearly everything: their names, their languages, their families, their freedom, their material possessions. But there were forms of knowledge that could not be confiscated -- those encoded in the body, in memory, in the habits of breath and movement. Rhythmic knowledge was chief among these. The patterns of polyrhythmic organization, the feel of specific bell patterns, the relationship between rhythm and dance, the call-and-response structures, the practice of collective music-making as communal bonding -- all of these traveled across the Atlantic in the bodies and memories of the enslaved.

This was not passive survival. It was an active, creative, and courageous process of cultural preservation under conditions of extreme violence and dehumanization. In the holds of slave ships, in the barracoons of the coast, on the plantations of the Americas, enslaved Africans maintained, adapted, transformed, and transmitted their musical knowledge -- creating, in the process, entirely new musical traditions that would eventually reshape the sound of the entire world.

### 3.2 Drum Bans in the Americas -- Fear of Communication and Revolt

European colonizers recognized the communicative power of African drumming and responded with fear and prohibition. The drums were not merely instruments of music -- they were instruments of organization, communication, and potentially of rebellion. The colonizers understood this, even if imperfectly.

The **Stono Rebellion** of 1739 in South Carolina -- the largest slave revolt in colonial America -- was a galvanizing event. The rebels marched south toward Spanish Florida, "waving a banner and beating a drum" to signal other enslaved people to join the revolt. In the aftermath, South Carolina enacted the **Negro Act of 1740**, which explicitly prohibited enslaved Africans from using drums, horns, and other loud instruments. Similar legislation followed in other colonies and territories across the Americas.

These drum bans had profound musical consequences. In the British colonies of North America, where the bans were most strictly enforced, enslaved Africans were compelled to find alternative means of rhythmic expression -- the human body itself became the instrument. Hand-clapping, foot-stomping, body percussion (*patting juba* or *hambone*), and the use of improvised instruments filled the void left by the prohibited drums. This forced innovation contributed to the development of distinctively African American rhythmic practices that would eventually feed into blues, jazz, gospel, rhythm and blues, rock and roll, funk, and hip-hop.

In the French and Spanish colonies -- particularly Louisiana, Cuba, Haiti, and Brazil -- the bans were less strictly enforced or applied differently. In these regions, African drumming traditions survived in more recognizable forms, often sheltered within religious contexts that the colonial authorities tolerated or could not fully suppress.

### 3.3 Survival and Transformation

#### Haiti: Vodou Drumming (Rada, Petro Rhythms)

Haiti represents perhaps the most direct and powerful survival of West African religious and musical traditions in the Americas. Haitian *Vodou* (from the Fon word *vodun*, meaning "spirit" or "deity") preserves a vast repertoire of sacred drumming tied to specific families (*nanchon*) of spirits (*lwa*).

The **Rada** family of rhythms is derived primarily from Fon (Dahomey, now Benin) traditions and is considered "cool" -- associated with benevolent, ancestral spirits. Typical Rada rhythms include *Yanvalou*, *Parigol*, *Mayi*, *Zepol*, and *Dawomen*, each associated with specific lwa and specific ritual functions. Rada ceremonies use a set of three drums called *manman*, *segond*, and *bula* (or *kata*), reflecting the three-drum ensemble structure found across West Africa.

The **Petro** (Petwo) family of rhythms originated in Haiti itself (with Central African and Creole influences) and is considered "hot" -- associated with fiery, warrior spirits like *Ezili Danto*, *Papa Simbi*, and *Gran Bwa*. Petro rhythms are sharper, more aggressive, and played on differently constructed drums. The Rada/Petro distinction is one of the organizing principles of Haitian Vodou and reflects the creative fusion of multiple African traditions in the crucible of the plantation system.

#### Cuba: Santeria/Lucumi Bata Drums, Rumba

Cuba received enormous numbers of enslaved Yoruba people (known in Cuba as *Lucumi*), and their religious and musical traditions survived with remarkable fidelity. *Santeria* (also called *Regla de Ocha* or *Lucumi*) preserves the Yoruba orisha system virtually intact, and the sacred *bata* drums -- the *iya*, *itotele*, and *okonkolo* -- continue to be played in Cuban ceremonies using rhythmic patterns directly descended from those of Yorubaland.

The *toque de santo* (saint's drum ceremony) is the central ritual occasion for bata drumming in Cuba, and the repertoire of rhythms for each orisha closely parallels the Yoruba originals, even as centuries of Cuban cultural context have added new layers of meaning and variation.

*Rumba* -- a secular tradition of drumming, singing, and dancing that emerged in the Black neighborhoods of Havana and Matanzas in the mid-to-late nineteenth century -- represents a more creolized expression of African rhythmic principles. The three main forms of rumba (*yambu*, *guaguanco*, and *columbia*) all feature polyrhythmic structures, call-and-response vocals, and a dynamic relationship between drumming and dance that clearly echo West African antecedents.

#### Brazil: Candomble, Samba, Capoeira

Brazil received more enslaved Africans than any other country in the Americas, and the cultural legacy is proportionally vast. *Candomble* -- an Afro-Brazilian religion centered in Bahia -- preserves Yoruba (*Nago*), Fon (*Jeje*), and Bantu (*Angola*) religious traditions, each with its own drumming repertoire and ensemble. The sacred drums of Candomble (called *atabaques*: *rum*, *rumpi*, and *le*) parallel the three-drum structure of both the Yoruba bata and the Haitian Rada ensemble.

*Samba* -- which emerged from the Afro-Brazilian communities of Rio de Janeiro and Bahia in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries -- carries West African polyrhythmic DNA in its layered percussion, its call-and-response vocal structures, and its intimate relationship between drumming and dance. The *samba de roda* of the Bahian Reconcavo (recognized by UNESCO as Intangible Cultural Heritage) is among the forms most closely connected to African antecedents.

*Capoeira* -- the Afro-Brazilian martial art/dance/game -- is accompanied by a musical ensemble (*bateria*) led by the *berimbau*, a single-stringed musical bow with probable Angolan and Mozambican antecedents. While the berimbau's specific introduction to capoeira appears to be a relatively recent development (early twentieth century), the practice itself traces to the *engolo* combat-dance of Angola, and its rhythmic foundation (*toques* played on the berimbau dictate the style and speed of the game) reflects the West and Central African principle that rhythm governs movement.

#### New Orleans: Congo Square, Second Line, Jazz

New Orleans occupies a unique position in the story of African rhythm in the Americas. Under French and Spanish colonial rule, enslaved Africans in Louisiana were permitted to gather on Sundays in a public space that became known as **Congo Square** (now part of Louis Armstrong Park in the Treme neighborhood). Here, from at least the 1740s through the 1850s, enslaved people performed African dances, played African drums, sang African songs, and maintained African musical practices in a way that was virtually unprecedented in North America.

The performances at Congo Square were derived from West African festival culture and proved formative to Black New Orleans culture through their blending of sacred and secular, their incorporation of African dance, dress, song, music, and instrumentation, and their frequent use of processions and masquerades. The *bamboula* rhythm -- a pattern analogous to the Afro-Cuban clave -- was one of the signature rhythms of Congo Square, and its structural principles can be traced directly into the brass band tradition, the **second line** parade, the music of the **Mardi Gras Indians**, and ultimately into **jazz** itself.

The second line -- the tradition of community members dancing behind brass bands in funeral processions and celebratory parades -- is arguably the most visible surviving expression of West African communal musical practice in the United States. The rhythmic foundation of second line drumming, with its syncopated bass drum patterns and interlocking snare and cymbal work, carries the imprint of the polyrhythmic principles that were maintained and transformed through Congo Square and the mutual aid societies of nineteenth-century Black New Orleans.

#### Trinidad: Calypso and Steelpan

Trinidad's *calypso* tradition traces directly to the West African griot -- the praise singer, social commentator, and keeper of history. As the tradition adapted to the Caribbean context, the griot role evolved into the *chantuelle* and eventually the *calypsonian* -- a figure who, like the griot, uses wit, wordplay, and musical virtuosity to comment on social and political affairs. The rhythmic foundations of calypso reflect West African syncopation and call-and-response patterns, filtered through the experience of Carnival and the cultural encounters of Trinidad's extraordinarily diverse population.

The *steelpan* -- the only acoustic instrument invented in the twentieth century -- emerged from the same crucible of creativity and prohibition. After British colonial authorities banned drumming (particularly the African-derived *tamboo bamboo* tradition), Trinidadian musicians discovered that discarded oil drums could be tuned to produce melodic and harmonic tones. The steelpan is thus, in a profound sense, a monument to the irrepressibility of African rhythmic culture: when drums were banned, Trinidadians invented an entirely new instrument.

### 3.4 The Tresillo as the "DNA of the New World"

If there is a single rhythmic pattern that can be called the genetic code of New World music, it is the *tresillo*: an asymmetric grouping of eight pulses into **3+3+2**. This pattern -- sounded as "ONE two three, ONE two three, ONE two" -- is the most fundamental duple-pulse rhythmic cell in sub-Saharan African music traditions and was carried to the Americas through the slave trade.

The tresillo is not merely one pattern among many. It is the structural seed from which an extraordinary range of New World musical forms has grown:

- The **habanera** (the first Cuban dance music to achieve global popularity, in the mid-nineteenth century) is rhythmically built on the tresillo and its variants
- The **tango** of Argentina received the habanera's rhythmic influence via the port of Buenos Aires
- **Ragtime** (particularly the "Spanish tinge" that Jelly Roll Morton identified as essential to jazz) incorporates tresillo-derived syncopation
- The **Bo Diddley beat** in rock and roll is a direct tresillo/clave pattern
- **Reggaeton** is built on a 3+3+2 foundation (the *dembow* riddim)
- Countless **pop, R&B, hip-hop, and electronic dance music** tracks worldwide employ tresillo-derived rhythmic patterns

The tresillo's power lies in its asymmetry. In a measure of eight pulses, the pattern 3+3+2 creates a sense of forward motion and rhythmic tension that is fundamentally different from the symmetrical 4+4 or 2+2+2+2 divisions of European-derived music. This asymmetric drive -- the sense that the rhythm is always slightly "leaning forward" -- is the quality that musicians and listeners around the world have found irresistible for centuries, even when they cannot name its source.

### 3.5 The Clave Concept -- From West African Timeline to Global Foundation

The *clave* (Spanish for "key" or "keystone") is the organizing rhythmic principle of Afro-Cuban music, and its conceptual origins lie directly in the West African timeline pattern. The son clave -- typically notated as a five-stroke pattern spanning two measures -- is a direct descendant of the asymmetric bell patterns played by the *gankogui* in Ewe music, the *agogo* in Yoruba music, and similar instruments across the region.

The clave functions in Afro-Cuban music exactly as the bell pattern functions in West African ensemble music: it is the rhythmic key that orients all other parts, the organizing principle against which melodies, harmonies, and improvisations are positioned. To play "in clave" is to be rhythmically aligned with this organizing pattern; to play "against clave" is the cardinal sin of Afro-Cuban musicianship.

The journey of the clave concept through global popular music is one of the great stories of cultural transmission:

1. **West African bell patterns** cross the Atlantic with enslaved Africans
2. In **Cuba**, these patterns crystallize into the clave concept within son, rumba, and other Afro-Cuban forms
3. **Afro-Cuban jazz** (Dizzy Gillespie, Chano Pozo, Machito) brings clave-based rhythms into the American jazz mainstream in the 1940s
4. **Bo Diddley** (1955) plays the son clave as a rock-and-roll guitar riff, creating one of the most influential beats in popular music history
5. The **New Orleans** R&B tradition (Fats Domino, Professor Longhair, Dr. John) maintains clave-related patterns through its deep connection to Afro-Caribbean rhythm
6. **Funk** (James Brown, Parliament-Funkadelic) builds on clave-influenced syncopation
7. **Hip-hop** inherits these rhythmic principles through sampling and through the direct influence of funk and soul
8. **Reggaeton**, **Afrobeats**, and contemporary global pop continue to build on clave-derived rhythmic foundations

The clave is thus a direct, traceable line of rhythmic transmission from pre-colonial West Africa to the global pop charts of the twenty-first century.

## 4. Modern Reflections

### 4.1 Fela Kuti and Afrobeat -- Political Resistance Through Rhythm

*Afrobeat* -- not to be confused with the contemporary genre "Afrobeats" -- was forged in Lagos, Nigeria, in the late 1960s and early 1970s by the bandleader, saxophonist, singer, and political activist **Fela Anikulapo Kuti** and the drummer **Tony Allen**. It remains one of the most powerful examples of how West African rhythmic traditions can be deliberately mobilized as instruments of political resistance.

Fela drew on Yoruba musical traditions, highlife, jazz, and the politically charged funk and soul he encountered during a transformative 1969 visit to the United States (where he was influenced by the Black Power movement through Sandra Isadore). But the rhythmic foundation of Afrobeat was Tony Allen's revolutionary drum kit approach.

Allen treated the drum kit not as a single unified instrument (as in Western rock or pop) but as **a collection of independent voices** -- each limb playing its own polyrhythmic line, in a direct translation of the West African multi-drum ensemble principle to a single player at a kit. The hi-hat, snare, bass drum, and cymbals each carried an independent rhythmic role, creating the impression of multiple drummers operating simultaneously. This was part Yoruba polyrhythm, part highlife swing, part calypso lilt, and part jazz sophistication -- a genuinely new rhythmic language that was nevertheless deeply rooted in West African principles.

Fela used this musical foundation as a vehicle for searing political commentary. Songs like *Zombie* (1976) -- which compared the Nigerian military to mindless zombies -- were not merely protest songs but acts of extraordinary courage. The consequences were severe: in 1977, a thousand soldiers attacked Fela's communal compound (the Kalakuta Republic), beating him nearly to death and throwing his elderly mother from a second-floor window -- injuries from which she later died. Yet Fela continued to perform, record, and agitate until his death from AIDS-related illness in 1997.

Tony Allen went on to become one of the most sought-after drummers in world music, collaborating with Damon Albarn, Charlotte Gainsbourg, and many others before his death in 2020. Brian Eno famously called him "perhaps the greatest drummer who has ever lived."

### 4.2 Highlife -- Ghana's Golden Age

**Highlife** emerged in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries along the coast of Ghana (then the Gold Coast) from a fusion of local Akan and Ga rhythmic traditions with European brass band music, sea shanties, church hymns, and eventually Afro-Caribbean influences. The name "highlife" reportedly derives from the fact that the dance venues where this music was played charged admission fees that only the African elite could afford -- hence it was the music of the "high life."

The genre developed in two main streams:

- **Dance band highlife**: Featuring brass instruments, saxophones, and Western-style orchestration, epitomized by **E.T. Mensah** and the Tempos. Mensah (1919-1996), known as the "King of Highlife," refined the genre into a sophisticated, danceable form that drew international attention. His fame soared after performing with Louis Armstrong during the jazz legend's visit to Accra in 1956. The Tempos' sound -- smooth horn arrangements, jazzy solos, and infectious dance rhythms -- became the soundtrack of Ghana's independence era.

- **Guitar band highlife**: Featuring acoustic and later electric guitars, with singing, hand drums, and claves. **E.K. Nyame** and his Akan Trio were pioneers of this stream, releasing over 400 records and bringing highlife to rural audiences across Ghana.

The period from Ghana's independence in 1957 through the 1970s is fondly recalled as the **Golden Age of Highlife**. The music was inseparable from the optimism and pride of independence: it was played at state events, nightclubs, and weddings, and it symbolized a modern African identity that was cosmopolitan yet culturally rooted. Other important figures include **Jerry Hansen** (and his Ramblers International), **Nana Ampadu** (and the African Brothers Band), and **Osibisa** (who brought highlife-influenced Afro-rock to international audiences).

### 4.3 Juju Music -- King Sunny Ade and the Talking Drum in Popular Music

*Juju* is a genre of Yoruba popular music that originated in Lagos in the 1920s with early practitioners like **Tunde King** and was developed through the mid-twentieth century by figures like **I.K. Dairo** (who introduced the talking drum and accordion to the juju ensemble). But it was **King Sunny Ade** (born Sunday Adeniyi Adegeye, 1946) who elevated juju to international recognition.

Ade's music is characterized by the central role of the **dundun** (talking drum), used not merely as a percussion instrument but as a melodic and conversational voice within the ensemble. His large bands (sometimes numbering twenty or more musicians) created shimmering polyrhythmic textures featuring multiple guitars, keyboards, and a dense percussion section centered on the talking drum. His albums *Juju Music* (1982) and *Synchro System* (1983), released on Island Records, introduced global audiences to the talking drum's extraordinary expressive range and earned him a Grammy nomination -- the first for a Nigerian artist.

Juju represents one of the most successful integrations of a deeply traditional instrument (the dundun) into a modern popular music context, demonstrating that traditional West African instruments and musical principles can not only survive modernity but can become its leading voices.

### 4.4 Modern Afrobeats -- Traditional Rhythmic DNA in Global Pop

Contemporary **Afrobeats** (distinct from Fela's Afrobeat) is a broad umbrella term for the popular music emerging from Nigeria, Ghana, and across West Africa since the 2000s, blending elements of highlife, juju, hip-hop, dancehall, R&B, and electronic dance music. The genre's leading figures -- **Burna Boy** (Damini Ogulu), **Wizkid** (Ayodeji Balogun), **Davido** (David Adeleke), **Tems**, **Asake**, **Rema**, and many others -- have achieved global chart success while maintaining unmistakable connections to West African rhythmic traditions.

The rhythmic DNA of Afrobeats is evident in several key features:

- **The primacy of rhythm**: In Afrobeats, the beat is not background -- it is a lead character, often of equal or greater importance than the melody or lyrics. This reflects the West African principle that rhythm is the primary organizing force in music.
- **Polyrhythmic layering**: Even in produced, electronic contexts, Afrobeats tracks typically feature multiple interlocking rhythmic patterns that create a sense of depth and movement characteristic of live West African drumming.
- **The body-rhythm connection**: Afrobeats is fundamentally dance music, and its rhythmic structures are designed to move bodies. This reflects the inseparable relationship between rhythm and dance in West African tradition.

Burna Boy's Grammy-winning album *Twice as Tall* (2020) and Wizkid's collaboration with Beyonce ("Brown Skin Girl") represent milestones in Afrobeats' global ascendancy, demonstrating that West African rhythmic principles can command the center of global popular culture.

### 4.5 Amapiano -- South African Roots, Pan-African Rhythmic Dialogue

**Amapiano** -- a genre that emerged from the townships of Gauteng, South Africa (particularly Pretoria and Johannesburg) in the mid-2010s -- is primarily a South African phenomenon, blending kwaito, deep house, jazz, and lounge music. However, its connection to broader African rhythmic traditions is evident in one of its most distinctive features: the **log drum** bass line.

The electronic log drum sound that defines amapiano's low end is derived from (and named after) the traditional West African log drum -- a hollowed wooden instrument struck with mallets. The log drum sound was introduced to South African popular music by kwaito pioneer **M'du Masilela** and became central to amapiano through producers like **MDU aka TRP** and **Kabza De Small**.

Amapiano's global explosion in the early 2020s and its subsequent fusion with Afrobeats (in what some call "Afropiano") demonstrates the ongoing dialogue between West African and Southern African musical traditions -- a pan-African rhythmic conversation that predates colonialism and continues to generate new forms.

### 4.6 The Global Djembe Movement -- Cultural Exchange vs. Cultural Appropriation

The djembe's journey from West African village squares to community centers, music schools, and festival stages worldwide is one of the most significant examples of cross-cultural musical transmission in the modern era. This global spread began in earnest with **Fodeba Keita's** founding of Les Ballets Africains in 1952 and accelerated through the work of master djembefolas like Mamady Keita, Famoudou Konate, and Babatunde Olatunji (whose 1960 album *Drums of Passion* was a landmark in introducing African drumming to American audiences).

This global spread raises important questions about the difference between cultural exchange and cultural appropriation:

**Concerns about appropriation**: The prominence of the djembe in Western "drum circle" culture has been criticized as involving "the partial appropriation and transformation" of African-based drumming -- stripping rhythms of their cultural context, spiritual significance, and historical depth, and often allowing non-African practitioners to profit from traditions developed by African communities over centuries. The simplification of complex traditional rhythms into generic "world music" or "wellness" experiences can erase the specificity, difficulty, and meaning of the original traditions.

**Arguments for exchange**: Others point to the djembe's ability to bring people together across cultural boundaries and note that many of the master djembefolas who brought the instrument to global attention did so deliberately, viewing the teaching of djembe as a form of cultural ambassadorship. Mamady Keita explicitly described his mission as preserving the tradition while sharing it with the world.

**A principled middle path**: Many practitioners and scholars advocate for an approach that combines genuine respect for tradition (studying with recognized masters, understanding cultural contexts, acknowledging sources) with openness to new creative expression. This is the approach BeatForge aspires to embody: celebrating these traditions with full acknowledgment of their origins, depth, and continuing vitality.

### 4.7 UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage

Several West African musical traditions have been recognized under UNESCO's 2003 Convention for the Safeguarding of the Intangible Cultural Heritage. These designations include:

- **Samba de roda of the Reconcavo of Bahia** (Brazil, 2005/2008) -- directly descended from West African traditions brought by enslaved people
- **The cultural space of Sosso-Bala** (Guinea, 2001/2008) -- the sacred balafon of the Sosso/Susu people, linked to the Sunjata epic
- **Congolese rumba** (Democratic Republic of Congo / Republic of Congo, 2021) -- rooted in African drumming traditions
- **Sanke mon, collective fishing rite of the Sanke** (Mali, 2009) -- including traditional drumming and masked dance
- Various other traditions across the continent that include musical components

These designations represent important international recognition of the value and vulnerability of these traditions, though they are not without critique -- some scholars and practitioners argue that the UNESCO framework risks freezing living traditions into museum pieces rather than supporting their ongoing, dynamic evolution.

## 5. Sociopolitical Context

### 5.1 Music and Resistance -- Drumming During Independence Movements

Music played a crucial role in the independence movements that swept across West Africa in the 1950s and 1960s. Drumming traditions that had been suppressed or marginalized under colonial rule were reclaimed as expressions of national pride and cultural sovereignty.

In **Ghana**, Kwame Nkrumah's independence movement embraced highlife as a soundtrack of liberation. Kpanlogo drumming, which emerged during this same period, embodied the youthful energy and optimism of the independence era. The reclamation of traditional drumming was itself a political act -- an assertion that African cultural practices, which colonial powers had denigrated as "primitive," were in fact sophisticated, valuable, and worthy of national pride.

In **Guinea**, Sekou Toure's post-independence government established **Les Ballets Africains** and other national cultural ensembles specifically to showcase and preserve traditional music and dance. This state patronage of traditional drumming was both a genuine preservation effort and a political strategy, using cultural heritage to build national identity in a newly independent state.

Across the region, songs of independence circulated -- from the Congolese rumba anthem "Independance Cha Cha" to highlife songs celebrating African self-determination -- carrying the rhythmic signatures of their respective cultures into the political sphere.

In the post-independence period, musicians continued to use rhythm as a vehicle for political commentary and resistance. Fela Kuti's Afrobeat is the most prominent example, but the tradition extends to Thomas Mapfumo's *chimurenga* music in Zimbabwe, Miriam Makeba's anti-apartheid activism, and countless others across the continent who have used musical tradition as a platform for political expression.

### 5.2 Music and Identity -- How Rhythmic Traditions Define Ethnic and National Identity

In West Africa, rhythmic traditions serve as powerful markers of ethnic and national identity. To hear a particular drum ensemble is to immediately know which people, which region, and often which specific community is being represented. The Ewe hear Agbekor and know their warrior ancestors; the Dagomba hear the lunsi and know their chieftaincy lineage; the Wolof hear the sabar and know the pulse of their culture.

This function of music as identity marker operates at multiple levels:

- **Ethnic identity**: Each ethnic group maintains distinct rhythmic traditions, drum types, ensemble structures, and repertoires. These differences are not superficial -- they reflect deep histories, distinct social structures, and unique aesthetic philosophies.
- **National identity**: Post-independence states have used music to forge national identities, sometimes celebrating the diversity of their constituent traditions (as in Ghana's embrace of both Ewe and Ashanti drumming) and sometimes privileging certain traditions over others.
- **Diasporic identity**: In the Americas, African-derived rhythmic traditions serve as powerful markers of diasporic identity -- connecting communities of African descent to their ancestral homelands across centuries and oceans.
- **Pan-African identity**: Events like PANAFEST (Pan-African Festival) in Ghana, which brings together Africans from the continent and the diaspora, use music and drumming as a medium for building pan-African solidarity and cultural connection.

### 5.3 The Economics of Music -- Griots, Patronage, and the Modern Industry

The economics of West African music have undergone dramatic transformations, from pre-colonial patronage systems to the globalized music industry of the twenty-first century.

**Traditional patronage**: In pre-colonial and early colonial West Africa, musicians (particularly griots) were supported through patronage systems. Noble families retained griots who served as their praise singers, historians, and counselors. In return, griots received food, clothing, housing, and gifts. This system was embedded in the broader caste structure of West African societies, where musicians occupied a specific social position -- respected for their knowledge and skill, but also bounded by caste restrictions on marriage and social mobility.

**Colonial disruption**: European colonization disrupted these patronage systems by undermining the authority of traditional rulers, imposing new economic structures, and introducing Western cultural values that often devalued indigenous music.

**Post-independence**: In the post-independence era, state patronage (through national ensembles, cultural ministries, and broadcasting corporations) partially replaced traditional patronage. Commercial recording and live performance created new economic models for musicians, though these were often exploitative, with record labels and promoters extracting the bulk of the profits.

**The digital age**: The internet, streaming platforms, and social media have created new opportunities and challenges. Nigerian Afrobeats artists like Wizkid and Burna Boy have leveraged digital distribution to build global careers. At the same time, traditional musicians in rural areas often remain economically marginalized, their knowledge and skill undervalued in a market that rewards novelty and celebrity.

### 5.4 Gender in West African Music -- Women Drummers and Changing Traditions

In many West African societies, drumming has been traditionally restricted to men -- a restriction rooted in a complex mix of spiritual beliefs, caste regulations, and patriarchal social norms. Among the Yoruba, spiritual taboos historically excluded women from playing the bata and dundun. In Mande societies, the djembe was traditionally played only by men. These restrictions were not merely customary -- they were often understood to have spiritual foundations, with violations believed to carry supernatural consequences.

However, these traditions are not static, and the twenty-first century has seen significant changes:

- **Ara Thunder** (Nigeria) has mastered the Yoruba dundun (talking drum), directly challenging the spiritual taboos that historically barred women from the instrument. Her work disrupts, as scholars have noted, "the Yoruba cultural belief systems that have historically excluded women from drumming due to spiritual taboos and patriarchal ideologies."
- **Nimbaya** -- an all-female djembe ensemble from Guinea -- has demonstrated that women can perform at the highest levels of djembe artistry, challenging the traditional male monopoly on the instrument.
- **DouDou N'Diaye Rose's** daughters in Senegal learned sabar drumming from their father, bypassing the tradition that restricted instruction to male students.
- The ensemble **Amazones** (from Guinea) brought female percussion to international stages.

These changes reflect broader social transformations across West Africa and the world, and they demonstrate that musical traditions, while deeply rooted, are also dynamic and responsive to changing social realities.

### 5.5 Cultural Preservation -- Documentation, Education, and Festivals

The preservation of West African rhythmic traditions in the face of globalization, urbanization, and generational change is an ongoing concern addressed through multiple strategies:

**Documentation and scholarship**: Ethnomusicologists -- both African and Western -- have produced extensive documentation of West African rhythmic traditions. Key scholarly works include J.H. Kwabena Nketia's *The Music of Africa* (1974), John Miller Chernoff's *African Rhythm and African Sensibility* (1979), David Locke's numerous studies of Ewe and Dagomba drumming, and the recordings and analyses of scholars like Gerhard Kubik, Simha Arom, and many others.

**Education programs**: Universities across West Africa and worldwide now offer programs in African music. Master drummers like Mamady Keita, Famoudou Konate, and C.K. Ladzekpo have taught at major Western universities (Keita through his Tam Tam Mandingue network, Ladzekpo at UCLA and Berkeley), creating formal pedagogical frameworks for traditions that were historically transmitted through apprenticeship.

**Festivals**: Major festivals serve as vital platforms for performance, transmission, and celebration of traditional music:
- **PANAFEST** (Pan-African Historical Theatre Festival) in Cape Coast, Ghana, brings together Africans from the continent and the diaspora for cultural exchange.
- **Chale Wote Street Art Festival** in Accra, founded in 2011, has grown into West Africa's most celebrated public arts festival, integrating traditional music with contemporary art, performance, and community engagement.
- **Festival sur le Niger** in Segou, Mali, showcases music from across the Sahel and beyond.
- **FESPACO** in Ouagadougou, Burkina Faso, while primarily a film festival, also includes significant musical programming.

## 6. Musicological Concepts

### 6.1 Polymeter vs. Polyrhythm -- The Academic Debate

The terms *polymeter* and *polyrhythm* are often used interchangeably in casual discussion, but they describe different (though related) phenomena, and the distinction has been a source of significant academic debate:

- **Polyrhythm**: Multiple rhythmic patterns occurring simultaneously within a single metric framework. For example, a 3-against-2 pattern (three equally spaced attacks against two equally spaced attacks within the same time span) is a polyrhythm -- both patterns share the same metric cycle but divide it differently.
- **Polymeter**: Multiple meters occurring simultaneously -- that is, patterns that imply different bar lengths or different groupings of beats, creating a texture in which the "downbeats" of different parts do not regularly coincide.

The question of whether West African music is fundamentally polyrhythmic (multiple rhythms within a shared meter) or polymetric (multiple meters coexisting) has been debated for decades. **Simha Arom**, in his monumental *African Polyphony and Polyrhythm: Musical Structure and Methodology* (1985/1991), argued through rigorous field research among the Banda Linda and other Central African peoples that the music is fundamentally organized around shared periodic structures (i.e., polyrhythmic rather than polymetric). Arom's innovative methodology -- providing individual musicians with headphone playback of the full ensemble while recording their parts separately -- allowed unprecedented clarity in analyzing how the parts relate to each other and to the overall metric framework.

David Locke, through his extensive work on Ewe drumming, has argued that the concept of "cross-rhythm" (first articulated by A.M. Jones in his *Studies in African Music*, 1959) is the most useful framework: the simultaneous sounding of patterns that contradict each other's metric implications while remaining organized within a shared periodic cycle. C.K. Ladzekpo's teaching emphasizes that the Ewe conceptualize their rhythms not as "multiple meters" but as a single unified field of rhythmic interaction in which cross-rhythmic tension is a source of aesthetic pleasure and communal engagement.

### 6.2 The "In-Between" Feel -- Beyond Western 12/8 and 4/4

One of the most challenging aspects of West African rhythm for Western musicians and analysts is the existence of rhythmic feels that do not fit neatly into either the duple (4/4) or compound (12/8, 6/8) categories of Western music theory. Many West African rhythms exist in an ambiguous zone between these categories, or move fluidly between them within a single piece.

This "in-between" quality is not a deficiency or imprecision -- it is a fundamental aesthetic feature. The swing of Ewe drumming, the lilt of highlife, the push-and-pull of Afrobeat -- these qualities arise precisely from rhythmic practices that do not conform to the grid of Western notation. Western notation, with its assumption of isochronous (equally spaced) subdivisions organized into neat hierarchical groups, is simply inadequate to capture the full rhythmic reality of West African music.

Some scholars have proposed the concept of **nontemporal spacing** -- the idea that the micro-timing of West African music is not a deviation from an ideal grid but an intentional, culturally specific practice of placing sounds in ways that create particular expressive qualities. This aligns with the broader recognition that rhythmic feel is a form of cultural knowledge -- something learned through immersion in a musical community, not something that can be fully captured in notation or reproduced by a metronome.

### 6.3 Additive vs. Divisive Rhythm Systems

The distinction between **additive** and **divisive** rhythm has been a source of significant confusion in the ethnomusicological literature on African music:

- **Divisive rhythm**: A time span is divided into equal subdivisions, which are then grouped into regular, symmetrical units (e.g., 4/4 = 2+2+2+2 eighth notes). This is the dominant principle in Western art music.
- **Additive rhythm**: A time span is built up from unequal groupings (e.g., 3+3+2 = 8, or 2+3+2+2+3 = 12). The resulting patterns are asymmetric.

For decades, scholars described West African timeline patterns (like the tresillo, 3+3+2) as "additive" because of their irregular groupings of attack points. However, this characterization has been challenged. The ethnomusicologist and theorist Willie Anku and others have argued that African rhythmic structure is fundamentally **divisive** -- based on the simultaneous division of a shared time span by different factors (e.g., dividing 12 pulses simultaneously by 3 and by 4, producing the foundational 3:2 cross-rhythm). The asymmetric patterns that result are not "added up" from smaller units but emerge from the interaction of these divisive operations.

As scholars have noted: "There is no evidence that the musicians themselves think [of their rhythms] as additive." The asymmetric patterns that Western analysts perceived as "additive" are better understood as specific configurations within a fundamentally divisive framework -- configurations that are fixed by tradition and serve as the organizational backbone of the ensemble.

### 6.4 Participatory Discrepancies -- Charles Keil

In a landmark 1987 essay, "Participatory Discrepancies and the Power of Music" (published in *Cultural Anthropology*), the ethnomusicologist **Charles Keil** proposed a theory that directly challenges the Western classical assumption that musical power resides in the composer's written score. Keil argued that the emotional power and social efficacy of music lie in small, systematic deviations from "perfect" time and "perfect" pitch -- what he called **"participatory discrepancies" (PDs)**.

Keil identified two types of PDs:

- **Processual**: Discrepancies in timing -- musicians playing slightly ahead of or behind each other, creating a sense of tension, swing, or groove
- **Textural**: Discrepancies in timbre and tuning -- the buzzing of a snare drum, the rasp of a rattle, the slight "out-of-tuneness" of a chorus singing in unison

Keil argued that these discrepancies are not flaws but features -- they are what make music feel alive, embodied, and socially engaging. Music that is "perfectly" in time (like a mechanical click track) feels dead; music that has appropriate participatory discrepancies feels like it is alive, breathing, and inviting participation.

This theory has profound implications for the study (and digital reproduction) of West African rhythm. The complex "feel" of a live Ewe drum ensemble, a Mande djembe troupe, or a Yoruba bata ceremony arises in large part from the subtle, systematic, culturally specific ways in which the musicians' timing deviates from mathematical regularity. Any drum machine that seeks to honor these traditions must grapple with the fact that metronomic precision is, from this perspective, not fidelity but distortion.

Keil elaborated on his theory in *Music Grooves: Essays and Dialogues* (1994, co-authored with Steven Feld), and while the theory has generated significant debate (some scholars question whether the discrepancies are truly "participatory" or merely artifacts of human imprecision), it remains one of the most influential frameworks for understanding why live groove music feels different from quantized digital music.

### 6.5 The Timeline Pattern -- Kubik, Locke, and Pressing

The concept of the **timeline pattern** -- an asymmetric, cyclic rhythmic pattern that serves as the temporal reference for an entire ensemble -- is one of the most important contributions of African music scholarship to general musicology.

Key scholars and their contributions:

- **A.M. Jones** (*Studies in African Music*, 1959): The pioneering comparative study of African rhythm, which first identified cross-rhythm as a fundamental principle and documented numerous timeline patterns across the continent.

- **Gerhard Kubik** (1972, "Oral Notation of Some West and Central African Time-Line Patterns"; *Theory of African Music*, 1994, 2010): Kubik, an Austrian ethnomusicologist who conducted extensive fieldwork across sub-Saharan Africa, preferred the term "time-line pattern" and argued for understanding these patterns as integral structural elements rather than mere rhythmic ornaments. His work documented the remarkable consistency of certain timeline patterns across vast geographical distances, suggesting deep historical connections between widely separated musical cultures.

- **David Locke** (numerous publications on Ewe and Dagomba music, Tufts University): Locke's meticulous transcriptions and analyses have provided some of the most detailed accounts of how timeline patterns function within specific ensemble contexts. His concept of "simultaneous multidimensionality" captures the way in which each part in an Ewe ensemble can be understood in relation to multiple different metric references simultaneously.

- **Jeff Pressing** ("Cognitive Complexity and the Structure of Musical Patterns," 1999): Pressing brought cognitive science perspectives to the study of African rhythm, examining how the structural properties of timeline patterns relate to their perceptual complexity and memorability. His work on cognitive complexity in polyrhythmic performance (with Summers and Magill, *Journal of Experimental Psychology*, 1996) provided empirical evidence for the cognitive demands of polyrhythmic coordination.

The timeline pattern concept has proven to be one of the most analytically productive ideas in ethnomusicology, providing a framework for understanding not only African music but also the rhythmic structures of Afro-diasporic musics in the Caribbean, Latin America, and North America.

### 6.6 Cross-Cultural Rhythm Cognition

Recent research in music cognition has begun to explore how listeners from different cultural backgrounds perceive and process the same rhythmic patterns differently. This research has significant implications for understanding both the universality and cultural specificity of West African rhythmic traditions.

A study published in *Frontiers in Psychology* (2015) tested East African and North American adults on perception, production, and beat-tapping for rhythms derived from both East African and Western music. The results demonstrated that **culture significantly influences rhythm processing**: participants performed better with rhythms from their own musical culture, suggesting that rhythmic perception is shaped by enculturation -- the long-term exposure to the rhythmic patterns prevalent in one's cultural environment.

This finding has important implications: it means that a listener raised in a Western musical environment will literally **hear** a West African polyrhythmic texture differently from a listener raised within that tradition. The "downbeat," the sense of where the pattern "starts," the perception of which parts are "foreground" and which are "background" -- all of these perceptual judgments are culturally conditioned. The same objective sound pattern is experienced as different musical realities by listeners from different cultures.

This does not mean that cross-cultural musical understanding is impossible -- clearly, the global appeal of West African-derived rhythms demonstrates that these patterns speak across cultural boundaries. But it does mean that the full depth and subtlety of these traditions can only be appreciated through sustained, immersive engagement -- not through casual listening or superficial appropriation.

For a project like BeatForge, this research underscores the importance of providing not just the rhythmic patterns themselves but the cultural, historical, and musicological context that allows users to begin to hear these patterns as their creators intended them to be heard.
