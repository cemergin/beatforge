# Brazilian Music Traditions: Rhythm, Percussion, and the African Diaspora

## A Deep Musicological, Historical, Sociopolitical, and Cultural Research Report

---

**Scope:** This report is devoted exclusively to Brazil -- the single most rhythmically complex and diverse musical culture on Earth. No other nation houses as many distinct percussion traditions, as many layers of African-Indigenous-European syncretism, or as many living, evolving rhythmic lineages flowing directly from the holds of slave ships to the stages of the 21st century.

**Notation conventions used throughout this report:**

```
All patterns notated in a grid/box notation system:
X = strong hit / open tone
x = ghost note / soft hit
O = open tone (hand drums)
S = slap
B = bass tone
. = rest/silence
| = bar line
> = accent

Time reference: each character = one 16th note unless otherwise noted
One full bar of 4/4 = 16 characters
One full bar of 2/4 = 8 characters (standard samba feel)

For berimbau:
  D = din (pressed string, high)
  T = ton (open string, low)
  X = buzz/chh (muted)
  ~ = sustain
```

# PART I: THE AFRICAN ROOTS

## Chapter 1: The Largest Forced Migration in the Americas

### 1.1 The Scale of the Crime

Between 1501 and 1866, approximately **4.9 million enslaved Africans** were brought to Brazil -- more than ten times the number transported to North America (388,000) and roughly 46% of all Africans transported across the Atlantic. This single demographic fact explains why Brazil became the deepest reservoir of African musical tradition in the Western Hemisphere.

The enslaved peoples came primarily from three broad cultural-linguistic groups:

**Yoruba (Nagô in Brazil)**
- Origin: Present-day Nigeria and Benin
- Peak importation: Late 18th and early 19th centuries (especially to Bahia)
- Musical legacy: Candomblé Ketu, the most widespread form; the atabaque drum ensemble; complex polyrhythmic structures organized around specific toques (rhythmic patterns) for deities; the concept of rhythmic communication with the divine
- Key instruments: Three-drum atabaque ensemble, agogô (double bell), xequerê (beaded gourd)

**Fon (Jeje in Brazil)**
- Origin: Present-day Benin (ancient Dahomey)
- Musical legacy: Candomblé Jeje; the concept of vodun (spirits), which influenced Umbanda; distinctive drum tuning and construction techniques; the use of curved sticks on drums
- Key instruments: Rum, rumpi, lé (their names for the three drums, which became standard across all Candomblé); the gã (single bell)

**Bantu (Congo-Angola peoples)**
- Origin: Present-day Angola, Congo, Mozambique
- Musical legacy: The largest group numerically; contributed the berimbau (from the Angolan hungu/mbulumbumba), capoeira, samba de roda, the word "samba" itself (from the Kimbundu "semba" -- belly bump or invitation to dance), coco, jongo, congada, maracatu; pentatonic melodies; call-and-response singing
- Key instruments: Berimbau, atabaque, various friction drums (ancestor of the cuica), ngoma drums

### 1.2 Why Brazil Preserved African Rhythms More Intact

Several factors made Brazil uniquely hospitable to the survival of African musical traditions:

**1. Sheer numbers:** In many regions (Bahia, Pernambuco, Maranhao, Minas Gerais), enslaved Africans and their descendants outnumbered Europeans 10 to 1 or more. Critical mass allowed cultural transmission.

**2. Continuous importation:** Brazil was the last country in the Americas to abolish slavery (1888). Continuous arrival of new captives from Africa meant constant cultural refreshment. A Yoruba priestess arriving in Bahia in 1840 could reinforce traditions already three centuries old.

**3. Relative tolerance for drumming:** While English and French colonies (the US South, Haiti, Jamaica) often imposed strict bans on drumming -- the 1740 Negro Act in South Carolina explicitly criminalized drums -- Portuguese colonial authorities were comparatively permissive. This was not benevolence: the Portuguese believed that allowing drumming and dance reduced the likelihood of revolt. The irony is that these very gatherings became the organizational cells of resistance.

**4. The quilombos (maroon communities):** Escaped enslaved people formed hundreds of autonomous settlements across Brazil. The most famous, Palmares (1605-1694), lasted nearly a century and housed up to 30,000 people in Alagoas. In quilombos, African cultural practices -- including music, religion, and martial arts -- were practiced freely and evolved on their own terms. Quilombo descendants (quilombolas) maintain distinct musical traditions to this day.

**5. The terreiros (religious houses):** Candomble temples became protected spaces where African languages, rhythms, dances, and belief systems were transmitted with remarkable fidelity across generations. The terreiro functioned as conservatory, church, community center, and archive simultaneously.

**6. Urban irmandades (brotherhoods):** In cities like Salvador, Recife, and Rio, Black Catholic brotherhoods (such as the Brotherhood of Our Lady of the Rosary) provided institutional cover for African cultural practice. Ostensibly Catholic organizations, they preserved African coronation ceremonies (congada, maracatu), drumming traditions, and social structures.

**7. The "nacao" (nation) system:** Brazilian slavery preserved ethnic identities more than elsewhere. Enslaved people were classified by "nation" -- Nagô, Jeje, Angola, Congo, Mina -- and these groups maintained distinct musical and religious practices. This diversity-within-unity created the extraordinary range of Brazilian rhythmic traditions.

## Chapter 2: Candomble -- The Sacred Drums

### 2.1 Overview

Candomble is the primary Afro-Brazilian religion, with an estimated 2-3 million practitioners (though the number of Brazilians who participate in ceremonies or consult Candomble leaders is far higher). It preserves Yoruba, Fon, and Bantu religious systems with remarkable fidelity, considering 400+ years of diaspora.

Music is not accompaniment in Candomble -- it is the mechanism of worship itself. Specific rhythmic patterns (toques) are understood to call specific Orixas (deities) into the bodies of initiated devotees. The rhythm is the telephone line to the divine. Without the correct toque, the Orixa will not descend. The alabe (master drummer) is therefore a religious officiant of the highest order.

### 2.2 The Three Sacred Drums: The Atabaque Ensemble

The atabaque ensemble consists of three drums, derived from the Fon/Jeje tradition but adopted across all Candomble "nations":

**Rum (also Ilu)** -- The Lead
- Largest drum (approximately 120cm tall)
- Deepest pitch
- Played with hands (Ketu tradition) or with one stick and one hand (Jeje/Angola traditions)
- Role: Improvisation, ornamentation, dialogue with dancers, "speaking" to the Orixa
- The rum is the voice of the alabe -- it comments, calls, responds, intensifies

**Rumpi** -- The Middle
- Medium drum (approximately 100cm tall)
- Middle pitch
- Role: Maintains the foundational pattern with moderate variation; bridges between the steady le and the improvisatory rum

**Le (also Run-le)** -- The Small
- Smallest drum (approximately 80cm tall)
- Highest pitch
- Role: Keeps the steady timeline, the rhythmic anchor; rarely deviates from its assigned pattern
- Equivalent in function to the "bell pattern" in West African drumming

**Auxiliary instruments:**
- **Agogo:** Double iron bell, plays the timeline pattern (similar function to the Yoruba agogo or the Ghanaian gankogui)
- **Xequere (Afoxe):** Beaded gourd shaker, provides a complementary rhythmic layer
- **Ado:** Small gourd rattle, used in specific rituals

### 2.3 The Toques: Rhythmic Patterns for Each Orixa

Each Orixa has one or more dedicated toques. The patterns below represent the foundational Ketu (Yoruba) tradition, the most widespread in Brazil. The Jeje and Angola nations have related but distinct patterns.

**Note:** Candomble toques are typically in 12/8 (compound quadruple) or 4/4 time. The bell pattern serves as the rhythmic key.

#### TOQUE 1: Agueré -- for Oxossi (Orixa of the Hunt, Forests)

Character: Quick, light, galloping -- evoking the chase through the forest. Oxossi is the divine hunter; his rhythm has the urgency and agility of pursuit.

Time feel: 12/8, fast tempo

```
Agogô (bell pattern - the timeline):
|X..X..X.X..X|X..X..X.X..X|
 1  &  a 2  &  a 3  &  a 4  &  a

Lé (high drum - steady):
|X..X..X..X..|X..X..X..X..|

Rumpi (middle drum):
|X.xX..X.xX..|X.xX..X.xX..|

Rum (lead - basic framework, heavily improvised):
|O..S..O.S..O|..S..O..S.O.|
```

Mnemonic phrase: "CA-va-lo CA-va-lo-de O-XOS-si" (Horse, horse of Oxossi) -- the galloping triple feel.

#### TOQUE 2: Alujá -- for Xangô (Orixa of Thunder, Justice, Fire)

Character: Powerful, thunderous, commanding. Xango is the king of Oyo, the wielder of the double-headed axe (oxe). His rhythm shakes the ground like thunder. Aluja is one of the most dramatic and physically demanding toques.

Time feel: 4/4, fast and forceful

```
Agogô (bell):
|X.X.X.X.X.XX.|X.X.X.X.X.XX.|
 1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a

Lé (high drum):
|X.X.X.X.X.X.X.|X.X.X.X.X.X.X.|

Rumpi (middle drum):
|X..X.XX..X.XX..|X..X.XX..X.XX..|

Rum (lead - framework):
|B..S..B.S.B.S..|..B.S..B..S.B..|
 (thunderous rolls and accents on the rum)
```

The aluja builds in intensity -- the rum player escalates dynamics and density, driving the possessed devotee into increasingly dramatic movement. The "thunder rolls" on the rum are iconic: rapid-fire bass tones that shake the terreiro.

---

#### TOQUE 3: Ijexá -- for Oxum (Orixa of Sweet Waters, Love, Fertility)

Character: Sensual, gentle, flowing like a river. Oxum is the goddess of rivers, gold, and feminine beauty. Her toque is perhaps the most widely known outside Candomble -- it became the basis of afoxe music and profoundly influenced samba-reggae.

Time feel: 4/4, moderate, swaying

```
Agogô (bell - the famous ijexá bell pattern):
|X..X.XX..X.XX..|
 1 e & a 2 e & a

Lé (high drum):
|..X...X...X...X.|

Rumpi (middle drum):
|X..X..X.X..X..X.|

Rum (lead):
|O...S.O...S.O...|
 (gentle, caressing strokes)

Xequerê (shaker - crucial in ijexá):
|X.xX.xX.xX.xX.x|
 (continuous, like water flowing)
```

The ijexá is the rhythm of the afoxe blocos (Carnival groups) in Salvador, most famously Filhos de Gandhy (Sons of Gandhi), founded in 1949 by Bahian dockworkers. When 10,000 members of Filhos de Gandhy process through Salvador during Carnival playing ijexá, it is one of the most hypnotic experiences in world music.

#### TOQUE 4: Ilu (Bata Pattern) -- for Yemanjá (Orixa of the Sea, Motherhood)

Character: Flowing, wave-like, majestic. Yemanja is the mother of waters, queen of the ocean. Her rhythm rises and falls like ocean swells.

Time feel: 12/8, moderate, undulating

```
Agogô (bell):
|X..X..XX.X..X|X..X..XX.X..X|

Lé (high drum):
|X.X..XX.X..X..|X.X..XX.X..X..|

Rumpi (middle drum):
|X...X..X...X..|X...X..X...X..|

Rum (lead):
|O..O..S..O...S|..O..S..O..O.S|
 (undulating, wave-like phrasing)
```

On February 2nd (Festa de Yemanja), millions of Brazilians -- including many who don't identify as Candomble practitioners -- bring offerings of flowers, perfume, and mirrors to the sea while this rhythm plays. It is one of the largest religious gatherings in the Americas.

#### TOQUE 5: Adarrum / Ogan -- for Ogum (Orixa of Iron, War, Technology)

Character: Aggressive, martial, driving. Ogum is the blacksmith god, the warrior, the opener of paths. His toque is relentless and forceful, evoking the hammering of iron on the anvil.

Time feel: 4/4, fast, martial

```
Agogô (bell - insistent, hammering):
|XXXX.XXX.XXXX.XX|
 1 e & a 2 e & a

Lé (high drum):
|X.XX.XX.X.XX.XX.|

Rumpi (middle drum):
|X.X.X.XX.X.X.XX.|

Rum (lead):
|B.BS.BS.B.BS.BS.|
 (driving, aggressive, martial rolls)
```

The adarrum is sometimes used as a universal "call to the Orixas" at the opening of ceremonies -- its intensity is understood as clearing the space and summoning divine attention.

#### TOQUE 6: Opanijé -- for Obaluaê/Omolu (Orixa of Disease, Healing, Earth)

Character: Heavy, earth-bound, slow, somber. Obaluae is the lord of infectious disease and its cure, associated with smallpox and all epidemics. His rhythm is the heaviest in the Candomble repertoire -- it feels like the earth itself is breathing.

Time feel: 4/4, very slow, weighted

```
Agogô (bell):
|X...X...X..X....|

Lé (high drum):
|..X.....X......X.|

Rumpi (middle drum):
|X.....X.....X....|

Rum (lead):
|B.......S...B....|
 (sparse, heavy, each stroke resonates)
```

When Obaluae "arrives" (possesses a devotee), the possessed person bends forward, covered in straw (a reference to the Orixa's scarred body), and moves in a heavy, earth-scraping shuffle that mirrors the weight of the rhythm.

---

#### TOQUE 7: Exu's Toque (Avania / Fon-Fon)

Character: Syncopated, tricky, unpredictable, playful-dangerous. Exu is the trickster, the messenger, the guardian of crossroads. Without Exu, no ceremony can proceed -- he must be honored first. His rhythm reflects his nature: off-balance, witty, constantly shifting accents.

Time feel: 4/4, medium-fast, heavily syncopated

```
Agogô (bell):
|X..XX.X..XX.X..X|

Lé (high drum):
|..X..X..X..X..X.|

Rumpi (middle drum):
|X.X..XX.X..X.X..|

Rum (lead):
|.S.O..S.O.S..O.S|
 (unexpected accents, displaced beats, "tricky")
```

Exu's toque is always performed first in any Candomble ceremony (the "pade de Exu" -- Exu's offering). Only after Exu is satisfied and "opens the way" can the other Orixas be called. This is not optional -- it is liturgical law.

### 2.4 Additional Toques

Beyond the seven major Orixas detailed above, there are toques for every deity in the pantheon:

- **Agabi** -- for Nanã (oldest Orixa, primordial mud)
- **Sató** -- Jeje nation toque for Bessem (the rainbow serpent)
- **Bravum** -- Angola nation toque with a distinctive 7-feel
- **Avania** -- a universal processional rhythm
- **Tonibobe** -- for Oxumarê (the rainbow serpent in Ketu)
- **Vassi** -- a common rhythm in the Ketu nation repertoire

A fully trained alabe (master drummer) must know 40-60 distinct toques, plus the improvisational vocabulary to "speak" with each Orixa through the rum drum. Training takes 7-14 years.

## Chapter 3: Umbanda -- The Syncretic Rhythms

### 3.1 Overview

Umbanda emerged in early 20th-century Brazil (formally established around 1908 in Rio de Janeiro, though its roots are older) as a syncretic religion blending:
- Candomble (Orixa worship, drumming)
- Kardecist Spiritism (European spirit mediumship, very popular in Brazil)
- Indigenous Tupi-Guarani traditions (caboclo spirits, forest knowledge)
- Catholic saints and imagery

### 3.2 Rhythmic Character

Umbanda's rhythms are generally simpler and more accessible than Candomble's, reflecting its syncretic, populist nature. The most common rhythm is the **ponto riscado** (scratched point -- a rhythmic-melodic chant) accompanied by:

**Atabaque patterns in Umbanda** (typically one or two drums rather than the full three):

```
Basic Umbanda toque (for "incorporation" -- spirit possession):
Atabaque: |X.xX.xX.xX.xX.x|
           1 e & a 2 e & a
Palmas:   |X...X...X...X...|
(handclaps)

Caboclo rhythm (for indigenous spirits):
Atabaque: |X..X.XX..X.XX..X|
           (brighter, more driving)
Palmas:   |X..X..X..X..X..X|
```

Umbanda pontos (songs) are in Portuguese (unlike Candomble, which preserves Yoruba, Fon, and Kimbundu liturgical languages), making them more accessible to the general population. This accessibility, combined with simpler rhythms, helped Umbanda spread to all social classes.

## Chapter 4: Capoeira -- The Martial Rhythm

### 4.1 Origins

Capoeira is an Afro-Brazilian martial art disguised as dance, developed by enslaved Bantu/Angola peoples. The disguise was strategic: by embedding combat techniques within what appeared to be play, dance, and music, enslaved people could train fighting skills under the noses of overseers.

The word "capoeira" likely derives from the Tupi-Guarani "ka'a puer'a" (cleared forest area) -- the spaces where practitioners would train.

Capoeira was criminalized from 1890 to 1937. Practitioners (capoeiristas) were arrested, deported, and imprisoned. In 1937, Mestre Bimba (Manuel dos Reis Machado) persuaded the Vargas government to legalize capoeira by demonstrating it as a legitimate Brazilian martial art. He codified Capoeira Regional -- a faster, more combative style. The older style, Capoeira Angola, was preserved by Mestre Pastinha (Vicente Ferreira Pastinha).

### 4.2 The Bateria (Orchestra) of the Roda

The roda (circle) is the fundamental structure of capoeira -- a ring of players who sing, clap, and play instruments while two capoeiristas play (fight/dance) in the center. The music *controls* the game. The berimbau dictates the speed, style, and energy of the physical interaction.

**The Instruments (in order of hierarchy):**

1. **Berimbau Gunga (Berra-boi)** -- The leader
   - Largest gourd resonator, deepest pitch
   - Plays the simplest pattern but sets the fundamental rhythm and tempo
   - The mestre (master) plays this instrument and controls the roda

2. **Berimbau Medio** -- The middle
   - Medium gourd, middle pitch
   - Plays a complementary pattern, slightly more complex
   - Provides rhythmic counterpoint

3. **Berimbau Viola** -- The improviser
   - Smallest gourd, highest pitch
   - Plays the most complex, improvised patterns
   - Ornaments, comments on, and embellishes the groove

4. **Atabaque** -- The drum
   - Single conical drum, played with hands
   - Provides the bass foundation

5. **Pandeiro** -- The tambourine
   - Maintains a steady rhythmic pattern
   - One or two pandeiros in the bateria

6. **Agogo** -- Double bell
   - Timeline pattern

7. **Reco-reco** -- Scraper
   - Adds a rasping rhythmic texture

### 4.3 The Berimbau: Anatomy and Technique

The berimbau is a single-string musical bow (verga) with a gourd resonator (cabaca). The player holds a stone or coin (dobrao) against the string to change pitch, and strikes the string with a thin stick (baqueta/vareta) while holding a small basket shaker (caxixi) in the same hand.

Three fundamental tones:
- **Ton (open):** String struck without the dobrao touching it -- deep, resonant tone
- **Din (pressed):** Dobrao pressed firmly against string, then struck -- higher, sharper tone
- **Chh/Buzz:** Dobrao lightly touching string when struck -- buzzing, muted sound

The cabaca (gourd) is moved toward and away from the belly to open and close the resonance, adding another dimension of tonal control.

### 4.4 The Toques of Capoeira

#### TOQUE 8: Sao Bento Grande de Angola

The signature rhythm of Capoeira Angola -- slow, close-to-the-ground, strategic, philosophical. Mestre Pastinha's tradition. The game is played low, with malicia (cunning).

```
Berimbau Gunga (leader):
|T.D.X.T.D.T.X.D|
 1 e & a 2 e & a

Berimbau Medio:
|D.T.D.X.T.D.T.X|

Berimbau Viola (improvised, framework):
|D.TD.TDX.TD.T.DX|

Atabaque:
|X..x.xX..x.xX..|

Pandeiro:
|X.xX.xX.xX.xX.x|
```

Tempo: Slow to moderate (60-90 BPM). Players must crouch, use deceptive movements, and demonstrate wisdom rather than athleticism.

Mestre Bimba's fast, aggressive, acrobatic style. The game is played standing, with high kicks, flips, and explosive movements.

```

 (faster tempo, driving)

Berimbau Medio:
|D.X.D.T.X.D.T.D|

Berimbau Viola:

 (rapid-fire variations)

|XxXxXxXxXxXxXxXx|

```

Tempo: Fast (120-160+ BPM). Regional games are athletic spectacles -- backflips, spinning kicks (meia lua de compasso), headspins (piao de cabeca).

#### TOQUE 10: Angola

The oldest and most fundamental toque. Pure Capoeira Angola.

```
Berimbau Gunga:
|T...D...T.D.T...|
 1 . . . 2 . . .

Berimbau Medio:
|D...T...D.T.D...|

Atabaque:
|X.....X.....X...|
 (sparse, deliberate)
```

Tempo: Very slow (50-70 BPM). This is capoeira at its most philosophical and ritualistic.

---

#### TOQUE 11: Cavalaria (Cavalry)

A warning rhythm -- historically played when police (on horseback) were approaching. When capoeiristas heard this toque, they would scatter. Today it is played ceremonially to honor the history of persecution.

```
Berimbau Gunga:
|D.D.D.T.D.D.D.T.|
 (urgent, repetitive warning)
```

---

#### TOQUE 12: Iuna

A special toque played only for graduated mestres (masters). When Iuna plays, only formed capoeiristas may enter the roda. The game becomes a demonstration of mastery.

```
Berimbau Gunga:
|T..D.T..X.T..D..|
 (melodic, dignified)
```

### 4.5 The Roda as Rhythmic Governance

The roda is a complete social-musical system:
- The berimbau gunga controls the tempo and energy
- Songs (ladainhas, corridos, quadras) are led by a soloist and answered by the roda in call-and-response
- The mestre can speed up or slow down the game by changing the toque
- The mestre can "buy the game" (comprar o jogo) by entering the roda
- Handclaps (palmas) from the roda create an additional rhythmic layer

The principle: **the music is not accompaniment -- it is the game's operating system.** A capoeirista who cannot hear the rhythm cannot play capoeira.

# PART II: SAMBA -- THE COMPLETE STORY

## Chapter 5: Origins -- From Terreiro to Tia Ciata's House

### 5.1 Etymology and Proto-Samba

The word "samba" derives from the Kimbundu (Bantu language of Angola) word **"semba"** -- an invitation to dance involving a belly-bump (umbigada). This movement was central to the batuque and lundu, circle dances practiced by enslaved Angolan/Congolese peoples throughout Brazil.

Proto-samba traditions include:
- **Batuque:** Generic Portuguese term for Afro-Brazilian circle drumming/dance, documented since the 16th century
- **Lundu:** A couples dance derived from batuque that entered the Portuguese colonial mainstream by the late 18th century; featured syncopated rhythms and sensual movement
- **Jongo:** Bantu circle dance from southeast Brazil (Rio, Minas Gerais, Sao Paulo); call-and-response; enigmatic, metaphorical song lyrics; the direct ancestor of partido alto samba
- **Coco:** Northeastern circle dance with foot-stomping rhythm (discussed in Part IV)
- **Maxixe:** The "Brazilian tango" -- a late 19th-century urban dance that mixed lundu, polka, and habanera; the first Brazilian popular music to gain international fame

### 5.2 The Bahian Migration

The critical historical event for samba's birth was the **great Bahian migration to Rio de Janeiro** in the late 19th century. After abolition (1888) and the collapse of Bahian sugar economy, thousands of Afro-Bahians -- many of them Candomble practitioners -- migrated to Rio, settling in the downtown area known as "Pequena Africa" (Little Africa), centered around Praca Onze.

### 5.3 The House of Tia Ciata

**Hilaria Batista de Almeida (1854-1924)**, known as **Tia Ciata** (Aunt Ciata), was a Bahian Candomble priestess (iyalorixá) who became the central figure in samba's genesis. Her house at Rua Visconde de Itauna, 117, near Praca Onze, was the most important of several "tias baianas" (Bahian aunts) who hosted gatherings where samba crystallized.

The house had three simultaneous musical levels:
1. **Front room (sala):** Respectable choro (instrumental) and polka for the middle-class visitors
2. **Back room (sala de jantar):** Samba de partido alto and lundu for the in-group
3. **Backyard (terreiro/quintal):** Batucada, Candomble drumming, capoeira -- the raw African roots

This three-room geography mirrors samba's social code-switching: presentable in the front, increasingly African as you go deeper.

The song often cited as the "first recorded samba" -- **"Pelo Telefone"** (1917), credited to Donga (Ernesto dos Santos) -- was a collective creation from Tia Ciata's sessions. The controversy over its authorship (Donga registered it individually, but many contributed) reflects the tension between samba's collective, oral tradition and the emerging commercial music industry.

### 5.4 The Morros (Favelas) as Creative Incubators

As Rio's downtown was "cleaned up" by the Pereira Passos urban reforms (1903-1906) -- modeled on Haussmann's Paris -- Afro-Brazilian communities were pushed to the morros (hills). These informal settlements, later called favelas, became the crucibles where samba evolved from living-room music to a mass urban art form.

Key morros in samba history:
- **Morro da Providencia** -- the first favela (1897), founded by veterans of the Canudos War
- **Morro de Sao Carlos** -- home of Ismael Silva, founder of the first samba school
- **Morro da Mangueira** -- home of Estacao Primeira de Mangueira (1928), the most storied samba school
- **Morro do Salgueiro** -- known for revolutionary Carnival themes about Black history

## Chapter 6: Samba de Roda -- The Root

### 6.1 Overview

**Samba de roda** (circle samba) from the Reconcavo Baiano (the region around the Bay of All Saints in Bahia) is the root form of all samba. It was designated a **UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity** in 2005.

### 6.2 Structure

Samba de roda is a circle (roda) where participants sing, clap, and play instruments while one dancer at a time enters the center. The dancer performs and then "invites" (with an umbigada -- belly bump, or a gesture) the next dancer.

**Instruments:**
- Viola (Brazilian 10-string guitar) -- distinctive strumming patterns
- Pandeiro
- Atabaque or timbau
- Prato-e-faca (plate and knife -- a scraped idiophone, unique to Bahia)
- Reco-reco
- Handclaps (palmas)

#### TOQUE 13: Samba de Roda Basic Pattern

```
2/4 time, moderate tempo

Atabaque:
|B.x.S.x.|B.x.S.x.|
 1 e & a  2 e & a

Pandeiro:
|X.xS.xX.xS.x|
 (thumb bass-tip-slap-tip-thumb bass-tip-slap-tip)

Palmas (claps) -- two interlocking patterns:
Pattern A: |X...X...|X...X...|
Pattern B: |..X...X.|..X...X.|

Prato-e-faca (plate & knife):
|x.x.x.xX.x.x.xX|
 (scraping with accent on "X")
```

### 6.3 Two Varieties

- **Samba chula:** The more formal variety; a solo singer (chuleiro) sings the verse (chula), and no one dances while singing. Only when the chorus responds does a dancer enter. More Angolan influence.
- **Samba corrido:** More relaxed; singing and dancing happen simultaneously. More participatory.

## Chapter 7: The Escola de Samba and The Bateria

### 7.1 The Samba School System

The first escola de samba (samba school) was **Deixa Falar** ("Let Them Speak"), founded in 1928 by Ismael Silva, Nilton Bastos, and others in the Estacio neighborhood. The name "escola" (school) was a witty reference to the nearby teachers' college -- Ismael joked, "We are the professors of samba."

Today, Rio's top samba schools compete in the Grupo Especial at the Sambadrome (designed by Oscar Niemeyer, opened 1984). Each school fields 3,000-5,000 members for a 75-minute parade including floats, costumes, dancing, singing, and -- the heart of everything -- **the bateria.**

### 7.2 The Bateria: A 200-400 Person Percussion Orchestra

The bateria is the single most powerful acoustic percussion ensemble on the planet. A full Grupo Especial bateria has 250-400 percussionists playing in precise unison and interlocking patterns. The sound is overwhelming -- a physical force that vibrates the chest cavity.

The bateria is led by the **mestre de bateria** (drum master), who directs everything using a repinique drum and a whistle (apito). The organizational hierarchy is:
- **Mestre de bateria:** Overall director
- **Diretores de bateria:** Section leaders
- **Puxadores de cada naipe:** Lead players for each instrument section

#### The Instruments, One by One:

---

### SURDO DE PRIMEIRA (First Surdo / Bass)

**Physical:** Large bass drum, 20-24 inches diameter, worn with a strap. Played with a padded mallet in the dominant hand; the non-dominant hand mutes and shapes the tone.

**Role:** The heartbeat of samba. Plays on **beat 2** of the 2/4 bar. This is the defining characteristic of samba -- the emphasis is on 2, creating a forward-leaning, propulsive feel.

#### TOQUE 14: Surdo de Primeira

```
2/4 time

|..B.|..B.|..B.|..B.|
 1 & 2 &  1 & 2 &
     ^         ^
  BEAT 2    BEAT 2

(B = deep bass hit on beat 2, hand releases to let drum resonate)
(Beats 1, &, & are either silent or lightly muted)
```

The surdo de primeira tells your body where "the one" *isn't* -- because the emphasis on beat 2 creates the samba's characteristic "falling forward" sensation.

---

### SURDO DE SEGUNDA (Second Surdo / Response)

**Physical:** Slightly smaller than the primeira (18-20 inches). Same playing technique.

**Role:** "Answers" the primeira by playing on **beat 1**. Together, primeira and segunda create a continuous bass pulse: 1-2-1-2. But the segunda is slightly softer, confirming the hierarchy where beat 2 remains primary.

#### TOQUE 15: Surdo de Segunda

```
2/4 time

|B...|B...|B...|B...|
 1 & 2 &  1 & 2 &
 ^         ^
BEAT 1   BEAT 1
```

**Combined primeira + segunda:**
```
Segunda:  |B...|B...|
Primeira: |..B.|..B.|
Together: |B.B.|B.B.|
           1 2  1 2
```

This interlocking bass creates the samba's ground -- the floor on which everything else dances.

---

### SURDO DE TERCEIRA (Third Surdo / "Cortador" -- The Cutter)

**Physical:** Smallest surdo (16-18 inches), brightest pitch. Some schools use a similar-sized drum called the surdo de corte.

**Role:** The cortador plays syncopated patterns *between* the primeira and segunda hits, "cutting" through the straight bass pulse with offbeat accents. This is where the surdo section becomes rhythmically interesting.

#### TOQUE 16: Surdo de Terceira (basic pattern)

```
2/4 time

|.B.B|.B.B|
 1 & 2 &

Variation 1 (common):
|.BB.|.BB.|

Variation 2 (more syncopated):
|.B.B|B..B|
```

**Full surdo section combined:**
```
Terceira: |.B.B|.B.B|
Segunda:  |B...|B...|
Primeira: |..B.|..B.|
Combined: |BBBB|BBBB|  <-- near-continuous bass pulse with accents
```

---

### CAIXA (Snare Drum)

**Physical:** A shallow snare drum, 12-14 inches, worn tilted at the hip. Played with two thin wooden sticks (baquetas). Buzz rolls are fundamental to the technique.

**Role:** The backbone of the bateria's high-frequency layer. Plays continuous patterns, primarily based on 16th notes, that provide the shimmering, buzzing texture that defines the bateria's overall sound.

#### TOQUE 17: Caixa Patterns

```
2/4 time (each character = 16th note, 8 per bar in 2/4)

Basic pattern:
|xxxxxxxx|xxxxxxxx|
 (continuous 16th notes, all roughly equal)

Standard pattern with accents:
|x.Xx.Xx.|x.Xx.Xx.|
 1 e & a 2 e & a

Buzz roll pattern:
|xzzxzzxz|xzzxzzxz|
 (z = buzz roll, creating a shimmering texture)

Advanced pattern (Mangueira style):
|Xx.xXx.x|Xx.xXx.x|
```

When 60-80 caixas play in unison, the effect is a wall of high-frequency rhythmic energy -- like a waterfall of snare.

---

### REPINIQUE (Lead Drum)

**Physical:** A high-pitched, two-headed drum (10-12 inches), made of metal with nylon or animal skin heads. Played with one stick in the dominant hand and open hand/fingers on the other head.

**Role:** The **lead drum and communication device** of the bateria. The mestre de bateria plays a repinique, and all section leaders play repiniques. It has three functions:
1. **Calls (chamadas):** Rhythmic signals that tell the bateria what to do -- start, stop, change pattern, break, return
2. **Improvisation (repicado):** Virtuosic fills and solos
3. **Pattern:** The standard groove between calls

#### TOQUE 18: Repinique Patterns

```
2/4 time (S = stick hit, H = hand slap, O = open hand tone)

Basic pattern:
|S.H.S.HS|S.H.S.HS|

Standard call (chamada) -- signals a section entrance:
|SSSS.SS.|SSSS.SS.|  (flam-like doubles on the stick)
                       followed by the pattern

Repicado (improvised fill):
|SHSHSHSHSHSHSH.S|
 (rapid alternation)

Break call (tells bateria to stop):
|S..S..S.S..S..S.|  (three hits = everyone stops)
```

The repinique is the "general's drum" -- it issues orders and the 300-person army obeys instantly. A good mestre de bateria can control a bateria with the precision of a symphony conductor, using purely rhythmic signals.

---

### TAMBORIM

**Physical:** A tiny frame drum, only 6 inches in diameter. Held in the non-dominant hand, struck with a flexible multi-rod stick (baqueta de tamborim) by the dominant hand. The non-dominant thumb mutes/opens the head from behind.

**Role:** Despite its small size, the tamborim is the **most complex melodic-rhythmic voice** in the bateria. Its patterns are intricate, syncopated, and feature the signature "virada" (turn) -- a flipping of the stick to hit the opposite side of the drum, creating a rapid-fire rhythmic ornament.

#### TOQUE 19: Tamborim Patterns

```
2/4 time (T = open tone, x = muted, > = accent)

Classic "telecoteco" pattern:
|T.xT.Tx.|T.xT.Tx.|
 1 e & a 2 e & a

Standard pattern with virada:
|T.xT.xTT|xT.xT.xT|
           ^ virada (rapid flip)

Mangueira-style pattern:
|Tx.Tx.TT|Tx.Tx.TT|

Imperatriz Leopoldinense-style:
|T..TxTx.|T..TxTx.|

The virada in detail:
|....TTTT|........|
 The virada is a rapid sextuplet or quintuplet created by
 flipping the stick to strike the underside of the drum head,
 then back to the top -- producing an unmistakable "frying" sound.
```

**Tamborim sections (30-50 players)** often play in **three interlocking groups**, each playing a different displaced version of the same pattern. The result is a hypnotic, granular cascade of tiny drum hits -- one of the most distinctive sounds in all music.

```
Tamborim group interlocking (3 groups):
Group A: |T.xT.Tx.|T.xT.Tx.|
Group B: |xT.xT.Tx|.xT.xT.T|
Group C: |Tx.xT.xT|Tx.xT.xT|
Combined: |TTTTTTTTT|TTTTTTTTT| (near-continuous with shifting accents)
```

---

### AGOGO (Double Bell)

**Physical:** Two (or three) conical bells welded together, different sizes/pitches. Struck with a wooden or metal stick.

**Role:** Plays a **timeline pattern** -- the rhythmic key that organizes all other parts. This is a direct African retention: the bell pattern in Brazilian samba serves the same function as the timeline in Yoruba, Ewe, and Mande music.

#### TOQUE 20: Agogo Pattern

```
2/4 time (H = high bell, L = low bell)

Standard samba agogo pattern:
|H.HL.LH.|H.HL.LH.|
 1 e & a 2 e & a

Variation:
|HH.LL.HH|.LL.HH.L|

Simple pattern:
|H.L.H.L.|H.L.H.L.|
```

---

### CUICA (Friction Drum)

**Physical:** A metal or gourd shell with a drumhead on one end. A thin bamboo stick is attached to the center of the head *inside* the drum. The player rubs the stick with a wet cloth (inside the drum) while pressing the drumhead from outside with the other hand. Pressure on the head changes the pitch; friction on the stick creates the sound.

**Role:** The cuica is the **voice** of the bateria -- it produces eerie, human-like sounds that laugh, cry, groan, and speak. Its role is semi-improvisatory, providing melodic-rhythmic commentary.

#### TOQUE 21: Cuica Patterns

```
2/4 time (H = high pitch/pressed, L = low pitch/open, ~ = glissando)

Basic pattern:
|H~L.H~L.|H~L.H~L.|

Laughing pattern:
|HHL.HHL.|HHL.HHL.|

Crying pattern:
|H~~~L...|H~~~L...|
 (long descending glissando)

Call-and-response with surdo:
Surdo:  |..B.|..B.|
Cuica:  |H~L.|H~L.|
```

---

### CHOCALHO / GANZA (Shaker)

**Physical:** A multi-platelet shaker (chocalho) made of rows of small metal jingles mounted on a frame, or a cylindrical tube shaker (ganza) filled with beads or shot.

**Role:** Continuous high-frequency rhythmic energy. Along with the caixas, provides the shimmering 16th-note texture.

#### TOQUE 22: Chocalho Pattern

```
2/4 time

|xxxxxxxx|xxxxxxxx|
 (continuous 16th notes, emphasis through wrist movement)

With accents:
|x.xX.xX.|x.xX.xX.|
```

---

### PANDEIRO (Brazilian Tambourine)

**Physical:** A 10-12 inch frame drum with metal jingles (platinelas) and a natural skin or synthetic head. Held in the non-dominant hand; the dominant hand plays bass (thumb), tips (fingers), slaps (palm), and muted strokes.

**Role:** The pandeiro is arguably the most versatile single instrument in Brazilian music. A master pandeirista can simulate the entire bateria on one instrument. In the bateria, pandeiros play supportive patterns. In smaller settings (pagode, choro, samba de roda), the pandeiro is often the sole percussion instrument.

#### TOQUE 23: Pandeiro -- Standard Samba Pattern

```
2/4 time (B = thumb bass, T = fingertip, S = slap, x = muted)

Standard samba:
|B.TS.TST|B.TS.TST|
 1 e & a 2 e & a

Partido alto feel:
|B.T.S.T.|B.T.S.T.|

Swing variation:
|B.TSxTST|B.TSxTST|
```

The pandeiro technique known as **"virada de pandeiro"** -- a rapid roll using thumb, fingers, and palm in sequence -- is one of the most demanding techniques in world percussion.

---

### RECO-RECO (Scraper)

**Physical:** A metal spring-scraper (modern) or notched bamboo tube (traditional). Scraped with a metal stick.

**Role:** Adds a continuous rasping texture, similar in function to the guiro in Cuban music.

 (continuous scraping with accents on beats)

### 7.3 How the Bateria Functions as a Unit

The full bateria groove, all instruments combined:

#### TOQUE 24: Full Bateria Composite (2/4, standard samba-enredo)

```
2/4 time, approx. 130-140 BPM

Surdo 1a:    |....B...|....B...|
Surdo 2a:    |B.......|B.......|
Surdo 3a:    |..B.B...|..B.B...|
Caixa:       |xxxxxxxx|xxxxxxxx|
Repinique:   |S.H.S.HS|S.H.S.HS|

Reco-reco:   |xxxxxxxx|xxxxxxxx|
```

**The "Breque" (Break):**
One of the most electrifying moments in a samba parade is the **breque** -- a rhythmic break where the entire bateria stops or plays a synchronized accent pattern. The mestre signals with the repinique, and 300+ drummers execute simultaneously:

```
Full bateria: |xxxxxxxx|xxxxxxxx| --> mestre's call
ALL STOP:     |X.......|........|  (single massive accent, then silence)
             or
BREAK PATTERN:|X..X..X.|X..X..X.|  (unison accents)
RETURN:       |xxxxxxxx|xxxxxxxx| (full pattern resumes)
```

The precision of this -- 300 people stopping on a dime and restarting in perfect sync -- is one of the most impressive feats of collective musicianship in the world.

## Chapter 8: Samba Sub-Genres

### 8.1 Samba-Enredo (Theme Samba)

Each samba school creates a new samba-enredo annually for Carnival. It tells the story (enredo) of the school's chosen theme -- which can be historical, political, mythological, or social. The bateria's role is to support this narrative, with specific sections (like a slower "piano" section) highlighting dramatic moments.

Structure: Long, multi-section song with verses and refrains; the refrains are sung by the entire school (3,000+ voices), creating an overwhelming wall of sound above the bateria.

### 8.2 Pagode

Pagode emerged in the late 1970s-1980s from the backyard samba parties (pagodes) in the suburbs of Rio and Sao Paulo. Key innovators: Beth Carvalho (who championed new composers), Zeca Pagodinho, Jorge Aragao, Almir Guineto, Fundo de Quintal.

**Key innovation:** The introduction of new instruments:
- **Tanta (repique de mao):** A hand-played drum that replaced the stick-played repinique
- **Banjo (Brazilian banjo):** A 4-string banjo with a specific strumming pattern
- **Rebolo:** A narrow, conical hand drum

#### TOQUE 25: Pagode / Partido Alto Pattern

```
2/4 time

Tanta (hand drum):
|S.xO.xS.|O.xS.xO.|
 (slap-mute-open alternation, very groovy)

Pandeiro (partido alto feel):
|B.T.S.TS|B.T.S.TS|

Surdo (simplified, one drum):
|..B...B.|..B...B.|

Palmas (the "partido alto" clap):
|X..X..X.|..X..X..|
 (the partido alto clap is THE defining rhythmic signature)
```

### 8.3 Samba de Partido Alto

The improvised, competitive samba tradition. "Partido alto" means "high-quality" or "the upper division." Singers take turns improvising verses over a fixed rhythmic-melodic structure (refrão), while the group maintains a syncopated groove.

The partido alto feel is defined by a distinctive syncopation:

#### TOQUE 26: Partido Alto Rhythmic Cell

```
2/4 time

The fundamental partido alto accent pattern:
|X..X..X.|.X..X..X|
 1 e & a 2 e & a

This can also be understood as a 3+3+2 grouping:
|X..X..X.|  = 3 + 3 + 2 (sixteenth notes)
```

This 3+3+2 grouping is one of the most important rhythmic cells in all Afro-diasporic music -- it appears in Cuban son (the tresillo), Afrobeat, and numerous West African traditions. In partido alto, it is expressed with particular swing and looseness.

### 8.4 Samba-Cancao

The slow, romantic samba that emerged in the 1930s-40s. Influenced by bolero and American popular song. Rhythmically, it relaxes samba's syncopation into a more gentle sway. Dolores Duran, Noel Rosa, Lupicinio Rodrigues were key artists. Samba-cancao was the immediate precursor to bossa nova -- Joao Gilberto essentially reinvented the samba-cancao guitar pattern.

# PART III: BOSSA NOVA

## Chapter 9: The Quiet Revolution

### 9.1 The Invention

In the late 1950s, in the apartments and bars of Rio's affluent Zona Sul (South Zone) -- Copacabana, Ipanema, Leblon -- a revolution occurred. **Joao Gilberto** (1931-2019), a guitarist from Bahia obsessed with samba rhythm, invented a new guitar technique that would change popular music worldwide.

What Gilberto did: He internalized the entire samba bateria into **a single guitar hand**. His right-hand thumb maintained the bass pattern (surdo) while his fingers played syncopated chord rhythms (tamborim + caixa). He sang in a cool, intimate whisper -- the opposite of the full-throated samba singer. The result was samba distilled to its rhythmic essence, stripped of volume but not of complexity.

### 9.2 The "Batida" (Beat)

The bossa nova batida (beat) is not a simplification of samba -- it is a **sophistication through reduction.** Every element of the bateria is present, but suggested rather than stated.

#### TOQUE 27: Joao Gilberto's Bossa Nova Guitar Batida

```
2/4 time

Thumb (bass notes, surdo function):
|B...B...|B...B...|
 1       2

Fingers (chord, tamborim/caixa function):
|..x.x.x.|.x.x..x.|
 (syncopated upstrokes)

Combined:
|B.x.x.x.|Bx.x..x.|
 1 e & a  2 e & a
```

The pattern is not perfectly regular -- it breathes, shifts, and floats. Gilberto himself played with infinite subtle variations, never repeating the exact same pattern twice. The key is the **displacement** between the steady bass (beats 1 and 2) and the syncopated chords, which creates the bossa nova's floating, weightless sensation.

### 9.3 Antonio Carlos Jobim

**Tom Jobim** (1927-1994) was the composer who gave bossa nova its harmonic language. A trained classical musician who loved Debussy, Ravel, Chopin, and jazz (particularly the "cool jazz" of Chet Baker, Gerry Mulligan, and Miles Davis), Jobim merged:
- Samba's rhythmic DNA
- Jazz harmony (extended chords, ii-V-I progressions)
- French Impressionist harmony (whole-tone scales, augmented chords, parallel motion)
- Brazilian melodic lyricism

Key compositions: "Garota de Ipanema" (The Girl from Ipanema), "Desafinado," "Corcovado," "Wave," "Aguas de Marco," "Samba de Uma Nota So."

### 9.4 Bossa Nova Drum Patterns

When bossa nova is performed by a full ensemble, the drum set pattern is a refined version of samba. The drummer plays with brushes or light sticks, maintaining the samba feel in miniature.

#### TOQUE 28: Bossa Nova Drum Set Pattern (Standard)

```
2/4 time

Hi-hat (closed, with stick):
|x.x.x.x.|x.x.x.x.|
 (steady 8th notes or light 16ths)

Cross-stick (rimclick on snare):
|....X...|....X...|
 (beat 2 -- the samba's emphasized beat)

Bass drum:
|X.......|X.......|
 (beat 1, gentle)

Combined:
HH: |x.x.x.x.|x.x.x.x.|
CS: |....X...|....X...|
BD: |X.......|X.......|
```

#### TOQUE 29: Bossa Nova Drum Variation (with syncopation)

```
2/4 time

Hi-hat: |x.x.x.x.|x.x.x.x.|
Snare:  |..x...X.|..x...X.|
Bass:   |X..x....|X..x....|

(The bass drum adds an "and of 1" ghost note,
 creating a subtle push -- still samba underneath)
```

### 9.5 Cultural Context

Bossa nova was the music of the **educated, middle-class, largely white Zona Sul** of Rio. It emerged during the optimistic presidency of Juscelino Kubitschek (1956-61), who built Brasilia and promised "50 years of progress in 5." The music reflected this optimism: modern, sophisticated, forward-looking.

The contrast with favela samba was not lost on anyone. Critics like Jose Ramos Tinhorao accused bossa nova of whitewashing samba -- stealing its rhythm while erasing its Blackness, poverty, and community. Defenders argued that bossa nova was a genuine evolution, not a theft.

The truth is complex: bossa nova owes everything to samba, which owes everything to Africa. Joao Gilberto himself never denied this -- he was a devoted student of samba de roda, partido alto, and Candomble rhythm. But the music he made from these sources was received and marketed in a context of racial and class privilege. This tension remains unresolved.

# PART IV: NORTHEASTERN BRAZIL

## Chapter 10: Baiao and Forro

### 10.1 Luiz Gonzaga -- The King of Baiao

**Luiz Gonzaga do Nascimento (1912-1989)**, born in Exu, Pernambuco, single-handedly put Northeastern Brazilian music on the national and international map. A master sanfoneiro (accordion player), Gonzaga codified the rhythms of the sertao (the arid, drought-stricken backlands of northeastern Brazil) into popular music: baiao, xaxado, xote, forró.

### 10.2 The Baiao Trio

Gonzaga's genius was to distill the region's music into a powerful trio:
- **Sanfona (accordion):** Melody, harmony, and rhythmic drive
- **Zabumba:** A large, two-headed bass drum played with a mallet (right hand) and a thin stick (left hand) simultaneously
- **Triangulo (triangle):** Far more rhythmically complex than its European counterpart; plays intricate syncopated patterns

#### TOQUE 30: Baiao Fundamental Pattern

```
2/4 time

Zabumba (R = right hand mallet/bass, L = left hand stick/rim):
|R..L.RL.|R..L.RL.|
 1 e & a 2 e & a
 (Bass on 1, stick on "a of 1", both on "& of 2", stick on "a of 2")

Triangle (O = open, M = muted):
|O.MO.MOM|O.MO.MOM|
 1 e & a 2 e & a

Simplified zabumba (for learning):
|B..x.Bx.|B..x.Bx.|
 B = bass (mallet), x = rim (stick)
```

The baiao feel is distinct from samba: where samba's 2/4 emphasizes beat 2 and has a "falling forward" quality, baiao has a more grounded, earthen bounce, reflecting the hardscrabble life of the sertanejo (backlands dweller).

### 10.3 Forro

Forro is both a genre and a social dance event. The word likely derives from the English "for all" (dances organized by British railroad engineers were "for all"), or possibly from the Bantu "forrobodo" (great party).

**Sub-genres:**
- **Forro pe-de-serra (foot of the mountain):** Traditional; the Gonzaga trio; rural authenticity. Key artists: Luiz Gonzaga, Jackson do Pandeiro, Dominguinhos
- **Forro universitario:** Revival movement starting in the 1990s, university students in Sao Paulo and Rio rediscovering Northeastern roots. Key: Falamansa, Trio Virgulino
- **Forro eletronico:** Modern, electronic; adds drum machines, synthesizers, bass guitar. Enormously popular in the Northeast since the 2000s. Bands: Avioes do Forro, Calcinha Preta. Purists decry it; millions love it.

## Chapter 11: Frevo -- The Fastest Rhythm in Brazil

### 11.1 Overview

Frevo is Pernambuco's Carnival music -- born in Recife in the late 19th century from the collision of military marching bands, capoeira, and African rhythmic sensibility. The word comes from "ferver" (to boil), because the dancing crowd seems to boil.

Frevo is the fastest major Brazilian rhythm -- tempos reach **150-200 BPM** in 2/4 time. The dancers (passistas) perform acrobatic steps descended from capoeira movements while holding a small, colorful umbrella (sombrinha) -- originally used as a weapon and shield in street fights between rival capoeira gangs.

### 11.2 Types of Frevo

- **Frevo de rua (street frevo):** Instrumental, brass-band driven, the most frenetic
- **Frevo de bloco (block frevo):** Vocal, with banjo/guitar accompaniment, slightly slower, more melodic
- **Frevo cancao (song frevo):** Slower, lyrical, influenced by bossa nova in the 1960s

#### TOQUE 31: Frevo Rhythm (Snare/Caixa Pattern)

```
2/4 time, FAST (160+ BPM)

Caixa (snare):
|X.xX.xX.xXx.xXx|
 1 e & a 2 e & a
 (driving, militant, relentless)

Surdo:
|..B.|..B.|
 (beat 2, like samba but much faster)

The brass section plays syncopated, punchy melodies over this
military-carnival groove -- the overall effect is joyous frenzy.
```

## Chapter 12: Maracatu -- The Coronation Drums

### 12.1 Maracatu Nacao (Nation Maracatu / Baque Virado)

Maracatu Nacao is an Afro-Brazilian coronation procession originating in Recife, Pernambuco, dating to at least the 17th century. It reenacts the crowning of **Congo Kings and Queens** -- a practice that began in the Catholic brotherhoods where enslaved Africans elected ceremonial royalty.

The procession (cortejo) includes:
- **Rei and Rainha (King and Queen):** In elaborate costumes
- **Dama do Paco:** Carrier of the calunga (sacred doll, representing ancestral spirits)
- **Caboclo de pena:** Figure representing indigenous heritage
- **Baianas:** Women in traditional dress
- **Batuqueiros:** The drummers

**The Sound:**
Maracatu Nacao is one of the heaviest, most hypnotic percussion traditions on Earth. The tempo is **slow** (60-80 BPM) and the large alfaia drums create a wall of low-frequency sound that is felt as much as heard.

**Instruments:**
- **Alfaias (drums):** Large (20-24 inch), rope-tuned wooden bass drums. A full maracatu group has 20-80 alfaias. Played with two large wooden mallets.
- **Caixa (snare):** Provides the rhythmic subdivision
- **Gongue (agogo/bell):** Single large bell, the timeline
- **Mineiro (shaker):** Cylindrical metal shaker
- **Abê (xequere):** Beaded gourd

#### TOQUE 32: Maracatu Nacao / Baque Virado (basic pattern)

```

Alfaia 1 (bass):
|B.......B.......|
 1               3

Alfaia 2 (response):
|....B.......B...|
     2           4

Alfaia 3 (virado -- the "turned" syncopated pattern):
|..B..B.B..B..B.B|
 (syncopated accents between the main beats)

Caixa:
|xxxxxxxxxxxxxxxx|
 (continuous 16th notes)

Mineiro:
|x.x.x.x.x.x.x.x|
 (steady 8th notes)

Abê:
|X..x..X..x..X..x|
```

The term **"baque virado"** (turned beat) refers to the syncopated alfaia pattern that "turns" against the straight pulse. When 40-80 alfaias play interlocking "virado" patterns, the result is a massive, churning sea of bass frequencies -- one of the most powerful sounds in acoustic music.

**The sacred dimension:** Many maracatu nacoes are connected to Candomble/Jurema (indigenous-African syncretic religion) terreiros. The calunga doll is a sacred object. Before Carnival processions, religious ceremonies are held. Maracatu is not just music -- it is ritual.

### 12.2 Maracatu Rural (Baque Solto / Maracatu de Baque Solto)

Maracatu Rural is the rural cousin of Maracatu Nacao, originating in the sugar-cane zone (Zona da Mata) of Pernambuco. It is faster, more aggressive, and features the spectacular **caboclo de lanca** (lance carrier) -- a dancer in elaborate, heavy costume with a long wooden lance, performing aggressive movements.

**Key differences from Nacao:**
- Faster tempo (100-130 BPM)
- Features trombone and brass alongside percussion
- More military/aggressive character
- The **mestre de cabocaria** leads with improvised shouts (sambas)

#### TOQUE 33: Maracatu Rural / Baque Solto Pattern

```
2/4 time, faster than Nacao

Gongue (bell):
|X.X.X.XX|X.X.X.XX|

Surdo/Bombo:
|B...B...|B...B...|

Tarol (small snare):
|xXx.xXx.|xXx.xXx.|

Mineiro:
|xxxxxxxx|xxxxxxxx|
```

## Chapter 13: Mangue Beat -- The Recife Revolution

### 13.1 Chico Science & Nacao Zumbi

In the early 1990s, Recife was an economic disaster zone -- one of the poorest major cities in Brazil. From this poverty, **Chico Science** (Francisco de Assis Franca, 1966-1997) and his band **Nacao Zumbi** created **Mangue Beat** (Mangrove Beat, also spelled Manguebit) -- a genre that fused:
- Maracatu Nacao and Rural rhythms
- Coco
- Embolada
- Hip-hop
- Funk
- Psychedelic rock
- Electronic music

The **"Manifesto Caranguejos com Cerebro"** (Crabs with Brains, 1992), written by Chico Science and Fred Zero Quatro (of the band Mundo Livre S/A), was a cultural declaration: Recife's mud and mangroves would be the source of artistic renewal, not a mark of shame.

Key albums: *Da Lama ao Caos* (From Mud to Chaos, 1994) and *Afrociberdelia* (1996) by Chico Science & Nacao Zumbi.

### 13.2 The Sound

Mangue Beat layers live alfaia drums (maracatu) with electric bass, distorted guitar, turntablism, and rap delivery. The effect is devastating -- ancestral Brazilian percussion with the aggression of punk and the groove of hip-hop.

#### TOQUE 34: Mangue Beat Fusion Pattern

```
4/4 time (adapted from maracatu)

Alfaia (2 drums interlocking):
|B..B..B.B..B..B.|
 (maracatu virado pattern, amplified)

Electric bass (following alfaia):
|B..B..B.B..B..B.|

Caixa:
|xxxxxxxxxxxxxxxx|

Drum kit (kick + snare, hip-hop feel overlaid):
Kick:  |X..X..X...X..X..|
Snare: |....X.......X....|

Gongue:
|X..X..X.X..X..X.|
```

Chico Science died in a car accident in 1997 at age 30. Nacao Zumbi continues; Mangue Beat influenced an entire generation of Brazilian musicians and remains Recife's signature sound.

## Chapter 14: Other Northeastern Traditions

### 14.1 Coco

Coco is a circle dance tradition from the Brazilian Northeast (Pernambuco, Paraiba, Alagoas, Rio Grande do Norte). Its defining feature is **foot-stomping** (the baque) as a primary rhythmic instrument.

Origin theories: (1) From the rhythms of coconut-cracking work songs; (2) From quilombo dance traditions; (3) Both. Likely Bantu-influenced, with possible indigenous elements.

#### TOQUE 35: Coco Basic Pattern

```
2/4 time

Foot-stomping (the "baque"):
|X...X...|X...X...|
 1       2

Ganza (shaker):
|xxxxxxxx|xxxxxxxx|

Zabumba (when used):
|B..x.Bx.|B..x.Bx.|
 (similar to baiao)

Pandeiro:
|B.TS.TST|B.TS.TST|
```

Key figure: **Coco Raizes de Arcoverde** -- a modern group that brought coco to international attention.

### 14.2 Ciranda

Ciranda is a large circle dance from Pernambuco, performed on beaches and in public squares, especially in the city of Itamaraca. It is one of the most inclusive of Brazilian popular traditions -- anyone can join the circle.

**Lia de Itamaraca** (Maria Madalena Correia do Nascimento, b. 1944) is the "Queen of Ciranda" and a living cultural treasure.

The rhythm is gentle, swaying, in a moderate 2/4 or 4/4, designed to accommodate a large circle of people moving together.

### 14.3 Embolada

Embolada is a tradition of rapid-fire vocal improvisation accompanied by pandeiro. Two singers face each other and trade improvised verses at breathtaking speed -- the vocal equivalent of a capoeira game. Topics range from humorous to political to insulting (similar to rap battles). The pandeiro provides a driving rhythmic foundation.

Key figures: Caju e Castanha, Condor, Jackson do Pandeiro (who bridged embolada and popular music).

# PART V: BAHIA -- THE AFRICAN HEART

## Chapter 15: The Bloco Afro Movement

### 15.1 Ile Aiye -- The First Bloco Afro

In 1974, in the neighborhood of Liberdade (Liberty) in Salvador, Bahia, a group of Black Bahians founded **Ile Aiye** -- the first "bloco afro" (Afro Carnival group). Until then, Salvador's Carnival had been dominated by white trio eletrico blocos and traditional afoxes. Ile Aiye was explicitly, unapologetically Black: only Black people could join, and the themes celebrated African and Afro-Brazilian history, beauty, and pride.

This was revolutionary in a country that promoted the myth of "racial democracy." Ile Aiye was initially vilified by Salvador's white establishment -- newspapers called them a "bloco racista" (racist group). But Ile Aiye persisted and spawned a movement.

### 15.2 Olodum and the Invention of Samba-Reggae

**Olodum** (Grupo Cultural Olodum, founded 1979) emerged from the Pelourinho neighborhood of Salvador -- at that time, a crumbling, dangerous historic district (it has since been controversially "restored"). Under the leadership of **Neguinho do Samba** (Antonio Luis Alves de Souza), Olodum developed **samba-reggae** -- a fusion of:
- Samba rhythms (the caixa patterns, the surdo pulse)
- Reggae (the offbeat emphasis, the slower tempo)
- Afro-Brazilian religious rhythms (ijexá, Candomble toques)

#### TOQUE 36: Samba-Reggae (Olodum Pattern)

```
4/4 time, moderate tempo (90-110 BPM)

Surdo 1 (marcacao -- "marking"):
|B.......B.......|
 1               3

Surdo 2 (resposta -- "response"):
|....B.......B...|
     2           4

Surdo 3 (virada/cortador):
|..B...B.B.B...B.|
 (syncopated pattern that gives samba-reggae its distinctive bounce)

Repinique (lead -- call patterns):
|S.SS.SS.S.SS.SS.|

Caixa (snare):
|xxxxxxxxxxxxxxxx|
 (continuous 16ths with accents)

Timbau (conical hand drum):
|..S.O.S...S.O.S.|
```

Olodum achieved international fame when **Paul Simon** featured them on *The Rhythm of the Saints* (1990) and **Michael Jackson** filmed the video for "They Don't Care About Us" in Pelourinho with Olodum's drummers (1996).

### 15.3 Timbalada and Carlinhos Brown

**Carlinhos Brown** (Antonio Carlos Santos de Freitas, b. 1962), from the Candeal neighborhood of Salvador, is one of Brazil's most creative percussionists and producers. In 1991, he founded **Timbalada**, a community percussion group centered on the **timbal** (a tall, conical, nylon-headed hand drum that Brown helped popularize).

#### TOQUE 37: Timbalada / Timbal Pattern

```
4/4 time, up-tempo

Timbal (S = slap, O = open, B = bass):
|S.OS.OS.S.OS.OS.|
 1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a

Surdo:
|B.......B.......|

Repinique:
|.SS..SS..SS..SS.|
```

Brown's innovation was making the timbal an accessible lead instrument -- lighter and simpler than the atabaque, with a bright, cutting tone that projected in outdoor settings. Timbalada became both a band and a community music school in Candeal, using percussion as a vehicle for social transformation.

### 15.4 Axe Music

Axe (from the Yoruba "ase" -- spiritual force, power) is the umbrella term for Salvador's Carnival pop music, emerging in the 1980s-90s. It blends samba-reggae, frevo, ijexá, and pop/funk elements into a high-energy, massively popular Carnival sound.

Key artists: Daniela Mercury, Ivete Sangalo, Claudia Leitte, Chiclete com Banana.

Axe is performed on the **trio eletrico** -- a massive, multi-story sound-truck that moves through Salvador's streets during Carnival with the artist performing on top and thousands of dancers (folioes) following behind in organized blocos.

# PART VI: SOUTH AND SOUTHEAST

## Chapter 16: Samba-Rock

Samba-rock emerged in 1960s-70s Sao Paulo from Black dance halls (bailes) where DJs mixed samba with soul, funk, and rock. The dance style -- a partner dance with intricate footwork -- is unique. Key figure: Jorge Ben Jor, whose song "Mas Que Nada" (1963) is an iconic samba-rock fusion.

## Chapter 17: MPB and Tropicalia

### 17.1 MPB (Musica Popular Brasileira)

MPB is less a genre than a cultural category encompassing sophisticated, artistically ambitious Brazilian popular music from the mid-1960s onward. Key figures:
- **Caetano Veloso:** Bahian genius, endlessly experimental
- **Gilberto Gil:** Bahian, master of rhythm and groove, later Minister of Culture
- **Milton Nascimento:** From Minas Gerais, extraordinary vocalist, blended Brazilian forms with jazz and choral music
- **Chico Buarque:** Poet-songwriter, master of samba cancao, fierce critic of the dictatorship
- **Elis Regina:** Perhaps the greatest Brazilian vocalist of the 20th century
- **Gal Costa:** Bahian, powerful voice, bridged MPB and tropicalia

### 17.2 Tropicalia (1968)

Tropicalia (Tropicalismo) was a brief (1967-1969) but seismically important cultural movement led by Caetano Veloso, Gilberto Gil, Tom Ze, Gal Costa, Os Mutantes, and the arranger Rogerio Duprat.

**The principle:** Anthropophagy (cultural cannibalism) -- a concept from the 1920s Modernist poet Oswald de Andrade. Brazil should devour all cultural influences -- native and foreign -- and synthesize something entirely new.

**In practice:** Tropicalia mixed:
- Samba, bossa nova, baiao, forro
- Psychedelic rock (Beatles, Hendrix)
- Avant-garde composition (John Cage, musique concrete)
- Brazilian popular poetry
- Pop art aesthetics
- Electric guitars (scandalous to MPB purists)

**The album:** *Tropicalia: ou Panis et Circenses* (1968) is the movement's manifesto-as-record.

**The repression:** The military dictatorship (installed by coup in 1964) viewed Tropicalia as subversive. In December 1968, after the AI-5 decree (Institutional Act No. 5, which suspended civil liberties), Caetano and Gil were arrested, imprisoned for months, and then exiled to London (1969-1972). Os Mutantes continued in Brazil but under heavy censorship.

Rhythmically, Tropicalia's contribution was the principle that any rhythm from any source could coexist with any other. This radical eclecticism became the DNA of subsequent Brazilian musical innovation.

# PART VII: MODERN BRAZIL

## Chapter 18: Funk Carioca / Baile Funk

### 18.1 Origins

Funk carioca (Rio funk, or baile funk) originated in the late 1980s-early 1990s in Rio de Janeiro's favelas. The immediate inspiration was **Miami bass** (2 Live Crew, Afrika Bambaataa, Freestyle) imported by DJ Marlboro and other early figures who brought records back from trips to Miami.

But the deeper roots are Afro-Brazilian: the emphasis on bass, the call-and-response structures, the communal dance party format (the baile -- ball/party, held in community halls, schools, and outdoor spaces in favelas).

### 18.2 The Tamborzao

The defining sonic innovation of funk carioca is the **tamborzao** (big drum) -- a bass-heavy beat pattern that became the genre's signature in the late 1990s/early 2000s.

#### TOQUE 39: Tamborzao (Funk Carioca Beat)

```
4/4 time, 130 BPM

Kick:     |X..X..X...X..X..|
Snare:    |....X..X....X..X.|
Sub-bass: |X.........X......|
 (extremely heavy sub-bass, often a descending tone)

Simplified:
Kick:  |X.X...X.X.X...X.|
Clap:  |....X.......X....|

The "Volt" pattern (modern funk 150bpm):
Kick:  |X..X..X.X..X..X.|
Snare: |..X..X....X..X...|
Hi-hat:|xxxxxxxxxxxxxxxx|
```

The tamborzao evolved from the "Volt Mix" -- a specific Miami bass break that DJs in Rio sampled and reconstructed. Over time, producers added increasingly heavy sub-bass, distortion, and local percussion samples.

### 18.3 Social Context

Baile funk is the music of the favela -- and its history is inseparable from the social conditions of Rio's urban poor. Bailes can attract thousands of people to favela community spaces, and they function as social gatherings, catharsis, and economic engines (selling food, drinks, sponsoring MCs).

Key MCs and producers: MC Cidinho & MC Doca, DJ Marlboro, Anitta (who brought funk carioca to global pop), MC Kevin o Chris, Ludmilla.

## Chapter 19: Tecnobrega

### 19.1 The Amazonian Electronic Revolution

Tecnobrega (techno-kitsch, techno-tacky) emerged in **Belem**, the capital of Para state at the mouth of the Amazon, in the early 2000s. It blends:
- Brega (cheesy, sentimental popular music of the Amazon region)
- Carimbo (Afro-Amazonian circle dance)
- Electronic production (synthesizers, drum machines)
- Caribbean influences (zouk, soca, cumbia)

### 19.2 The Aparelhagem

The **aparelhagem** (sound system) is the delivery vehicle for tecnobrega -- massive, elaborately decorated mobile sound systems (some costing $500,000+) that function like Jamaican sound systems. They are the center of huge outdoor parties in Belem's periphery.

Tecnobrega's economic model bypasses the traditional music industry entirely: music is distributed free on CDs at markets and online; artists make money from live performances at aparelhagem events.

### 19.3 Carimbo -- The Root Rhythm

Carimbo is the Afro-Amazonian rhythm that underlies much of Para's popular music, including tecnobrega.

## Chapter 20: Piseiro, Hip-Hop, and Contemporary Currents

### 20.1 Piseiro

Piseiro (from "pisar" -- to step) is the latest viral Northeastern genre (exploding circa 2019-2022). It's essentially a sped-up, electronic-tinged forro with autotune vocals. Artists: Joao Gomes, Ze Vaqueiro, Baroes da Pisadinha. The rhythm is a streamlined, high-energy version of baiao/forro.

### 20.2 Brazilian Hip-Hop

Brazil has the **largest hip-hop scene outside the United States.** Based primarily in Sao Paulo's periferia (urban periphery -- the Brazilian equivalent of the inner city), Brazilian hip-hop emerged in the late 1980s.

**Racionais MC's** (Mano Brown, Ice Blue, Edi Rock, KL Jay) are the most important group. Their 1997 album *Sobrevivendo no Inferno* (Surviving in Hell) is considered a masterpiece of social commentary -- it was the first hip-hop album included in the vestibular (university entrance exam) reading list.

Other key artists: Criolo, Emicida, Djonga, Flora Matos, Rincon Sapiencia.

Brazilian hip-hop integrates local percussion -- samba breaks, maracatu samples, berimbau loops -- more than most non-American hip-hop scenes, creating a distinctly Brazilian variant of the genre.

### 20.3 Brazilian Electronic Music

Brazil has produced significant electronic music artists who channel Brazilian rhythmic DNA through electronic production:
- **Amon Tobin:** Born in Brazil, raised in multiple countries; his sample-based music draws on Brazilian percussion
- **Gui Boratto:** Sao Paulo house/techno producer; subtle samba syncopation in his 4/4 grooves
- **Tropkillaz:** Bass music duo who sample and reinterpret Brazilian funk, baile funk, and samba
- **Badsista:** Producer who merges funk carioca, dancehall, and experimental electronics
- **Anitta:** Pop megastar who has brought funk carioca rhythms to global mainstream pop

# PART VIII: INDIGENOUS BRAZILIAN MUSIC

## Chapter 21: The First Music of Brazil

### 21.1 Overview

Brazil is home to over **300 indigenous groups** speaking approximately **274 languages** across multiple language families (Tupi, Macro-Je, Arawak, Carib, and others). Each group has distinct musical traditions, making it impossible to generalize about "indigenous Brazilian music" as a single entity.

### 21.2 Common Elements (with massive variation)

**Instruments:**
- **Maraca (rattle):** The most widespread indigenous Brazilian instrument; made from gourds filled with seeds or stones; central to shamanic and ceremonial practice
- **Flutes:** Various bamboo and wood flutes; in many traditions (e.g., Kamayura, Wauja), sacred flutes are played in gender-restricted ceremonies
- **Log drums:** Hollowed tree trunks used for signaling and ceremony
- **Stamping tubes:** Bamboo tubes stamped on the ground for rhythmic effect
- **Body percussion:** Foot-stomping, hand-clapping, and thigh-slapping

**Musical characteristics (generalizing carefully):**
- Call-and-response singing
- Repetitive rhythmic cycles (some very long -- 30+ minutes)
- Vocal music is primary; instruments are often rhythmic accompaniment
- Music is inseparable from ritual, myth, and daily life
- Certain songs "belong" to specific spirits, animals, or ceremonial contexts
- Microtonal tuning systems that don't conform to Western scales

### 21.3 The Threat

The ongoing destruction of the Amazon rainforest and encroachment on indigenous territories represents an existential threat to these musical traditions. When a community is displaced, disrupted, or decimated, its musical knowledge -- often entirely oral, held by specific elders -- can be lost permanently.

Between 1500 and today, the indigenous population of what is now Brazil has declined from an estimated 3-5 million to approximately 900,000. Hundreds of languages and their associated musical traditions have already been lost.

### 21.4 Preservation Efforts

- **Video nas Aldeias (Video in the Villages):** A project providing indigenous communities with video equipment to document their own cultural practices
- **Instituto Socioambiental (ISA):** Supports indigenous cultural preservation
- **Indigenous musicians recording and sharing traditions:** Artists like Marlui Miranda (who has spent decades recording and arranging indigenous music), Djuena Tikuna, and Kunumi MC (a young Guarani rapper) are bridging indigenous musical traditions and contemporary audiences
- **The Aldeia Multiétnica** at Brazil's annual GoiAniaemia gathering creates space for inter-tribal musical exchange

# PART IX: THE SOCIOPOLITICAL DIMENSION

## Chapter 22: Slavery's Musical Legacy

### 22.1 The Numbers Again

4.9 million enslaved Africans. 388 years of slavery (1500-1888 -- longer than any other country in the Americas). Last country in the Western Hemisphere to abolish.

The **musical consequence** is that virtually every major Brazilian popular music genre has direct African roots:
- Samba: Bantu batuque, lundu, jongo
- Bossa nova: Samba (i.e., African, at one remove)
- Forro/baiao: African zabumba tradition + European accordion
- Maracatu: Congo coronation ceremonies
- Frevo: Capoeira + military band
- Axe/samba-reggae: Candomble + samba
- Funk carioca: African-American (i.e., African diasporic) bass music + Brazilian favela culture
- Capoeira: Bantu martial art

Brazil's music IS African music -- evolved, syncreted, transformed, but rooted in the experience and genius of enslaved peoples and their descendants.

### 22.2 The Racial Democracy Myth

The sociologist Gilberto Freyre, in *Casa-Grande & Senzala* (The Masters and the Slaves, 1933), argued that Brazil's racial mixing (mesticagem) created a unique, harmonious racial democracy, distinct from the rigid segregation of the United States.

This myth has been powerfully challenged:
- Brazil has the largest Afro-descendant population outside Nigeria
- Afro-Brazilians earn approximately 57% of what white Brazilians earn
- Afro-Brazilians are disproportionately victims of police violence (75% of homicide victims are Black or mixed-race)
- The music industry systematically undercompensated Black creators while profiting enormously from their innovations

The myth of racial democracy is especially pernicious in the musical sphere: because Brazil "celebrates" its African musical heritage, the actual economic and social marginalization of Afro-Brazilians is obscured. Samba is the "national music," but the sambistas of the morros remain poor. Maracatu is UNESCO heritage, but the maracatuzeiros of Recife's periphery live in precarious conditions.

## Chapter 23: Music and Resistance

### 23.1 Samba as Black Resistance

Samba was **criminalized** in the early 20th century. The 1890 Penal Code's vagrancy laws were used to arrest sambistas, capoeiristas, and practitioners of Afro-Brazilian religions. Carrying a pandeiro or guitar could result in arrest.

The transformation of samba from criminalized Black expression to "national identity" occurred under the Getulio Vargas dictatorship (1930-1945), which co-opted samba as part of a nation-building project. This co-optation was double-edged: it brought legitimacy and economic opportunity, but it also diluted samba's radical edge and allowed white and middle-class appropriation.

Key resistance moments:
- **Cartola, Nelson Cavaquinho, Ze Keti** and other morro sambistas who maintained samba's roots during the bossa nova era
- **Clementina de Jesus** (1901-1987), who was "discovered" at age 63 and brought the raw, African-rooted vocal style of jongo and samba de roda to national attention
- **The quilombo-as-metaphor** in samba-enredo themes, asserting Black historical agency

### 23.2 MPB vs. The Dictatorship

During the military dictatorship (1964-1985), MPB became the primary vehicle for political dissent:
- **Chico Buarque:** Master of coded lyrics that evaded censors. "Apesar de Voce" (In Spite of You, 1970) was ostensibly a love song but transparently addressed the dictator. It was banned after selling 100,000 copies.
- **Geraldo Vandre:** "Pra Nao Dizer Que Nao Falei das Flores" (1968) became the anthem of resistance. Vandre was forced into exile.
- **Gilberto Gil and Caetano Veloso:** Arrested and exiled after AI-5 (December 1968).
- **Censorship board (DCDP):** All lyrics had to be submitted for approval before recording. Thousands of songs were banned.

The **AI-5 decree** (December 13, 1968) was the turning point: it suspended habeas corpus, closed Congress, imposed censorship, and gave the military unlimited power. For musicians, it meant that coded protest was no longer enough -- the state was listening and punishing.

### 23.3 Funk as Favela Voice

Funk carioca is the latest in the line of criminalized Black Brazilian music:
- Multiple attempts to ban baile funk parties (2000s-2010s)
- MCs arrested for "apologia ao crime" (apology for crime) in lyrics
- Police raids on bailes
- The 2019 "CPI do funk" (parliamentary investigation) attempted to classify funk as a crime
- **Counter-movement:** In 2009, funk was officially recognized as a "cultural movement" by Rio state legislature
- The pattern repeats: Black Brazilian music is first criminalized, then co-opted, then celebrated, then claimed as "national heritage" -- while its creators remain marginalized

## Chapter 24: Carnival as Political Expression

### 24.1 The Samba-Enredo as Political Commentary

The annual samba-enredo (Carnival theme samba) of Rio's samba schools is one of Brazil's most powerful vehicles for political expression. Because Carnival reaches tens of millions of Brazilians (and a global television audience), a samba school's choice of theme is inherently political.

Landmark political enredos:
- **Salgueiro (1960): "Quilombo dos Palmares"** -- the first school to center an Afro-Brazilian historical theme, breaking the tradition of "official" patriotic narratives
- **Mangueira (2019): "Historia Para Ninar Gente Grande"** (A Story to Put Grown-Ups to Sleep) -- reimagined Brazilian history from the perspective of marginalized peoples (indigenous, Black, women, LGBTQ+), with the memorable lyric: "Brasil, o teu nome e Dandara" (Brazil, your name is Dandara -- Dandara was a warrior queen of the Quilombo dos Palmares)
- **Paraiso do Tuiuti (2018):** A samba-enredo about slavery that contained a thinly veiled critique of the then-current political crisis, with a character called the "vampiro neoliberal" (neoliberal vampire)

### 24.2 The Class Structure of Carnival

Carnival itself is a site of class tension:
- **The samba schools** are community organizations rooted in favelas and working-class neighborhoods, but the Sambadrome parade has been increasingly commercialized, with expensive tickets and corporate sponsorship
- **The trio eletrico** blocos of Salvador charge hundreds of dollars for admission (via the abada -- the uniform), creating de facto economic segregation
- **Camarotes (VIP boxes)** at the Sambadrome cost thousands of dollars
- Meanwhile, the actual cultural creators -- the composers, drummers, passistas -- often receive little compensation
- **The pipoca (free street Carnival)** remains the most democratic space

## Chapter 25: Cultural Appropriation and the Question of Credit

### 25.1 The Pattern

The history of Brazilian music follows a recurring pattern:
1. Black Brazilians create a new musical form (samba, bossa nova's rhythmic source, samba-reggae, funk carioca)
2. The music is initially criminalized or stigmatized
3. White or lighter-skinned Brazilians adopt, adapt, and popularize it
4. The music becomes commercially successful
5. The original Black creators receive minimal credit and compensation
6. The music is eventually claimed as "Brazilian national heritage"
7. Repeat

### 25.2 Specific Cases

- **Samba:** Created in Black communities; "Pelo Telefone" was registered by a single composer from a collective creation; the Vargas regime promoted a "whitened" version of samba for national branding
- **Bossa nova:** Built entirely on samba's rhythmic foundation, but marketed as a middle-class Zona Sul invention; its international success (Stan Getz/Joao Gilberto, *Getz/Gilberto*, 1964) benefited white American and European musicians more than Black Brazilian sambistas
- **Samba-reggae:** Olodum's rhythms were used by Paul Simon and Michael Jackson, bringing international attention but also raising questions about fair compensation and credit
- **Funk carioca:** Originally stigmatized as favela music; now that artists like Anitta have brought it to global pop, the question of who profits from the genre's success is urgent

### 25.3 The Ongoing Debate

This is not a resolved question. Brazil's musical culture is genuinely syncretic -- the boundaries between "Black" and "white" music are blurrier than in the United States. Many of Brazil's greatest musicians (Caetano Veloso, Gilberto Gil, Carlinhos Brown, Anitta) are themselves of mixed heritage and actively acknowledge African roots.

But acknowledgment is not the same as structural equity. The favelas where samba, funk, and hip-hop are created still lack basic infrastructure. The terreiros where Candomble's sacred rhythms are preserved still face religious persecution. The quilombola communities that maintained African traditions for centuries still fight for land rights.

Brazilian music is a miracle of human creativity born from the worst crime in human history. To celebrate the music without confronting its origins in slavery, and its ongoing context of racial inequality, is to hear only the surface of the rhythm while missing the deeper beat.
