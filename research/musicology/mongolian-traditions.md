# PART I: THE STEPPE SOUNDSCAPE

## 1. The Vast Landscape and Music: How the Endless Steppe Shaped Mongolian Musical Aesthetics

Mongolia's landmass stretches across 1.56 million square kilometers of grassland, desert, and mountain---a territory roughly the size of Western Europe yet home to only 3.3 million people. This radical ratio of space to human presence is not merely geographic backdrop; it is the generative condition of Mongolian musical aesthetics. The steppe is not silence---it is a constant, layered drone of wind, grass, distant animal calls, and sky. Mongolian music emerged as a response to, and dialogue with, this sonic environment.

### Long Tones and the Aesthetics of Space

Mongolians describe their most valued song form, the urtyn duu (long song), with words that could equally describe the landscape itself: "spacious," "wide," "long-lasting," "of great size." This is not coincidence. The melodic contour of traditional songs, generally absent of conjunct (stepwise) movement, traces shapes that musicologists have described as "hill-shaped"---with ascents and descents mirroring the topography in which they were performed. Long songs performed in mountain areas characteristically feature descending contours; those performed on the open steppe, where singers gaze upward at distant peaks, start low and ascend.

In the urtyn duu, a single syllable can sustain for thirty seconds or more, adorned with elaborate ornamental melismas. A four-minute performance may contain only seven or eight words. The musical logic is not textual progression but spatial occupation---the voice claiming territory in the vast acoustic space of the steppe, much as a herder's song must carry across kilometers to reach another human ear.

### Wide Intervals and Pentatonic Foundations

The intervallic vocabulary of Mongolian music favors fourths, fifths, and octaves---the widest intervals of the pentatonic scale that forms its harmonic foundation. These "open" intervals, lacking the tension and directed motion of semitones, create a sense of harmonic stasis and expansiveness. The pentatonic system (typically anhemitonic, without half-steps) mirrors the steppe itself: vast, uncluttered, resonant.

### Environmental Acoustics

The steppe is a natural reverb chamber. Sound travels extraordinary distances across flat grassland, and Mongolian musical practice evolved to exploit this. Throat singing (khoomii) and long song were developed to project across open space, using the body as a resonating chamber that amplifies specific overtones---a technique that becomes more effective the further the sound must travel. The open-air performance context, rather than an enclosed concert hall, is the native acoustic environment of Mongolian music, and this shapes everything from timbre to tempo.

## 2. Nomadic Life and Music: Songs of Herding, Riding, Milking, and the Seasonal Cycle

Mongolian music is not an art form abstracted from daily life and placed on a stage. It is functional, embedded in the rhythms of pastoral nomadism. Every major activity of herding life has its associated musical form, and the boundary between "music" and "work" is porous to the point of nonexistence.

### The Daily Cycle

- **Dawn**: Herders rise and sing to their animals as they prepare for the day's grazing. The morning milking of mares is accompanied by specific melodies believed to calm the animals and increase milk production.
- **Riding**: The rhythms of horseback travel generate the foundational rhythmic vocabulary of Mongolian music (see Section 15). Hours spent in the saddle, with the horse's gait providing a constant rhythmic pulse, shaped the temporal structures of song.
- **Herding**: Songs accompany the movement of flocks, with specific calls and melodies for directing animals. The herder's voice functions as both musical expression and practical tool.
- **Evening**: The return to camp, the milking, the preparation of airag (fermented mare's milk)---each has its associated songs. The ger (yurt) becomes an intimate performance space after nightfall.

### The Seasonal Cycle

- **Spring**: Songs of renewal, the birth of young animals, and the breaking of camp for seasonal migration. Spring is also the season of the greatest animal vulnerability, and protective songs (magtaal) are sung to ensure the survival of newborns.
- **Summer**: The season of Naadam festivals, communal gatherings, and celebratory music. The longest days allow for extended musical performances.
- **Autumn**: Songs of harvest (in the limited agricultural sense), preparation for winter, and the fattening of herds. Autumn is associated with melancholic long songs.
- **Winter**: The harshest season, when families are confined to their gers. This is the traditional season for epic recitation, storytelling, and the performance of extended musical works.

### Animal Bonding Through Music

One of the most distinctive Mongolian musical practices is the use of song to cause a mother animal to accept a rejected offspring. This practice, documented by UNESCO, involves specific melodic formulas for each species:

- **Sheep**: *Toiglokh* (calling a ewe to accept its young)
- **Goat**: *Cheeglekh / tseeglekh* (calling a nanny-goat to accept its young)
- **Camel**: *Hoosl okh* (singing to a mare-camel), often accompanied by the tsuur (end-blown flute), morin khuur, or other instruments
- **Horse**: Specific melodies tied to the morin khuur tradition
- **Cattle**: Vocal calls integrated with milking rhythms

The practice reveals music as a technology of animal husbandry---a practical tool with measurable outcomes (the acceptance or rejection of young) rather than a purely aesthetic endeavor.

## 3. The Five Animals: Musical Associations of Horse, Camel, Cow, Sheep, and Goat

The "five snouts" (*tavan khoshuu mal*)---horse, camel, cow, sheep, and goat---are the foundation of Mongolian pastoral economy and the symbolic vocabulary of Mongolian culture. Each animal carries distinct musical associations.

### Horse (*Morin / Aduu*)

The horse is the supreme animal of Mongolian culture and music. The morin khuur (horse-head fiddle) is named for it. The entire rhythmic vocabulary of Mongolian music derives from horse gaits (see Section 15). Horses are the subjects of the greatest praise songs (magtaal), and winning racehorses at Naadam receive elaborate sung tributes. The horse's whinny is imitated in morin khuur technique, and the gallop rhythm is the most fundamental pulse in Mongolian percussion.

**Associated rhythmic character**: Driving, forward-moving, varied gaits from walking 4/4 to galloping subdivisions.

### Camel (*Temee*)

The camel is associated with the Gobi Desert regions and carries a different musical personality---slower, more ponderous, with a swaying quality that reflects the camel's gait. The famous "Camel Herder's Song" (*Temee Chinee*) uses a rocking rhythm that mimics the camel's distinctive two-beat sway. Camel songs tend toward lower registers and more sustained tones, reflecting the animal's deep, resonant vocalizations.

**Associated rhythmic character**: Slow, swaying, two-beat rocking pattern, often in compound meter.

### Cow/Yak (*Uker / Sarlag*)

Cattle songs are associated with milking rhythms---steady, repetitive, calming. The milking rhythm is one of the most basic pulse patterns in Mongolian domestic music: a gentle, even beat that keeps the animal calm and the milk flowing. Yak songs in particular carry a quality of weight and endurance appropriate to the high-altitude regions where yaks are herded.

**Associated rhythmic character**: Steady, even, moderate tempo; the "working" rhythm of pastoral life.

### Sheep (*Khonin*)

Sheep songs are the most numerous in the Mongolian pastoral repertoire, reflecting the sheep's importance to the economy. They tend toward gentler, more flowing melodic lines with softer rhythmic profiles. The sound of a flock of sheep---a collective murmur rather than individual voices---influences the textural quality of sheep-associated music.

**Associated rhythmic character**: Gentle, flowing, collective quality; moderate tempo with soft articulation.

### Goat (*Yamaa*)

Goat songs reflect the animal's more agile, unpredictable character. Goat-associated melodies tend to be more rhythmically varied and melodically angular than sheep songs, with quicker ornamental figures that mirror the goat's nimble movements.

**Associated rhythmic character**: Quick, agile, more rhythmically varied; lighter articulation than horse rhythms.

## 4. Music as Spiritual Practice: Tengrism, Shamanism, and the Drum as Gateway Between Worlds

### Tengrism: The Eternal Blue Sky

At the foundation of Mongolian spiritual life is Tengrism (*Tengerism*)---the worship of the Eternal Blue Sky (*Mongke Tengri*). Unlike the personalized deities of monotheistic religions, Tengri is an impersonal, all-encompassing force: the sky itself as divine presence. Tengri "does not speak to people directly" and "does not demand worship familiarly." This theology of vast, impersonal presence directly parallels the aesthetic of Mongolian music---wide, open, non-narrative, dwelling in sustained states rather than progressing through dramatic arcs.

The Tengrist cosmology posits a three-layered universe:
- **Upper world**: The realm of Tengri and celestial spirits
- **Middle world**: The human realm, the steppe
- **Lower world**: The realm of Erlik Khan and ancestral spirits

Music---specifically the shaman's drum---is the technology of transit between these worlds.

### Shamanism and Sound

The Mongolian shaman (*bo* or *udgan* for female shamans) is defined by the ability to move between worlds, and the drum is the vehicle of this movement. Shamanic cosmology holds that the drum is literally the shaman's horse: the drum skin (often horse hide) is the horse's body, the drumstick (*orba*) is the whip, and the rhythm of drumming is the rhythm of riding between worlds.

The shaman's ceremony involves:
1. **Invocation**: Calling spirits through specific melodic chants in archaic Mongolian
2. **Rhythmic acceleration**: The drum begins at a walking pace and gradually accelerates
3. **Trance induction**: Sustained rapid drumming (approximately 4-4.5 beats per second, the frequency range associated with theta brainwave states) induces altered consciousness
4. **Spirit dialogue**: The shaman communicates with spirits, and the drum rhythm shifts to reflect the nature of the encounter
5. **Return**: Gradual deceleration back to the walking rhythm, signaling the shaman's return to the middle world

The constant singing and drum beating, combined with physical movement---dancing and jumping---puts the shaman into a trance state where the soul is understood to leave the body. This altered state enables the shaman to "journey to the spirit world, negotiate with spirits, retrieve lost souls, or battle malevolent forces."

### The Animist Soundscape

In the Tengrist/shamanic worldview, the natural world is alive with spirits (*ongon*). Mountains, rivers, rocks, and trees all possess spiritual presence. This means that the soundscape of the steppe is not mere noise but a chorus of spiritual voices. Mongolian music, particularly throat singing and long song, can be understood as the human attempt to join this chorus---to harmonize with the spiritual frequencies of the natural world rather than to impose human narrative upon it.

# PART II: PERCUSSION AND RHYTHM

## 5. The Shaman's Drum (Hengereg / Dungur)

### Construction

The Mongolian/Siberian shaman's drum (*hese hengereg*) is the central instrument of shamanic practice---more important than any melodic instrument and considered a living entity rather than an object.

**Frame**: Constructed from birch wood, bent into a round or slightly oval shape. The frame diameter ranges from 30 to 50 centimeters (12 to 20 inches). Birch is chosen for both practical qualities (flexibility, resonance) and spiritual significance (the birch tree is sacred in Mongol-Siberian shamanism, considered a world-tree connecting the three realms).

**Skin**: Traditionally goat skin, deer skin, or horse skin. The choice of animal skin carries spiritual meaning:
- **Horse skin**: The drum becomes a horse for riding between worlds
- **Deer skin**: Associated with forest spirits and hunting shamanism
- **Goat skin**: Common in steppe shamanism, associated with agility and mountain spirits

The skin is stretched while wet and secured to the frame with leather thongs or sinew. As it dries, it tightens, and the pitch rises. This means the drum's pitch varies with humidity and temperature---the drum literally responds to weather, reinforcing the belief that it is alive and connected to the natural world.

**Symbolic decorations**: The drum face may be painted with cosmological symbols:
- The world tree connecting upper, middle, and lower worlds
- Sun and moon symbols
- Animal spirits (often horses, deer, birds)
- The shaman's spirit helpers

**Interior attachments**: Mongolian and Siberian drums characteristically have bells and metal objects attached to the inside of the frame, so the drum functions as both a struck and a shaken instrument. Ribbons and *khadags* (ceremonial silk scarves) are tied to the top and sides, representing "the crown and ears of the drum."

**The drumstick (orba)**: A distinctive spoon-shaped beater, 15 to 30 centimeters long, with fur or soft leather covering the striking side. The two sides of the orba produce different timbres: the covered side gives a deep, resonant tone; the uncovered wooden side produces a sharper, more cutting attack.

### Playing Technique and Rhythmic Patterns

The shaman's drumming is not metronomic repetition but a sophisticated rhythmic language:

**The Journey Rhythm (basic trance-induction pattern)**:
```
Basic pulse (walking tempo, ~120 BPM):
|: X . X . X . X . :|
  1   2   3   4

Accelerating journey (building to ~240-270 BPM):
|: X X X X X X X X :|
  1 + 2 + 3 + 4 +

With bell accents from shaking (S = shake):
|: X . S . X . S . :|  -> |: X S X S X S X S :|
```

**Rhythmic phases of a shamanic ceremony**:

1. **Calling rhythm** (slow, deliberate):
```
. . X . . . X . . . X . . . X .
        (spacious, summoning)
```

2. **Horse-riding rhythm** (medium, steady):
```
X . x . X . x . X . x . X . x .
(mimicking hoofbeats, the "mount" carrying the shaman)
```

3. **Trance rhythm** (fast, sustained):
```
X x X x X x X x X x X x X x X x
(continuous rapid pulse, theta-wave frequency range)
```

4. **Spirit-encounter rhythm** (variable, improvisatory):
```
X . . X X . X . . . X . X X . .
(irregular, responding to the spirit dialogue)
```

5. **Return rhythm** (decelerating):
```
X x X x X x X x -> X . x . X . x . -> X . . . X . . .
(gradual slowing, bringing the shaman back)
```

### The Drum as Living Entity

The hengereg is understood to possess its own spirit (*ongon*). A new drum must be "awakened" through ritual before it can be used:

1. The drum is consecrated by a senior shaman
2. Offerings (milk, vodka, incense) are made to the drum's spirit
3. The drum is "introduced" to the shaman's spirit helpers
4. The first beatings activate the drum's spiritual power

A drum that is not properly awakened is considered spiritually inert---a mere object. A properly awakened drum is a sentient ally. Shamans report that their drums "speak" to them, indicating when to accelerate, when to shift rhythm, and when the ceremony is complete. The drum can also "die"---if the skin breaks, the drum's spirit has departed, and the drum must be ritually buried or burned, not simply repaired.

## 6. Mongolian Court Music: The Khuree (Monastery) Musical Tradition

### Pre-Buddhist Court Music

Before the widespread adoption of Tibetan Buddhism in the 16th century, Mongolian court music was rooted in shamanic and martial traditions. The Mongol khans maintained court musicians whose primary functions were:
- Ceremonial fanfares for state occasions
- Military signaling (see Section 19)
- Praise songs (*magtaal*) honoring the khan
- Entertainment at feasts

This pre-Buddhist court repertoire was largely transmitted orally and much of it was lost during the religious transformations of later centuries.

### Buddhist Transformation

The conversion of the Mongol nobility to Tibetan Buddhism, beginning with Altan Khan's alliance with the Dalai Lama in 1578, fundamentally altered Mongolian court music. Buddhist monasteries (*khure*) became the primary centers of organized musical activity, and the monastery orchestra replaced the shamanic drum circle as the dominant ensemble form.

The Qing Dynasty court maintained a specific Mongolian music division called *Fanbu Hezou* (literally "barbarian ensemble"), which performed music adapted from earlier Mongol khan court traditions. This music was popular among the Manchu rulers, and the ensemble was documented by several court painters, providing some of the earliest visual records of Mongolian musical practice.

### The Monastery Orchestra

The Buddhist monastery orchestra typically consisted of approximately twenty musicians arranged in two rows:
- **Front row**: Percussion (drums, cymbals, bells)
- **Back row**: Wind instruments (trumpets, shawms, conches) and additional cymbals

This ensemble performed for:
- Daily monastic rituals
- Tsam dance ceremonies (see Section 7)
- Festival celebrations
- Funeral rites
- Consecration of new buildings or religious objects

The repertoire was largely derived from Tibetan Buddhist musical traditions but gradually incorporated Mongolian melodic sensibilities, creating a hybrid sacred music tradition unique to Mongolia.

## 7. Tsam (Cham) Dance: The Buddhist Percussion Ensemble

### Historical Origins

The Tsam dance traces its roots to Tibetan Cham dance, first performed in Buddhist monasteries as ritual drama. In the 16th century, Mongolian Buddhist monks adopted and adapted this tradition, incorporating distinctive elements of Mongolian culture. The Tsam became one of the most spectacular expressions of Buddhist practice in Mongolia, performed annually at major monasteries.

### The Tsam Orchestra: Instruments

The religious orchestra (*khuree duulakh*) consists of:

**Percussion**:
- **Khengereg** (big drum): The foundational pulse instrument, a large frame drum or barrel drum providing the basic rhythmic framework
- **Tsan** (cymbals): Both small and large varieties, made from bell metal with knob-like handgrips. The cymbals provide rhythmic accents and textural variety
- **Duudaram** (gong-chimes): Pitched metallic percussion providing harmonic color
- **Damar** (double-headed hourglass drum): A small hand drum of Tibetan origin, played with a rapid wrist-rotating technique that produces a buzzing, continuous sound

**Wind**:
- **Ikh buree** (grand trumpet): Massive copper or brass trumpets, sometimes 3-4 meters long, producing deep, resonant tones
- **Bishguur** (shawm): A double-reed instrument providing melodic content
- **Ganlin** (human-bone trumpet): A short trumpet traditionally made from a human femur, used in wrathful deity invocations
- **Dun buree** (conch trumpet): The Buddhist ceremonial conch shell

### Rhythmic Structure of the Tsam

The Tsam orchestra does not play in the metrically regular fashion of Western percussion ensembles. Instead, the rhythm serves a liturgical function, with each character in the ritual drama performing "individual actions either to invoke, bless, or demolish a spiritual essence through the appropriate musical rhythms."

**Basic Tsam percussion pattern (processional)**:
```
Khengereg:  X . . . X . . . X . . . X . . .
Tsan:       . . X . . . X . . . X . . . X .
Damar:      x x x x x x x x x x x x x x x x

(Slow, stately tempo, ~60 BPM)
```

**Wrathful deity entrance**:
```
Khengereg:  X . X . X X . . X . X . X X . .
Tsan:       . . . X . . X X . . . X . . X X
Ikh buree:  ~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~
            (sustained drone underneath)

(Accelerating, building tension)
```

**Climactic exorcism passage**:
```
Khengereg:  X X X X X X X X
Tsan:       X . X . X . X .
Damar:      x x x x x x x x
Ganlin:     . . . . X . . .

(Fast, driving, all instruments at maximum intensity)
```

### Ceremonial Context

The Tsam was traditionally performed on the 9th day of the last summer month, commencing at sunrise and continuing until sunset---a full day of ritual performance. The dancers, wearing elaborate masks representing Buddhist deities, demons, and animal spirits, move in prescribed patterns while the orchestra provides the rhythmic framework that governs their movements.

The deep, resonant sounds of the ensemble "symbolize the cosmic vibrations that drive away negative energy and invoke blessings." The Tsam was effectively destroyed during the Communist purges of the 1930s, when most of Mongolia's monasteries were demolished and thousands of monks were executed. It has been partially revived since 1990.

## 8. The Morin Khuur (Horse-Head Fiddle): Rhythmic Bowing Patterns

While the morin khuur is a bowed string instrument rather than a percussion instrument, its rhythmic bowing patterns constitute one of the most important sources of rhythmic vocabulary in Mongolian music. The instrument's unique technique---strings are not pressed against the neck but pushed lightly from the side by a knuckle or nail---produces a fluid, slightly indeterminate pitch that emphasizes rhythm and timbre over precise intonation.

### Horse Gait Imitations

The morin khuur repertoire includes an entire genre of pieces that directly imitate horse movements. The most important include:

**Walk (*Alkhakh*)**:
```
Bow direction: Down  Up  Down  Up
Rhythm:        X  .  x  .  X  .  x  .
               1     2     3     4
               (even, steady, 4/4 feel)
```

**Amble (*Joroo*)**:
The *joroo* (ambler) is a prized gait in Mongolian horse culture. The piece "Joroo Morinii Yavdal" (Gait of the Ambler) imitates this distinctive two-beat lateral gait:
```
Bow:    Down-Up  Down-Up  Down-Up  Down-Up
Rhythm: X  x .  X  x .  X  x .  X  x .
        1  + 2  1  + 2  1  + 2  1  + 2
        (syncopated, lilting, compound feel)
```

**Trot (*Zhoro*)**:
```
Bow:    Down  Up  Down  Up
Rhythm: X  x  X  x  X  x  X  x
        1  +  2  +  3  +  4  +
        (quick, even, doubled tempo)
```

**Canter/Gallop (*Guyeh / Khataruulakh*)**:
```
Bow:    Down-Down-Up  Down-Down-Up
Rhythm: X  x  x  .   X  x  x  .
        1  +  a  2    1  +  a  2
        (triple subdivision, asymmetric, driving)

At full gallop:
        X x x X x x X x x X x x
        1 + a 2 + a 3 + a 4 + a
        (fast triplet feel, relentless forward motion)
```

### Neighing and Other Horse Sounds

The morin khuur can produce remarkably convincing imitations of horse vocalizations through techniques including:
- **Harmonics**: Light finger pressure produces whistling overtones mimicking a horse's whinny
- **Glissando**: Sliding the finger along the string imitates the rising pitch of a neigh
- **Rapid bowing**: Quick, short bow strokes near the bridge create a rough, breathy tone evoking a horse's snort

### Cultural Significance

The morin khuur was inscribed on UNESCO's Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2008 (as "Traditional Music of the Morin Khuur"). UNESCO recognizes the instrument as "most suitable to accompany the traditional long and short songs and Mongolian classical dance bielgee." The carved horse head at the top of the instrument's neck, with the five herding animals often represented below it, makes the instrument a physical symbol of the unity between Mongolian musical and pastoral culture.

## 9. The Yatga (Zither) and Its Rhythmic Patterns

### Construction and History

The yatga is a Mongolian plucked zither with roots extending over 2,000 years, related to the Chinese guzheng, Japanese koto, and Korean gayageum. Historical forms had 10 to 12 strings; the modern instrument typically has 21 nylon or gut strings stretched across a long, trapezoidal wooden soundbox with movable bridges.

The instrument's documented Mongolian use dates to the 12th-14th centuries, during the Mongol Empire period, when it served in both court and folk contexts.

### Playing Technique

The performer plucks the strings with the fingernails of the right hand while the left hand presses on the strings behind the bridges to bend pitches, add vibrato, and create ornamental slides. The left hand can also play bass strings without plectrums.

### Rhythmic Characteristics

The yatga's rhythmic vocabulary is distinctive within Mongolian music:

**Galloping pattern** (*zhavkhlant*):
```
Right hand: X . x . X . x . X . x .
            1   +   2   +   3   +
            (alternating strong/weak plucks)
```
Descriptions of yatga playing note that "the rhythm is brisk, the plucking is powerful, as if the hooves of horses are galloping, showing the generosity and passion of Mongolian knights."

**Arpeggio pattern** (court music style):
```
Right hand: x x x x X . . . x x x x X . . .
            (rapid ascending run to accented note)
```

**Epic recitation accompaniment** (*tuuli*):
```
Right hand: X . . x . . X . . x . .
            1       +     2       +
            (sparse, improvisatory, with glissandi)
```

The yatga's role in Mongolian music straddles the functional boundary between melody and rhythm instruments. In ensemble contexts, particularly in the modern State Academic Orchestra of Mongolia, the yatga provides both harmonic foundation and rhythmic drive through its plucked articulation.

# PART III: VOCAL TRADITIONS WITH RHYTHMIC SIGNIFICANCE

## 10. Khoomii (Throat Singing): Rhythmic Patterns Through Overtone Manipulation

### The Technique

Khoomii (also spelled khoomei, xoomii, or hoomii) is the art of producing two or more simultaneous pitches from a single voice. The singer produces a low, sustained fundamental drone while shaping the mouth, throat, tongue, and lips to selectively amplify specific overtones from the harmonic series, making them audible as a separate, higher melody.

The fundamental pitch is typically around E or G below middle C. By manipulating the oral cavity, the singer can isolate and amplify the 6th, 7th, 8th, 9th, 10th, 12th, and 13th partials of this fundamental, creating a whistling melody in the upper register while the drone continues below.

Khoomii was inscribed on the UNESCO Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2010 as "Mongolian traditional art of Khoomei."

### Major Styles

The two primary classifications are:

1. **Kharkhiraa** (deep khoomii): The singer produces a normal-register drone while emphasizing the *undertone* or subharmonic one octave below the fundamental. This creates an extraordinarily deep, rattling bass tone---a sound that seems to come from the earth itself.

2. **Isgeree khoomii** (whistled khoomii): The overtones *above* the fundamental are emphasized, creating a high-pitched, flute-like whistle that floats above the drone. This is the more commonly recognized style internationally.

Additional sub-styles include:
- **Shakhaa**: Nasal resonance-based technique
- **Khamryn khoomii**: Nasal khoomii
- **Bagalzuuryn khoomii**: Throat khoomii
- **Tseejiin khoomii**: Chest khoomii
- **Khevliin khoomii**: Belly khoomii

### Rhythmic Dimensions of Khoomii

While khoomii is often discussed primarily in terms of pitch and timbre, it possesses significant rhythmic dimensions:

**Articulatory rhythm**: The rapid manipulation of tongue position, lip shape, and jaw angle to shift between overtones creates a rhythmic pattern of timbral change. Each shift from one overtone to the next is a discrete articulatory event, and the speed and regularity of these shifts constitutes a rhythmic layer independent of the fundamental drone.

**The "galloping" pattern**:
```
Overtone melody: . 8 . 9 . 10 . 9 . 8 . 9 . 10 . 9
Fundamental:     X ~~~ ~~~ ~~~ ~~~ ~~~ ~~~ ~~~ ~~~ ~~~
                 (continuous drone)

Articulation:    t  t  t  t  t  t  t  t  t  t  t  t
                 (tongue movements creating rhythmic pulse
                  above the sustained drone)
```

**Rhythmic breathing pattern**: The need to maintain a continuous drone while manipulating overtones creates a characteristic respiratory rhythm. Skilled throat singers develop circular breathing techniques or learn to phrase their overtone melodies within the constraints of a single breath, creating a rhythmic structure dictated by human physiology.

**Imitation of natural rhythms**: Khoomii performers frequently imitate natural sounds---the rhythm of a stream, wind in grass, a horse's gallop---through the patterning of their overtone shifts. This connects the rhythmic dimension of throat singing directly to the environmental soundscape discussed in Section 1.

## 11. Urtyn Duu (Long Song): Free Rhythm and the Breath

### Characteristics

The urtyn duu is the supreme vocal art of Mongolia---a genre that embodies the aesthetic values of the steppe more completely than any other form. Its defining characteristics are:

- **Free rhythm**: No fixed meter, no regular pulse. The rhythm follows the breath of the singer and the semantic weight of the words.
- **Extremely slow tempo**: Each syllable is extended through elaborate ornamental melismas that can last 30 seconds or more.
- **Wide vocal range**: Often spanning two octaves or more.
- **Abundant ornamentation**: Including trills, turns, portamenti, and falsetto passages.
- **Pentatonic basis**: Built on the anhemitonic pentatonic scale.

A four-minute urtyn duu performance may contain only ten words. The "long" in "long song" refers not primarily to the total duration (though performances can be quite extended) but to the extreme elongation of each syllable.

### Rhythmic Freedom as Aesthetic Choice

The absence of fixed meter in urtyn duu is not a lack of rhythmic organization but a positive aesthetic choice. The rhythm is determined by:

1. **Breath**: The singer's respiratory capacity shapes phrase length. Long phrases demonstrate mastery and are aesthetically valued.
2. **Semantic weight**: Important words receive longer, more elaborately ornamented treatment. The rhythm thus reflects meaning rather than abstract metrical pattern.
3. **Emotional arc**: Acceleration and deceleration serve expressive purposes rather than structural ones.
4. **Landscape**: Performers describe adapting their rhythmic pacing to the performance context---more spacious phrasing outdoors on the steppe, slightly more compact in the ger.

### Rhythmic notation attempt (illustrative):
```
Text:      Mo  -  -  -  -  -  -  n  -  -  gol  -  -  -  -  -
Rhythm:    ~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~  ~~~  ~~~~~~~~~~~~~~
Ornament:     ~^v~^v~^v~           ~^v~^v~^v~^v~
Breath:    [=======================================] (one breath)

(Where ~^v represents melismatic ornamentation:
 rising, falling, turning within a single syllable)
```

### UNESCO Recognition

The urtyn duu was proclaimed a UNESCO Masterpiece of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity in 2005 (joint nomination by Mongolia and China, reflecting the tradition's vitality in both countries) and inscribed on the Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity in 2008.

### Social Context

Urtyn duu functions as "a ritual form of expression associated with important celebrations and festivities," performed at:
- Weddings
- Inauguration of a new ger
- Birth celebrations
- Branding of foals
- Naadam festivals
- Seasonal transitions

## 12. Bogino Duu (Short Song): Fixed Rhythm and Dance

### Characteristics

The bogino duu ("short song") is the complement and counterpart to the urtyn duu. Where the long song is free, spacious, and melismatic, the short song is:

- **Fixed rhythm**: Clear, regular meter, typically duple (2/4 or 4/4)
- **Syllabic text-setting**: One note per syllable, in contrast to the long song's melismatic approach
- **Moderate tempo**: Suitable for dancing and communal singing
- **Strophic form**: Repeated verse structure with memorable refrains
- **Narrower range**: Generally contained within an octave or less
- **Pentatonic**: Sharing the modal language of the long song but with less ornamentation

### Rhythmic Structures

**Basic bogino duu pattern (dance song)**:
```
Vocal:      X  x  X  x  | X  x  X  x  |
            1  2  3  4  | 1  2  3  4  |
Accent:     >     >     | >     >     |
            (clear duple meter, strong beats on 1 and 3)
```

**Refrain pattern (communal)**:
```
Solo:       X  x  X  x  X  x  X  .  |
Chorus:     .  .  .  .  .  .  .  X  | X  x  X  x  X  x  X  . |
            (call-and-response structure)
```

### Social Function

Bogino duu emerged as a practical counterpart to the urtyn duu---shorter, easily memorizable strophic pieces suitable for dances, feasts, and communal celebrations. Where the urtyn duu is a solo art demanding years of training, the bogino duu is communal, participatory, and accessible. Its "directness, singability, and strong sense of pulse make it accessible to both trained vocalists and community singers."

## 13. Magtaal (Praise Songs): Rhythmic Declamation

### Form and Function

The magtaal is a lyrical praise hymn with shamanic poetry origins, performed at all key occasions in nomadic life. It is a genre devoted to:
- Sacred mountains and landscapes
- Great wrestlers (especially at Naadam)
- Victorious racehorses
- Historical heroes (particularly Chinggis Khan)
- Lamaist deities and spirits of nature

The magtaal occupies a middle ground between speech and song---a form of heightened, rhythmic declamation that can range from near-spoken recitation to fully melodic singing.

### Rhythmic Characteristics

**Declamatory magtaal (wrestler's praise)**:
```
Voice:   X  x  x  X  .  X  x  x  X  .
         "Ar - slan - ii  mag - taal..."
         (speech-rhythm based, strong accents on key words)
```

**Melodic magtaal (horse praise)**:
```
Voice:   X  .  x  .  X  x  .  x  X  .  .  .
         (more sustained, with melodic contour,
          but still syllabically driven)
```

**Chinggis Khaanii Magtaal** (In Praise of Chinggis Khan) is one of the most famous examples, frequently performed with throat singing and morin khuur accompaniment. Its rhythmic profile combines the free timing of the urtyn duu tradition with the declamatory force of praise poetry.

### Performance Context

"No Naadam worth its name would be complete without a magtaal." The form remains vibrant in contemporary Mongolia, with performers like Batzorig Vaanchig bringing magtaal to international audiences through social media and concert performance.

### Folk Dance Rhythms

# PART IV: SPECIFIC RHYTHMIC PATTERNS

## 15. Horse Gait Rhythms: The Fundamental Rhythmic Vocabulary

The horse gait is to Mongolian rhythm what the heartbeat is to Western rhythm or the tabla bol is to Hindustani rhythm: the foundational organizing principle from which all other rhythmic patterns derive. Mongolians spend hours daily on horseback, and the rhythms of different gaits are internalized from childhood as deeply as the rhythms of speech.

### Walk (Alkhakh): Steady, Even 4/4

The walk is the most basic gait---four even beats, each hoof striking the ground in sequence.

```
Grid (16th-note resolution, tempo ~80 BPM):

Beat:    |  1  .  .  .  |  2  .  .  .  |  3  .  .  .  |  4  .  .  .  |
L-Hind:  |  X  .  .  .  |  .  .  .  .  |  .  .  .  .  |  .  .  .  .  |
L-Fore:  |  .  .  .  .  |  X  .  .  .  |  .  .  .  .  |  .  .  .  .  |
R-Hind:  |  .  .  .  .  |  .  .  .  .  |  X  .  .  .  |  .  .  .  .  |
R-Fore:  |  .  .  .  .  |  .  .  .  .  |  .  .  .  .  |  X  .  .  .  |

Composite: |  X  .  .  .  |  X  .  .  .  |  X  .  .  .  |  X  .  .  .  |

Musical feel: Even quarter notes, steady and grounding.
Used in: Opening sections of morin khuur pieces, walking songs,
         the shaman's initial drum rhythm.
```

### Amble/Pace (Joroo): Syncopated 2-Beat

The *joroo* (ambler) is a lateral gait where both legs on the same side move together, creating a distinctive side-to-side rocking motion prized in Mongolian horse culture.

```
Grid (16th-note resolution, tempo ~100 BPM):

Beat:    |  1  .  +  .  |  2  .  +  .  |
L-side:  |  X  .  .  .  |  .  .  .  .  |
R-side:  |  .  .  X  .  |  .  .  .  .  |
L-side:  |  .  .  .  .  |  X  .  .  .  |
R-side:  |  .  .  .  .  |  .  .  X  .  |

Composite: |  X  .  X  .  |  X  .  X  .  |

Musical feel: Rocking, lilting, with a syncopated quality
              from the lateral weight shift.
Used in: "Joroo Morinii Yavdal" and related morin khuur pieces,
         medium-tempo dance songs.
```

### Trot (Zhoro): Quick, Even 2-Beat

The trot is a diagonal gait---front left/back right strike together, then front right/back left.

```
Grid (8th-note resolution, tempo ~120 BPM):

Beat:    |  1  +  |  2  +  |  1  +  |  2  +  |
Diag-1:  |  X  .  |  .  .  |  X  .  |  .  .  |
Diag-2:  |  .  .  |  X  .  |  .  .  |  X  .  |

Composite: |  X  .  |  X  .  |  X  .  |  X  .  |

Musical feel: Brisk, bouncy, even eighth notes at a quick tempo.
              The suspension between hoof-strikes gives a light,
              airborne quality.
Used in: Medium-fast morin khuur pieces, riding songs,
         the accelerating phase of shamanic drumming.
```

### Canter (Guyeh): 3-Beat Asymmetric

The canter is a three-beat gait with an asymmetric feel---two quick beats followed by a longer stride.

```
Grid (triplet resolution, tempo ~140 BPM):

Beat:    |  1  +  a  |  2  +  a  |
Hind-L:  |  X  .  .  |  .  .  .  |
Hind-R+  |  .  X  .  |  .  .  .  |
 Fore-L: |           |           |
Fore-R:  |  .  .  X  |  .  .  .  |
(air):   |  .  .  .  |  X  .  .  |

Composite: |  X  x  x  |  .  .  .  |
           or in 3/4 feel:
           |  X  .  x  .  x  .  |

Musical feel: Rolling, triple-time, with a "da-da-DUM" quality
              that naturally suggests 6/8 or 3/4 meter.
Used in: Galloping songs, energetic morin khuur pieces,
         wrestling ceremony music.
```

### Gallop (Khataruulakh): Fast, Driving

The full gallop is the fastest gait, with all four hooves leaving the ground simultaneously in each stride.

```
Grid (16th-note resolution, tempo ~160-200 BPM):

Beat:    |  1  e  +  a  |  2  e  +  a  |
Hind:    |  X  .  .  .  |  X  .  .  .  |
Fore:    |  .  .  X  .  |  .  .  X  .  |
(air):   |  .  .  .  X  |  .  .  .  X  |

Composite: |  X  .  X  X  |  X  .  X  X  |

Simplified musical pattern:
|: X . X X | X . X X | X . X X | X . X X :|

Musical feel: Relentless, driving, with a slight swing from the
              uneven spacing of hoof-strikes. The moment of
              full suspension (all hooves airborne) creates a
              characteristic "lift" in the pattern.
Used in: Climactic sections of morin khuur pieces, the peak
         of shamanic trance drumming, Naadam horse race
         celebrations, The Hu's heaviest passages.
```

### The Horse-Gait Continuum in Practice

In actual musical performance, these gaits are not isolated patterns but points on a continuum. A morin khuur player performing "Jonon Khar" (a famous horse-praise piece) will transition smoothly through walk, trot, canter, and gallop, creating a rhythmic narrative that mirrors the experience of riding---the gradual building of speed, the exhilaration of the gallop, and the eventual return to the walk.

## 16. Biyelgee (Body Dance): The Rhythmic Vocabulary of the Upper Body

### UNESCO Status

Biyelgee was inscribed on the UNESCO List of Intangible Cultural Heritage in Need of Urgent Safeguarding in 2009, reflecting both its cultural significance and the threat to its survival. It is performed by dancers from ethnic groups in Mongolia's Khovd and Uvs provinces (Western Mongolia), particularly among the Oirat peoples (Bayad, Durvud, Zakhchin, Torguud, Uriankhai, and others).

### Spatial Constraints and Rhythmic Innovation

Biyelgee's most distinctive feature is its spatial limitation: traditionally performed "on the limited space before the hearth" inside the ger, the dance "makes practically no use of the feet." Dancers perform seated, kneeling, or half-sitting, using "only the upper part of their bodies"---shoulders, arms, hands, head, and torso---to create rhythmic expression.

This spatial constraint drove remarkable rhythmic innovation. Unable to use footwork (the primary rhythmic resource in most world dance traditions), biyelgee developed an extraordinarily sophisticated vocabulary of upper-body rhythm:

### Movement Vocabulary and Associated Rhythms

**Shoulder pulse (*murnii khodel*)**:
```
Pattern:  R  L  R  L  R  L  R  L
Accent:   >  .  >  .  >  .  >  .
Tempo:    ~120-160 BPM

(Alternating shoulder lifts/drops creating
 a visible rhythmic pulse)
```

**Hand flutter (*garyn develtei*)**:
```
Pattern:  RL RL RL RL RL RL RL RL
          (rapid alternating wrist rotations)
Accent:   >     >     >     >
          1  +  2  +  3  +  4  +

(Creates a tremolo-like rhythmic texture)
```

**Torso sway (*bienii khodel*)**:
```
Pattern:  R . . L . . R . . L . .
          1 2 3 1 2 3 1 2 3 1 2 3
          (slow, weighted side-to-side movement in 3/4)
```

### Narrative Content

The movements encode specific aspects of Mongolian nomadic life:
- **Milking**: Rhythmic hand motions mimicking the milking of mares
- **Riding**: Shoulder and torso movements evoking horseback riding
- **Archery**: Arm extensions and releases
- **Felting**: The rhythmic work of making felt
- **Cooking**: Stirring, kneading, serving motions

Each of these narrative gestures carries its own inherent rhythm, derived from the actual physical rhythm of the activity being depicted.

### Musical Accompaniment

Biyelgee is accompanied by:
- **Tovshuur**: A two-stringed plucked lute, providing the primary rhythmic pulse
- **Morin khuur**: Adding melodic and rhythmic layers
- **Ikhel**: A bowed instrument

The interplay between the dancer's visible body rhythms and the audible instrumental rhythms creates a poly-rhythmic texture unique to the biyelgee tradition.

## 17. Wrestling Ceremony Rhythms: The Eagle Dance

### The Devekh (Eagle Dance)

Before and after each match at Naadam and other wrestling competitions, every wrestler performs the *devekh*---a ritualized dance based on the flight of the mythical Garuda bird (in Mongolian tradition) or the eagle/falcon. The dance symbolizes "power, bravery, grace and invincibility."

### Origins and Variations

The devekh has its origins in shamanistic rituals predating Buddhism. Regional variations exist:
- **Mongolia (Khalkha tradition)**: The wrestler imitates falcons or phoenixes taking flight (*devekh* = "to soar")
- **Inner Mongolia (Ujumchin tradition)**: The dance mimics lions or tigers prancing (*magshikh*)

### Movement and Rhythm

The devekh involves:
1. Spreading the arms wide like wings
2. Slow, swooping movements mimicking flight
3. Turning in prescribed directions (always going north first, as the elders and highest-honored guests sit in the north)
4. Gradual lowering toward the ground and rising back up
5. Powerful stamping steps that serve as both dance and warm-up

**Rhythmic structure of the devekh**:
```
Phase 1 - Approach (slow, stately):
Steps:    X . . . X . . . X . . . X . . .
Arms:     ~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~
          (sustained, wing-like spreading)

Phase 2 - Soaring (moderate, swooping):
Steps:    X . X . X . . . X . X . X . . .
Arms:     /  \  /  \  ~~~~~~~~  /  \  /  \  ~~~~~~~~
          (rising and falling with swooping arc)

Phase 3 - Power display (accelerating):
Steps:    X . X . X . X . X X X X
Stamps:   . . . . . . . . X X X X
          (building to powerful stamping finish)
```

### Musical Accompaniment

During the devekh, the wrestler's trainer (*zasuul*) sings a praise song (*uukhai*) proclaiming the wrestler's lineage, victories, and heroic qualities. This creates a rhythmic interplay between the sung text (with its speech-derived rhythms) and the dancer's physical movements.

## 18. Naadam Festival Music

### Overview

Naadam ("games") is Mongolia's most important cultural festival, held annually July 11-13 in Ulaanbaatar and in smaller local celebrations throughout the country. Inscribed on UNESCO's Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity, Naadam is organized around the "three manly sports" (*eriin gurvan naadam*): wrestling, archery, and horse racing.

### Opening Ceremony

The festival opens with an elaborate ceremony featuring dancers, musicians, athletes, and horse riders in the National Sports Stadium. The musical program follows a prescribed sequence:

1. **Urtyn duu**: The holiday traditionally begins with a long song---the most formal and prestigious vocal genre opening the most formal cultural event
2. **Biyelgee**: Traditional body dance follows
3. **Orchestral performance**: The state ensemble performs patriotic and traditional works
4. **Magtaal**: Praise songs honoring Mongolia, its history, and its heroes

### Wrestling Music

Each wrestling match is framed by:
- The *devekh* (eagle dance) before the match (see Section 17)
- The *zasuul*'s praise song during the dance
- The victory *devekh* by the winner after the match
- Magtaal for accomplished wrestlers who achieve higher titles

### Horse Racing Music

The Naadam horse races are unique: cross-country events of 15-30 kilometers with child jockeys aged 6-12. Musical elements include:
- **Pre-race songs**: Blessing songs for the horses
- **Giingoo**: A rhythmic chant shouted by the child jockeys during the race, believed to encourage the horses. The word "giingoo" itself is an onomatopoeia of hoofbeats.
- **Post-race magtaal**: Elaborate praise songs for the winning horses, particularly for the five top finishers
- **Consolation song**: A special song sung to the horse that finishes last, encouraging it to do better next year---a characteristically Mongolian blend of competition and compassion

### Archery Music

Mongolian archery at Naadam uses *sur* (small woven cylinders) as targets rather than bullseye boards. When an archer hits a target, judges raise their hands and sing "*uukhai!*"---a traditional exclamation. This collective sung response creates its own rhythmic punctuation of the archery competition.

# PART V: HISTORICAL CONTEXT

## 19. The Mongol Empire: War Drums and Cavalry Coordination

### Chinggis Khan and Military Music

The military music of the Mongol Empire (1206-1368) represents one of history's most effective uses of percussion as a battlefield technology. The Mongol army's use of drums was not merely ceremonial---it was a sophisticated tactical communication system governing the movements of the largest contiguous land empire ever assembled.

### The War Drums (Guangu / Khengereg)

The primary Mongol war drum was a massive kettledrum, known variously as:
- **Khengereg** (Mongolian)
- **Guangu** (Chinese historical records, literally "jar drum")
- **Dagu** (large drum)
- **Jungu** (military drum)

These drums were mounted on the backs of camels or carried on carts, positioning them above the battlefield for maximum acoustic projection. Marco Polo recorded that before battle, "all soldiers would wait for the sound of the Guangu from their commander, and when the commander's drum played, most soldiers would play their own instruments and sing."

### Tactical Signaling

The drum signals governed:
- **Charge**: A rapid, continuous roll signaling the cavalry to advance
- **Halt**: A specific rhythmic pattern commanding troops to stop
- **Regroup**: A repeated pattern calling scattered units to reform
- **Retreat**: A distinctive signal for withdrawal
- **Directional commands**: Different patterns indicating left, right, center movements

```
Charge signal (reconstructed):
|: X X X X X X X X :|  (continuous rapid roll)

Halt signal:
X X X . . . X X X . . . X X X . . .
(three-stroke groups with silence between)

Regroup:
X . X . X . . . . . X . X . X . . . . .
(repeated grouping pattern)
```

### Psychological Warfare

The sheer volume of Mongol military drums---amplified by the hundreds of smaller drums carried by individual units---created a wall of sound designed to terrify enemies before combat even began. Historical accounts describe the sound of the Mongol approach as an earthquake-like rumble that demoralized defenders of besieged cities. This is one of the earliest documented examples of what modern military theory calls "shock and awe"---the use of overwhelming sensory assault to break the enemy's will before physical engagement.

### The Tug System

Beyond drums, the Mongol army used yak-tail standards (*tug*) as visual signals to complement the acoustic signaling of drums. The nine-tailed white *tug* of Chinggis Khan himself was a sacred object that combined political, spiritual, and military significance.

## 20. The Mongol Peace (Pax Mongolica): Musical Exchange Across Eurasia

The Mongol Empire at its height (mid-13th to mid-14th century) created conditions for unprecedented cultural exchange across Eurasia. The so-called *Pax Mongolica*---the relative peace and security of travel routes under Mongol protection---facilitated the movement of musicians, instruments, and musical ideas along the Silk Road.

### Musical Exchanges

- **East to West**: Mongolian instrumental techniques, pentatonic melodies, and throat singing traditions spread westward, influencing Turkic musical cultures from Central Asia to Anatolia.
- **West to East**: Persian court music traditions reached the Mongol court, particularly during the Ilkhanate period. The morin khuur may have been influenced by Persian bowed string instruments.
- **China-Mongolia**: The yatga tradition shows deep connections with the Chinese guzheng, and the Mongol Yuan Dynasty (1271-1368) created conditions for extensive Sino-Mongolian musical exchange.
- **Religious music**: Buddhist musical traditions from Tibet, shamanic practices from Siberia, and Islamic musical concepts from Persia all circulated within the Mongol-controlled territories.

The Mongol Empire was history's most effective facilitator of cross-continental musical exchange before the age of electronic media.

---

## 21. Tibetan Buddhist Influence (16th Century Onward)

### The Conversion

The adoption of Tibetan Buddhism as Mongolia's primary religion, beginning with Altan Khan's patronage of the Third Dalai Lama in 1578, fundamentally transformed Mongolian musical culture. The conversion was gradual and never total---shamanic practices persisted alongside Buddhism---but the institutional infrastructure of Buddhist monasteries became the dominant framework for organized musical activity.

### Musical Transformations

1. **The monastery orchestra**: Replaced shamanic drum circles as the primary organized ensemble. Tibetan instruments (dungchen long trumpets, gyaling shawms, silnyen cymbals, damaru hand drums) were adopted and adapted.
2. **Chanting traditions**: Tibetan Buddhist chanting---with its deep, resonant drone and prescribed melodic formulas---influenced Mongolian vocal technique. Some scholars argue that the monastery chanting tradition influenced the development of kharkhiraa (deep throat singing).
3. **The Tsam dance**: Imported from Tibet and adapted to Mongolian cultural context (see Section 7).
4. **Calendar of performance**: The Buddhist liturgical calendar imposed a new temporal structure on Mongolian musical life, with specific musical repertoire associated with specific dates and festivals.
5. **Suppression of shamanism**: Buddhist authorities actively suppressed shamanic practice, driving the shaman's drum underground in many areas. Shamanic drumming survived most strongly in the periphery---northern Mongolia, Buryatia, and remote areas less subject to monastic authority.

## 22. Qing Dynasty (1691-1911): Manchu Rule and Its Impact

### Incorporation into Empire

The Khalkha Mongols of Outer Mongolia submitted to the Qing Dynasty in 1691 after being overrun by the Dzungar Khanate. For the next 220 years, Mongolia existed as a subordinate territory within the Manchu-ruled Qing Empire. Inner Mongolia had been incorporated earlier, in the early 17th century.

### Musical Impact

**The Banner system**: The Manchu replacement of traditional Mongol clan structures with the military-administrative Banner system disrupted traditional patterns of musical patronage and transmission. Musical traditions tied to specific clans were displaced by new social structures.

**Court exchange**: The Qing court maintained a Mongolian music division (*Fanbu Hezou*) within its banquet music ensembles, performing music adapted from the repertoire of earlier Mongol khans. This music was documented by court painters, providing valuable visual records of Mongolian instruments and performance practice, but it also represented the appropriation and institutionalization of Mongol musical culture within a foreign imperial framework.

**Patron-performer dynamics**: Music associations reflected the social hierarchy, with "Manchu-Mongolian nobility learning musical repertoire as patrons of lowly blind itinerant street performers whom they invited to their mansions." This patron-performer relationship created new channels for musical transmission while reinforcing social stratification.

**Monastic flourishing**: Paradoxically, the Qing period saw the flourishing of Mongolian Buddhist monasteries and their musical traditions. The Manchu rulers promoted Tibetan Buddhism as a means of pacifying the Mongols, and the monastery system reached its greatest extent during this period. The musical traditions of the Tsam dance, monastery orchestra, and Buddhist chanting all developed significantly under Qing rule.

**Stifled autonomy**: The Manchu government "stifled any idea of autonomy in Mongolia," and this political suppression inevitably affected the content if not the form of Mongolian music. Heroic songs glorifying Chinggis Khan and the Mongol Empire took on seditious political meaning under foreign rule, becoming coded expressions of national identity and resistance.

---

## 23. Soviet Period (1924-1990): Russification, Nationalization, and the Folk Ensemble Model

### The Socialist Transformation

The Mongolian People's Republic, established in 1924 as the world's second communist state, undertook a comprehensive transformation of cultural life that profoundly affected music. The relationship between Soviet ideology and Mongolian music was complex---neither simple suppression nor simple preservation, but a thorough restructuring.

### Key Policies and Their Musical Effects

**Destruction of monasteries (1930s)**: The most violent impact came in the late 1930s, when the Soviet-aligned government destroyed approximately 700 Buddhist monasteries and executed an estimated 17,000-30,000 monks. This annihilated the institutional infrastructure of Mongolian Buddhist music overnight. The Tsam dance tradition, the monastery orchestra, and the repertoire of Buddhist chanting were devastated.

**Suppression of shamanism**: Shamanic practice, already marginalized by Buddhism, was further driven underground by anti-religious Soviet policies. The shaman's drum became a hidden, forbidden object.

**Nationalization of folk music**: The state categorized diverse regional and ethnic musical forms under the unified label of "folk music" (*ardyn khyogjim*) while constructing a notion of national musical identity. The number of state-supported folk music institutions grew from 9 in 1960 to 26 in 1988. This both preserved and distorted tradition: musical forms were maintained but standardized, taught in conservatories rather than through oral transmission, and stripped of their ritual and social context.

**The "folk ensemble" model**: Following the Soviet pattern, Mongolia created large folk ensembles modeled on the Russian folk orchestra. Traditional instruments were modified for ensemble performance:
- The morin khuur was standardized in size and tuning
- New instruments (such as bass and alto morin khuurs) were created to fill orchestral roles
- Ensemble arrangements replaced solo and small-group performance as the prestige format

**"Mongolian opera"**: The state created a Mongolian opera tradition, grafting European operatic conventions onto Mongolian musical material. This produced hybrid works that satisfied ideological requirements for "progressive" art while incorporating traditional melodies and instruments.

**Restricted repertoire**: "The types of music that could be performed were restricted." Religious music was banned. Music celebrating pre-socialist Mongolian history (particularly the Mongol Empire) was controlled. New compositions were required to reflect socialist values.

### Ambiguities

The Soviet period was not merely destructive. State support created professional opportunities for musicians, preserved (in altered form) traditions that might otherwise have been lost to urbanization, and produced accomplished performers trained in both Mongolian and Western techniques. The modern Mongolian conservatory system, the State Morin Khuur Ensemble, and the tradition of large-scale folk orchestral performance all have their origins in this period.

However, the cost was the severing of music from its living context---the steppe, the ger, the herding life, the shamanic ceremony---and its repackaging as national cultural commodity.

## 24. Modern Mongolia (1990-Present): Revival, UNESCO, and the Digital Age

### The Democratic Transition

The collapse of Soviet influence in 1990 and Mongolia's transition to democracy triggered a cultural renaissance. The key developments include:

**Shamanic revival**: From near-extinction under communism, shamanic practice has experienced a dramatic revival. Shamans now practice openly in Ulaanbaatar, and the shaman's drum has returned to public life. This revival is complex---partly genuine spiritual renewal, partly nationalist identity politics, partly response to the dislocations of capitalism.

**Buddhist restoration**: Monasteries have been rebuilt and Tsam dance performances revived, though the unbroken lineage of transmission was severed during the communist period. Contemporary Tsam performances are reconstructions based on historical records, photographs, and the memories of elderly survivors.

**UNESCO recognition**: Mongolia has been extraordinarily successful in securing UNESCO recognition for its musical traditions:
- **2003**: Morin khuur traditional music (Masterpiece of Oral and Intangible Heritage)
- **2005**: Urtyn duu/long song (Masterpiece, joint with China)
- **2008**: Morin khuur (Representative List)
- **2008**: Urtyn duu (Representative List)
- **2009**: Biyelgee (Urgent Safeguarding List)
- **2010**: Khoomii/throat singing (Representative List)
- **2010**: Naadam (Representative List)

This concentration of UNESCO recognitions reflects both the richness of Mongolian musical heritage and the urgency of preservation efforts.

**Revival of Chinggis Khan**: The figure of Chinggis Khan, suppressed during the communist period, has been rehabilitated as the supreme national hero. Magtaal praise songs honoring Chinggis Khan, once forbidden, are now central to national cultural expression.

**The conservatory vs. the steppe**: A tension exists between conservatory-trained musicians who perform in concert halls and traditional practitioners whose music is rooted in pastoral life. The question of "authenticity" is fiercely debated: is a morin khuur player trained at the Ulaanbaatar conservatory performing "traditional" music, or a modern interpretation of tradition?

# PART VI: MODERN MONGOLIAN MUSIC

## 25. The Hu: Hunnu Rock and the Global Stage

### Formation and Style

The Hu, formed in 2016 in Ulaanbaatar, is the most internationally successful Mongolian musical act in history. The band's self-described genre, "hunnu rock," is named after the Xiongnu (Hunnu)---the ancient nomadic empire based in Mongolia that may be ancestral to the Huns.

Their sound fuses:
- **Morin khuur** (horse-head fiddle)
- **Tovshuur** (Mongolian guitar/lute)
- **Tumur khuur** (jaw harp)
- **Khoomii** (throat singing, both kharkhiraa and isgeree styles)
- **Modern rock instrumentation**: Bass guitar, drums, electric guitar

The vocal approach combines "a cavernous bellow that borders on a death-metal growl" with "whistle-like harmonics, an element of traditional throat singing." This is not mere fusion but a synthesis that reveals structural affinities between Mongolian traditional music and heavy metal: both favor low fundamental frequencies, overtone-rich timbres, pentatonic scales, and rhythmic power.

### Rhythmic Analysis

The Hu's rhythmic approach bridges the horse-gait rhythms of Mongolian tradition with rock drumming:

**"Yuve Yuve Yu" (basic groove)**:
```
Kick:    X . . X . . X . X . . X . . X .
Snare:   . . . . X . . . . . . . X . . .
Morin K: ~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~
Throat:  X-------X-------X-------X-------
         (driving 4/4 with gallop-influenced kick pattern)
```

**"Wolf Totem" (verse)**:
```
Kick:    X . X . X . X . X . X . X . X .
Snare:   . . . . X . . . . . . . X . . .
Tovshur: x x x x x x x x x x x x x x x x
         (relentless 8th-note pulse evoking horse gallop)
```

### Global Impact

- April 2019: "Wolf Totem" reached #1 on Billboard's Hard Rock Digital Song Sales---the first Mongolian act to top any Billboard chart
- November 2019: Received the Order of Genghis Khan, Mongolia's highest state honor
- Their music appeared in *Star Wars Jedi: Fallen Order* (2019) and *Star Wars Jedi: Survivor* (2023)
- Supported Iron Maiden on their 2024 North American tour
- Performed at Glastonbury Festival, Coachella, and other major international festivals

The Hu have been officially designated as Cultural Envoys of Mongolia and UNESCO Artists for Peace, formalizing their role as instruments of national soft power.

## 26. Hanggai: Inner Mongolian Folk Rock

Hanggai represents the distinct musical perspective of Inner Mongolia (China). The band creates "a medium through which it is effectively able to express the voice of a generation yearning to reconnect with its ethnic roots by playing Mongolian folk songs while also incorporating modes of popular music."

### Musical Approach

The core sound is built on:
- **Morin khuur** and **tovshuur** (traditional instruments)
- **Throat singing** in Mongolian
- **Electric guitars, bass, computer programming** (modern elements)
- Influences from "Pink Floyd, Radiohead, Rage Against the Machine"

Many songs are adaptations of Mongolian folk songs, recontextualized within rock and punk frameworks. The band's approach to rhythm combines the regular meters of rock with the free-time sensibility of urtyn duu, creating passages where the groove dissolves into spacious, steppe-like expanses before reforming.

### Significance

Hanggai occupies a politically complex position as ethnic Mongolian musicians operating within China's cultural landscape. Their music functions as both artistic expression and cultural preservation, keeping Mongolian musical traditions alive for Inner Mongolian audiences who face different preservation challenges than their counterparts in independent Mongolia.

## 27. Altan Urag: Progressive Mongolian Rock

Altan Urag (meaning "Golden Lineage," a reference to the descendants of Chinggis Khan) was formed in 2002 and is considered the pioneer of Mongolian folk-rock.

### Instrumentation

Their performances typically include:
- **Morin khuur** (horse-head fiddle)
- **Ikh khuur** (grand horse-head fiddle---a larger, bass-register version)
- **Bishguur** (traditional horn)
- **Yoochin** (hammered dulcimer)
- **Khoomii** (throat singing)
- **Long song vocals**
- Modern rock instruments

### Film and Television

Their music has featured prominently in:
- *Khadak* (2006)
- *Mongol* (2007, Academy Award-nominated)
- *Marco Polo* (Netflix series)

These placements have introduced Mongolian musical traditions to international audiences through cinematic context.

## 28. Electronic Music in Ulaanbaatar

### The Scene

A thriving electronic music scene has emerged in Ulaanbaatar, with DJs and producers experimenting with the fusion of traditional Mongolian sounds and electronic production.

### Key Figures

- **Kh.Biliguudei**: Named one of 10 influential DJs from rising EDM scenes worldwide (ranked 7th). Studied music in Beijing and Berlin, and is considered a pioneer of the Mongolian underground electronic music scene.
- **BEME**: A DJ/Producer from Los Angeles and Mongolia whose "musical influences come from Techno and Mongolian infused long songs incorporating khoomi (throat singing), Morin Khuur, and other traditional music from Mongolia."

### Festivals

The **INTRO International Electronic Music Festival** in Ulaanbaatar (launched 2019) presented 9 international DJs and 45 local DJs, demonstrating the scale of Mongolia's electronic music community. The **Playtime Festival** and **Spirit of Gobi** festival further represent the growing intersection of electronic music and Mongolian landscape.

### Rhythmic Implications

The entry of traditional Mongolian rhythms into electronic music production represents a new chapter in the horse-gait rhythm tradition. When a producer samples a morin khuur gallop pattern and loops it within a 4/4 techno framework, or layers throat singing overtones above a 128 BPM kick drum, the ancient rhythmic vocabulary encounters entirely new contexts of time, space, and reception.

## 29. Inner Mongolian Hip-Hop

### The Scene

Hip-hop has emerged as a significant cultural force among young ethnic Mongolians in Inner Mongolia (China), serving as a vehicle for cultural identity expression in a politically constrained environment.

### Cultural Elements

Mongolian hip-hop incorporates:
- **Khoomii** (throat singing) as a vocal texture
- **Morin khuur** samples
- References to Mongolian history, landscape, and cultural identity
- Lyrics in the Mongolian language

### Political Dimension

Inner Mongolian hip-hop operates in a sensitive political space. Rapper **Syrlig** was detained by authorities in 2011 after writing "Stand up, Inner Mongolians!" MC **Bater** of the group **PTS** (one of Inner Mongolia's most successful hip-hop groups) stated: "There are some lyrics we'd sing in shows, but if we published them we'd be arrested."

The scene survives through a combination of "low profile" and "a degree of self-censorship." Against the backdrop of Chinese cultural assimilation policies, "Mongolian poetry and song are being mobilized as a tacit and non-violent mode of resistance."

### Rhythmic Innovation

Inner Mongolian hip-hop represents a new synthesis: the rhythmic patterns of American hip-hop production (trap hi-hats, 808 kick drums, syncopated snares) combined with Mongolian vocal techniques and instrumental samples. The result is music where horse-gait rhythms and trap beats coexist---a rhythmic dialogue between the steppe and the global digital present.

# PART VII: CROSS-CULTURAL CONNECTIONS

## 30. Tuvan Music: The Sister Tradition

### Shared Roots

Tuva (officially the Tuva Republic, a federal subject of Russia) lies directly north of Mongolia, in the Altai-Sayan mountain region of southern Siberia. The Tuvan and Mongolian musical traditions are sister traditions sharing common roots in the nomadic herding cultures of Inner Asia.

Throat singing (called *khoomei* in Tuvan, *khoomii* in Mongolian) "originated among the indigenous Turko-Mongol tribes of the Altai and Sayan mountains of southern Siberia and western Mongolia." The practice is found across "Tuva, Mongolia, Inner Mongolia, Xinjiang Mongol regions, Buryatia, Kalmykia and Altay"---a geographic spread that maps the historical range of Mongol-Turkic pastoral nomadism.

### Huun-Huur-Tu

**Huun-Huur-Tu** (the name means "sun propeller," referring to vertical light ray separation at sunrise/sunset) is the most internationally recognized Tuvan musical ensemble, active since the 1990s. They have been the primary international ambassadors of Tuvan throat singing, much as The Hu have become for Mongolian music.

### Differences Between Tuvan and Mongolian Traditions

While sharing fundamental techniques, the two traditions have developed distinctive characteristics:
- **Tuvan khoomei** tends toward more varied sub-styles (sygyt, kargyraa, borbangnadyr, ezengileer, khoomei proper)
- **Mongolian khoomii** emphasizes the two major categories (kharkhiraa and isgeree) with regional sub-variations
- Tuvan music has been more extensively documented by ethnomusicologists due to earlier international exposure
- Mongolian tradition places greater emphasis on the connection to horse-gait rhythms

### Gender and Innovation

In Tuva, **Tyva Kyzy** (founded 1998) is an all-female folk ensemble performing Tuvan throat singing---"the first and only women's group in Tuva that performs all styles of Tuvan throat singing." This ensemble has challenged the traditional taboo against female throat singing.

## 31. Kazakh Music: The Dombra Tradition Within Mongolian Borders

### The Kazakh Community in Bayan-Olgii

Mongolia's Bayan-Olgii province in the far west is home to a significant ethnic Kazakh population who maintain their own musical traditions within the Mongolian state. The most important instrument is the **dombra** (also *dombra*, *dombura*)---a pear-shaped, long-necked, two-stringed plucked lute.

### The Dombra as Cultural Marker

Research shows that "Kazakh residents in Bayan-Olgii province use production and performance of the long-necked lute dombra to construct and reconstruct Kazakh perceptions of identity, homeland and place." The dombra is not merely a musical instrument but a technology of identity: "I take my dombra and sing to remember my homeland."

### Dombra Kuy

The art of **dombra kuy** refers to "a short solo composition performed on a traditional pear-shaped, long-necked, two-stringed, plucked musical instrument." It was inscribed on the UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2014. These compositions "aim to connect people to their historic roots and traditions through classical and improvised pieces that engage the audience at a spiritual and emotional level."

### Aitys Performance

The *aitys* is a competitive improvised poetry/song performance where **aqyns** (poet-singers) accompany themselves on dombra. This tradition, with its rapid improvisation and competitive structure, adds a uniquely Kazakh rhythmic sensibility to Mongolia's musical landscape.

## 32. Chinese-Mongolian Fusion: Navigating Dual Identity

### The Inner Mongolian Condition

Ethnic Mongolian musicians in Inner Mongolia navigate a complex dual identity---culturally Mongolian but politically Chinese. This position produces distinctive musical strategies:

- **Cultural export as preservation**: "Mongolian musicians in China view exporting their culture as essential for preservation amidst sociopolitical pressures."
- **Strategic incorporation**: Some musicians incorporate elements of Han Chinese musical traditions to reach broader audiences and satisfy government cultural policies.
- **Digital resistance**: Social media and streaming platforms allow Inner Mongolian musicians to reach global Mongolian audiences without passing through Chinese cultural gatekeepers.

### Musical Implications

The Chinese government's cultural policies, "framed as promoting unity among ethnic groups, often limit the freedom of Mongolian music, incorporating elements of Han culture into the narrative." This produces a tension between:
- State-sponsored "ethnic minority" musical performances (colorful, depoliticized, folkloric)
- Underground and diaspora musical expressions (politically charged, identity-affirming)
- Fusion projects that genuinely bridge Chinese and Mongolian musical traditions

The urtyn duu's joint UNESCO nomination by both Mongolia and China reflects the shared heritage and the political complexity: the same musical tradition is claimed by two nations with vastly different relationships to Mongolian cultural identity.

---

## 33. The Central Asian Continuum

### Shared Musical DNA

Mongolian music exists within a broader Central Asian musical continuum stretching from Anatolia to Manchuria. Shared elements include:

- **Throat singing**: Found from the Altai Mountains through Mongolia, Tuva, and into the Inuit traditions of the Arctic (though with significant technical differences)
- **Horse-gait rhythms**: Common to all equestrian nomadic cultures of the steppe
- **Pentatonic systems**: The anhemitonic pentatonic scale is the common harmonic language from Turkey through Central Asia to Mongolia and beyond
- **Bowed string instruments**: The morin khuur belongs to a family of horse-hair bowed fiddles found across Inner Asia (igil in Tuva, kobyz in Kazakhstan, erhu in China)
- **Plucked lutes**: The tovshuur/dombra/dutar/saz family extends from the Balkans through the Silk Road to Mongolia
- **Shamanic drumming**: Frame drum traditions connect Mongolian, Siberian, Turkic, and even Sami (northern European) shamanic practices

### The Silk Road Legacy

The Silk Road was not just a trade route but a musical highway. Instruments, scales, techniques, and repertoire traveled in both directions. The Mongol Empire's facilitation of this exchange (see Section 20) accelerated a process that had been occurring for millennia, creating a musical web that connects Mongolian throat singing to Turkish *maqam*, Kazakh *kuy* to Chinese *guzheng* music, and Siberian shamanic drumming to the devotional drumming of Sufi Islam.

# PART VIII: SOCIOPOLITICAL DIMENSIONS

## 1. Nomadism and Urbanization

### The Great Migration

The single greatest threat to Mongolian musical traditions tied to landscape is the rapid urbanization of Mongolian society. Nearly half of Mongolia's 3.3 million people now live in Ulaanbaatar, and the population of the capital "almost tripled" between 1991 and 2020. This migration is driven by:

- **Climate catastrophe**: Mongolia is warming at nearly three times the global rate. Devastating *dzud* (winter storms) have killed millions of livestock, destroying the economic basis of nomadic life.
- **Overgrazing**: Degradation of pastureland reduces the carrying capacity for herds.
- **Education and opportunity**: Families migrate to the city for schooling and economic prospects.

### Musical Consequences

Music that evolved to be performed on horseback, projected across open steppe, tied to the rhythms of herding and the seasonal cycle, faces an existential challenge when transplanted to apartment blocks in Ulaanbaatar's ger districts.

However, the relationship is not simply one of loss. Paradoxically, "traditional music thrives in cities, with musically inclined former nomads, freed from the task of survival, wearing traditional dress and playing weddings and festivals." The urban context creates new audiences, new performance contexts (concert halls, recording studios, social media), and new economic models for musicians. Traditional music programs in universities and high schools provide institutional support for transmission that the steppe cannot.

The question is whether urban traditional music, severed from its original context, retains the qualities that made it meaningful---or whether it becomes a performance of nostalgia rather than a living tradition.

---

## 2. Cultural Diplomacy: Music as Soft Power

### Mongolia's Strategic Position

Sandwiched between Russia and China, Mongolia is a small, landlocked democracy seeking to maintain sovereignty and international visibility. Music has become a primary tool of cultural diplomacy.

### The Hu as Diplomatic Instrument

The Hu's designation as Cultural Envoys of Mongolia formalizes a strategy that emerged organically: using the global appeal of their music to raise Mongolia's international profile. Their impact includes:

- Drawing international media attention to Mongolia
- Attracting tourism (The Hu's music has been studied as an influence on Mongolia's "tourism nation branding program")
- Creating cultural connections that support diplomatic relationships
- Projecting an image of Mongolia as culturally rich, historically significant, and contemporary

Mongolia's climb to 108th place in the 2025 Brand Finance Global Soft Power Index is partly attributed to cultural visibility generated by musical ambassadors.

### The UNESCO Strategy

Mongolia's systematic pursuit of UNESCO recognitions for its musical traditions (six inscriptions between 2003 and 2010) represents a deliberate cultural diplomacy strategy: using international institutional frameworks to validate and protect national cultural heritage.

---

## 3. Inner Mongolia vs. Mongolia: Divergent Preservation Challenges

### Mongolia (Independent)

**Advantages**:
- State sovereignty allows autonomous cultural policy
- Government support for traditional arts
- Freedom to celebrate Chinggis Khan and Mongol imperial heritage
- UNESCO engagement
- Growing international cultural economy (tourism, recording, performance)

**Challenges**:
- Urbanization threatens steppe-based traditions
- Commercialization risks superficiality
- Limited economic resources for cultural programs
- The gap between conservatory and pastoral traditions

### Inner Mongolia (China)

**Advantages**:
- Chinese government investment in ethnic minority cultural preservation (on its own terms)
- Access to the massive Chinese cultural market
- Academic institutions studying Mongolian music
- The urtyn duu UNESCO inscription includes China's Mongolian tradition

**Challenges**:
- Government policies encouraging nomads to "abandon their grazing lands for flats in the cities" accelerate cultural disruption
- Cultural expression is constrained by political sensitivities around ethnic identity and separatism
- The Chinese framing of Mongolian culture as an "ethnic minority" tradition within a unified national narrative
- Self-censorship in musical expression (see Section 29 on hip-hop)
- Language policies threatening the Mongolian script and language, which are foundational to musical traditions

The divergent political contexts mean that the "same" musical tradition---throat singing, long song, morin khuur---exists in fundamentally different social conditions on either side of the border, producing different pressures, different adaptations, and different futures.

## 4. Language and Music: How Mongolian Shapes Musical Rhythm

### Prosodic Characteristics

Khalkha Mongolian (the standard dialect of Mongolia) possesses distinctive prosodic features that shape musical rhythm:

- **Vowel harmony**: A system where vowels within a word must agree in phonological class, creating long chains of harmonically related vowels. This produces a flowing, vowel-rich sound that is naturally suited to melismatic singing.
- **No lexical stress**: Research on Khalkha Mongolian prosody has proposed that "there is no lexical stress"---meaning that syllables are not inherently strong or weak as in English. This absence of fixed stress contributes to the free-rhythm quality of urtyn duu, where no syllable has an inherent metrical position.
- **Long vs. short vowels**: The phonemic contrast between long and short vowels (with long vowels having approximately 208% the duration of short) provides a natural rhythmic resource. In music, this duration contrast maps onto the distinction between long and short notes.
- **Complex syllable structure**: Mongolian allows clusters of up to three consonants syllable-finally, creating rhythmic density at the ends of syllables that contrasts with the open vowel beginnings.

### Musical Implications

The most distinctive feature of Mongolian vocal music---"each syllable of text is extended for a long duration"---directly reflects the language's phonological character. Mongolian's rich vowel system and absence of fixed stress create conditions where syllabic extension feels natural rather than forced, and where rhythm can follow meaning rather than abstract metrical pattern.

---

## 5. Climate Change: Environmental Threats to Musical Traditions

### The Ecological Crisis

Mongolia faces a cascading ecological crisis that directly threatens the material basis of its musical traditions:

- **Temperature rise**: 2.1 degrees Celsius over the past 70 years, with projections exceeding 5 degrees Celsius by century's end under high-emissions scenarios
- **Dzud intensification**: The devastating winter storms that kill livestock are increasing in frequency and severity due to climate change
- **Desertification**: Expanding desert threatens grasslands that support pastoral nomadism
- **Water stress**: Rivers and lakes are shrinking, affecting the ecosystems that support herding life

### Musical Implications

When herds die in a dzud, the economic basis of nomadic life is destroyed, and families migrate to the city. With them go:
- The daily rhythms of herding that generate musical form
- The acoustic environment of the steppe that shaped vocal technique
- The social contexts (seasonal camps, herding cooperation, ger gatherings) in which music was transmitted
- The experiential knowledge of horse gaits, animal sounds, and landscape that infuses musical content with meaning

Climate change is not merely an environmental threat to Mongolia---it is a musicological catastrophe, destroying the lived experience from which an entire musical civilization derives its content and form.

The camel, already the rarest of the five herding animals, is particularly threatened. The loss of camel herding traditions would take with it an entire repertoire of camel songs, camel-gait rhythms, and the specific musical practices associated with camel husbandry.

---

## 6. Gender in Mongolian Music

### Historical Restrictions

Mongolian and broader Inner Asian musical traditions have historically imposed gender restrictions on certain practices:

**Throat singing**: The strongest taboo concerned women performing throat singing. "Female performance of throat-singing was thought to cause infertility or to bring misfortune on the performers' menfolk for seven generations." Custom and superstition discouraged women from throat singing, though this "varied by region."

**Certain instruments**: While specific documentation is limited, traditions of gendered instrument assignment existed, with the morin khuur and certain percussion instruments associated primarily with male performers.

**Shamanic practice**: While both male (*bo*) and female (*udgan*) shamans exist in Mongolian tradition, and the *udgan* has a long and respected history, specific musical and ritual practices within shamanism have been gendered.

### Breaking Barriers

Since the late 20th century, women have increasingly challenged musical gender restrictions:

- **Valentina Salchak** performed throat singing in public in 1979, one of the earliest documented challenges to the taboo
- **Tyva Kyzy** (founded 1998, Tuva) is the first and only women's group performing all styles of Tuvan throat singing
- In Mongolia, female throat singers have emerged in both traditional and contemporary contexts
- Women are now prominent in all areas of Mongolian music, from conservatory performance to folk-rock fusion

The gender dynamics of Mongolian music remain an active area of social change, with traditional restrictions being contested and renegotiated in the context of broader social transformations.
