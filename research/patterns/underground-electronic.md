# Underground Electronic Step-Sequencer Patterns

> Patterns for underground, regional, and emerging electronic movements.
> Each position = one 16th note step. | = bar line.

```
Legend:
x = hit (normal), X = accented, o = open tone, . = rest
G = ghost note, + = closed/muted
KK = kick, SN = snare, CP = clap, CH = closed hi-hat, OH = open hi-hat
RS = rimshot, CB = cowbell, PR = perc, SB = sub bass
TB = tabla/darbuka, BL = balafon/marimba, TM = tumbi/melodic perc
Each position = one 16th note step | = bar line
```

## MENA ELECTRONIC

### 1. Mahraganat / Electro Shaabi (Egypt)

**Origin:** Cairo working-class neighborhoods, mid-2000s
**BPM:** 100-115 (program at 105)
**Time Signature:** 4/4
**Difficulty:** 3/5
**Cultural Context:** Egyptian street festival music. The rhythm derives from
traditional maqsoum/baladi patterns but electronified with heavy kicks and
synthetic tabla. Wedding DJs layer auto-tuned vocals over these driving beats.
The doum-tek (low-high) pattern of the darbuka is translated to kick-snare.

```
         |1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a|
KK       |X . . . . . X . X . . . . . . .|
SN       |. . . . X . . . . . . . X . . .|
CP       |. . . . X . . . . . . . X . . .|
CH       |x . x . x . x . x . x . x . x .|
OH       |. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . x|
TB(hi)   |. . x . . . . x . . x . . x . .|
TB(lo)   |. . . . . . x . . . . . . . x .|
PR       |. . . . . . . . . . x . . . . .|
```

**Notes:** The tabla hits (TB hi/lo) create the characteristic shaabi swing.
The kick pattern follows a maqsoum-derived structure (doum-tek-tek-doum-tek).
Add rolls and fills on the tabla for authentic feel. CP doubles SN for punch.

### 2. Electronic Dabke (Omar Souleyman Style)

**Origin:** Levant (Syria/Lebanon/Palestine), evolved 2000s
**BPM:** 170-180 (fast galloping 6/8 feel counted in 4/4)
**Time Signature:** 4/4 (with 6/8 feel via triplet grouping)
**Difficulty:** 4/5
**Cultural Context:** Omar Souleyman and keyboardist Rizan Sa'id transformed
traditional dabke wedding music into electronic form. The rhythm drives the
communal stamping dance (dabke). The "laff" rhythm is a syncopated dum-tak
punch. At high BPMs this creates an exhilarating galloping feel.

```
         |1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a|
KK       |X . . X . . X . . . X . X . . .|
SN       |. . . . . . . . X . . . . . X .|
CH       |x x x x x x x x x x x x x x x x|
OH       |. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .|
TB(hi)   |. . x . . x . . . x . . . . . x|
TB(lo)   |x . . . x . . x . . . x . x . .|
RS       |. . . . . . . . x . . . . . x .|
```

**Notes:** At 170+ BPM, the 16th notes become very fast -- this creates the
characteristic galloping feel. The kick pattern (X..X..X...X.X...) gives the
asymmetric drive of dabke. TB(lo) represents the doum (bass drum hit) of the
tableh baladi. Reduce to 8th-note CH at lower tempos.

### 3. Shamstep (47Soul Style, Dabke + Electronic)

**Origin:** Coined by 47Soul (Palestine/Jordan), 2013
**BPM:** 120-130
**Time Signature:** 4/4
**Difficulty:** 3/5
**Cultural Context:** Shamstep fuses dabke's stamping rhythmic foundation with
analog synthesizers, drum machines, and hip-hop/reggae influences. The name
combines "Sham" (Arabic for Levant) with "step." More accessible BPM than
pure dabke, designed for international audiences while keeping the core groove.

```
         |1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a|
KK       |X . . . . . X . X . . . . . X .|
SN       |. . . . X . . . . . . . X . . .|
CP       |. . . . X . . . . . . . X . . .|
CH       |x . x . x . x . x . x . x . x .|
OH       |. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . x|
PR(shak) |. x . x . x . x . x . x . x . x|
TB(hi)   |. . . x . . . . . . . x . . . .|
SB       |X . . . . . X . X . . . . . X .|
```

**Notes:** The shaker (PR) provides reggae-influenced off-beat push. The kick
retains dabke's syncopated stamp pattern. SB (sub bass) follows kick for
electronic weight. The reduced tabla and added shaker differentiate shamstep
from pure dabke -- it's friendlier to non-Arabic audiences while keeping the groove.

## SOUTH ASIAN ELECTRONIC

### 4. Desi Bass / Indian Bass (Nucleya Style)

**Origin:** India, pioneered ~2012 by Nucleya
**BPM:** 110-140 (program at 120)
**Time Signature:** 4/4
**Difficulty:** 3/5
**Cultural Context:** Nucleya pioneered "desi bass" by fusing Indian street
sounds (dhol drum, tumbi, Bollywood samples) with electronic bass music.
The dhol's characteristic "dha-ge-na-ti-na" pattern translates to the
kick-snare interaction. Heavy 808 sub-bass underneath connects to global
bass music while Indian percussion keeps the identity.

```
         |1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a|
KK       |X . . . . . X X X . . . . . X .|
SN       |. . . . X . . . . . . . X . . .|
CP       |. . . . X . . . . . . . X . . .|
CH       |x . x . x . x . x . x . x . x .|
OH       |. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . x|
TM(dhol) |X . . x . x X . X . . x . x . .|
PR(shak) |. . x . . . x . . . x . . . x .|
SB       |X . . . . . . X X . . . . . . .|
```

**Notes:** TM(dhol) represents the dhol drum pattern -- the heart of Punjabi
bhangra rhythm adapted to electronic context. "Dha" hits (accented) align
with kick, "ge/na" hits (ghost) fill the spaces. The double kick at positions
7-8 (X X) creates the dhol's characteristic gallop. SB provides the heavy
808 bass weight that distinguishes desi bass from traditional bhangra.

### 5. Bollywood EDM (Festival Remix Style)

**Origin:** India, festival/remix culture, 2010s
**BPM:** 128-132
**Time Signature:** 4/4
**Difficulty:** 2/5
**Cultural Context:** Bollywood EDM takes iconic Hindi film music melodies and
drops them into festival-style progressive house/big room structures. The
rhythm is more conventional 4/4 EDM with Indian percussion layered on top.
Massive at Indian music festivals and NRI (non-resident Indian) events.

```
         |1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a|
KK       |X . . . X . . . X . . . X . . .|
SN       |. . . . X . . . . . . . X . . .|
CP       |. . . . X . . . . . . . X . . .|
CH       |x . x . x . x . x . x . x . x .|
OH       |. . . . . . . x . . . . . . . x|
TM(tabl) |. . x . . . . . . . x . . . x .|
CB       |. . . . . . . . x . . . . . . .|
PR       |x . . . x . . . x . . . x . . .|
```

**Notes:** Four-on-the-floor kick is standard EDM. The tabla hits (TM) and
cowbell add Indian flavor to an otherwise Western dance pattern. During
builds, remove kick and add tabla rolls. During drops, bring back the full
four-on-the-floor kick with all the weight.

### 6. Sufi Electronic (Qawwali + Electronic Build)

**Origin:** Pakistan/India, modern fusion
**BPM:** 90-100 (slow build), accelerating to 120+
**Time Signature:** 4/4 (with 6/8 feel in clapping)
**Difficulty:** 4/5
**Cultural Context:** Sufi electronic takes the rhythmic framework of qawwali
(devotional music, especially the Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan tradition) and maps
it to electronic production. Qawwali builds from slow, meditative openings to
ecstatic, fast climaxes -- this structure translates perfectly to electronic
builds and drops. The taali (hand-clapping) pattern is central.

```
         |1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a|
KK       |X . . . . . . . X . . . . . . .|
SN       |. . . . . . . . . . . . X . . .|
CP       |X . . X . . X . X . . X . . X .|
CH       |. . x . . x . . . . x . . x . .|
TM(tabl) |x . . x . . . x x . . x . . . x|
PR(dholak)|. . x . x . . . . . x . x . . .|
SB       |X . . . . . . . X . . . . . . .|
```

**Notes:** The CP pattern represents the qawwali taali (clapping) -- this is
the engine of the groove. The rhythm is sparse and meditative at the base
tempo (90 BPM). For the ecstatic build: gradually increase BPM to 120+,
add more CP hits, fill in CH to 16th notes, and add tabla rolls. The
acceleration IS the form -- start slow, end in ecstasy.

## EAST ASIAN ELECTRONIC

### 7. Shanghai Bass (SVBKVLT Style)

**Origin:** Shanghai, China, SVBKVLT label, ~2013+
**BPM:** 130-145
**Time Signature:** 4/4 (often disrupted)
**Difficulty:** 5/5
**Cultural Context:** Shanghai's SVBKVLT label produces hyper-futuristic club
tracks drawing from IDM, noise, and industrial alongside Chinese musical
references. 33EMYBW and Gooooose create frantic, glitchy productions.
The pattern below captures the mutant-club aesthetic -- recognizable
rhythmic framework deliberately disrupted.

```
         |1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a|
KK       |X . . . . . . X . . X . . . . .|
SN       |. . . . X . . . . . . . . X . .|
CP       |. . . . . . . . . . . . X . . .|
CH       |x . x x . x . . x . x . . x x .|
OH       |. . . . . . . x . . . . . . . .|
RS       |. . . . . x . . . . . . . . x .|
PR       |. . x . . . . . . x . . . . . x|
SB       |X . . . . . . X . . X . . . . .|
```

**Notes:** The irregular CH pattern (not a steady pulse) and offset SN/CP
create the "deconstructed" feel. The RS adds metallic texture. This pattern
should be treated as a framework -- SVBKVLT artists constantly mutate and
glitch their patterns in real-time. Add random fills, remove elements
unexpectedly, distort individual hits.

### 8. Thai Molam Electronic

**Origin:** Bangkok/Isan, Thailand, 2009+
**BPM:** 125-140 (program at 130)
**Time Signature:** 4/4
**Difficulty:** 3/5
**Cultural Context:** Molam is the traditional music of Thailand's Isan region.
The Paradise Bangkok Molam International Band pairs elder molam musicians
(khaen, phin) with modern rhythm sections. Electronic molam translates the
khaen's rhythmic drone and phin's cyclical patterns into synth sequences,
with funk and dub influences underneath.

```
         |1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a|
KK       |X . . . . . X . X . . . . . X .|
SN       |. . . . X . . . . . . . X . . .|
CH       |x . x . x . x . x . x . x . x .|
OH       |. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . x|
RS       |. . . . . . . . . . x . . . . .|
PR(khaen)|x . x x . x . x x . x x . x . x|
CB       |. . . . . . . . x . . . . . . .|
SB       |X . . . . . X . . . . . . . X .|
```

**Notes:** PR(khaen) represents the rapid, cyclical pattern of the khaen
(bamboo mouth organ) -- it creates a constant rhythmic texture, almost like
a melodic hi-hat. The kick pattern includes the syncopation characteristic
of Isan music. The dub-influenced SB (sub bass) adds modern weight.
Add delay/echo to RS for psychedelic depth.

### 9. Cambodian Space Disco

**Origin:** Cambodia 1960s-70s / revival 2000s
**BPM:** 120-135 (program at 125)
**Time Signature:** 4/4
**Difficulty:** 2/5
**Cultural Context:** Revival of Cambodia's pre-Khmer Rouge psychedelic rock
and pop scene. The original music blended surf rock, psychedelia, and Khmer
vocal traditions. Modern electronic versions sample vintage recordings and
add beats. The drum pattern has a surf/garage rock feel with reverb-drenched
simplicity.

```
         |1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a|
KK       |X . . . . . X . X . . . X . . .|
SN       |. . . . X . . . . . . . X . . .|
CH       |x . x . x . x . x . x . x . x .|
OH       |. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . x|
RS       |. . x . . . . . . . x . . . . .|
CB       |x . . . x . . . x . . . x . . .|
PR(tamb) |. x . x . x . x . x . x . x . x|
```

**Notes:** The pattern evokes 1960s garage rock simplicity. CB plays a
steady pulse reminiscent of surf music cowbell. The tambourine (PR) provides
constant 8th-note motion. RS adds the characteristic rimshot crack of
60s-70s Cambodian recordings. Apply heavy spring reverb and tremolo
to the full mix for authentic feel. Fuzz and phaser on instruments.

## LATIN UNDERGROUND

### 10. Guaracha / Aleteo (Circuit Style)

**Origin:** Colombia, late 2000s
**BPM:** 128-132 (program at 130)
**Time Signature:** 4/4
**Difficulty:** 2/5
**Cultural Context:** Guaracha (zapateo/aleteo) fuses tribal house with cumbia
elements. The "aleteo" (fluttering) refers to the twinkling synth patterns
that mimic bird-like movements. The rhythm is designed for high-energy Latin
club nights and beach parties. Circuit-style builds create tension before
explosive drops.

```
         |1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a|
KK       |X . . . X . . . X . . . X . . .|
SN       |. . . . X . . . . . . . X . . .|
CP       |. . . . X . . . . . . . X . . .|
CH       |x . x . x . x . x . x . x . x .|
OH       |. . . . . . . x . . . . . . . x|
PR(conga)|x . . x . . x . x . . x . . x .|
CB       |. . x . . . x . . . x . . . x .|
PR(tamb) |. x . x . x . x . x . x . x . x|
```

**Notes:** Four-on-the-floor kick is the tribal house foundation. Conga
pattern adds Latin percussive flavor. Cowbell provides the tribal accent.
During builds: strip to just kick, CH, and a rising synth. During drops:
bring everything back with full percussion. The "aleteo" effect is
created by the synth, not the drums -- add fluttering arpeggiated synths.

### 11. Neo-Perreo (Deconstructed Reggaeton)

**Origin:** Chile/Argentina/Spain, ~2016-2018
**BPM:** 90-105 (program at 95)
**Time Signature:** 4/4
**Difficulty:** 3/5
**Cultural Context:** Neo-perreo revisits classic reggaeton's dembow riddim
through an experimental, abrasive lens. Coined by Tomasa del Real and
Ms Nina. Sinister synths replace bright tones, copious auto-tune, and
dark atmospheres. Feminist and queer-forward reclamation of reggaeton.

```
         |1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a|
KK       |X . . X . . X . . . X . . . X .|
SN       |. . . X . . . . X . . . . X . .|
CP       |. . . . . . . . X . . . . . . .|
CH       |x . x . x . x . x . x . x . x .|
OH       |. . . . . . . x . . . . . . . x|
PR       |. x . . . x . . . x . . . x . .|
SB       |X . . X . . X . . . X . . . X .|
```

**Notes:** The KK+SN pattern is the dembow riddim (boom-ba-boom-boom-ba)
-- the DNA of reggaeton. The difference from standard reggaeton: the CP is
sparse (not doubling every snare), creating negative space. The SB follows
kick for that heavy, dark bass weight. Distort the SB and add bitcrushing
for the neo-perreo aesthetic. Auto-tune everything.

### 12. Cumbia Digital (ZZK Records Style)

**Origin:** Buenos Aires, Argentina, ZZK Records, ~2007
**BPM:** 90-105 (program at 95)
**Time Signature:** 2/4 cumbia feel (written in 4/4)
**Difficulty:** 3/5
**Cultural Context:** ZZK Records artists take Colombian cumbia and cumbia
villera, mix with reggaeton, dancehall, hip-hop, and electronic beats.
Chancha Via Circuito's "slow-rolling, dubby, psychedelic" approach defines
the sound. Heavy dub effects (reverb, delay, echo) are essential.

```
         |1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a|
KK       |X . . . . . X . X . . . . . X .|
SN       |. . . . X . . . . . . . X . . .|
CH       |. . x . . . x . . . x . . . x .|
OH       |. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . x|
PR(guiro)|x . x x . x x . x . x x . x x .|
PR(conga)|. . . . x . . . . x . . x . . .|
RS       |. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .|
SB       |X . . . . . X . . . . . . . X .|
```

**Notes:** The guiro pattern (PR) is essential -- it provides cumbia's
characteristic scraping rhythm, the backbone of the genre. CH plays only
on off-beats (the cumbia "lift"). The kick and sub bass create the
"slow-rolling" feel of digital cumbia. Apply HEAVY dub delay to SN and RS.
Use reverb generously on everything. The dub effects are not optional --
they define the ZZK sound.

## AFRICAN UNDERGROUND

### 13. Balani Show (Mali, Distorted)

**Origin:** Bamako, Mali, late 1990s
**BPM:** 150-170 (program at 160)
**Time Signature:** 4/4
**Difficulty:** 4/5
**Cultural Context:** Balani Show replaces djembe with drum machine and samples
looped balafon (wooden xylophone) patterns. At 170 BPM, the music is
intense and relentless. Sound is often deliberately distorted and blown out
through overdriven PA systems. Solar-powered sound systems enable parties
in neighborhoods without electricity. Named after the balafon itself.

```
         |1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a|
KK       |X . . . X . . . X . . . X . X .|
SN       |. . . . X . . . . . . . X . . .|
CH       |x x x x x x x x x x x x x x x x|
PR(balaf)|X . x X . x X . x X . x X . x .|
BL(lo)   |. x . . x . . x . . x . . x . x|
CB       |. . . . . . x . . . . . . . x .|
RS       |. . x . . . . . . . x . . . . .|
```

**Notes:** PR(balaf) represents the cut-up, looped balafon samples that
define the genre. BL(lo) is the bass balafon notes. CH runs constant 16ths
at 160 BPM for frantic energy. The KK pattern is the electronic djembe
replacement -- note the double kick at the end (X.X.) which drives into the
next bar. DISTORT EVERYTHING. Clip the master. This music is meant to be
blown out, overdriven, raw.

### 14. Afro-House (Black Coffee Style)

**Origin:** South Africa, 1990s
**BPM:** 120-125 (program at 122)
**Time Signature:** 4/4
**Difficulty:** 3/5
**Cultural Context:** Black Coffee pioneered a deep, soulful strain of
afro-house that won a Grammy in 2022. The rhythm combines classic house's
four-on-the-floor kick with rich African percussion -- congas, bongos,
shakers. Unlike amapiano, afro-house is deeper, more meditative, built
for long DJ sets. Warm, spiritual, organic-sounding.

```
         |1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a|
KK       |X . . . X . . . X . . . X . . .|
SN       |. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .|
CP       |. . . . X . . . . . . . X . . .|
CH       |x . x . x . x . x . x . x . x .|
OH       |. . . . . . . x . . . . . . . x|
PR(conga)|x . . x . . x . . x . x . . x .|
PR(shak) |. x . x . x . x . x . x . x . x|
PR(bongo)|. . x . . . . . . . x . . . . .|
```

**Notes:** Four-on-the-floor KK is the house foundation. NO snare -- just a
clap on 2 and 4, keeping things deep and restrained. The conga pattern
provides African rhythmic complexity. The shaker runs constant 8ths for
forward motion. Bongo is sparse -- just accents. For Black Coffee's style:
keep it MINIMAL. Less is more. Every element should breathe. Warm pads,
vocal chants, and organic textures complete the picture.

### 15. Alte (Nigerian Alternative)

**Origin:** Lagos, Nigeria, ~2007-2010s
**BPM:** 95-115 (program at 105)
**Time Signature:** 4/4
**Difficulty:** 3/5
**Cultural Context:** Alte is Nigeria's alternative music movement --
genre-fluid, internet-savvy, lo-fi. Artists like Cruel Santino and Odunsi
blend afrobeats, dancehall, R&B, and indie. The production is more spacious
and atmospheric than mainstream afrobeats, with experimental vocal
processing and a deliberately anti-pop sensibility.

```
         |1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a|
KK       |X . . . . . X . . . X . . . . .|
SN       |. . . . . . . . X . . . . . . .|
CP       |. . . . X . . . . . . . . . X .|
CH       |x . x . x . x . x . x . x . x .|
OH       |. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . x|
PR(shak) |. x . . . x . . . x . . . x . .|
RS       |. . . . . . . x . . . . . . . .|
SB       |X . . . . . X . . . . . . . . .|
```

**Notes:** The groove is laid-back and syncopated -- SN on beat 3 (not 2)
gives it that off-kilter alte feel. CP moves around rather than doubling SN.
Shaker is sparse, not constant. The overall feel is SPACIOUS -- lots of
negative space. Apply reverb generously. Lo-fi processing (vinyl crackle,
tape hiss) is part of the aesthetic. The kick is soft, not punchy.

### 16. Azonto (Ghana)

**Origin:** Coastal Accra, Ghana, ~2010-2013
**BPM:** 105-115 (program at 108)
**Time Signature:** 4/4
**Difficulty:** 3/5
**Cultural Context:** Azonto emerged from the Ga Kpanlogo dance tradition in
Accra's coastal towns. The gahu timeline rhythm dominated the dance craze.
Producers like E.L. and Killbeatz re-created festival drum patterns
electronically on MPCs. Dance-driven: specific mime-like gestures define
the movement. "Hiplife 2.0" with a new dance vocabulary.

```
         |1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a|
KK       |X . . . . . X . X . . . . . X .|
SN       |. . . . X . . . . . . . X . . .|
CH       |x . x . x . x . x . x . x . x .|
OH       |. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . x|
PR(bell) |x . x . . x . x . . x . . x . x|
PR(shak) |. x . x . x . x . x . x . x . x|
CB       |x . . . . . . . x . . . . . . .|
```

**Notes:** The bell pattern (PR) is based on the gahu timeline -- the
asymmetric bell that organizes the entire groove. This bell is the key
element that makes azonto rhythmically distinct. The shaker provides
constant 8th-note motion. The kick is syncopated in a way that connects
to West African drum ensemble traditions. Dance should be inseparable
from the music -- the tempo allows for the characteristic gesturing moves.

## EASTERN EUROPEAN

### 17. Russian Hardbass

**Origin:** Saint Petersburg, Russia, late 1990s
**BPM:** 150-170 (program at 160)
**Time Signature:** 4/4
**Difficulty:** 2/5
**Cultural Context:** Hardbass evolved when St. Petersburg producers increased
pumping house BPM to 150+. The "donk" metallic bass sound (invented by
Klubbheads, 1997) is the genre's signature. Associated with gopnik youth
culture and squat dancing. Despite meme status, represents a genuine Russian
rave tradition rooted in post-Soviet club culture.

```
         |1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a|
KK       |X . . . X . . . X . . . X . . .|
SN       |. . . . X . . . . . . . X . . .|
CH       |x . x . x . x . x . x . x . x .|
OH       |. . . . . . . x . . . . . . . x|
CB(donk) |. . X . . . X . . . X . . . X .|
SB       |X . . . X . . . X . . . X . . .|
PR       |. . . . . . . . x . . . . . . .|
```

**Notes:** The CB(donk) is THE hardbass sound -- a metallic, resonant bass
hit with overpowered filter resonance. It plays on off-beats, creating the
characteristic "bounce." Four-on-the-floor KK at 160 BPM drives the energy.
SB doubles kick for maximum low-end impact. The pattern is deliberately
SIMPLE -- complexity comes from the speed and the donk's tonal character.
The donk should be pitched/tuned to create a melodic element.

### 18. Romanian Minimal / Micro House (Rominimal)

**Origin:** Romania, 2000s
**BPM:** 124-128 (program at 126)
**Time Signature:** 4/4
**Difficulty:** 4/5
**Cultural Context:** The [a:rpia:r] trio (Rhadoo, Petre Inspirescu, Raresh)
defined rominimal: ultra-reduced arrangements with micro-variations
unfolding over extended runtimes. A philosophical commitment to groove
over everything. Built for long DJ sets, audiophile systems, and after-hours.
Detail over density -- tiny percussive inflections, whispered textures.

```
         |1 e & a 2 e & a 3 e & a 4 e & a|
KK       |X . . . X . . . X . . . X . . .|
SN       |. . . . . . . . . . . . . . . .|
```
