# Modern African Music -- Step-Sequencer Patterns
## Playable Patterns for BeatForge Web Drum Machine

### Notation Key
- Each character = one subdivision position in the cycle
- 16-step patterns = 4/4 time (grouped 4+4+4+4)
- 12-step patterns = 12/8 or 6/8 time (compound meter)
- `x` = hit (normal velocity), `X` = accented hit, `.` = rest
- `G` = ghost note (very low velocity), `o` = open tone, `+` = closed/muted
- `s` = slap, `B` = bass tone, `K` = muted edge
- Pipe `|` marks beat/bar divisions
- KK = kick, SN = snare, HH = closed hi-hat, OH = open hi-hat
- CL = clap, TM = tom, CB = cowbell, SH = shaker
- CG = conga, TB = talking drum (high), TL = talking drum (low)
- AG = agogo/bell, SK = shekere, FE = ferrinho, RR = reco-reco

### === 1. SOUKOUS / CONGOLESE RUMBA ===
Genre: Congolese rumba (rumba section)
Origin: Democratic Republic of Congo (Kinshasa)
BPM: 95-115
Time Sig: 4/4 (with 12/8 feel)
Difficulty: Intermediate
Description: The rumba section of a Congolese song -- the slower, vocal-led opening before the sebene. The drum kit pattern has a lilting, triplet-inflected feel inherited from Cuban son via Congolese reinterpretation. Congas provide the polyrhythmic depth. The cowbell pattern is essential -- it is the timeline around which everything else orbits. Franco, TPOK Jazz, Grand Kalle.

### === 2. SEBENE (Guitar-Break Drum Pattern) ===
Genre: Sebene / Soukous (dance section)
Origin: Democratic Republic of Congo
BPM: 135-165
Time Sig: 4/4
Difficulty: Intermediate
Description: The sebene is the climactic dance section of Congolese rumba/soukous. The drummer shifts into the cavacha pattern -- driving sixteenth-note hi-hats with syncopated snare accents. Everything accelerates. The kick locks into a steady pulse while the snare creates the characteristic cavacha syncopation that Zaiko Langa Langa's drummer Meridjo codified. This is where the guitars go wild and the dance floor ignites.

```

OH:  |....|..x.|....|..x.|  Open hat on "and" of beat 2 and 4

CB:  |x..x|..x.|x..x|..x.|  Cowbell timeline continues

The groove is optimized for the energetic coupe-decale dance style.

Genre: Highlife (Ghanaian guitar-band era)
Origin: Ghana (Accra/Kumasi)
BPM: 105-125

Difficulty: Beginner-Intermediate

This is the sound of E.T. Mensah, Ebo Taylor, and the guitar-band era -- celebratory, cosmopolitan, and irresistibly danceable. The 12/8 feel gives it a swinging quality that distinguishes it from straight 4/4.

### === 7. JUJU (Talking Drum + Kit) ===
Genre: Juju music
Origin: Nigeria (Lagos, Yoruba)
BPM: 105-125
Time Sig: 4/4 with 12/8 polyrhythmic feel
Difficulty: Advanced (due to polyrhythmic density)
Description: Juju is defined by its extraordinary percussion density -- multiple talking drums, congas, shekere, and kit all interlocking. King Sunny Ade's African Beats could have 3+ talking drums playing simultaneously. The talking drum (dundun/gangan) reproduces Yoruba tonal speech patterns, so it "speaks" proverbs and greetings over the groove. The pedal steel guitar (KSA's innovation) provides shimmering texture.

SN:  |....|x...|....|x...|  Snare -- backbeat (restrained)
KK:  |x...|..x.|x...|...x|  Kick -- syncopated
TB:  |.x.x|.x..|.x.x|.x..|  Talking drum high -- melodic/tonal

SK:  |x.x.|x.x.|x.x.|x.x.|  Shekere -- steady eighths

### === 8. FUJI (Shekere + Talking Drum + Congas) ===
Genre: Fuji music
Origin: Nigeria (Southwestern, Yoruba Muslim community)
BPM: 105-140
Time Sig: 4/4
Difficulty: Advanced (percussion-only ensemble, high polyrhythmic complexity)
Description: Fuji is possibly the most percussion-heavy popular music in the world -- NO guitars, no harmonic instruments. Everything is drums and voice. The gangan (talking drum) is the lead voice, sakara provides a bright tonal layer, shekere adds constant rhythmic texture, and agogo anchors the timeline. Ayinde Barrister invented it, Kollington Ayinla modernized it, KWAM 1 took it mainstream.

**Full Pattern (16 steps):**

```
AG:  |x..x|.x.x|..x.|x.x.|  Agogo -- asymmetric timeline
SK:  |x.x.|x.x.|x.x.|x.x.|  Shekere -- steady eighths
TB:  |.x..|x.x.|..x.|x..x|  Gangan (talking drum) -- lead pattern
TL:  |x...|..x.|x...|..x.|  Dundun -- bass pulse
CG-H:|..x.|...x|..x.|...x|  High conga -- offbeat accents
CG-L:|x...|.x..|x...|.x..|  Low conga -- counter pattern
BT:  |...x|x...|...x|x...|  Bata drum -- cross-rhythm accent
AR:  |.x.x|.x.x|.x.x|.x.x|  Aro (iron) -- metallic pulse
```

**Drum Kit Adaptation:**
```

### === 9. MBALAX (Sabar Drum Patterns) ===

The defining rhythmic feature is the anticipated backbeat -- the strongest bass tones land just BEFORE beats 2 and 4, creating an urgent, rushing feel. The nder (lead sabar) plays virtuosic bursts called "bakks." Youssou N'Dour's Super Etoile de Dakar is the gold standard.

### === 11. BIKUTSI (Rapid 6/8) ===
Genre: Bikutsi
Origin: Cameroon (Yaounde, Beti/Ewondo people)
BPM: 135-175
Time Sig: 6/8 (12-step cycle)
Difficulty: Intermediate-Advanced
Description: "Beat the earth" -- a fast, driving 6/8 pattern rooted in women's traditional music. The balafon (xylophone) provides the melodic skeleton while the rhythm has a relentless galloping quality. Les Tetes Brulees electrified this in the 1980s. The bass drum follows a bembe clave pattern, the cymbal attacks the first two notes of each triplet group, and the snare plays cross-rhythms.

## RHYTHMIC CONCEPTS FOR MODERN AFRICAN GENRES

### 1. The Tresillo Across the Continent
The tresillo (3+3+2) appears in an extraordinary number of these genres:
- **Kuduro:** Snare pattern directly based on tresillo
- **Gqom:** Kick drum often follows tresillo or its variants
- **Soukous/Sebene:** Cowbell timeline contains tresillo elements
- **Makossa:** Bass line syncopation rooted in tresillo
- **Gengetone:** Dem bow (reggaeton) pattern is a tresillo derivative

This is not coincidence -- the tresillo originated in sub-Saharan Africa, traveled to the Americas via the slave trade, evolved into the habanera/son clave/dem bow, and is now returning to Africa through global pop music. The circle continues.

### 2. The Four-on-the-Floor Adaptation
When house music arrived in Africa (1980s-1990s), African producers did not simply adopt it -- they transformed it:
- **Kwaito:** Slowed it down to 100-115 BPM
- **Kuduro:** Added tresillo syncopation to the basic pattern
- **Gqom:** Broke the kick pattern into irregular fragments
- **Shangaan electro:** Accelerated it to 180+ BPM
- **Singeli:** Pushed it past 200 BPM into texture territory
- **Amapiano:** Added log drums and jazz-inflected harmonies

In each case, the "four-on-the-floor" became something distinctly African through rhythmic manipulation.

### 3. Speed as a Spectrum
These genres span an extraordinary tempo range:
- **Morna:** 60-90 BPM (contemplative)
- **Kizomba:** 70-95 BPM (sensual)
- **Bongo Flava:** 92-128 BPM (moderate)
- **Gengetone:** 98-118 BPM (moderate)
- **Kwaito:** 100-115 BPM (slow house)
- **Highlife:** 105-130 BPM (moderate dance)
- **Gqom:** 118-130 BPM (dark dance)
- **Benga:** 125-155 BPM (energetic)
- **Kuduro:** 130-145 BPM (fast electronic)
- **Funana:** 132-150 BPM (fast dance)
- **Bikutsi:** 135-175 BPM (rapid 6/8)
- **Sebene:** 135-165 BPM (fast soukous)
- **Shangaan electro:** 180-240 BPM (extreme)
- **Singeli:** 200-300 BPM (the fastest)

### 4. The Broken Beat
Several modern African electronic genres deliberately break the regular kick drum pattern:
- **Gqom:** Stuttering, irregular kick is the genre's identity
- **Singeli:** At extreme tempos, the kick alternates between regular and broken patterns
- **Amapiano:** Uses irregular bass patterns (log drum) rather than steady kick
- **Late-period kuduro:** Increasingly fragmented kick patterns

This "broken beat" aesthetic stands in contrast to the steady timelines of traditional African music and the four-on-the-floor of house. It represents a distinctly 21st-century African electronic sensibility.

### 5. The Percussion-Only Tradition
Some genres maintain a percussion-only or percussion-primary approach:
- **Fuji:** No guitars, no harmonic instruments -- drums and voice only
- **Sabar/Mbalax:** Traditional sabar ensemble is purely percussion
- **Singeli:** Essentially percussion + synth riffs + voice
- **Gqom:** Minimal melody; percussion carries everything

This connects these modern genres back to the deep African tradition of percussion as a complete musical system -- not merely "rhythm section" but the entire musical universe.
